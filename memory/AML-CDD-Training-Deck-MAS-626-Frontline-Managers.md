**For:** Frontline Financial Sales Managers (Corporate Banking)  
**Regulatory Framework:** MAS Notice 626 (Revised 30 June 2025, effective 1 July 2025)  
**Duration:** 30 minutes (hybrid delivery)  
**Audience:** 50+ managers, below-average CDD knowledge  
**Products Covered:** Cash accounts, insurance, remittances, loans

## 🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, participants will be able to:

1. **Explain** why CDD/AML matters (personal liability + client protection)
2. **Identify** the 4 CDD triggers under MAS Notice 626
3. **Recognize** 5+ red flags in corporate banking products
4. **Apply** risk rating methodology (low/medium/high)
5. **Know** when to escalate vs. proceed with onboarding

#### Slide 6: The 4 CDD Triggers (When You Must Do CDD)

**Visual:** 4 trigger icons

**Text:**
```
When Must You Perform CDD?

1. Establishing a Business Relationship
   - Opening a corporate account
   - First-time loan application

2. Occasional Transactions ≥ S$20,000
   - Single transaction or linked transactions
   - Remittances, FX, etc.

3. Suspicion of Money Laundering/Terrorism Financing
   - Regardless of amount
   - Even for existing customers

4. Doubt About Customer Identity
   - Documents appear forged
   - Information doesn't match records
```

**Facilitator Notes:**
> "Memorize this slide. These are the 4 triggers. Most of you will encounter Trigger 1 daily — new account openings. Trigger 2: if someone walks in with S$19,000 cash, then comes back tomorrow with S$19,000, that's structuring — it's still CDD territory. Trigger 3 and 4: if something feels off, trust your gut. That's what we're training today."

**Time:** 2 minutes

---

#### Slide 7: The 5 CDD Elements (What You Must Collect)

**Visual:** 5 checklist items

**Text:**
```
CDD Information Requirements

✓ Customer Identity
  - Name, registration number, registered address
  - For individuals: NRIC/passport, DOB, nationality

✓ Beneficial Ownership (BO)
  - Who ultimately owns or controls the customer?
  - Typically 25%+ ownership or significant control

✓ Purpose & Nature of Business
  - What does this company actually do?
  - Expected transaction patterns

✓ Source of Wealth (SoW)
  - How did the customer accumulate their wealth?
  - Business profits, inheritance, investments, etc.

✓ Source of Funds (SoF)
  - Where is the money for THIS transaction coming from?
  - Bank account, sale of asset, loan, etc.
```

**Facilitator Notes:**
> "These 5 elements are non-negotiable. The most common gaps I see: Beneficial Ownership and Source of Funds. 'Beneficial owner' means the human being at the end of the ownership chain — not another company. Source of Wealth is how they got rich. Source of Funds is where this specific money came from. Different questions. Both matter."

**Time:** 2 minutes

#### Slide 8: Risk Rating Methodology (Low/Medium/High)

**Visual:** Traffic light (green/yellow/red)

**Text:**
```
Customer Risk Rating

LOW (Green)
• Singapore government entities
• Public listed companies (SGX)
• Low-risk products (savings accounts)

MEDIUM (Yellow)
• Private Singapore companies (normal business)
• Standard corporate accounts
• Typical transaction volumes

HIGH (Red) → Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) Required
• PEPs (Politically Exposed Persons)
• High-risk jurisdictions (FATF grey/black list)
• Complex ownership structures
• Unusual transaction patterns
• Cash-intensive businesses
• Dual-use goods traders (new 2025 requirement)
```

**Facilitator Notes:**
> "Risk rating determines your workload. Low risk = standard CDD. High risk = Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD). EDD means: senior management approval, more frequent reviews, deeper SoW/SoF verification. If you're unsure, rate it higher. Better to over-flag than under-flag. MAS penalties are for under-flagging, never for over-flagging."

**Time:** 1.5 minutes

### SECTION 3: RED FLAGS & RISK INDICATORS (8 minutes)

---

#### Slide 9: Red Flags — Corporate Accounts

**Visual:** Warning signs

**Text:**
```
Red Flags: Corporate Account Opening

⚠ Reluctance to provide beneficial ownership information
⚠ Complex ownership structures with no clear business reason
⚠ Registered address is a residential property or virtual office
⚠ Company newly incorporated (< 3 months) with large transaction expectations
⚠ Business activity doesn't match customer profile (e.g., F&B company expecting S$10M/month FX)
⚠ Customer unable to explain nature of business clearly
⚠ Multiple companies with same directors/addresses opening accounts simultaneously
```

**Facilitator Notes:**
> "These are the patterns I see in enforcement cases. The big one: complex ownership with no business reason. If a company has 5 layers of holding companies across 3 jurisdictions, ask why. If the answer is 'tax planning' or 'privacy,' that's not a business reason. Another: newly incorporated companies expecting massive transactions. Red flag. Ask for contracts, invoices, proof of business pipeline."

**Time:** 2 minutes

---

#### Slide 10: Red Flags — Remittances

**Visual:** Wire transfer graphic

**Text:**
```
Red Flags: Remittances & Wire Transfers

⚠ Missing or incomplete originator information
⚠ Frequent transfers to high-risk jurisdictions
⚠ Round-dollar amounts (e.g., exactly S$50,000 repeatedly)
⚠ Transfers inconsistent with customer's business (e.g., manufacturing company sending money to offshore gambling jurisdictions)
⚠ Structuring: Multiple transfers just below S$20,000 threshold
⚠ Rapid movement: Funds in, funds out within 24–48 hours
⚠ Third-party payments with no clear relationship
```

**Facilitator Notes:**
> "Remittances are high-risk. MAS requires full originator information on all cross-border transfers. If it's missing, you must follow up — or reject the transfer. Structuring is when someone breaks a large transaction into smaller ones to avoid thresholds. Three S$19,000 transfers in one week? That's S$57,000. That's CDD territory. Rapid movement — money in, money out immediately — that's layering. Ask why."

**Time:** 2 minutes

---

#### Slide 11: Red Flags — Loans

**Visual:** Loan document graphic

**Text:**
```
Red Flags: Loan Applications

⚠ Early repayment without reasonable explanation
⚠ Loan proceeds to be paid to unrelated third parties
⚠ Collateral source unclear or inconsistent with customer profile
⚠ Customer indifferent to interest rates or loan terms
⚠ Loan purpose vague ("business expansion" with no specifics)
⚠ Guarantor relationship unclear (why is this person/company guaranteeing?)
⚠ Request to disburse to multiple accounts or jurisdictions
```

**Facilitator Notes:**
> "Loan red flags are subtler. Early repayment is a big one — why borrow at 5% and repay immediately? Often it's to legitimize funds. Third-party disbursement: the loan is for Company A, but they want funds sent to Company B in a different jurisdiction. Ask why. If the answer doesn't make business sense, escalate. Customer indifference to terms: legitimate borrowers care about rates. Money launderers don't — they're paying for cleaning, not borrowing."

**Time:** 2 minutes

---

#### Slide 12: Red Flags — Insurance

**Visual:** Insurance policy graphic

**Text:**
```
Red Flags: Insurance Products

⚠ Single-premium policies with large lump-sum payments
⚠ Early surrender requests (within 6–12 months)
⚠ Surrender proceeds to be paid to third parties
⚠ Overpayment followed by request for refund
⚠ Customer unable to explain why this product suits their needs
⚠ Policy beneficiary has no insurable interest (e.g., unrelated company)
⚠ Frequent changes to beneficiary designations
```

**Facilitator Notes:**
> "Insurance is a classic laundering vehicle. Buy a policy with dirty money, surrender it early, get 'clean' insurance proceeds. Single-premium policies over S$20,000 require CDD. Early surrender — especially with loss acceptance — is suspicious. Why lose 10% to get money back? Because the money is now clean. Third-party surrender proceeds: huge red flag. Who benefits? If it's not the policyholder, ask why."

**Time:** 2 minutes

#### Slide 13: Case Study 1 — The Rushed Onboarding

**Visual:** Timeline graphic

**Text:**
```
Case Study 1: The Rushed Onboarding

Scenario:
• Company incorporated 2 weeks ago
• Directors: 2 foreigners (no Singapore presence)
• Business: "Trading in electronics"
• Expected turnover: S$5M/month
• Documents: Minimal (no contracts, no invoices)
• RM pressure: "This is a premium client. Let's fast-track."

What Should Have Happened:
✓ Request business plan + contracts
✓ Verify source of initial capital
✓ Understand trading counterparties
✓ Rate as HIGH risk (new company, large expected volumes)
✓ EDD + senior management approval

What Actually Happened:
✗ Account opened with minimal CDD
✗ S$3M received from offshore entity in Week 1
✗ S$2.8M transferred out to different offshore entity in Week 2
✗ Account frozen by MAS investigation
✗ RM and branch manager fined + banned

Lesson: Pressure is not a reason to skip CDD.
```

**Facilitator Notes:**
> "This is real. Happened in Singapore 2024. RM was pressured to 'fast-track' a 'premium client.' Result: account used to launder S$3M. RM banned for 2 years. Branch manager fined. Here's the question: would you rather lose a client or lose your career? If the answer isn't obvious, you're not ready for this role. CDD is not optional. Ever."

**Time:** 2 minutes

#### Slide 14: Case Study 2 — The Helpful Customer

**Visual:** Customer service graphic

**Text:**
```
Case Study 2: The "Helpful" Customer

Scenario:
• Existing customer (2 years, low-risk rating)
• Requests to add new signatory
• New signatory: nephew of original director
• Customer says: "He's tech-savvy, will help with online banking"
• No change in beneficial ownership declared

Red Flags Missed:
⚠ Why add a signatory without changing BO?
⚠ Nephew's background: no employment records, no clear role
⚠ Customer unusually eager to complete quickly
⚠ New signatory has PEP connection (discovered later)

Correct Action:
✓ Treat as CDD trigger (change in control)
✓ Verify new signatory's identity + background
✓ Update beneficial ownership assessment
✓ Re-rate customer risk if needed
✓ Document decision rationale

Lesson: Existing customers can become high-risk. CDD is ongoing.
```

**Facilitator Notes:**
> "This one's subtle. Customer was 'low-risk' for 2 years. Then they add a signatory. RM thought: 'Existing customer, just a formality.' Wrong. Change in control = CDD trigger. The nephew turned out to be connected to a PEP. Customer's account was being used to layer funds. RM didn't re-assess. Lesson: CDD isn't one-time. It's ongoing. Any material change = re-assess."

**Time:** 2 minutes

#### Slide 15: Case Study 3 — The Structuring Pattern

**Visual:** Transaction timeline

**Text:**
```
Case Study 3: The Structuring Pattern

Scenario:
• Corporate account (retail business)
• 6 months of normal activity
• Suddenly: 8 cash deposits in 10 days
• Each deposit: S$18,000–S$19,500
• Total: S$152,000
• Customer explanation: "Busy season, cash sales"

Red Flags:
⚠ Deposits just below S$20,000 threshold
⚠ Pattern change (no history of large cash deposits)
⚠ Retail business with suddenly high cash volume
⚠ Customer evasive when asked for sales records

Correct Action:
✓ Request sales records/invoices
✓ File Suspicious Transaction Report (STR)
✓ Consider account restriction
✓ Escalate to AML compliance team

Lesson: Patterns matter more than individual transactions.
```

**Facilitator Notes:**
> "Structuring is when someone breaks large amounts into smaller transactions to avoid thresholds. Eight deposits of S$18K–19K in 10 days? That's not coincidence. That's intentional. RM asked for sales records. Customer couldn't provide. STR filed. Account frozen. Later investigation: funds were from unlicensed money lending. RM caught it because she noticed the pattern. This is what good CDD looks like."

**Time:** 2 minutes

When to Escalate to AML Compliance Team

**Facilitator Notes:**

#### Slide 17: Documentation Standards

**Visual:** Checklist

**Text:**
```
Documentation Requirements

✓ All CDD information recorded in system

Because it does."

**Opening Reframe:**
> "In Buddhist practice, there's a concept called 'clear seeing' — seeing things as they are, not as we wish them to be. CDD is clear seeing. You're not being suspicious. You're being clear. You're seeing the customer as they are, not as you hope they are."

**On Red Flags:**
> "Red flags aren't accusations. They're invitations to look deeper. When you see a red flag, don't react with fear or judgment. Respond with curiosity. Ask the question. Get the answer. Document it. That's clear seeing."

> "Escalation is not failure. It's wisdom. In the Buddhist tradition, there's no shame in asking for help. The shame is in pretending you know when you don't. Escalate when you're unsure. That's not weakness. That's professionalism."
