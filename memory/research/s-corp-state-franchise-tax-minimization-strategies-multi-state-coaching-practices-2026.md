## Executive Summary

Multi-state coaching practices operating as S-Corps face a complex landscape of state franchise taxes, minimum fees, and compliance requirements that can erode 3-8% of net income if not strategically managed. This research identifies actionable minimization strategies across high-tax jurisdictions (California, New York) and compares entity structuring options for coaches exceeding $150K net income.

## California S-Corp Franchise Tax: The $800 Floor + 1.5% Reality

California imposes the **greater of $800 minimum franchise tax or 1.5% of net income** on S-Corps doing business in the state. Key 2026 considerations:

**First-Year Exemption:** Newly incorporated or qualified S-Corps are exempt from the $800 minimum tax for their first tax year (though still owe 1.5% of net income if profitable). This creates a planning opportunity for coaches testing California markets before committing to permanent nexus.

**LLC vs. S-Corp Comparison:** For coaching practices with $200K+ gross revenue and $80K+ net profit, S-Corp status typically saves $1,400-$3,200 annually compared to LLC fees, which are based on gross receipts rather than net income. An LLC generating $650K gross/$120K net owes ~$3,200 in state LLC fees; an S-Corp with identical profits owes $800 + $1,800 (1.5% of $120K) = $2,600 total.

**Multi-State Apportionment:** California requires Schedule R apportionment for S-Corps doing business in multiple states. Coaches can reduce California taxable income by documenting time spent, client locations, and income sourced outside California. However, California's "doing business" thresholds are aggressive: $647,785 in sales, $64,778 in property, or $64,778 in payroll within California triggers full franchise tax liability.

**Practical Strategy:** For coaches with <20% California-sourced income, consider operating as a foreign S-Corp (e.g., Nevada or Wyoming entity) and carefully track California activity to stay below "doing business" thresholds. Once exceeded, file Form 100S with Schedule R to apportion income rather than paying 1.5% on 100% of net income.

## New York S-Corp Franchise Tax: Fixed Dollar Minimum Based on Receipts

New York requires a **separate state S-Corp election (Form CT-6)**—federal S-Corp status does not automatically confer New York S-Corp treatment. Without this election, coaches pay C-Corp franchise taxes with no flow-through benefits.

**Fixed Dollar Minimum Tax:** New York S-Corps pay a fixed dollar minimum tax based on New York receipts (not net income), ranging from $25 (receipts ≤$100K) to $4,500+ (receipts >$25M). For typical coaching practices with $200K-$500K NY receipts, expect $175-$500 annual minimum tax.

**Metropolitan Surcharge Exemption:** Unlike C-Corps, New York S-Corps are exempt from the Metropolitan Transportation Business Tax surcharge, saving 17-30% on franchise tax liability for NYC-based coaches.

**Shareholder-Level Taxation:** Nonresident shareholders pay New York tax only on NY-sourced S-Corp items (determined at corporate level). Resident shareholders pay on their pro-rata share of all pass-through items. This creates planning opportunities for coaches with multi-state ownership structures.

**Practical Strategy:** File Form CT-6 immediately upon federal S-Corp election. For coaches with <50% New York-sourced income, document client locations and service delivery points to minimize NY receipts apportionment. Consider whether NY nexus exists at all—remote coaching delivered from outside NY to NY clients may not create filing obligations if no physical presence or employees exist in NY.

## Entity Structuring by State: LLC vs. S-Corp vs. C-Corp

**High-Franchise-Tax States (CA, NY, NJ, DC):**
- S-Corp generally preferred for coaching practices with $150K+ net income
- Payroll tax savings (15.3% SE tax avoided on distributions) typically exceed franchise tax costs
- Exception: Real estate holding companies should remain LLCs (S-Corps destroy capital gains treatment)

**No-Income-Tax States (TX, FL, NV, WY, SD):**
- Texas imposes 0.375-0.75% franchise tax on gross revenue (not net income)
- Nevada has commerce tax only if gross revenue exceeds $4M annually
- Wyoming: $50 annual report fee, no franchise tax
- Florida: $138.75 annual filing fee, no franchise tax

**Low-Tax States (ND, PA, IN, CO, UT):**
- North Dakota: 2.9% top marginal rate, $50 annual fee
- Pennsylvania: 3.07% flat rate, $70 annual fee (Capital Stock Tax phased out by 2025)
- Indiana: 3.23% flat rate, $30 annual fee (lowest compliance cost in nation)
- Colorado: 4.55% flat rate, $10 periodic report fee

**Practical Strategy:** For location-independent coaches, consider incorporating in Wyoming or Indiana for minimal ongoing compliance costs. However, nexus rules require filing in states where you actually do business—incorporation state choice affects only your "home" filing, not obligations in client states.

## Composite Filing Cost-Benefit Analysis

Some states (California, New York, Maryland) allow S-Corps to file composite returns and pay tax on behalf of nonresident shareholders, simplifying compliance but potentially increasing total tax burden.

**California Composite Filing:**
- Voluntary election (Form FTB 3832)
- Pays CA tax on nonresident shareholders' pro-rata income
- Rate: 1.5% corporate + shareholder-level tax (up to 13.3%)
- Benefit: Nonresident shareholders avoid filing CA Form 540NR

**When Composite Makes Sense:**
- Single or few nonresident shareholders
- Shareholders prefer simplicity over potential tax optimization
- Shareholder's home state offers credit for CA taxes paid

**When to Avoid:**
- Multiple nonresident shareholders in different states
- Shareholders in no-income-tax states (they'd pay CA tax unnecessarily)
- Shareholders with losses or deductions that would offset S-Corp income on personal returns

## Actionable Minimization Checklist for 2026

1. **Document Nexus Thresholds:** Track days worked, client locations, and revenue sourced to each state. Stay below "doing business" thresholds where possible.

2. **Elect S-Corp Status in Every Required State:** Federal election doesn't auto-apply to CA, NY, NJ, DC. File state-specific elections (CT-6 for NY, automatic for CA).

3. **Apportion Income Aggressively:** Use Schedule R (CA) and equivalent forms to allocate income based on actual business activity, not incorporation state.

4. **Consider First-Year Exemptions:** Time entity formation to maximize first-year minimum tax exemptions (CA, NY).

5. **Evaluate Composite Filing:** Run numbers on composite vs. individual shareholder filings for nonresident owners.

6. **Layer Deductible Benefits:** Health insurance, retirement plan contributions, and HSA funding reduce net income subject to 1.5% (CA) or fixed dollar minimum (NY).

7. **Monitor Quarterly Estimated Payments:** Both CA and NY require estimated tax payments if expecting >$1,000 liability. Penalties accrue quarterly.
