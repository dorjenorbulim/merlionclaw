# Section 704(c) Remedial Allocation Methods and PTET Book-Tax Disparity Tracking 2026

**Research Date:** April 7, 2026  
**Word Count:** ~650 words

---

## Executive Summary

Pass-Through Entity Tax (PTET) elections create previously unrecognized book-tax disparities in partnerships using Section 704(c) remedial allocation methods. When partnerships hold contributed property with built-in gains and elect PTET at the entity level, the interaction between federal tax deductions and book capital account treatment requires careful tracking to avoid economic distortions among partners.

---

## The Core Problem: PTET Creates New Book-Tax Disparities

### Traditional Section 704(c) Framework

Section 704(c) prevents partners from shifting pre-contribution gain or loss to other partners when property is contributed to a partnership. Three allocation methods exist:

1. **Traditional Method** - Simple but subject to ceiling rule limitations
2. **Traditional Method with Curative Allocations** - Fixes ceiling rule problems with offsetting allocations
3. **Remedial Method** - Creates notional tax items to eliminate ceiling rule distortions

The remedial method is the most sophisticated: it creates hypothetical tax deductions or gains to ensure contributing partners bear the full economic burden of built-in gains, even when the ceiling rule would otherwise limit allocations.

### PTET Complication

PTET elections introduce a new layer of complexity:

- **Federal Treatment:** PTET payments are generally deductible by the partnership for federal tax purposes (reducing taxable income on Form 1065)
- **Book Treatment:** PTET payments may be treated differently for book capital account purposes depending on partnership agreement language
- **Result:** New book-tax disparities emerge that interact with existing Section 704(c) layering

---

## Key Coordination Challenges

### 1. Ceiling Rule Limitations Amplified

When partnerships hold appreciated real estate or other contributed property with significant built-in gains, the ceiling rule limits tax allocations to the total tax item available. PTET deductions can exacerbate this problem:

**Example:** Partnership contributes property with $1M built-in gain. Annual depreciation creates $50K book-tax disparity. PTET payment of $30K reduces federal taxable income but may not reduce book income proportionally. The remedial method must now track:
- Original Section 704(c) layer from contribution
- New PTET-related disparity
- Interaction between the two when property is sold

### 2. Reverse Section 704(c) on Revaluation Events

When partnerships revalue assets under Section 704(b) regulations (partner admissions, withdrawals, or distributions), reverse Section 704(c) layers are created. PTET elections affect this analysis:

- **Question:** Do PTET payments trigger revaluation events?
- **Current Practice:** Most partnerships treat PTET as a tax payment, not a revaluation trigger
- **Risk:** If PTET reduces book capital accounts differently than tax capital accounts, new reverse Section 704(c) layers may be required

### 3. Remedial Allocation Tracking Complexity

Partnerships using the remedial method must track:
- Notional tax items created for each Section 704(c) layer
- PTET deductions allocated among partners
- State-specific PTET treatment (some states don't conform to federal PTET deductions)

**Documentation Requirement:** Partnership agreements should specify whether PTET payments are:
- Book items (reducing book capital accounts)
- Tax-only items (reducing only tax capital accounts)
- Allocated proportionally or specially among partners

## State-Specific Variations

### California Approach
- PTET deductible for federal purposes
- Book treatment depends on partnership agreement
- Complex basis tracking for tiered partnerships

### New York Approach
- PTET creates refundable credits for partners
- Book-tax disparity tracking required for K-1 reporting
- Specific Schedule K-1 codes for PTET allocations

### New Jersey Approach
- PTET mandatory for some partnerships, elective for others
- Different book treatment for mandatory vs. elective PTET
- Coordination with NJ-specific Section 704(c) rules

## Practical Implementation Steps

### 1. Partnership Agreement Review

Ensure partnership agreements address:
- Whether PTET payments are book or tax items
- Allocation methodology (pro rata vs. special allocations)
- Interaction with existing Section 704(c) layers
- Authority to make PTET elections and revocations

### 2. Capital Account Maintenance

Implement dual tracking:
- **Book Capital Accounts:** Track PTET impact per Section 704(b) regulations
- **Tax Capital Accounts:** Track PTET deductions and Section 704(c) layers separately
- **Reconciliation:** Document M-1/M-3 adjustments for PTET-related disparities

### 3. K-1 Reporting

For each partner, report:
- PTET deductions allocated (federal)
- PTET credits available (state-specific)
- Section 704(c) remaining built-in gain/loss
- Combined impact on partner's basis calculations

### 4. Audit Defense Documentation

Maintain records showing:
- PTET election calculations
- Section 704(c) allocation methodologies
- Book-tax disparity reconciliations
- Partner-level impact statements

## Strategic Considerations

### When Remedial Method Adds Value

The remedial method is worth the complexity when:
- Partnerships hold appreciated property with significant built-in gains ($500K+)
- Partner turnover is expected (admissions/withdrawals trigger revaluations)
- Multiple PTET elections across states create layered disparities
- Partners have different tax profiles (some benefit more from PTET than others)

### When to Simplify

Consider traditional method with curative allocations when:
- Built-in gains are modest (<$200K)
- Partnership has stable ownership
- PTET elections are limited to one state
- Administrative burden outweighs tax savings

## Emerging Issues (2026)

### IRS Guidance Gaps

No specific IRS guidance addresses PTET-Section 704(c) interactions. Key uncertainties:
- Whether PTET deductions create "tax items" subject to ceiling rule
- Treatment of PTET refunds carried forward to future years
- Coordination with Section 734(b)/743(b) basis adjustments

### State Conformity Divergence

Growing divergence in state treatment:
- Some states decouple from federal PTET deductions
- Others conform but with modifications
- Creates multi-state book-tax disparity tracking burden

---

## Bottom Line for Practitioners

PTET elections transform simple Section 704(c) tracking into a multi-dimensional coordination challenge. Partnerships using the remedial method must:

1. **Update partnership agreements** to address PTET treatment explicitly
2. **Implement layered tracking** for Section 704(c) and PTET disparities
3. **Document allocation methodologies** for audit defense
4. **Model combined tax impact** before making PTET elections

The compliance burden is real, but the federal tax savings from PTET elections often justify the complexity for partnerships holding appreciated assets with built-in gains exceeding $500,000.
