# Pass-Through Entity Tax (PTET) Elections and SALT Cap Workarounds for Multi-State Coaching Practices 2026

**Research Date:** April 6, 2026  
**Word Count:** ~650 words

## Executive Summary

The Pass-Through Entity Tax (PTET) election remains one of the most powerful tax-saving strategies for S-Corp and LLC coaching practices in 2026, even with the new $40,400 SALT deduction cap introduced by the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA). For multi-state coaching practices generating $400K+ in net income, PTET elections can save $6,000–$35,000+ annually in federal taxes by converting nondeductible personal state taxes into fully deductible business expenses.

---

## Why PTET Still Matters After the $40,400 SALT Cap

The OBBBA raised the SALT deduction cap from $10,000 to $40,400 for 2026–2029, but this benefit is limited:

- **Phase-out for high earners:** Taxpayers with AGI exceeding $505,000 (2026) see the cap reduced by 30% of income above that threshold, dropping to a $10,000 minimum
- **High-tax state reality:** Coaching practices in California, New York, New Jersey, and similar jurisdictions often pay $60,000–$200,000+ in state income taxes—far exceeding even the uncapped $40,400 limit
- **AMT exposure:** Individual SALT deductions are added back entirely in AMT calculations, but PTET deductions reduce business income before AMT applies

**The advantage:** PTET bypasses all these limitations because the deduction occurs at the entity level under IRC §164(a)(3), never appearing on Schedule A.

---

## How PTET Works: Three-Step Mechanism

1. **Make the Election:** The entity (S-Corp, partnership, or LLC taxed as partnership) files an annual PTET election with the state. Deadlines vary significantly and are non-negotiable.

2. **Entity Pays State Tax:** The entity calculates and remits state income tax at the entity level. This payment is a federally deductible business expense, reducing the net income reported on each owner's K-1.

3. **Owners Receive State Credit:** Each owner gets a credit on their personal state return equal to their share of PTET paid, preventing double taxation.

**Critical update for 2026:** Section 70120 of the OBBBA explicitly preserves PTET deductibility for all pass-through businesses and removes prior restrictions on Specified Service Trades or Businesses (SSTBs). This means coaching practices—previously potentially limited as "consulting" businesses—now have unrestricted PTET access.

## Real-World Savings Examples for Coaching Practices

### Scenario 1: California S-Corp Owner
- **Net income:** $600,000
- **CA state tax:** ~$72,000 (9.3% effective rate)
- **Without PTET:** Only $40,400 deductible; $31,600 nondeductible
- **With PTET:** Full $72,000 deductible at entity level
- **Federal savings:** $11,692 at 37% marginal rate

- **Combined state taxes:** ~$120,000
- **Each partner's share:** $60,000 (exceeds $40,400 cap by $19,600)
- **With PTET:** Full $120,000 deducted at entity level
- **Combined federal savings:** ~$14,500

## Tradeoffs to Model Before Electing

**1. QBI Deduction Impact**  
PTET reduces K-1 income, which reduces your Section 199A Qualified Business Income deduction (20% of qualified business income). For owners near QBI thresholds, this offset can partially reduce PTET benefits. Model both effects together.

**2. Retirement Contribution Base**  
For partnership/LLC owners, reduced distributive share can shrink the self-employment income base for SEP-IRA or solo 401(k) contributions. S-Corp owners are generally unaffected since retirement contributions tie to W-2 salary.

**3. Cash Flow Planning**  
PTET payments reduce distribution cash available to owners. Ensure operating accounts retain sufficient liquidity for both tax payments and owner distributions.

**4. Multi-State Coordination**  
Coaching practices serving clients across state lines may need to evaluate PTET elections in each jurisdiction where income is sourced. Cross-border optimization (e.g., NY PTET + NJ resident credit) can multiply benefits but requires technical compliance.

## Action Steps for Coaching Practices

1. **Confirm entity eligibility:** S-Corps, partnerships, and LLCs taxed as partnerships qualify. Sole proprietors should consider late S-Corp election for 2026.

2. **Check state deadlines immediately:** Some states require prepayment or early-year elections. California's June 15 prepayment deadline is still actionable for 2026.

3. **Model the numbers:** Calculate PTET savings net of QBI reduction. Use tax software or engage a CPA familiar with multi-state PTET coordination.

4. **Obtain owner consent:** California and some states require all owners to consent to the election. Document consent in writing.

5. **Coordinate with estimated tax payments:** Adjust federal and state estimated payments to reflect reduced K-1 income after PTET deduction.

6. **Document everything:** Maintain records supporting the entity-level tax election and corresponding individual tax credit claims for audit defense.
