# Section 704(c) Property Contributions and PTET Allocation Methodologies 2026

**Research Date:** April 7, 2026  
**Word Count:** ~650 words

---

## Executive Summary

When partnerships holding appreciated property make Pass-Through Entity Tax (PTET) elections, the interaction with Section 704(c) allocation methods creates complex tax planning challenges. This research examines how PTET benefits should be allocated among partners when built-in gains exist, and provides practical guidance for partnership agreement drafting and audit defense.

---

## The Core Tension: PTET vs. Section 704(c)

Section 704(c) exists to prevent partners from shifting pre-contribution tax consequences among themselves. When a partner contributes property with built-in gain (fair market value exceeds tax basis), the contributing partner must be allocated the tax consequences of that built-in gain when the property is sold or depreciated.

PTET complicates this framework because:

1. **PTET reduces federal taxable income** at the partnership level (as a business expense under IRC Section 164), but does not reduce book income
2. **PTET creates book-tax disparities** similar to those Section 704(c) was designed to address
3. **PTET benefits may not align** with which partners actually bear the state tax burden

---

## Three Section 704(c) Allocation Methods

Partnerships must choose one of three methods for allocating built-in gains:

### 1. Traditional Method
- Allocates built-in gain to the contributing partner when the partnership sells the property
- Simple to apply but can create "ceiling rule" limitations if partnership income is insufficient
- **PTET Impact:** PTET deductions reduce federal taxable income, potentially exacerbating ceiling rule problems

### 2. Traditional Method with Curative Allocations
- Partnership makes offsetting allocations of other tax items to cure ceiling rule limitations
- More flexible but requires careful tracking
- **PTET Impact:** PTET deductions can serve as curative allocation items, but only if properly documented in partnership agreements

### 3. Remedial Allocation Method
- Creates notional tax items to fully eliminate book-tax disparities
- Most complex but provides complete flexibility
- **PTET Impact:** PTET can be integrated into remedial allocation calculations, but requires sophisticated tax accounting systems

## PTET Allocation: Proportional vs. Special Allocations

### The Default Approach: Proportional Allocation
Most states require PTET to be allocated proportionally among all partners based on their distributive shares. New York's PTET FAQ explicitly states that electing entities "must include all partners" and cannot selectively exclude partners from the PTET regime.

**Advantages:**
- Simpler administration and K-1 reporting
- Reduces audit risk from state tax authorities
- Aligns with most state PTET statutes

**Disadvantages:**
- May allocate PTET benefits to partners who don't bear the state tax burden (e.g., partners resident in non-PTET states)
- Creates economic distortion when partners have different state tax profiles

### The Sophisticated Approach: Special Allocations
Partnerships can specially allocate PTET benefits to partners who actually bear the state tax burden, but this requires:

1. **Explicit partnership agreement language** authorizing special PTET allocations
2. **Substantial economic effect** under Section 704(b) regulations
3. **Consistent capital account maintenance** reflecting the special allocations
4. **State-specific compliance** (some states may challenge special allocations)

**When Special Allocations Make Sense:**
- Partnerships with partners resident in multiple states
- Tiered partnership structures where upper-tier and lower-tier partners have different state nexus
- Partnerships with both individual and corporate partners (different PTET treatment)

## Book-Tax Disparities and PTET

PTET payments create a new type of book-tax disparity:

- **Book Treatment:** PTET is typically recorded as an expense, reducing book income
- **Tax Treatment:** PTET is deductible at the entity level, reducing federal taxable income
- **The Mismatch:** If PTET is treated differently for book vs. tax purposes, Section 704(c) principles may apply by analogy

**Practical Solution:** Most partnerships treat PTET consistently for both book and tax purposes (as an expense), avoiding the need for Section 704(c)-style allocations. However, this should be explicitly documented in the partnership's accounting policies.

---

## Partnership Agreement Drafting Recommendations

To defend PTET allocations on audit, partnership agreements should include:

1. **Explicit PTET Authorization:** "The Partnership may make PTET elections in any state where the Partnership has nexus, and the Partnership Administrator has discretion to determine the optimal election strategy."

2. **Allocation Methodology:** "PTET deductions and credits shall be allocated [proportionally among all partners / specially to partners bearing the state tax burden] in accordance with Section 704(b) regulations."

3. **Capital Account Maintenance:** "Capital accounts shall be maintained in accordance with Treasury Regulation Section 1.704-1(b)(2)(iv), and PTET allocations shall be reflected in capital account adjustments."

4. **Amendment Procedures:** Include procedures for amending the agreement to address new PTET states or changing partner compositions.

---

## Audit Defense Strategies

When PTET allocations diverge from standard profit-sharing ratios:

1. **Document the Business Purpose:** Maintain contemporaneous records showing why special allocations were adopted (e.g., partner tax profile analysis, state tax burden calculations).

2. **Consistent Application:** Apply the same allocation methodology across all PTET states unless there's a documented business reason for variation.

3. **Partner Consent:** Obtain written consent from all partners for special PTET allocations, ideally through partnership agreement amendments or unanimous written consents.

4. **State Compliance:** Verify that special allocations don't violate state-specific PTET statutes (some states require proportional allocation).

---

## Practical Insights for Coaching Practices

For multi-state coaching practices organized as partnerships:

- **Small Practices (2-5 partners):** Use proportional PTET allocation for simplicity; the compliance cost of special allocations likely exceeds the tax benefit.

- **Larger Practices (6+ partners) or Multi-Tier Structures:** Consider special allocations if partners have significantly different state tax profiles.

- **Partnerships Holding Appreciated Property:** If the partnership holds real estate or other appreciated assets contributed by partners, coordinate PTET allocations with Section 704(c) tracking to avoid compounding book-tax disparities.

- **Year-End Planning:** Review PTET allocations before year-end to ensure they align with partner expectations and state filing requirements.

---

## Conclusion

The interaction between PTET and Section 704(c) is an emerging area of partnership taxation with limited authoritative guidance. Partnerships should adopt clear allocation methodologies, document them in partnership agreements, and apply them consistently. When in doubt, proportional allocation provides the safest audit position, while special allocations require sophisticated tax planning and robust documentation.
