## Executive Summary

The International Coaching Federation (ICF) released updated Core Competencies in September 2025—the first major revision since 2019. This evolution reflects six years of professional maturation, with refinements in ethics, technology use, mental well-being, and diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI). Importantly, **current credentialing exams (ACC, PCC, MCC) remain based on the 2019 model**, but the 2025 updates signal where the profession is heading.

---

## Key Changes at a Glance

### What Stayed the Same
- **8 Core Competencies** across 4 categories remain unchanged
- Foundational belief: "The client is creative, resourceful, and whole"
- Coaching as partnership that empowers client self-discovery

### What's New
- **5 new sub-competencies** added
- **11 existing sub-competencies** revised
- **New Glossary of Terms** for clarity
- Updated definitions reflecting modern coaching demands

## Major Updates by Competency

### 1. Embodies a Coaching Mindset (Most Significant Changes)

**Updated Definition:** Now explicitly includes "Engages in ongoing personal and professional learning and development as a coach. Works with coaching supervisors or mentor coaches as needed."

**New Sub-competencies:**
- **2.09:** Nurtures openness and curiosity in oneself, the client, and the coaching process
- **2.10:** Remains aware of the influence of one's thoughts and behaviors on the client and others

**Revised Sub-competencies:**
- **2.02:** Now includes "remaining aware of current coaching best practices and use of technology"
- **2.04:** Added explicit awareness of "biases" alongside context and culture
- **2.06:** Changed from "regulate" to "manage" one's emotions (subtler, more sustainable framing)
- **2.07:** Expanded to maintain "emotional, physical, and mental well-being" before, during, and after sessions

**Practical Implication:** Coaches must now demonstrate ongoing supervision/mentor engagement and explicit attention to their own well-being as ethical practice, not optional self-care.

### 2. Establishes and Maintains Agreements

**New Sub-competency:**
- **3.12:** Revisits the coaching agreement when necessary to ensure the coaching approach is meeting the client's needs

**Revised:**
- **3.01:** Now requires describing one's "coaching philosophy" when defining what coaching is
- **3.02:** Added "including commitment to working toward coaching goals"
- **3.11:** Changed from "honors" to "respects" the client and coaching experience

**Practical Implication:** Agreements are now dynamic documents. Coaches must proactively check if the original contract still serves the client as their goals evolve mid-engagement.

### 3. Maintains Presence

**New Sub-competency:**
- **5.03:** Remains aware of what is emerging for self and client in the present moment

**Practical Implication:** Coaches are expected to notice subtle shifts in energy, tone, and body language—and get curious about them in real-time. This moves presence from passive "being there" to active attunement.

### 4. Evokes Awareness

**Revised:**
- **7.11:** Now includes sharing "knowledge" (not just observations and feelings) without attachment, acknowledging that relevant information can serve clients when offered skillfully.

### 5. Facilitates Client Growth

**New Sub-competency:**
- **8.07:** Partners with the client to integrate learning and sustain progress throughout the coaching engagement

**Revised:**
- **8.08:** Changed from "celebrates" to "acknowledges" progress and successes (less prescriptive about how clients experience recognition)

## What This Means for Coaching Practice

### Immediate Actions for Practicing Coaches

1. **Document Your Supervision:** If you're not already working with a mentor coach or supervisor, start. This is now explicit in the competencies.

2. **Review Your Coaching Agreement:** Add language about revisiting agreements mid-engagement. Make it a scheduled checkpoint (e.g., session 3 of 6).

3. **Well-being as Ethics:** Frame your self-care routine as ethical practice, not personal indulgence. The competencies now make this connection explicit.

4. **Technology Awareness:** Stay current on digital coaching tools, platforms, and their ethical implications (confidentiality, data security, accessibility).

5. **Bias Work:** Engage in ongoing reflection about your own biases and how they may influence coaching. This is no longer optional cultural competence—it's core competency.

### For Coaches Preparing for Credentialing

**Good news:** Exams still test the 2019 model. However, situational judgment questions increasingly reflect the values embedded in the 2025 updates:

- Questions about managing your internal state before sessions
- Scenarios where client goals shift mid-engagement
- Items testing awareness of non-verbal communication
- Ethics questions involving technology use

**Study strategy:** Master the 2019 competencies for the exam, but internalize the 2025 framework for your actual practice.

## The Bigger Picture

The 2025 updates confirm coaching's maturation as a profession. Key themes:

- **Sustainability:** Coach well-being is now recognized as essential to ethical practice (you can't serve clients from an empty cup)
- **Adaptability:** Agreements are living documents; flexibility is a skill, not a compromise
- **Self-Awareness:** The coach's internal state is part of the coaching system, not separate from it
- **Inclusion:** DEI is woven throughout, not siloed as a standalone competency
