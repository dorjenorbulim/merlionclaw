# IRS FBAR and Form 3520 Compliance for Offshore Crypto Trusts 2026

**Research Date:** April 5, 2026  
**Word Count:** ~650 words

---

## Executive Summary

U.S. persons holding cryptocurrency in foreign trusts face layered reporting obligations with severe penalties for non-compliance. This research clarifies when FBAR (FinCEN Form 114), Form 3520, and Form 3520-A apply to offshore crypto trust structures, and provides actionable compliance guidance for 2026.

---

## Core Reporting Requirements

### FBAR (FinCEN Form 114)

**Threshold:** $10,000 aggregate across all foreign financial accounts at any point during the calendar year.

**Crypto-Specific Rule:** Pure cryptocurrency-only accounts are currently **not reportable** on FBAR per FinCEN Notice 2020-2. However, **hybrid accounts** holding crypto alongside fiat currencies (USD, EUR, GBP, etc.) **are reportable**.

**Who Must File for Trusts:**
- U.S. grantors who are treated as owners of the foreign trust under IRC §§671-679
- U.S. trustees if the trust holds foreign bank accounts
- U.S. beneficiaries with >50% beneficial interest in trust assets or income

**Deadline:** April 15 with automatic extension to October 15.

**Penalties (2026):**
- Non-willful: Up to $16,117 per violation (per *United States v. Bittner*, penalties are per report, not per account)
- Willful: Greater of $100,000 or 50% of account balance, plus potential criminal penalties

---

### Form 3520 (Annual Return to Report Transactions with Foreign Trusts)

**Triggering Events:**
1. Creating a foreign trust or transferring property (including crypto) to one
2. Receiving any distribution from a foreign trust (regardless of amount)
3. Being treated as owner of any portion of a foreign trust under grantor trust rules

**Key Point:** No dollar threshold—any transfer or distribution triggers filing.

**Deadline:** Due with your Form 1040 (April 15, extensions available).

**Penalties:**
- 35% of gross value of distributions or transfers received
- Minimum penalty: $10,000 per failure
- Separate penalties apply for each triggering event

---

### Form 3520-A (Annual Information Return of Foreign Trust with U.S. Owner)

**Who Files:** The foreign trust itself (or the U.S. owner if the trust fails to file).

**Requirement:** Mandatory if any U.S. person is treated as owner of any portion of the trust under grantor trust rules.

**Deadline:** March 15 with 6-month extension available.

**Penalty:** 5% of gross value of trust assets owned by U.S. person.

## Cryptocurrency-Specific Considerations

### Form 8938 (FATCA)

While pure crypto accounts may escape FBAR, **Form 8938 has broader reach**. Most tax professionals treat foreign cryptocurrency as a "specified foreign financial asset" reportable on Form 8938 when thresholds are met:

- Single filers living in U.S.: $50,000 on last day of year, or $75,000 at any point
- Married filing jointly: $100,000 / $150,000
- Higher thresholds apply for U.S. citizens living abroad

**Strategic Implication:** You may owe Form 8938 even when FBAR doesn't apply to pure crypto holdings.

### Form 8621 (PFIC)

If your foreign trust invests in cryptocurrency ETFs or mutual funds, these likely qualify as **Passive Foreign Investment Companies (PFICs)**. Form 8621 filing is required for each PFIC held, with complex tax calculations and potential interest charges on deferred tax.

## Practical Compliance Framework

### Step 1: Map Your Structure

Document:
- Trust jurisdiction and type (grantor vs. non-grantor)
- Account types (pure crypto vs. hybrid)
- Aggregate values at peak during calendar year
- All U.S. persons with any interest (grantor, trustee, beneficiaries)

### Step 2: Determine Filing Obligations

| Scenario | FBAR | Form 3520 | Form 3520-A | Form 8938 |
|----------|------|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| Pure crypto in foreign trust, < $10K | ❌ | ✅ if transfer/distribution | ✅ if U.S. owner | ✅ if threshold met |
| Hybrid account > $10K | ✅ | ✅ if transfer/distribution | ✅ if U.S. owner | ✅ if threshold met |
| Crypto ETF/mutual fund | ❌ | ✅ if transfer/distribution | ✅ if U.S. owner | ✅ + Form 8621 |

### Step 3: Cost Basis Tracking

Foreign trusts must track:
- Acquisition dates for each crypto asset
- Cost basis in USD at time of transfer to trust
- Fair market value at distribution
- Holding periods for long-term vs. short-term capital gains

**Challenge:** Crypto volatility + cross-border transfers create complex basis calculations. Use specialized software (Koinly, CoinTracker, TokenTax) that supports trust structures.

### Step 4: Avoid Quiet Disclosure

If you're behind on filings:
- **Non-willful:** Use Streamlined Filing Compliance Procedures (reduced/eliminated penalties)
- **Willful:** Use IRS Voluntary Disclosure Program (higher penalties but avoids criminal prosecution)
- **Never** simply start filing forward—this creates "quiet disclosure" risk and may trigger audits

## Red Flags and Risk Mitigation

**High-Risk Scenarios:**
- Large crypto transfers to offshore trusts without Form 3520 filing
- Receiving distributions without reporting
- Assuming crypto-only accounts have no reporting requirements (FBAR may not apply, but Form 8938 and 3520 likely do)
- Multiple U.S. beneficiaries each assuming someone else filed

**Best Practices:**
1. File all required forms—even if no tax is due, informational filings avoid penalties
2. Document reasonable cause for any late filings (supports penalty abatement requests)
3. Engage a Board-Certified Tax Law Specialist for offshore disclosure if behind
4. Maintain contemporaneous records of trust transactions, valuations, and filings

---

## Bottom Line for Coaches and Advisors

If you're guiding clients with offshore crypto structures:

1. **Pure crypto ≠ no reporting.** Form 3520 and 8938 likely still apply.
2. **Penalties dwarf tax liability.** A $500K distribution could trigger $175K in Form 3520 penalties alone.
3. **Hybrid accounts trigger FBAR.** Even small fiat balances alongside crypto create FBAR obligations.
4. **Get specialist help.** Offshore crypto trust compliance is not DIY territory—refer clients to international tax attorneys with Board Certification.
