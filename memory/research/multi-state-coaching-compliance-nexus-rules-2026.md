## Executive Summary

Coaches serving clients across state lines face a complex web of tax nexus rules, business registration requirements, and jurisdiction-specific compliance obligations. The shift from physical presence to economic nexus standards has created significant exposure for remote coaching practices. This research synthesizes 2026 nexus thresholds, registration requirements, and practical strategies for compliant multi-jurisdictional practice.

## Understanding Nexus: The 2026 Landscape

### What Creates Nexus for Coaches?

**Nexus** is the connection between your business and a state that creates a legal obligation to register, collect taxes, or file returns. For coaches in 2026, nexus can be triggered by:

1. **Physical Presence Nexus**
   - Maintaining an office, home office, or co-working space in a state
   - Traveling to a state for in-person coaching sessions (even temporarily)
   - Having employees or contractors working from a state
   - Storing materials or equipment in-state

2. **Economic Nexus**
   - Exceeding state-specific revenue thresholds from clients in that state
   - Most states: $100,000+ annual revenue OR 200+ transactions
   - Some states have lower thresholds (e.g., California: $500,000; Texas: $500,000)
   - Thresholds apply to gross revenue, not profit

3. **Click-Through/Affiliate Nexus**
   - Paying commissions to in-state referrers or affiliates
   - Using in-state marketing partners who generate coaching clients

### Key 2026 Changes

- **Remote Worker Nexus**: Having a contractor or virtual assistant working from a state can create nexus even if you've never visited (Todd Unger, Esq., 2026)
- **Economic Nexus Expansion**: More states now enforce economic nexus for services, not just tangible goods
- **Increased Enforcement**: States are using data-matching to identify unregistered remote businesses (Galvix, 2026)

## State-by-State Registration Requirements

### When You Must Register

| Scenario | Registration Required |
|----------|----------------------|
| Physical office in state | ✅ Yes, immediately |
| Regular in-person sessions in state | ✅ Yes |
| Revenue exceeds economic nexus threshold | ✅ Yes, for sales tax |
| Only online coaching, under thresholds | ❌ Generally no |
| Occasional workshop/seminar | ⚠️ Depends on frequency |

### High-Risk States for Coaches

These states have aggressive nexus enforcement and lower thresholds:

- **California**: $500,000 revenue threshold; strict on service providers
- **New York**: $500,000 OR 100 transactions; requires registration for digital services
- **Texas**: $500,000 threshold; coaching services may be taxable
- **Washington**: $100,000 threshold; B&O tax applies to coaching revenue
- **Illinois**: $100,000 OR 200 transactions; service taxation expanding

### States with No Income Tax (Lower Risk)

Coaches residing in these states face simpler compliance:
- Nevada, Wyoming, Florida, Texas, Washington, South Dakota, Tennessee, New Hampshire

## Business Structure Considerations

### LLC Registration Strategy

**Home State Registration:**
- Always register your LLC in your state of residence
- This is your "domestic" LLC
- File annual reports and pay franchise taxes as required

**Foreign Qualification:**
- If you have physical presence or exceed economic nexus in another state, you may need to register as a "foreign LLC" in that state
- Cost: $100-$500 per state + annual fees
- **Risk**: Registering in too many states creates administrative burden and double taxation exposure

**Delaware/Wyoming LLCs:**
- Popular for asset protection and privacy
- **But**: You still must register as foreign LLC in your home state if operating from there
- No tax advantage if you're a single-member LLC (pass-through taxation)

### S-Corp Election

- Consider S-Corp election if net income exceeds $80,000-$100,000
- Reduces self-employment tax burden
- Requires payroll setup and additional compliance
- Must file in every state where you have nexus

## Sales Tax on Coaching Services

### Is Coaching Taxable?

**General Rule:** Most states do **not** tax coaching services as they're considered professional services, not tangible goods.

**Exceptions (2026):**
- **New Mexico**: Gross receipts tax applies to most services including coaching
- **Hawaii**: GET (General Excise Tax) applies to all business income
- **Ohio**: Commercial Activity Tax (CAT) on gross receipts over $150,000
- **Washington**: B&O tax on service revenue
- **Texas**: Some coaching services taxable if bundled with tangible products

**Digital Products:**
- If you sell recorded courses, worksheets, or digital downloads, these may be taxable in many states
- Economic nexus thresholds apply to digital product sales
- Use platforms like Teachable/Kajabi that handle sales tax collection automatically

## Practical Compliance Strategies

### 1. Track Client Locations Meticulously

- Maintain a spreadsheet of client billing addresses
- Monitor revenue per state quarterly
- Set up alerts when approaching $75,000 in any state (buffer before $100k threshold)

### 2. Use Contracts to Limit Nexus Exposure

- Specify governing law in coaching agreements (your home state)
- Include venue selection clauses for dispute resolution
- Clarify that services are delivered remotely from your home state
- Avoid creating "permanent establishment" through long-term in-person commitments

### 3. Leverage Independent Contractor Status

- Hire contractors (VA, marketing, admin) in states where you want to avoid nexus
- Ensure they're truly independent (multiple clients, their own equipment)
- Document contractor relationships carefully to avoid employee reclassification

### 4. Consider Nexus Study

- If you've been coaching across state lines for years, consider a professional nexus study
- Identifies past exposure and voluntary disclosure opportunities
- Many states offer amnesty programs with reduced penalties for voluntary registration

### 5. Insurance and Liability

- Professional liability insurance should cover multi-state practice
- Verify policy doesn't exclude claims from states where you're unregistered
- Consider umbrella policy if revenue exceeds $250,000

## Red Flags That Trigger Audits

- Revenue mismatch between 1099s received and tax returns filed
- Client complaints or disputes that reference state consumer protection laws
- Marketing to residents of high-enforcement states (CA, NY, MA)
- Using in-state payment processors or merchant accounts
- Claiming home office deduction while frequently traveling to client states

## Bottom Line

Most coaches serving clients remotely from a home base can operate compliantly without multi-state registration **if** they:
1. Stay under economic nexus thresholds ($100k revenue or 200 transactions per state)
2. Avoid physical presence in client states
3. Use clear contracts specifying home-state governance
4. Track revenue by state diligently

However, coaches scaling beyond $250k+ revenue or regularly traveling for in-person work should invest in professional tax advice to avoid costly penalties and back-tax assessments.
