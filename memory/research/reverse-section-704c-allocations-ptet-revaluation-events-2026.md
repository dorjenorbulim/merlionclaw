# Reverse Section 704(c) Allocations and PTET Revaluation Events 2026

**Research Date:** April 7, 2026  
**Word Count:** ~650 words

---

## Executive Summary

The interaction between Pass-Through Entity Tax (PTET) elections and partnership revaluations under Section 704(b) creates complex book-tax disparities that require careful tracking. While PTET has emerged as a critical SALT cap workaround following the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) of July 2025, its treatment in partnership revaluation scenarios remains an evolving area requiring disciplined documentation and allocation methodology.

---

## Key Findings

### 1. PTET Payments and Revaluation Triggers

**Critical Question:** Do PTET payments trigger partnership revaluation events under Section 704(b)?

**Current Understanding:** PTET payments themselves do **not** automatically trigger revaluation events. Revaluations occur only upon specific events:
- Admission of new partners
- Withdrawal or redemption of existing partners
- Distribution of partnership property
- Liquidation of partnership interests

However, PTET creates an indirect complication: when PTET reduces federal taxable income but not book income, it generates new book-tax disparities that must be tracked alongside existing Section 704(c) layers from contributed property or prior revaluations.

**Practical Implication:** Partnerships should maintain separate tracking accounts for:
1. Pre-contribution built-in gains/losses (traditional Section 704(c))
2. Revaluation-based book-tax differences (reverse Section 704(c))
3. PTET-induced disparities (emerging third layer)

---

### 2. Allocation Methodology Coordination

Partnerships have three allocation methods for Section 704(c) property:

| Method | PTET Coordination Challenge |
|--------|----------------------------|
| **Traditional** | Simplest, but may create distortions when PTET benefits don't align with tax allocations |
| **Remedial** | Creates notional tax items that complicate PTET credit tracking |
| **Curative** | Most flexible for PTET coordination but requires detailed partnership agreement language |

**Best Practice:** The curative method offers the most flexibility for coordinating PTET allocations with reverse Section 704(c) tracking, particularly when partnerships hold appreciated real estate or other Section 704(c) property with significant built-in gains.

---

### 3. Book-Up Timing and PTET Election Coordination

When partnerships revalue assets (book-up) in conjunction with partner admissions or withdrawals, the timing of PTET elections becomes critical:

**Scenario Analysis:**

- **Pre-Revaluation PTET Election:** If PTET is elected before a book-up, the PTET benefit should generally be allocated according to pre-revaluation profit-sharing ratios unless the partnership agreement specifies otherwise.

- **Post-Revaluation PTET Election:** If PTET is elected after a book-up, partnerships face a choice: allocate PTET benefits proportionally among all partners or specially allocate to partners who economically bear the state tax burden.

**Risk Area:** Partnerships that fail to coordinate PTET timing with revaluation events risk creating unintended economic distortions, particularly when new partners enter mid-year or when departing partners receive distributions in PTET electing states.

---

### 4. Documentation Standards for Audit Defense

Given the novelty of PTET-revaluation interactions, documentation is paramount:

**Required Documentation:**
1. **Partnership Agreement Amendments:** Explicit language addressing PTET allocation methodology, especially for partnerships with reverse Section 704(c) layers
2. **Capital Account Ledgers:** Separate tracking of book capital, tax capital, PTET credit accounts, and state-specific basis adjustments
3. **Revaluation Memos:** Contemporaneous documentation of revaluation events, including asset appraisals, allocation methodologies, and PTET election timing decisions
4. **K-1 Supporting Schedules:** Detailed breakdowns showing PTET allocations separate from ordinary profit/loss allocations

**Audit Defense Strategy:** In the event of IRS or state audit, partnerships should be prepared to demonstrate that PTET allocations have substantial economic effect under Section 704(b) regulations and do not constitute prohibited shifting of tax consequences.

---

### 5. State-Specific Variations

PTET treatment varies significantly by state, creating additional complexity for multi-state partnerships:

| State | Revaluation-Specific PTET Rules |
|-------|--------------------------------|
| **California** | Requires careful coordination with California's specific basis adjustment rules; no explicit revaluation guidance |
| **New York** | More developed PTET regulations but silent on revaluation interactions |
| **New Jersey** | Allows flexible allocation methods but requires consistent application |
| **Massachusetts** | Recent 2025 budget changes may affect PTET treatment for revalued partnerships |

**Practical Guidance:** Multi-state partnerships should maintain state-specific PTET tracking schedules and consider whether separate PTET elections by state (vs. blanket elections) better serve partners' economic arrangements.

## Practical Recommendations

### For Partnership Tax Preparers:

1. **Review Partnership Agreements:** Ensure PTET allocation language addresses revaluation scenarios explicitly
2. **Implement Layer Tracking:** Use tax software or spreadsheets that can track multiple Section 704(c) layers simultaneously
3. **Document Election Timing:** Create contemporaneous memos documenting PTET election decisions relative to revaluation events
4. **Coordinate with State Filings:** Ensure PTET allocations on federal K-1s match state composite or withholding filings

### For Partnerships Holding Appreciated Assets:

1. **Consider Timing Elections:** If a revaluation is anticipated, consider whether accelerating or deferring PTET elections better serves partner economics
2. **Model Allocation Scenarios:** Run pro forma allocations under different PTET timing scenarios before making irrevocable elections
3. **Communicate with Partners:** Ensure partners understand how PTET allocations interact with their capital accounts and basis calculations

---

## Emerging Issues to Monitor

1. **IRS Guidance:** No formal IRS guidance yet addresses PTET-revaluation interactions; practitioners should monitor for future notices or revenue rulings
2. **State Conformity:** Some states may issue specific guidance on PTET allocation methods for revalued partnerships
3. **Audit Activity:** As PTET matures, expect increased scrutiny of PTET allocations in partnerships with complex capital structures

---

## Conclusion

The intersection of PTET elections and reverse Section 704(c) allocations represents a frontier in partnership taxation. While the core principles of Section 704(b) and 704(c) remain stable, PTET introduces a new variable that requires intentional planning, rigorous documentation, and ongoing monitoring. Partnerships that proactively address these interactions will be better positioned for audit defense and will ensure that PTET benefits flow to partners in accordance with their economic deal.
