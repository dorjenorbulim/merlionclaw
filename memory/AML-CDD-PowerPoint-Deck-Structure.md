
\# AML/CDD Training Deck — PowerPoint Structure
## Risk & Client Due Diligence Screening (MAS Notice 626)

**Format:** Export-ready structure for PowerPoint/Keynote  
**Total Slides:** 29 slides  
**Aspect Ratio:** 16:9  
**Theme:** Professional, clean, MAS compliance (blue/white/grey palette)  
**Font:** Arial or Helvetica (clean, readable)  
**Duration:** 30 minutes  

---

## 🎨 Design Guidelines

### Color Palette
| Color | Hex Code | Usage |
|-------|----------|-------|
| Primary Blue | #003366 | Headers, titles, MAS branding |
| Secondary Blue | #0052A3 | Subheaders, accents |
| Alert Red | #D32F2F | Red flags, warnings, HIGH risk |
| Caution Yellow | #FBC02D | MEDIUM risk, warnings |
| Safe Green | #388E3C | LOW risk, correct answers |
| Neutral Grey | #757575 | Body text, secondary info |
| White | #FFFFFF | Background |

### Typography
- **Title:** 36–44pt, Bold, Primary Blue
- **Subtitle:** 24–28pt, Regular, Secondary Blue
- **Body:** 18–20pt, Regular, Neutral Grey
- **Bullet Points:** 18pt, Regular, Neutral Grey

### Imagery
- Use MAS logo (top right corner, small)
- Use bank logo (top left corner, small)
- Icons: Flat, minimalist style
- No clipart or cartoon graphics
- Photos: Professional, diverse, Singapore context

### Layout
- Consistent header on all slides (logo + title)
- Footer: Slide number + "Confidential — Internal Use Only"
- White space: Minimum 20% of slide
- Text per slide: Maximum 6 bullets, 6 words per bullet (6x6 rule)

### SLIDE 7: The 5 CDD Elements

**Layout:** Vertical checklist (5 items)

**Text:**
```
CDD Information Requirements

✓ Customer Identity
  Name, registration number, registered address
  For individuals: NRIC/passport, DOB, nationality

✓ Beneficial Ownership (BO)
  Who ultimately owns or controls the customer?
  Typically 25%+ ownership or significant control

✓ Purpose & Nature of Business
  What does this company actually do?
  Expected transaction patterns

✓ Source of Wealth (SoW)
  How did the customer accumulate their wealth?
  Business profits, inheritance, investments, etc.

✓ Source of Funds (SoF)
  Where is the money for THIS transaction coming from?
  Bank account, sale of asset, loan, etc.

REMEMBER: SoW = how they got rich. SoF = where this money came from.
```

**Visual Elements:**
- Checkmarks in green
- SoW vs. SoF comparison box at bottom (highlighted)
- Icons next to each element

**Animation:** Each element slides in from left

**Speaker Notes:**
> "These 5 elements are non-negotiable. The most common gaps I see: Beneficial Ownership and Source of Funds. 'Beneficial owner' means the human being at the end of the ownership chain — not another company. Source of Wealth is how they got rich. Source of Funds is where this specific money came from. Different questions. Both matter."

**Time:** 2 minutes

### SLIDE 9: Red Flags — Corporate Accounts

**Layout:** Table format (3 columns)

**Text:**
```
Red Flags: Corporate Account Opening

| Red Flag | Why It Matters | What to Ask |
|----------|----------------|-------------|
| Company < 3 months old | No track record | "Show me contracts, invoices" |
| Expected turnover unrealistic | S$5M/month for new F&B? | "How will you achieve this?" |
| Registered address: residential/virtual | May indicate shell company | "Where do you operate from?" |
| Complex ownership, no business reason | 5 layers across 3 jurisdictions? | "What's the business purpose?" |
| Reluctance to provide BO | Who are we dealing with? | "I need all humans with 25%+" |
| Multiple companies, same directors | Potential mule accounts | "What's the relationship?" |
| Business doesn't match profile | Manufacturing expecting S$10M FX? | "Walk me through your flow" |
```

**Visual Elements:**
- Warning icon (⚠) next to each red flag
- Table with alternating row colors (white/light grey)
- "What to Ask" column in blue text

**Animation:** Each row slides in from left

**Speaker Notes:**
> "These are the patterns I see in enforcement cases. The big one: complex ownership with no business reason. If a company has 5 layers of holding companies across 3 jurisdictions, ask why. If the answer is 'tax planning' or 'privacy,' that's not a business reason. Another: newly incorporated companies expecting massive transactions. Red flag. Ask for contracts, invoices, proof of business pipeline."

**Time:** 2 minutes

### SLIDE 10: Red Flags — Remittances

**Layout:** Table format (3 columns)

**Text:**
```
Red Flags: Remittances & Wire Transfers

| Red Flag | Why It Matters | What to Ask |
|----------|----------------|-------------|
| Missing originator information | MAS requirement | "I need full originator details" |
| Frequent transfers to high-risk jurisdictions | FATF grey/black list | "What's the business purpose?" |
| Round-dollar amounts repeatedly | Exactly S$50K each time? | "I notice a pattern. Help me understand." |
| Transfers inconsistent with business | Retail company → offshore gambling? | "How does this relate to your business?" |
| Structuring (below S$20K threshold) | Avoiding CDD | "These together exceed S$20K" |
| Rapid movement (in/out 24–48 hours) | Layering technique | "Why the urgency?" |
| Third-party payments, no relationship | Who are we dealing with? | "What's your relationship?" |
```

**Visual Elements:**
- Wire transfer graphic (top right corner)
- Warning icons in red
- "MAS Requirement" badge next to originator info

**Animation:** Each row slides in from left

**Speaker Notes:**
> "Remittances are high-risk. MAS requires full originator information on all cross-border transfers. If it's missing, you must follow up — or reject the transfer. Structuring is when someone breaks a large transaction into smaller ones to avoid thresholds. Three S$19,000 transfers in one week? That's S$57,000. That's CDD territory. Rapid movement — money in, money out immediately — that's layering. Ask why."

**Time:** 2 minutes

### SLIDE 11: Red Flags — Loans

**Layout:** Table format (3 columns)

**Text:**
```
Red Flags: Loan Applications

| Red Flag | Why It Matters | What to Ask |
|----------|----------------|-------------|
| Early repayment without explanation | Borrowing at 5% then repaying? | "What's driving early repayment?" |
| Proceeds to unrelated third parties | Why not to borrower? | "What's the relationship?" |
| Collateral source unclear | Where did this asset come from? | "Document how you acquired this" |
| Customer indifferent to interest rates | Legitimate borrowers care | "You seem unconcerned. Why?" |
| Loan purpose vague ("business expansion") | No specifics = no verification | "Which business? What expansion?" |
| Guarantor relationship unclear | Why guaranteeing? | "What's the relationship?" |
| Disbursement to multiple accounts/jurisdictions | Layering risk | "Why multiple accounts?" |
```

**Visual Elements:**
- Loan document graphic (top right)
- Warning icons in red
- "Layering" callout box next to relevant flags

**Animation:** Each row slides in from left

**Speaker Notes:**
> "Loan red flags are subtler. Early repayment is a big one — why borrow at 5% and repay immediately? Often it's to legitimize funds. Third-party disbursement: the loan is for Company A, but they want funds sent to Company B in a different jurisdiction. Ask why. If the answer doesn't make business sense, escalate. Customer indifference to terms: legitimate borrowers care about rates. Money launderers don't — they're paying for cleaning, not borrowing."

**Time:** 2 minutes

### SLIDE 12: Red Flags — Insurance

**Layout:** Table format (3 columns)

**Text:**
```
Red Flags: Insurance Products

| Red Flag | Why It Matters | What to Ask |
|----------|----------------|-------------|
| Single-premium, large lump sums | S$100K+ paid upfront | "Explain the source of funds" |
| Early surrender (within 6–12 months) | Classic laundering pattern | "What's changed since purchase?" |
| Surrender proceeds to third parties | Who benefits? | "What's your relationship?" |
| Overpayment followed by refund request | Testing the system | "Why was there an overpayment?" |
| Customer can't explain product suitability | Why this product? | "Why does this fit your needs?" |
| Beneficiary has no insurable interest | Unrelated company as beneficiary? | "What's the relationship?" |
| Frequent changes to beneficiary | Potential layering | "Why multiple changes?" |
```

**Visual Elements:**
- Insurance policy graphic (top right)
- Warning icons in red
- "Classic Laundering Pattern" callout next to early surrender

**Animation:** Each row slides in from left

**Speaker Notes:**
> "Insurance is a classic laundering vehicle. Buy a policy with dirty money, surrender it early, get 'clean' insurance proceeds. Single-premium policies over S$20,000 require CDD. Early surrender — especially with loss acceptance — is suspicious. Why lose 10% to get money back? Because the money is now clean. Third-party surrender proceeds: huge red flag. Who benefits? If it's not the policyholder, ask why."

**Time:** 2 minutes
