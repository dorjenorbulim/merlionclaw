# Madhyamaka Study Guide — Beginner's Path

**Started:** April 4, 2026  
**For:** Michael K C Lim  
**Tradition:** Madhyamaka (Middle Way) — Nāgārjuna's philosophy of emptiness

---

## 🪷 How to Use This Guide

**Pace:** Gentle but consistent. Better 20 minutes daily than 3 hours once a week.

**Method:** Read → Contemplate → Rest → Return

**Attitude:** Curious, not grasping. Let understanding deepen naturally.

---

## STAGE 1: ORIENTATION (Weeks 1–4)

### Goal
Understand the basic view: emptiness, two truths, dependent origination.

---

### Week 1: What Is Madhyamaka?

**Reading:**
- [ ] Dalai Lama — *The Middle Way* (Introduction + Chapter 1)
  - Alternative: Read the summary I provided above (Four Schools comparison)

**Contemplation:**
- What draws me to Madhyamaka?
- What do I hope to understand?
- What's my current view of reality?

**Practice:**
- 10 minutes daily: Sit quietly, notice thoughts arise and pass
- Ask: "Where did this thought come from? Where does it go?"

**Journal prompt:** What does "emptiness" mean to me right now?

---

### Week 2: Dependent Origination

**Reading:**
- [ ] Epstein — *Introduction to Emptiness* (Chapters 1–3)
- [ ] MMK Chapter 1 (Causation) — Siderits/Katsura translation

**Key insight:** Nothing exists independently. All things depend on causes, conditions, parts, concepts.

**Contemplation:**
- Pick an object (cup, phone, tree)
- Trace its dependencies: materials, makers, causes, your concept of it
- Ask: "Can I find the thing itself, separate from all this?"

**Practice:**
- 15 minutes daily: Analyze one phenomenon using dependent origination
- Rest in the space when analysis exhausts itself

**Journal prompt:** What surprised me about how things depend on other things?

---

### Week 3: The Two Truths

**Reading:**
- [ ] MMK Chapter 24 (Two Truths) — the most important chapter
- [ ] Epstein — *Introduction to Emptiness* (Chapters 4–5)

**Key insight:** 
- Conventional truth = how things appear and function
- Ultimate truth = how things actually are (empty)
- Both are true; neither exists inherently

**Contemplation:**
- Pick a problem in your life
- View it conventionally: What's happening? What can be done?
- View it ultimately: What is inherently real about this problem?
- Hold both together

**Practice:**
- 15 minutes daily: Two truths meditation
- Morning: Set intention to remember emptiness during the day
- Evening: Review — when did I grasp? When did I remember emptiness?

**Journal prompt:** How do the two truths apply to something I'm struggling with?

---

### Week 4: The Self and Emptiness

**Reading:**
- [ ] MMK Chapter 18 (Self)
- [ ] MMK Chapter 15 (Inherent Existence)

**Key insight:** The self exists conventionally (you experience yourself) but not inherently (no findable "you" separate from body, mind, causes).

**Contemplation:**
- Where is "I"?
  - In the body? Which part?
  - In the mind? Which thought?
  - In both? How?
  - In neither? Then what is experiencing?

**Practice:**
- 20 minutes daily: Self-inquiry meditation
- Throughout the day: When strong emotion arises, ask "Who is feeling this?"

**Journal prompt:** What happens when I look for the self?

---

## STAGE 1 CHECK-IN (End of Week 4)

**Questions to ask:**
1. Do I understand dependent origination?
2. Can I explain the two truths?
3. Have I noticed any shift in how I grasp at things?
4. What's unclear? What needs more time?

**Decision point:**
- [ ] Continue to Stage 2 (deeper study)
- [ ] Repeat Stage 1 (more grounding)
- [ ] Adjust pace (slower/faster)

---

## STAGE 2: DEEP STUDY (Months 2–4)

### Goal
Work through root texts systematically; develop contemplative stability.

---

### Month 2: Full MMK Reading

**Text:** *Mūlamadhyamakakārikā* — complete (27 chapters)

**Translation:** Siderits & Katsura (clearest) or Garfield (more philosophical)

**Pace:** 1 chapter per week (some weeks may need 2)

**Method:**
1. Read chapter once (don't worry about understanding everything)
2. Read again, slowly, with commentary
3. Contemplate the main argument
4. Rest in the conclusion (emptiness of that phenomenon)

**Key chapters to emphasize:**
- Chapter 1: Causation
- Chapter 2: Motion
- Chapter 10: Fire and Fuel
- Chapter 15: Inherent Existence
- Chapter 18: Self
- Chapter 24: Two Truths (re-read)

**Practice:**
- 20–30 minutes daily: Analysis + rest
- Weekly: Write a summary of each chapter in your own words

---

### Month 3: Āryadeva's Four Hundred Verses

**Text:** *Catuḥśataka* — selected chapters

**Translation:** Ruth Sonam (Snow Lion)

**Focus:**
- Chapters 1–8: Ethical preparation (don't skip — ethics ground emptiness)
- Chapters 9–12: Emptiness of phenomena
- Chapter 17: Emptiness of self

**Why this text:**
- More accessible than MMK
- Adds ethical dimension
- Āryadeva was Nāgārjuna's direct disciple

**Practice:**
- Continue MMK contemplation
- Add: Notice how ethics and emptiness support each other

---

### Month 4: Candrakīrti's Introduction

**Text:** *Madhyamakāvatāra* — Chapter 6 (on wisdom)

**Translation:** Padmakara Translation Group

**Why this text:**
- Defends Prāsaṅgika view (consequence-only)
- Integrates compassion and emptiness
- Massive, detailed — take your time

**Pace:** This chapter alone may take a month

**Practice:**
- 30 minutes daily
- Weekly review: What's the main argument? What's unclear?

---

## STAGE 3: COMMENTARIES (Months 5–8)

### Goal
Understand different interpretations; deepen view through debate.

---

### Month 5–6: Tsongkhapa's View

**Text:** Selections from *Ocean of Reasoning* (commentary on MMK)

**Why:** Most detailed philosophical analysis; Gelug standard

**Key questions:**
- How does Tsongkhapa define the "object of negation"?
- How do emptiness and dependent origination support each other?

**Practice:**
- Continue meditation
- Add: Debate both sides of key questions

---

### Month 7: Gorampa's Critique

**Text:** *Distinguishing the Views* (selections)

**Why:** Sakya perspective; challenges Tsongkhapa

**Key questions:**
- Does Tsongkhapa make emptiness too conceptual?
- Is the goal "correct understanding" or "freedom from all views"?

**Practice:**
- Read Tsongkhapa and Gorampa side-by-side
- Notice your own view emerging

---

### Month 8: Mipham's Synthesis

**Text:** *Beacon of Certainty* (selections)

**Why:** Nyingma perspective; Madhyamaka + Dzogchen

**Key questions:**
- How do emptiness and luminosity relate?
- What is the "natural state"?

**Practice:**
- Rest more, analyze less
- Notice the union of clarity and emptiness

---

## STAGE 4: INTEGRATION (Ongoing)

### Goal
Madhyamaka becomes lived reality, not just intellectual understanding.

---

### Practices:

**1. Daily Meditation (30–45 minutes)**
- Śamatha (calm abiding): 15 minutes
- Vipaśyanā (emptiness analysis): 20 minutes
- Rest (non-conceptual): 10 minutes

**2. Post-Meditation**
- Remember: All day is like a dream
- Act compassionately while knowing actions are empty
- When grasping arises: "This is empty, dependently originated"

**3. Weekly Review**
- What did I understand this week?
- Where did I grasp?
- What needs more contemplation?

**4. Monthly Retreat (half-day or full-day)**
- Deepen contemplation
- Re-read key chapters
- Rest more, think less

---

## SUPPORTING PRACTICES

### Ethics (Śīla)
- Emptiness without ethics becomes nihilism
- Keep basic precepts: non-harming, truthfulness, generosity
- Remember: Conventional truth matters

### Compassion (Karuṇā)
- Emptiness reveals interdependence
- All beings suffer from not seeing emptiness
- Wish for all beings to be free

### Study Group (if possible)
- Find others studying Madhyamaka
- Debate together
- Share insights and difficulties

### Teacher (if possible)
- Madhyamaka is subtle; easy to misunderstand
- A qualified teacher can correct errors
- Look for: Gelug, Sakya, or Kagyu lineages

---

## COMMON OBSTACLES

| Obstacle | Sign | Antidote |
|----------|------|----------|
| Nihilism | "Nothing matters" | Remember conventional truth functions |
| Eternalism | "Emptiness is a thing" | Remember emptiness is also empty |
| Fear | "If no self, what am I?" | Trust the process; compassion arises |
| Pride | "I understand this" | Remember understanding is also empty |
| Discouragement | "I'll never get it" | Even partial insight helps |
| Confusion | "I don't understand" | Slow down; re-read; contemplate more |
