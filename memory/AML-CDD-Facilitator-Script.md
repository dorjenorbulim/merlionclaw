### SLIDE 5: What is MAS Notice 626? (4:30–6:30)

**SAY:**
> "MAS Notice 626 is the rulebook. It applies to all banks licensed in Singapore. That's us.
>
> It was just updated. June 30, 2025. Effective July 1, 2025.
>
> The big change? Proliferation Financing. PF. That's financing for weapons of mass destruction. Nuclear. Chemical. Biological.
>
> If you're onboarding a company that trades in dual-use goods — things that can be civilian or military — you need to flag it. We'll cover what this means in practice when we talk about red flags.
>
> Notice 626 covers six areas:
>
> Customer Due Diligence — which is our focus today. Risk assessment. Ongoing monitoring. Suspicious transaction reporting. Record keeping. And training — which is what we're doing now.
>
> The guideline document is about 300 pages. I'm not going to read you all 300 pages. I'm going to give you what you need to do your job.
>
> Let's talk about the 4 triggers. When must you perform CDD?"

### SLIDE 6: The 4 CDD Triggers (6:30–8:30)

**SAY:**
> "Memorize this slide. These are the 4 triggers.
>
> **Trigger 1: Establishing a business relationship.** Opening a corporate account. First-time loan application. This is what most of you encounter daily.
>
> **Trigger 2: Occasional transactions of S$20,000 or more.** Single transaction. Or linked transactions. If someone walks in with S$19,000 cash, then comes back tomorrow with S$19,000, that's structuring. That's still CDD territory.
>
> **Trigger 3: Suspicion of money laundering, terrorism financing, or proliferation financing.** Regardless of amount. Even for existing customers. If something feels off, it probably is.
>
> **Trigger 4: Doubt about customer identity.** Documents appear forged. Information doesn't match records. Trust your gut.
>
> Most of you will encounter Trigger 1 daily. Trigger 2 less often. Triggers 3 and 4 — if you see them, don't ignore them. That's what we're training today.
>
> Any questions on the triggers?"

### SLIDE 7: The 5 CDD Elements (8:30–10:30)

**SAY:**
> "These 5 elements are non-negotiable. Every customer. Every time.
>
> **One: Customer Identity.** Name. Registration number. Registered address. For individuals: NRIC or passport, date of birth, nationality. Straightforward.
>
> **Two: Beneficial Ownership.** This is where I see gaps. Beneficial owner means the human being at the end of the ownership chain. Not another company. Not a trust. A human being. Typically 25% or more ownership. Or significant control. If you can't identify the beneficial owner, you can't onboard the customer. Period.
>
> **Three: Purpose and Nature of Business.** What does this company actually do? Not the ACRA description. What do they actually do? Expected transaction patterns. How many transactions per month? What value? What counterparties?
>
> **Four: Source of Wealth.** How did the customer accumulate their wealth? Business profits? Inheritance? Investments? Sale of property? This is about their overall wealth.
>
> **Five: Source of Funds.** Where is the money for THIS transaction coming from? This is specific. Not how they got rich. Where this money came from. Bank account? Sale of asset? Loan? Different questions. Both matter.
>
> The most common gaps I see: Beneficial Ownership and Source of Funds. Don't skip these. Don't assume. Ask. Verify. Document.
>
> Questions?"

**[Pause 5 seconds.]**

### SLIDE 8: Risk Rating Methodology (10:30–12:00)

**SAY:**
> "Risk rating determines your workload.
>
> **Low risk:** Singapore government entities. SGX-listed companies. Low-risk products like savings accounts. Standard CDD. Annual review. RM approval.
>
> **Medium risk:** Private Singapore companies. Normal business. Standard corporate accounts. Typical transaction volumes. Same as low risk: standard CDD, annual review, RM approval.
>
> **High risk:** This is where it changes. PEPs. High-risk jurisdictions. Complex ownership structures. Unusual transaction patterns. Cash-intensive businesses. Dual-use goods traders — that's the new 2025 requirement.
>
> High risk requires Enhanced Due Diligence. EDD means: senior management approval. More frequent reviews — quarterly, not annual. Deeper source of wealth and source of funds verification. Enhanced transaction monitoring.
>
> Here's the key: if you're unsure, rate it higher. Better to over-flag than under-flag. MAS penalties are for under-flagging. Never for over-flagging.
>
> Questions on risk rating?"

**[Pause 5 seconds.]**

### SLIDE 9: Red Flags — Corporate Accounts (12:00–14:00)

**SAY:**
> "Now we get practical. Red flags. By product.
>
> Corporate accounts. These are the patterns I see in enforcement cases.
>
> Company incorporated less than 3 months ago. No track record. Red flag. Ask for contracts. Invoices. Proof of business pipeline.
>
> Expected turnover unrealistic. S$5 million a month for a new F&B company? Red flag. Ask: 'Help me understand how you'll achieve this volume.'
>
> Registered address is residential or virtual office. May indicate shell company. Ask: 'Where do you actually operate from? Can I see utility bills?'
>
> Complex ownership with no business reason. Five layers of holding companies across three jurisdictions? Ask: 'What's the business purpose of this structure?' If the answer is 'tax planning' or 'privacy,' that's not a business reason.
>
> Reluctance to provide beneficial ownership. Who are we dealing with? You need to identify all humans with 25% or more ownership. If they refuse, you can't onboard.
>
> Multiple companies with same directors and addresses opening accounts simultaneously. Potential mule accounts. Ask: 'What's the relationship between these companies?'
>
> Business activity doesn't match profile. Manufacturing company expecting S$10 million in FX per month? Ask: 'Walk me through your typical transaction flow.'
>
> Red flags aren't accusations. They're invitations to look deeper. Ask the question. Get the answer. Document it.
>
> Questions?"

**[Pause 5 seconds.]**

**[Click to Slide 10.]**

**TIME:** 2 minutes

### SLIDE 10: Red Flags — Remittances (14:00–16:00)

**SAY:**
> "Remittances. High-risk. MAS requires full originator information on all cross-border transfers.
>
> Missing or incomplete originator information. If it's missing, you must follow up. Or reject the transfer. MAS requirement.
>
> Frequent transfers to high-risk jurisdictions. FATF grey list or black list countries. Ask: 'What's the business purpose of transfers to [jurisdiction]?'
>
> Round-dollar amounts repeatedly. Exactly S$50,000 each time? Red flag. Say: 'I notice a pattern. Help me understand.'
>
> Transfers inconsistent with business. Retail company sending money to offshore gambling jurisdiction? Ask: 'How does this relate to your core business?'
>
> Structuring. Multiple transfers just below S$20,000 threshold. Avoiding CDD. Three S$19,000 transfers in one week? That's S$57,000. That's CDD territory. Say: 'These transfers together exceed S$20,000. I need to do CDD.'
>
> Rapid movement. Funds in, funds out within 24 to 48 hours. Layering. Classic laundering technique. Ask: 'Why the urgency? Can the funds stay in the account longer?'
>
> Third-party payments with no clear relationship. Who are we dealing with? Ask: 'What's your relationship with the sender or receiver?'
>
> Patterns matter more than individual transactions. Look at the whole picture.
>
> Questions?"

**[Pause 5 seconds.]**

**[Click to Slide 11.]**

**TIME:** 2 minutes

### SLIDE 11: Red Flags — Loans (16:00–18:00)

**SAY:**
> "Loan red flags are subtler.
>
> Early repayment without reasonable explanation. Why borrow at 5% and repay immediately? Often it's to legitimize funds. Ask: 'What's driving the early repayment?'
>
> Loan proceeds to be paid to unrelated third parties. Why not to the borrower? Ask: 'What's the relationship with the third party?' If the answer doesn't make business sense, escalate.
>
> Collateral source unclear. Where did this asset come from? Ask: 'Can you document how you acquired this collateral?'
>
> Customer indifferent to interest rates or loan terms. Legitimate borrowers care about cost. Money launderers don't. They're paying for cleaning, not borrowing. Say: 'You seem unconcerned about the rate. Can you help me understand?'
>
> Loan purpose vague. 'Business expansion' with no specifics. No verification possible. Ask: 'Can you be more specific? Which business? What expansion?'
>
> Guarantor relationship unclear. Why is this person or company guaranteeing? Ask: 'What's the relationship between borrower and guarantor?'
>
> Request to disburse to multiple accounts or jurisdictions. Layering risk. Ask: 'Why multiple accounts? What's the business need?'
>
> Loan red flags require judgment. If something doesn't make business sense, it probably isn't business.
>
> Questions?"

**[Pause 5 seconds.]**

**[Click to Slide 12.]**

**TIME:** 2 minutes

### SLIDE 12: Red Flags — Insurance (18:00–20:00)

**SAY:**
> "Insurance. Classic laundering vehicle.
>
> Single-premium policies with large lump-sum payments. S$100,000 or more paid upfront. Ask: 'Can you explain the source of these funds?'
>
> Early surrender requests. Within 6 to 12 months. Classic laundering pattern. Buy with dirty money. Surrender early. Get 'clean' insurance proceeds. Ask: 'What's changed since you purchased the policy?'
>
> Surrender proceeds to be paid to third parties. Who benefits? Ask: 'What's your relationship with the surrender recipient?'
>
> Overpayment followed by request for refund. Testing the system. Laundering. Ask: 'I need to understand why there was an overpayment.'
>
> Customer unable to explain why this product suits their needs. Why this product? Ask: 'Help me understand why this policy fits your needs.'
>
> Policy beneficiary has no insurable interest. Unrelated company as beneficiary? Ask: 'What's the relationship with the beneficiary?'
>
> Frequent changes to beneficiary designations. Potential layering. Ask: 'I notice multiple beneficiary changes. What's driving this?'
>
> Insurance is particularly vulnerable because surrender proceeds look clean. They're from an insurance company. Legitimate. But the source might not be.
>
> Questions on red flags?"

### SLIDE 13: Case Study 1 — The Rushed Onboarding (20:00–22:00)

> "Case studies. Real scenarios. Based on enforcement cases.
>
> Case Study 1: The Rushed Onboarding.
>
> Company incorporated 2 weeks ago. Directors: 2 foreigners. No Singapore presence. Business: 'Trading in electronics.' Expected turnover: S$5 million a month. Documents: minimal. No contracts. No invoices. RM pressure: 'This is a premium client. Let's fast-track.'

> What should have happened? Request business plan and contracts. Verify source of initial capital. Understand trading counterparties. Rate as HIGH risk. EDD. Senior management approval.

>
> Lesson: Pressure is not a reason to skip CDD.

### SLIDE 14: Case Study 2 — The Helpful Customer (22:00–23:30)

**SAY:**
> "Case Study 2: The Helpful Customer.
>
> Existing customer. 2 years. Low-risk rating. Requests to add new signatory. New signatory: nephew of original director. Customer says: 'He's tech-savvy. Will help with online banking.' No change in beneficial ownership declared. Customer unusually eager to complete quickly.
>
> Red flags missed: Why add signatory without changing BO? Nephew's background: no employment records. Customer unusually eager. New signatory has PEP connection. Discovered later.
>
> Correct action: Treat as CDD trigger. Change in control. Verify new signatory's identity and background. Update beneficial ownership assessment. Re-rate customer risk if needed. Document decision rationale.
>
> Lesson: Existing customers can become high-risk. CDD is ongoing.
>
> This one's subtle. Customer was 'low-risk' for 2 years. RM thought: 'Existing customer. Just a formality.' Wrong. Change in control equals CDD trigger. The nephew turned out to be connected to a PEP. Customer's account was being used to layer funds. RM didn't re-assess.
>
> Any material change. Re-assess.

### SLIDE 15: Case Study 3 — The Structuring Pattern (23:30–25:00)

**SAY:**
> "Case Study 3: The Structuring Pattern.
>
> Corporate account. Retail business. 6 months of normal activity. Suddenly: 8 cash deposits in 10 days. Each deposit: S$18,000 to S$19,500. Total: S$152,000. Customer explanation: 'Busy season. Cash sales.'
>
> Red flags: Deposits just below S$20,000 threshold. Pattern change. No history of large cash deposits. Retail business with suddenly high cash volume. Customer evasive when asked for sales records.
>
> Correct action: Request sales records and invoices. File Suspicious Transaction Report. Consider account restriction. Escalate to AML compliance team.
>
> Lesson: Patterns matter more than individual transactions.
>
> Structuring is when someone breaks large amounts into smaller transactions to avoid thresholds. Eight deposits of S$18,000 to S$19,500 in 10 days? That's not coincidence. That's intentional.
>
> RM asked for sales records. Customer couldn't provide. STR filed. Account frozen. Later investigation: funds were from unlicensed money lending. RM caught it because she noticed the pattern.
>
> This is what good CDD looks like.
>
> Questions on case studies?"

### SLIDE 16: When to Escalate (25:00–26:30)

>
> Any HIGH-risk customer rating. 2 or more red flags identified. Customer refuses to provide CDD information. Inconsistencies in customer explanations. Suspicion of ML, TF, or PF — regardless of amount. PEP identified. High-risk jurisdiction connection. Unusual transaction patterns.
>
> Escalation pathway: Document your concerns. Email and system notes. Notify your branch manager. Submit to AML Compliance Team via [internal system name]. Do NOT tip off the customer.
>
> Remember: You are protected when you escalate in good faith. You are NOT protected when you ignore red flags.
>
> Escalation is not failure. Escalation is professionalism. If you see 2+ red flags, escalate. If a customer refuses to provide information, escalate. If something feels off, escalate.

### SLIDE 17: Documentation Standards (26:30–28:00)

**SAY:**
> "Documentation.
>
> Before submitting application: Customer identity verified. Original documents sighted. Beneficial ownership chart completed and attached. All beneficial owners identified. 25% or more ownership or control. Purpose and nature of business documented. Expected transaction patterns recorded. Source of Wealth verified and documented. Source of Funds verified and documented. Risk rating rationale. Why low, medium, or high? Red flags identified and how they were addressed. Escalation notes if applicable. All documents uploaded to system. Your name or ID and date recorded.
>
> Retention period: Minimum 5 years. MAS requirement.
>
> Golden rule: If it's not documented, it didn't happen.
>
> MAS examiners don't take your word for it. They look at the system. If it's not documented, it didn't happen. Beneficial ownership: draw the chart. Source of funds: note what you verified. Risk rating: write why. Red flags: note what you asked, what the customer said, why you're satisfied or why you escalated.

> Five years minimum retention. Document like your career depends on it. Because it does.

## 🎤 Delivery Tips

### Voice & Presence
- **Pace:** Slow down. Nerves make us speed up. Breathe.
- **Volume:** Project to back of room. Test before session.
- **Eye contact:** Scan room. Don't fixate on slides or notes.
- **Pauses:** Use silence. Let key points sink in.
- **Tone:** Serious but not scary. Professional but human.

### Handling Questions
- **If you know:** Answer clearly and concisely.
- **If you don't know:** "Good question. Let me check with AML Compliance and get back to you." Document it. Follow up within 24 hours.
- **If question is off-topic:** "That's outside today's scope. Let's talk after the session."
- **If question is challenging:** Stay calm. Don't get defensive. "I understand your concern. Here's the regulatory requirement..."

### Managing Time
- **Watch the clock:** Keep timer visible.
- **If running long:** Skip detailed quiz explanations. Move to closing.
- **If running short:** Expand on case studies. Ask: "Has anyone encountered a similar situation?"
- **Never rush closing:** Key takeaways matter.

### Hybrid-Specific Tips
- **Acknowledge virtual participants:** "For those on Zoom..."
- **Repeat questions:** "Someone on Zoom asked..."
- **Check tech:** "Can everyone see the slides? Hear me clearly?"
- **Assign co-host:** Someone to monitor Zoom chat while you present

> "You're not compliance officers. You're relationship managers. But you're also the first line of defense for your clients, your institution, and yourself. Protect all three. With clarity. With wisdom. With care."
