# Madhyamaka — Week 1: What Is the Middle Way?

**Dates:** April 4–10, 2026  
**Goal:** Understand what Madhyamaka is and why it matters

---

## DAY 1: First Encounter

### Reading

**Madhyamaka** (Sanskrit: मध्यमक) means "Middle Way."

It's called "middle" because it avoids two extremes:

| Extreme | View | Problem |
|---------|------|---------|
| **Eternalism** | Things truly, permanently exist | Leads to attachment, fear of loss |
| **Nihilism** | Nothing exists at all | Leads to despair, denial of meaning |

**The Middle Way:**
- Things exist **conventionally** (like a dream, like an illusion)
- Things don't exist **inherently** (not independently, not permanently)
- Both statements are true simultaneously

### The Founder

**Nāgārjuna** (c. 150–250 CE, India)

- Buddhist philosopher, possibly the most sophisticated ever
- Wrote *Mūlamadhyamakakārikā* (MMK) — "Root Verses on the Middle Way"
- 27 chapters, 450 verses
- Method: Deconstruct all fixed views without asserting his own

**Famous verse:**
> *"The Buddha's teaching of the Dharma is based on two truths:  
> A truth of worldly convention and an ultimate truth.  
> Those who do not understand the distinction between these two truths  
> Do not understand the Buddha's profound truth."* — MMK 24.8–9

### Why This Matters

Madhyamaka isn't abstract philosophy. It's **liberation technology**.

**The problem:** We suffer because we grasp at things as if they were permanent, independent, and inherently real.

**The solution:** See through the grasping. Realize emptiness. Suffering loses its footing.

---

### Practice (10 minutes)

1. Sit comfortably, eyes open or closed
2. Notice thoughts arising
3. Ask: "Where did this thought come from?"
4. Don't answer intellectually — just look
5. Notice: The thought appears, stays briefly, disappears
6. Rest in the space between thoughts

**Intention:** *"May I begin to see how things appear but don't exist as they appear."*

## DAY 2: The Four Noble Truths (Review)

### Reading

Madhyamaka is built on the Buddha's **Four Noble Truths**:

| Truth | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **1. Dukkha** | Suffering exists (unsatisfactoriness, stress) |
| **2. Samudaya** | Suffering has a cause (craving, grasping) |
| **3. Nirodha** | Suffering can end (when grasping stops) |
| **4. Magga** | There's a path (Eightfold Path) |

**Madhyamaka's contribution:** Explains *why* grasping causes suffering.

**Answer:** We grasp at things that don't exist as we think they do. It's like grasping at a mirage — frustration is inevitable.

### The Medical Analogy

The Buddha taught like a physician:

1. **Diagnosis:** You're sick (dukkha)
2. **Cause:** Poison (craving/ignorance)
3. **Prognosis:** Cure is possible (nirvāṇa)
4. **Treatment:** Medicine (Eightfold Path)

Madhyamaka deepens the diagnosis: The poison is **ignorance of emptiness**.

---

### Practice (10 minutes)

1. Recall a recent moment of suffering (frustration, anxiety, anger)
2. Ask: "What was I grasping at?"
3. Ask: "Did I believe something was more real/permanent than it is?"
4. Don't judge — just notice

**Intention:** *"May I see how grasping creates suffering."*

---

### Journal Prompt

> Recall a recent suffering. What was I clinging to? Did I see it as more permanent or real than it was?

## DAY 3: Dependent Origination

### Reading

**Pratītyasamutpāda** (Sanskrit): Dependent Origination

**The principle:** All things arise dependent on causes and conditions. Nothing exists independently.

**Formula:**
> *"When this exists, that comes to be.  
> With the arising of this, that arises.  
> When this does not exist, that does not come to be.  
> With the cessation of this, that ceases."*

**Examples:**

| Thing | Depends On |
|-------|------------|
| A seedling | Seed, soil, water, sun, time |
| A thought | Previous thoughts, senses, brain, concepts |
| "You" | Body, mind, memories, relationships, causes |
| Suffering | Ignorance, craving, grasping |

**Nāgārjuna's insight:**
> *"Whatever is dependently originated, that is explained to be emptiness."* — MMK 24.18

**Translation:** If something depends on other things, it can't exist independently. If it can't exist independently, it's empty of inherent existence.

---

### Practice (15 minutes)

**Contemplation: The Cup**

1. Imagine a cup (or use a real one)
2. Trace its dependencies:
   - Materials: clay, glaze, water
   - Causes: potter, kiln, design
   - Concepts: "cup," "drinking vessel"
   - Your perception: eyes, mind, recognition
3. Ask: "Can I find the cup itself, separate from all this?"
4. Rest in the space when you can't find it

**Intention:** *"May I see how things depend on other things."*

---

### Journal Prompt

> Pick an object. List its dependencies. What happens when I look for the thing itself?

## DAY 4: Emptiness (Śūnyatā)

### Reading

**Emptiness** (Sanskrit: *śūnyatā*) is the most misunderstood concept in Buddhism.

**What emptiness IS:**
- Empty of inherent existence (svabhāva)
- Empty of independence
- Empty of permanence
- Empty of findability under analysis

**What emptiness IS NOT:**
- Nothingness (nihilism)
- A void or vacuum
- A mystical experience
- Something things "have"

**Key:** Emptiness is not a property. It's what things *are* when seen correctly.

**Analogy:** A rainbow
- Appears vividly (conventional truth)
- Can't be grasped, has no substance (ultimate truth)
- Not fake — but not solid either
- Depends on: sun, rain, your position, your eyes

**You are like a rainbow:**
- Appear vividly (you experience yourself)
- Can't be found as a fixed thing (no inherent self)
- Not fake — but not solid either
- Depend on: body, mind, causes, concepts

---

### Practice (15 minutes)

**Contemplation: The Self**

1. Ask: "Where is 'I'?"
2. Look in the body: Is "I" in the hand? Foot? Brain?
3. Look in the mind: Is "I" in this thought? That feeling?
4. Look everywhere: Can you find a fixed, independent "you"?
5. Rest in the not-finding

**Don't panic:** You still exist conventionally. You just don't exist *as you thought you did*.

**Intention:** *"May I see through the illusion of a fixed self."*

---

### Journal Prompt

> When I look for "myself," what do I find? What don't I find?

## DAY 5: The Two Truths

### Reading

Madhyamaka operates on **two levels of truth**:

| Conventional Truth | Ultimate Truth |
|--------------------|----------------|
| How things appear | How things actually are |
| Language, concepts | Beyond concepts |
| Functions (eat, sleep, work) | Empty of inherent existence |
| Suffering is real (conventionally) | Suffering is empty (ultimately) |
| Path is real (conventionally) | Path is empty (ultimately) |

**Crucial:** Both truths are true. Neither is more "real."

**Mistake 1 (Eternalism):** Treat conventional truth as ultimate
- "My suffering is really real!" → grasping, more suffering

**Mistake 2 (Nihilism):** Dismiss conventional truth
- "Nothing matters, it's all empty" → despair, no ethics

**The Middle:**
- Suffering is real enough to address (conventional)
- Suffering has no inherent existence (ultimate)
- Therefore: Work to end suffering, but don't grasp at it

**Nāgārjuna:**
> *"Without relying on convention, the ultimate is not taught.  
> Without understanding the ultimate, nirvāṇa is not attained."* — MMK 24.10

---

### Practice (15 minutes)

**Two Truths Meditation**

1. Pick a current problem or concern
2. **Conventional view:** What's happening? What can be done? Take it seriously.
3. **Ultimate view:** What is inherently real about this? Can you find it?
4. Hold both together: Real enough to act, empty enough to not grasp

**Intention:** *"May I hold both truths without contradiction."*

---

### Journal Prompt

> Pick a current struggle. How does it look from the conventional view? From the ultimate view? Can I hold both?

## DAY 6: The Tetralemma

### Reading

The **Tetralemma** (Catuṣkoṭi) is Nāgārjuna's four-cornered negation.

For any proposition, four positions are possible:

| Corner | Proposition | Example: "The self exists" |
|--------|-------------|---------------------------|
| 1 | Exists | The self exists |
| 2 | Doesn't exist | The self doesn't exist |
| 3 | Both exists and doesn't | The self both exists and doesn't |
| 4 | Neither exists nor doesn't | The self neither exists nor doesn't |

**Nāgārjuna negates all four:**

1. ❌ **Not exists** — If self existed inherently, it would be permanent, unchanging. But you change.
2. ❌ **Not doesn't exist** — If self didn't exist at all, there'd be no experience. But you experience.
3. ❌ **Not both** — Contradictory properties can't inhere in the same thing.
4. ❌ **Not neither** — Still trapped in the existence/non-existence framework.

**What remains?** Not a fifth position. But **freedom from needing a position**.

**Purpose:** Exhaust conceptual proliferation (*prapañca*). When the mind stops grasping at views, reality appears as it is.

---

### Practice (15 minutes)

**Tetralemma Contemplation**

1. Pick a question: "Does happiness exist inherently?"
2. Examine each corner:
   - Exists? (look for it)
   - Doesn't exist? (but you experience it)
   - Both? (contradiction)
   - Neither? (escape hatch)
3. Notice what happens when all four collapse
4. Rest in the gap

**Intention:** *"May I be free from needing fixed views."*

---

### Journal Prompt

> Apply the tetralemma to something I care about. What happens when all four corners collapse?

## DAY 7: Integration and Rest

### Reading

**Review the week:**

- Madhyamaka = Middle Way (avoids eternalism and nihilism)
- Dependent origination = Things arise depending on causes/conditions
- Emptiness = Things lack inherent existence
- Two truths = Conventional (appears) + Ultimate (empty)
- Tetralemma = All four positions negated → freedom from views

**This is just the beginning.**

Nāgārjuna's MMK has 27 chapters. You've touched the surface. But the surface contains the depth.

---

### Practice (20 minutes)

**Open Meditation**

1. Sit comfortably
2. No specific object — just rest
3. When thoughts arise, notice: "This is dependently originated"
4. When emotions arise, notice: "This is empty"
5. When grasping arises, notice: "This is the habit I'm unlearning"
6. Rest in openness

**Intention:** *"May this week's understanding deepen naturally."*
