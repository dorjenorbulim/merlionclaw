I have mentioned earlier that the practice of going for refuge is extremely
important. Of the three object of refuge, the Buddha is the one who shows
the refuge. When one goes for refuge to the Buddha, essentially it means
that one recognizes the Buddha to be the guide and one is making a pledge
to practice his instruction exactly as he taught it. The purpose of going
for refuge to the Buddha is generating the promise or pledge “I will
practice His Teachings exactly as He intended”.
In order to be able to make this kind of promise or pledge, one must first
understand how the Buddha is an unparalleled teacher, and there is no other
guide like him. In order to have this kind of conviction, one must hear the
teachings and reflect on it in order to develop the understanding and
conviction that the Buddha is an unparalleled teacher. When this kind of
faith and conviction in that the Buddha being an unparalleled teacher
arises, then one will naturally and easily be able to make the conviction
“I am going to practice His Teachings exactly as the way He taught it.”
The Buddha is the unparalleled/unsurpassed teacher. The
unparalleled/unsurpassed refuge is the Dharma. It is said that this
unsurpassed refuge, the Dharma, is that which:
    • protects us from all the undesirable things and
    • protects us from samsara and the lower realms
By seeing this, then one goes for refuge to the Dharma. When one has such
conviction arising in the heart, that is going for refuge to the Dharma. By
going refuge, one makes a pledge or commitment to practice the Dharma
purely.
    • The unsurpassed teacher - the Buddha.
    • The unsurpassed refuge – the Dharma.
    • The unsurpassed guide – the Sangha.
The unsurpassed guide is referring to those who guide us and help us in our
practice. They are role model for us. Essentially they are the superior or
the Arya or superior Sangha. So going for refuge to the Sangha is making
the commitment to practice like these Arya Sanghas.
Going for refuge to the Buddha, Dharma and Sangha is essentially making
this pledge or commitment. This is what refuge is. With such a commitment,
one’s practice then begins. On the basis of knowing well what exactly the
Buddha, Dharma and Sangha are one then can make a strong pledge or
commitment.
Engaging in the Bodhisattva Deeds
We started on the section on confession. The teaching tells us that we
should engage in the practice of confession through applying the four
powers. Of the four powers, we have begun with the power of eradication.
The text talks about generating regret for:
- all the negativities accumulated since beginningless lifetimes
- the negativities that are accumulated by oneself
- the negativities accumulated when one caused or had made others engage in
negativities.
One also confesses having rejoiced in the negativities that were performed
by others.
One also confesses all the negativities accumulated in relation to the
Buddha, Dharma and Sangha, in relation to our parent and so forth.
By bringing to mind all the results that one will experience in the future
when the negativities that one has accumulated ripened, and contemplating
on these, one develops regret.
If we were to die without having purified the negativities that we have
accumulated, it will be a cause for regret.
In the text, it talks about bringing to mind of how we will regret if we
were to die without having purified those negativities; especially since
death is certain and the time of death is uncertain.
The next verse talks about generating regret for having accumulated the
negativities:
-  for the sake of our loved ones,
-  for the sake of our resources, and
-  for ourselves
Generating regret by bringing to mind how all these will not last.
The text is saying that many a time we accumulate negativities for the sake
of our loved ones, our resources and our possessions, not knowing that at
the end of the day, we have to leave all these behind.
The text is also telling us that many a times, we accumulate negativities
out of attachment for the love ones and out of dislike and anger for the
enemies in the quest of trying to defeat or destroy them, so it is
inappropriate to accumulate negativities for the sake of the loved ones and
the enemies.

Verse 2.34
Leaving all behind, I must depart alone.
Through not having understood this
I committed various kinds of negativities
For the sake of my friends and foes.
Verse 2.35
Even my foes will become nothing.
My friends will also become nothing.
I too will become nothing.
Likewise all will become nothing.
If one tries very hard to destroy the enemies, or even if one doesn’t do
anything, sooner or later, they will die. Likewise for the loved ones, one
tries so hard to sustain them and to keep them, overtime they will
gradually decline and will also die.
We ourselves will one day also cease to exist. We will die. It will also
come a day that the world will also cease to exist. For that reason, it
becomes meaningless to accumulate negativities for their sake. By thinking
in this way, how it is inappropriate to accumulate negativities for their
sake, one generates regret.
Why it is meaningless? You try all ways and means to sustain all these
relationship, but at the end of the day, they will all become just an
object of memory; nothing else.

Verse 2.36
Just like an experience in a dream,
Whatever things I enjoy
Will become a memory.
Whatever has passed will not be seen.
Come the time of death, the last day of your life, no matter how much
enjoyments you had in that life, no matter how many friends or loved ones
or possessions that you have, they are just objects of memory. They can't
help you beyond being just an object of memory. This is evident if you
think about it. Just imagine at the very last day of your lives, all those
past experiences are past and finished. If you bring to mind, they are just
objects of memory. So it is evident that all these things are unreliable.
You can see directly with your own eyes.

Verse 2.37
Even within this brief life
Many friends and foes have passed away,
But whatever terrible negativities I committed for them
Remains before me.
It is our experience and is something that we can directly witness with our
own eyes. Be it loved ones, friends or enemies, at the end of the day,
friends that have been nice that we have sustained our relationship, our
enemies who had harmed us, those we would like to harm, and so forth, all
will die. When they die, they are gone, but the negativities that we
accumulated in relation to our friends and enemies remain.
The teaching is telling us that the negativities that we have accumulated
for the sake of loved ones and enemies will remain with us even after they
have died. Our negativities accompany us like our shadow and we will have
to experience the sufferings that these negativities will bring about.
It is clearly evident that whatever benefits that we received from our
loved ones and whatever harms that we received from the enemies only last
for as long as they exist; they only last for this life. But the
negativities that we accumulate in order to overcome the enemies and in
order to sustain friends and loved ones continue with us from life to life.
Although the time of death is uncertain, but because of us being
predisposed to believing that everything is unchanging and permanent, we
create a lot of negativities.
The teaching is telling us to generate regret for having lived in such a
way through remembering the uncertainty of the time of death.

Verse 2.39
Remaining neither day nor night,
This life is always slipping by
And never lengthening.
Why will death not come to one like me?
“Remaining neither day nor night” means time is slipping by. Time doesn’t
stop and nothing is being added to our lifespan. It is definite that we
will die, and we still have with us all the negativities that we have
accumulated. Bringing this to mind, we generate regret.
Because the time of death is uncertain, therefore we must strive to purify
the negativities.
The teaching gives an analogy of a pond. Imagine there is a pond that does
not have any inflow of water from other sources. Then under certain
condition of hot weather and so forth, the pond will start to dry up. It
will come a time that the body of water will cease to exist. This is like
our lifespan.
If we do not purify our negativities then we would have to experience its
result which is suffering even in this life. If we do not purify our
negativities, even our friends, our loved ones and relatives would not be
able to do anything.

Verse 2.40
While I am lying in bed,
Although surrounded by my friends and relatives,
The feeling of life being severed
Will be experienced by me alone.
Verse 2.41ab
When seized by Yama’s messengers,
What benefit will friends and relatives afford?
When we are lying on our death bed, we may be surrounded by our loved ones,
friends and relatives. They may do all sort of things to keep us alive, but
there is nothing they can do to prevent us from experiencing the suffering
of death. We have to go through the experience of dying ourselves alone.
The terrifying fear or suffering at the time of death can only be
experience by oneself and not by anyone else.
At the time of death, we undergo the death process where all the various
subtle elements in the body undergo the dissolution process. If one has
accumulated lots of negativities, all kinds of frightening, scary, and
terrifying images will be projected to the mind. One will have to
experience all these fearful, frightening visions and appearances by
oneself. Nobody can protect us from that; not the doctor nor our loved
ones.
Next the thing that can protect us is our merits.

Verse 2.41cd
My merit alone shall protect me then,
But upon that I have never relied.
The actual unsurpassed protection, the protector, is our own virtuous
karma. We did not accumulate virtuous karma, and by thinking about this,
bringing this fact to mind, we develop regret.

Verse 2.42
O Protectors! I, so unconscientious,
Unaware of such terror as this,
Performed many negativities
For the sake of this impermanent life.
This is like an expression of regret.
(BP1 L13) This verse refers to generating regret. It begins with “O
Protectors!” One is calling the buddhas and bodhisattvas, who are in the
nature of great compassion, and declaring to them, ‘I have engaged in so
many negative actions to sustain my loved ones, motivated by attachment and
to defeat and harm my enemies because of not knowing how everything is
impermanent, how it is pointless and meaningless to work for the affairs of
this life. I have engaged in negativities with my body, speech and mind,
not having faith and conviction in karma and forgetting that I have to die
with all these fears. I am now generating regret for my ignorance.’ One
invokes the buddhas and bodhisattvas and generates regret for the
negativities that are motivated by the three mental poisons, which will
throw one into the lower realms.

Verse 2.43
One who is being led today to the spot where his limbs will be mutilated
Will be petrified.
With dry mouth, sunken eyes, and so forth
His entire appearance is transfigured.
Verse 2.44
What need to mention the tremendous despair
When stricken with the disease of terror,
Being clasped by the frightful physical forms
Of the messengers of Yama?
If a person is led to the execution ground or place where he/she would be
killed, that person would be very frightened. By looking at the physical
appearance of that person, you can see all these signs of being very
terrified. If that is the case, then it goes without saying that at the
time of death when we have the vision of the messengers of the Lord of
Death coming to catch and take us away, that would be very frightening.
This is what may happen at the time of death. Then what will happen in the
next life?

Verse 2.45
“Who can afford me good protection
From this terror?”
With terrified, bulging eyes agape
I shall search the four directions for refuge.
Verse 2.46
Then seeing no refuge in the four directions
I shall become enveloped in gloom.
If there should be no refuge there,
At that time what shall I do?
It is said that there are people who died experience this - they are
terrified, they look around for help and they don’t find any protection.
Their minds become completely enveloped by darkness, literally become very
gloomy. In the next life if you are born in the hells, when you look
around, you could not find any help, and it is terrifying. That is the time
when the Buddha will not be able to protect you because you were born there
due to very powerful negative karma. When the karma ripens, there is
nothing the Buddha can do.
Up to this point here, verses V2.27 to 2.46 come under the power of
eradication.

The very essence of generating regret for having accumulated negativities
through remembering:
    • how death is certain,
    • time of death is uncertain,
    • at the time of death nothing help us except the dharma.
By thinking about these, one develops regret for having accumulated all the
negativities. Like what we saw earlier, in order to overcome those we don’t
like (the enemies), we really have to put in so much effort. Likewise we
also put in a lot of effort to support and sustain those who are in
agreement with us. Through any of these ways, we accumulate a lot of
negativities. But at the time of death, none of them is of any benefit. By
remembering and keeping this in mind, in the first place, as much as
possible we should abstain or refrain from accumulating negativities. In
the case when we do accumulate negativities, as much as possible we should
purify them without any delay by remembering how the time of death is
uncertain.
We have to remember that we can sustain and support our love ones through
attachment, and overcome the enemies, there will come a time they will
cease to exist but the negativities we have accumulated in relation to
doing things for them will remain with us, and we will have to experience
the results. By remembering this, we should refrain from accumulating
negativities.
Next is the power of support, starting with going for refuge to the Three
Jewels right now.

Verse 2.52
 And I go for refuge to Vajrapani,
Upon the sight of whom all hateful beings,
Such as the messengers of Yama,
Flee in terror to the four directions.
There are the eight great sons or the eight close bodhisattvas –
Samantabhadra, Manjushri, Avalokiteshvara, Vajrapani, Ksitigarbha,
Akashagarbha, Sarvanivaranaviskambini, and Maitreya. Bring to mind these
bodhisattvas then one takes refuge in them. One brings to mind all the
negativities. After having gone for refuge, one should practice according
to the advice.

(BP1 L13) We offer our bodies to our Protector Avalokiteshvara, someone who
infallibly acts with compassion. He is in the nature of great compassion;
all his activities and his conduct do not contradict this mind of
compassion.
Vajrapani works to remove the opposing conditions of the bodies , speech
and minds of the buddhas of the three times. From the time these buddhas
generate bodhicitta till the time they achieve enlightenment, and from the
time they turn the wheel of Dharma till they pass away to nirvana -
throughout these periods, Vajrapani will be present to help eliminate the
opposing conditions to their bodies, speech and minds. The common
explanation is that Vajrapani works to protect oneself from obstacles. From
the tantric viewpoint, Vajrapani is the embodiment of the fully developed
powers of the buddhas. He is called Vajrapani because he holds a vajra in
his hand. In Tibetan is chagna dorje; chag is hand, dorje is vajra and na
is in. Literally, it means “vajra in hand”. Vajrapani is the supreme
protector who protects us from the opposing conditions of the body, speech
and mind.

Verse 2.53
Previously I transgressed your words,
But now upon seeing this great fear
I go to you for refuge.
By doing so, please swiftly clear away this fear.
“Previously I transgressed your word” - “I” here refer to the one who has
accumulated all the non-virtuous negative karma. “I”, this person, who has
accumulated so much negative karma because of not following “your
teachings, have gone against your advice and have engaged in those things
that need to be discarded and not adopting those things that need to be
adopted”. As a result, one would have to experience all the sufferings and
problems. By remembering this, one is kind of like making a plea or
request, then going for refuge to them.
That is the power of support, in this case is going for refuge to the Three
Jewels and the practice of relying on the eight close sons of the buddhas

Verse 2.54
If I need to comply with a doctor’s advice
When frightened by an ordinary illness,
Then what need to mention that it is so when perpetually diseased
By the hundreds of faults of attachment and so forth?
When people fall sick, out of fear for their health and their lives, they
would look for doctor to seek a consultation and a diagnosis. Whatever the
doctor advises, they will usually follow exactly even though it may involve
some hardships or inconveniences. If that is the case for an ordinary
sickness, then it goes without saying that for someone who is being
afflicted by hundreds of faults of attachment, anger, ignorance and pride
etc., needs to rely and follow exactly the advice given by the Omniscience
One, the Buddha. This mental sickness, the afflictions, has so much more
short comings, and the medicine to cure these afflictions is very rare.

Verse 2.55
If all the people dwelling in Jambudvipa
Can be crushed by just one of these,
And if no other medicine to cure them
Is to be found in all the directions
In Gyaltsab Je’s commentary it is said that if even one moment of anger
towards a bodhisattva can destroy all the human beings in this world and
cause them to be born in the hells, the medicine for such a sickness which
is meditation on the path and so forth is so rare that one cannot find it
anywhere else apart from the teachings that revealed them. So other than
the teachings that revealed them, one will not be able to find such a
medicine anywhere else. You will not even be able to find it in the
celestial realm of Brahma.
Even just one affliction can harm so many sentient beings. The medicine to
cure the afflictions is only the Dharma. The sickness/disease of the
afflictions is so much more powerful and the cure for that is only the
dharma.
We will finish the rest of Chapter 2 in the next lesson. After that we will
begin with Chapter 3, the chapter on adopting bodhicitta.

(BP1 L13) Verse 2.55 refers to the great faults of the three mental
poisons. We do not have to consider all the three poisons. Just one of the
three is sufficient to overcome all the living beings on this earth: “Can
be overcome by just one of these”. Entertaining an instant of anger towards
a bodhisattva will create the cause to go to the lower realms and take
rebirth in the unrelenting Avici hell. If everyone on this Earth were to
generate anger towards a bodhisattva for a single instant, that instant of
anger would wipe out everyone by throwing him/her into the lower realms in
his/her next rebirth. The three poisons are, therefore, very deadly and
very dangerous.
What is the medicine that can cure us of these three mental poisons? The
supreme antidote for removing the three mental poisons is to meditate on
the path. The path here refers to the true path, the mind that directly
realises emptiness. This is the only antidote that can completely remove
all the afflictions from their roots.
How do we generate this wisdom? When we look in all the ten directions for
the instructions for generating this wisdom, we will only find them in the
teachings of the Buddha, nowhere else. We can say that nothing else, other
than the teachings of the Buddha, can help us to generate this wisdom. This
is the direct antidote to all the afflictions. It is not enough just to say
that and then sit quietly and do nothing about it. We have to investigate.
We do not say things for no reason. We need to know why such a statement is
made. We have to investigate the teachings of the Buddha. Are the teachings
of the Buddha perfect? Why is it that the teachings of the Buddha contain
the antidotes to remove the three mental poisons? We have to understand all
this.
