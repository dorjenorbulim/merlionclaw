The Third Chapter: Thoroughly Embracing Bodhicitta
The seven-branch worship is a practice to accumulate merit and to purify
the mind of obscurations.
In chapter two, the chapter on Confession, we have looked at the branches
of:
1. Homage
2, Offerings and
3. Confession.
Here in chapter three, chapter on Adopting Bodhicitta, we will cover the
remaining branches of:
4. Rejoicing
5. Urging the turning the wheel of dharma
6. Requesting not to pass away into parinirvana and
7. Dedication.

|A Rejoicing in virtue                               |

For the branch of rejoicing, there are:
- rejoicing in the virtue of the cause and effect relating to high status,
- rejoicing in the virtue of the cause and effect relating to liberation
- rejoicing in the virtue of the cause and effect and related to
enlightenment

|1 Rejoicing in the virtues of the causes for and    |
|effects of higher rebirths                          |


Verse 3.1
With joy I rejoice
In the virtue that relieves the suffering of unfortunate rebirths
Of all sentient beings
And in the abiding in happiness of those subject to suffering.
Here one brings to mind all those who are accumulating the virtues that
will result in the attainment of high status either as a human being or as
a god. Here we are rejoicing in virtues that will lead to high status that
are not conjoined with either renunciation, bodhicitta or the correct view.
Even though the virtues that will result in high status either as human
being or god are not conjoined with renunciation, bodhicitta or the correct
view, nevertheless it is a cause for high status or good rebirth. So one
bring to mind these virtues and rejoice.
One should also rejoice in those sentient beings who have achieved good
rebirths. In those rebirths, they have excellent bodies, wealth, power,
prestige, fame and so forth. When you see such things that are happening to
others, rather than feeling jealous or having the negative sense of
competitiveness, you should feel happy that these sentient beings are
enjoying these things because they are enjoying the fruits of the virtuous
karma that they themselves have accumulated in the past.
When you see sentient beings accumulating virtue, even though it may be a
small virtue, but nevertheless will results in a high status, good rebirth,
rather than feeling jealous, you should feel happy for them and rejoice.
Similarly, when you see sentient beings enjoying the good things in life,
such as having good body, prestige, power and fame, enjoyments, wealth and
so forth, you should also feel happy for them.  You should rejoice rather
than getting jealous because rejoicing helps to counteract jealousy. When
you rejoice, you accumulate vast amount of merits, and your minds do not
get disturbed.
The point is that we should not feel disturbed when we see something good
happening to others. Rather than feeling disturbed, we should feel happy
and rejoice, for if we are able to feel happy for them, it will be of great
benefit to us. We have to bear in mind that our discussion here is in the
context of moving towards bodhicitta. Bodhicitta is the spirit of wanting
to benefit others. So in the light of that spirit, the practice of
rejoicing is the preliminary to generating bodhicitta.

|2 Rejoicing in the virtues of the causes for  |
|and effect of mere liberation                 |


Verse 3.2
I rejoice in that accumulation of virtue
That is the cause for enlightenment,
I rejoice in the definite liberation of embodied creatures
From the suffering of cyclic existence.
Here we rejoice in all the virtues that are done for the sake of achieving
either the Hearer’s enlightenment or the Solitary Realizer’s enlightenment.
So we rejoice in the virtue that is conjoined with the determination to be
free or renunciation, and the correct view.
We also rejoice in the virtue of the effect i.e. attainments, related to
just liberation. We rejoice in those who have achieved temporary
attainments Hearer’s or Solitary Realizer’s enlightenment. Here “temporary
attainments” refer to the attainments of:
- Hearers who are stream enterer,
- Hearers who are once-returner,
- Hearers who are non-returner, and
- Those who have achieved the actual Hearer’s enlightenment, the arhatship,
i.e. those who have liberated themselves from cyclic existence.
So we rejoice in all these effects.
(BP1 L15) Both Hearers and Solitary Realizers are Hinayanists, but they
differ in their motivation, behavior, merit, and wisdom. Solitary Realizers
are superior to Hearers in all these respects.
There are eight types of Hearers, according to the level of delusions they
have abandoned:
1. Approachers to the state of a stream enterer,
2. Abiders in the state of a stream enterer,
3. Approachers to the state of a once returner,
4. Abiders in the state of a once returner,
5. Approachers to the state of a never returner,
6. Abiders in the state of a never returner,
7. Approachers to the state of a foe destroyer, and
8. Abiders in the state of a foe destroyer.
A stream enterer is on the path of seeing and will never again be reborn in
the three lower realms,
A once returner will return to the desire realm only one more time
A never returner will never again return to the desire realm.
A Hearer is a Hinayana disciple who strives for nirvana on the basis of
listening to teachings from a teacher, while a Solitary Realizer is a
Hinayana disciple who strives for nirvana in solitude, without relying on a
teacher.

|3 Rejoicing in the causes for and effect of   |
|unsurpassable enlightenment                   |


Verse 3.3
I rejoice in the enlightenment of the Protectors
And also in the grounds of their children.
Verse 3.4
With joy I rejoice
In the ocean of virtue of generating the mind of enlightenment
That causes all sentient beings happiness,
As well as in the deeds that benefit sentient beings.
Here we rejoice focusing on those who have actualized the complete
abandonment and realizations.
We rejoice in the effects of temporary enlightenment; meaning that we bring
to mind the qualities of realizations and abandonments of those abiding on
the grounds; from the 1st ground onwards.
We have gone through little bit of the Paths and Grounds. If you know
something about the Paths and Grounds, for example the qualities of
realizations and abandonments that are achieved on those various grounds,
then you will have a lot of things to work with in terms of rejoicing.
There are so many things that you can bring to mind.
In chapter one, we looked at the benefits of both the aspirational and
engaged bodhicitta. Just the benefits of generating the aspirational
bodhicitta alone are innumerable. And those who on that basis practice the
six perfections, their merits that they accumulate are even much more
powerful. You bring to mind the benefits that are accumulated by those who
have developed the complete thought of achieving omniscient or full
enlightenment for all sentient beings.
In the King of Prayers, five types of person are mentioned, and they are
object of rejoicing;
   (1-2)    Buddhas and bodhisattvas in the ten directions
      4) Solitary Realizer and Hearers still training and those beyond
        (5)      All ordinary beings
We should also rejoice in the paths of these three kinds of person (small,
middling and great capacities) as mentioned in the Lamrim.
It is said in the teachings that:
 • If we rejoice in the virtues of those whose level of mind is lower than
   ours, we accumulate more merit than that person.
 • If we rejoice in the virtues of our peers, those who are of the same
   level of mind as ours, we accumulate the same amount of virtues.
 • If we rejoice in the virtues of those whose level of mind is higher than
   ours, we get a portion of it.
When you see others doing good, they are accumulating virtues or things are
going well for them, you are told to feel happy for them and rejoice. If
you don’t, then in its place very often there is jealousy. Then you get
into the negative sense of wanting to compete with this person. The mind
gets disturbed, and there is no peace. This is very clear and is so evident
from your own experience. Rather than feeling jealousy, you can really feel
happy and rejoice, then your mind doesn’t get disturbed. On top of that,
there is peace in the heart. And on top of that, you accumulate merits.
It is difficult to actually measure the amount of merits that you can
collect from rejoicing. That is not possible to express definitely. But it
is very obvious that if you rejoice, you will experience peace in your
heart.
It seems that it is easier to rejoice in the virtues of the Buddhas and
bodhisattvas. Of course it is really difficult to rejoice in the virtues
and the good things that are happening to enemies. Unless we are able to
feel happy, rejoice in the virtues and good things that are happening to
the enemies, than other kind of rejoicing is just like that.
The same for generating or thinking about bodhicitta, you get the feeling;
“It is very nice. I will achieve enlightenment for sentient beings.” You
feel nice about it.  If you think about it, a lot of times somehow the
enemy is excluded from these sentient beings. As such, talking about
bodhicitta is just mere talk. Then going back to rejoicing in the good
things that happen to the enemies, and rejoicing in their qualities, even
if we are currently not able to entertain thoughts of benefiting them, but
we have to start from being able to feel happy for them when they
experience good things or when they are engaging in virtues, and not to
hold on to grudges.
The point I am trying to make is that it is very important for us to learn
and train to be able to rejoice in the good things that are happening to
those people whom we don’t like; so-called enemies. We should be able to
feel happy for the good things that are happening to them and rejoice when
they are engaging in virtues. Most of us cannot do that. When we see our
enemies, we cannot feel happy for the virtues that they are accumulating.
We should train our mind to think that it is pointless to let our mind to
remain being disturbed. There is no point in getting jealousy or being
disturbed. Rather we should think of the benefits of rejoicing; there is
peace in our hearts and we can accumulate merits.

Jealousy causes great disturbance to the mind. As such we need to do

(BP1 L15) “Protectors” refers to the buddhas. “I rejoice in the
enlightenment of the buddhas” is rejoicing in the final effect.
“Children” refers to the bodhisattvas. That is rejoicing in the temporal
effect. We are specifically rejoicing here in what the superior
bodhisattvas have attained. Superior bodhisattvas are those bodhisattvas
abiding in the 1st ground up to the 10th ground. We are rejoicing in the
states that they have attained.
“Rejoicing in the causes for unsurpassable enlightenment” – What are we
rejoicing in here? We rejoice in the fact that these bodhisattvas have
generated bodhicitta. From the time of generating bodhicitta, they enter
into the practices of the six perfections. They train in the six
perfections and, along the way, they perform limitless works and activities
for the benefit of sentient beings. We rejoice in all these activities.
The generation of aspirational bodhicitta yields immeasurable benefits and
it goes without saying that there are many more benefits that come from
generating engaged bodhicitta. This was already explained in Chapter 1 of
this text. We should reflect on these benefits and rejoice in them.
We have the accumulation of merit and the purification of negativities. Of
these two categories, the practice of rejoicing falls into the category of
accumulating merit. Various texts have explained the many benefits of
rejoicing. By rejoicing, one subdues pride. When we practise rejoicing, we
will see there are many practical benefits. When we rejoice in the virtues,
good things, possessions, qualities and the practices of others, it serves
to weaken our negative emotions such as jealousy, pride and
competitiveness.
|B Requesting [the teacher] to turn the |
|wheel of dharma                        |


Verse 3.5
With folded hands I beseech
The Buddhas of all directions
To shine the lamp of Dharma
For all bewildered in the darkness of suffering.
After the Buddha manifested the deed of achieving full enlightenment, he
did not teach dharma for 49 days. After the 49 days, the gods Indra and
Brahma requested the Buddha to start teaching the Dharma. Indra offered a
white right-turning conch shell to the Buddha and Brahma offered a thousand-
spoked golden Dharma wheel to request the Buddha to teach the Dharma. So it
was due to the request of the gods Brahma and Indra that Buddha then turns
the wheel of dharma.
When one requests to turn the wheel of dharma, it says that we should
request with our palms folded with respect. This is an outer expression of
one’s inner desire to really want to hear the teaching and really want the
dharma. In fact that is how it should be. One must want the dharma. From
ones’ side, one must really want to hear the teachings. If one hears the
teachings with the intense heartfelt desire for the dharma, then hearing
the dharma/teachings will be very effective and beneficial for the mind.
Therefore, as mentioned in the Lamrim teachings, according to the code of
ethical conduct for the ordained, the vinaya, one should not teach without
being requested.
If we look at some of the accounts in the Jataka tales which describe the
Buddha’s past lives while he was in samsara, there is an account of Buddha
in one of his past life, he used his body as light offering by planting a
thousand wicks burning on his body in order just to receive one verse of
dharma/teaching. Also in the life story of the great Tibetan yogi,
Milarepa, of how much hardship he undertook before he got the teachings.
All these point to the need to have this intense desire for the dharma. The
point is that the need of being able to bear hardship for the Dharma. If
you have these qualities then receiving and hearing the teachings will be
beneficial for the mind.
From your own side, you want the dharma so much and the intense heartfelt
desire for the dharma. You see why you need it, and you see that if you
don’t have it, it is not right. Likewise, you should see the same thing for
others. So when you request the teachings, it is not just for yourselves
but also understand how others are in the similar predicament, they need
the dharma and they must have the dharma. So with that kind of thought, one
requests the Buddhas of the three times to turn the wheel of dharma. You
can visualize the offering of the thousand-spoked golden dharma wheel to
accompany that request. You should also think that the Buddha accept your
request.
Those of you who are doing the practices of Guru Yoga, visualize your guru
as inseparable from the merit field, from the Buddha. Then you request them
to turn the wheel of dharma, and accompany that request with an offering of
the thousand-spoked golden dharma wheel. Then think that your request is
accepted. This is how you can practice. If you are able to do this practice
of requesting/ imploring/urging the buddhas to turn the wheel of dharma, it
is said that in the future lives, you will not be a holder of wrong views,
and you will be able to continually hear the teachings, and to receive
teachings.
Wrong view is the greatest obstacle to practice the dharma, because when
you have wrong view there is no basis to practice. It is very important to
be free from wrong view, because if you have wrong view, say in relation to
cause and effect, then there is no mental space for dharma practice. It
will be beneficial if from life to life, you do not end up being people who
hold wrong views. So doing this practice of imploring the buddhas to turn
the wheel of dharma will help in that.

|C Requesting him not to pass  |
|into nirvana                  |


Verse 3.6
With folded hands I beseech
The Victors who wish to enter nirvana
Not to leave migrating beings in blindness
And to remain for countless aeons.
When the Buddha was about to pass away into parinirvana, there was a
householder by the name of Chunda requested Buddha not to pass away. As a
result of that, the Buddha delayed his parinirvana by three months.
There is this verse in the Golden Light Sutra that says that the buddhas
don’t pass away into parinirvana, and there will not be a time when the
dharma is not heard or taught. But the Buddha showed the deed of passing
into nirvana in order to ripen the minds of sentient beings.
Buddhas do not die. Buddhas never dies. Buddhas are completely freed from
birth, aging, sickness and death. But the buddhas show various aspects
including passing away in order to ripen the minds of sentient beings. This
is a way to moving sentient beings’ heart and persuading them to practice
the dharma.
The buddhas showed themselves in various aspects to benefit sentient
beings. All the supreme emanation bodies manifest the deed of passing away
into nirvana; but the enjoyment body never demonstrate passing into
nirvana.  So when the buddhas manifest the deed of passing into paranirvana
it is not that the buddhas really dies but rather they are showing the
aspect in order to ripen the mind of sentient beings.
So you bring to your mind all the buddhas and also your own guru, and with
respect you request the buddhas and the guru do not pass away into
paranirvana. You request the buddhas and the guru to abide for a long time.
Then you think that they accept your request. You request them to remain
for eons in order to eliminate the darkness of ignorance of not just for
yourself but also for the sentient beings, especially those who are objects
to be tamed or subdued by them.
Requesting all the buddhas to not pass away into parinirvana is the cause
for our own life obstacles to be pacified and a cause for one’s own long
life.

Next is dedicating the virtues that were accumulated.  There are:
- General dedication
- Dedication for those who are sick
- Dedication to alleviate the sufferings of hunger and thirst
- Dedication to fulfill all wishes.

|1 Dedicating in |
|general         |


Verse 3.7
Thus by the virtue accumulated
Through all that I have done,
May all the suffering of all sentient beings
Be dispelled.
In actuality, dedication of merit means dedicating one’s virtue and merit
to the achievement of full and complete enlightenment. Here in this context
we are dedicating the merit to be able to actualize bodhicitta. So, here in
this context doing this practice of dedicating the merit is to be able to
actualize bodhicitta. The development of bodhicitta has its roots in
compassion, which is the love and the mercy that wishes sentient beings to
be free of sufferings. When we dedicate the merit by using verse 3.7, we
are dedicating the merit for all sentient beings to be free of sufferings,
in a way it is a condition to think about compassion and to bring forth the
compassion.
One dedicates the virtue accumulated in the past, in the present and the
future towards the elimination of the suffering of all sentient beings.
Whether it is generating the motivation or dedicating the virtue, these are
actions that move the mind in a certain direction. Essentially that is what
generating motivation and dedicating does - moving the mind in a certain
direction. Here in this context, one generates the motivation to eliminate
the sufferings of others and also one is dedicating the merit for all
sentient beings to be free of sufferings. So, essentially it is moving the
mind towards compassion.
Whether it is generating motivation or dedicating the merit, it is an
exercise to push or nudge the mind in a certain direction. As such, it has
to come from the heart in order for the mind to move in a certain
direction. Here in this context, dedicating for sentient beings’ suffering
to be eliminated has to come from the heart. When it comes from the heart,
one’s mind will be nudged and pushed in that direction. Dedication is not
so much about reciting the prayer. It is not about recitation but the
thought that has to accompany the recitation.
(BP1 L16) When we talk about dedication, there is a difference between
dedicating and making aspirational prayers. There is a common locus between
the two of them. All dedication are necessarily aspirational prayers, but
this does not necessarily apply the other way round. Not all aspirational
prayers are dedication. The difference between dedication and making
aspirational prayers is posited on the basis of whether there is something
to be dedicated or not. If there is something to be dedicated and if we
dedicate it, then it becomes the act of dedication and as well as the act
of making an aspirational prayer. But if there is nothing to be dedicated
and we just generate the thought, ‘I will become a buddha for the benefit
of sentient beings, ‘then that becomes an aspirational prayer and not a
dedication.
In this context, we are dedicating the merit that we have accumulated
through the earlier practices of making prostrations, making offerings,
confessing negativities, rejoicing in virtue, beseeching the buddhas to
turn the wheel of Dharma and requesting them not to pass away into nirvana.
We have accumulated merit by doing these different practices. So there is
something for us to dedicate. We dedicate the virtues that we have
accumulated from the earlier practices, together with all the merit
accumulated from the past, present and future. We dedicate them all, making
the wish, ‘May all the sufferings of the lower realms and the sufferings in
cyclic existence of all sentient beings be completely cleared away.’ This
is dedication in general - dedicating the merit for the purpose of
eliminating the sufferings of all sentient beings.

|2 Dedicating for the   |
|sake of the ill        |


Verse 3.8
May I be the doctor and the medicine
And may I be the nurse
For all sick migrating beings in the world
Until their sicknesses are healed.
Like what is being mentioned in the verse, you dedicate the merit
accumulated in the three times, so for the benefit of the sick one becomes
a doctor, the medicine and the nurse for them. Like what I have just said,
it is a condition and exercise to nudge and move the mind towards
compassion. When you dedicate your merit, you dedicate in this way. In this
case it is the condition that helps to move the mind towards compassion. On
a practical level, when you encounter those who are sick, as much as
possible you also have to take care of them by attending to them, by
helping them, giving them medicine and so forth.
There is an account of Buddha himself attending to a sick fully ordained
monk. This fully ordained monk was pretty much incapacitated that he had
soiled himself. Then the Buddha personally attended to him and washed him.
The Buddha then said that monks should also do this i.e. should also attend
to the sick.

|3 Dedicating in order to alleviate|
|hunger and thirst                 |


Verse 3.9
May a rain of food and drink descend
To clear away the suffering of thirst and hunger,
And during the eon of famine
May I myself turn into food and drink.
As said here, we dedicate the merit to be a cause for rain of food and
drink to descend in order to clear away the suffering of hunger and thirst.

There is an eon of famine, a long period of time in which sentient beings
have to endure long period of time without any food and drink. One
dedicates one’s merit to be a cause for food and drink to appear.
There is also the eon of sickness and the eon of weapons.
During the eon of sickness, it is said that many sentient beings will
suffer from all kinds of sicknesses. Here one dedicates the merit that one
has accumulated such that during that time one can become the medicine for
those sentient beings and become the nurse and doctor for them.
During the eon of weapons, it is said that sentient beings will attack one
another with weapons. One dedicates the merit that one has accumulated that
during this eon of weapons, all the weapons will be transformed into
flowers.
In one sutra, the Buddha said to Ananda that if any sentient being was to
abandon killing just for one day and night, those roots of virtue will
cause that sentient being to not be born during the eon of weapons.
The Buddha also said to Ananda that anyone who offers an arura?, literally
some medicine to the Sangha, then by depending on the roots of virtue of
having offered that medicine to the Sangha, they will not be born during
the eon of sickness.
Buddha also said to Ananda that any sentient being who offers even a little
bit of food to the Sangha, in dependence on those roots of virtue, they
will not be born during the eon of famine.
(BP1 L16) Verse 3.9 refers to the eon of famine. The eon of sickness and
the eons of weapons are also predicted to happen in the future.
The eon of famine will happen when the lifespan of human beings will be
about thirty years only. During this time, famines will occur for seven
years, seven months and seven days and all the beings of that time will be
on the verge of death.
During the eon of sickness , the lifespan of human beings will drop to
twenty years. During this time, there will be epidemics and contagious
diseases will spread everywhere for seven years, seven months and seven
days and a lot of people will die.
The eon of weapons will happen during the time when human beings will have
a lifespan of ten years. For seven days during this period, people will
kill one another whenever they meet. Whatever they pick up - be it a stone,
a twig or tree branch - will turn into a weapon and they will start killing
one another. This information can be found in the text, the Treatises on
the Bhumis composed by Arya Asanga.
I thought to give some explanation on what an eon is. In Sanskrit it is
kalpa.
We talked about accumulating merit and wisdom over a period of three
countless great eons. What is one great eon? One great eon is made up of
four periods:
  1. Period of formation – formation of the world system
  2. Period of abiding
  3. Period of destruction
  4. Period of emptiness (a period of nothingness).
One great eon is made up of four eons:
1. Eon of formation
2. Eon of abiding
3. Eon of destruction
4. Eon of nothingness
The duration of these four eons are the same.
The eon of formation is made up of 20 intermediate eons. Similarly, the eon
of abiding is made up of 20 intermediate eons and so forth. Altogether 80
intermediate eons or kalpa make up a Great Eon.

Let’s put our attention on the 20 intermediate eons of abiding.
There is this cycle of the lifespan of sentient beings during this eon. At
the beginning the lifespan goes down, and at the very end of the 20th eon,
the lifespan goes up. In between it is continuous cycle of lifespan
decreasing and then lifespan increasing.
The eon of abiding is made up of these 20 intermediate eons. In our world
system, we talk about thousand Buddhas descending. The thousand Buddhas
will descend during these 20 intermediate eons.
At the beginning of the eon of abiding, the sentient beings’ lifespan is
said to be very long. The lifespan of those sentient beings gradually
decrease over time, but the rate of decrease is very slow.
From the innumerable long life, the lifespan slowly decreases to 80,000
years.
From 80,000 years, it decreases to 40,000 years.
When the lifespan of the sentient beings during this eon of abiding reach
40,000 years that was when the first of the thousand Buddhas appeared in
this world.
So when the sentient beings’ lifespan was 40,000 years, the first Buddha,
Krakucchanda Buddha (in Tibetan is Khorvadjig), came and he taught the
Dharma.
Then the Dharma declined, and when the sentient beings lifespan reach an
average of 30,000 years, the second Buddha, Kanakamuni Buddha, descended.
When the sentient beings’ average lifespan were declined to 20,000 years,

According to the sutric tradition, it is said that Buddha Shakyamuni’s
teaching will abide for just 5000 years.
If you break up these 5000 years into periods of 500 years, then you have
ten periods of 500 years.
During the first 500 years, many sentient beings achieve arhatship.
During the second 500 year period, the number of sentient beings achieving
arhatship was lesser but there were many who achieved the state of a non-
returner.
During the third 500 year period, the number of sentient beings achieving
the state of a non-returner became lesser but there were many who achieve
the state of a stream enterer.
The first three 5000 year periods are known as the three periods of
actualizing the fruits.
The next three 500 year are three periods of actualization or attainments,
which are related to the three higher trainings.
Of the three, the first one is related to the higher training in wisdom.
That is the fourth 500 year period.
During the fourth 500 year period, the number of beings actualizing the
fruit becomes much lesser but there are people attaining wisdom – the
wisdom realizing selflessness.
During the fifth 500 year period the number of people actualizing the
wisdom realizing selflessness becomes much lesser but there are many people
who are practicing and actualizing single-pointed concentration.
During the sixth 500 year period, the number of sentient beings practicing
and actualizing concentration becomes much lesser, but there are many
people who are practicing ethical discipline.
So going by this time-line, we are now in the period where there is still
the practice of ethical discipline.
Khen Rinpoche (in English): No attainment, no wisdom, no concentration.
Just try to keep some precepts is the time here.
Then the next three 500 year period are called the period of transmission,
which are related to the three scriptural collections:
1. Abhidharma or knowledge
2. Sutra
3. Vinaya
In the seventh 500 year period, the numbers of people who are engaged in
the practice of the three higher trainings become very few but there are
many who are studying the scriptural collection on knowledge or the
Abhidharma.
In the eighth 500 year period, the number of people who are studying and
learning the scriptural collection on Abhidharma becomes very few and
people are just concentrating on learning the Sutra scriptural collection.
In the ninth 500 year period, the number of people who are expounding on
the Sutra pitaka becomes very few, but most of the people who are teaching
are only teaching on the Vinaya scriptural collection.
Then in the final tenth 500 year period, there will be ordained people but
the ordained people are without any vows and without any knowledge of the
Vinaya. This final period is called the period of the mere sign, which
means that you only see people who look like ordained people i.e. they are
bearing the sign of wearing robes but they have no vows and they have no
knowledge of the Vinaya. This will happen during the last 500 year period.
When that finishes, Buddha Shakyamuni’s teaching will come to a complete
end.
This is how the Buddhist teachings will pan out over the 5000 year period.
This is like a timeline that shows how Buddha Shakyamuni’s teachings will
decline over time.

Presently human beings average lifespan is about 100 years. There will come
a time when the average lifespan will decrease all the way to 30 years old,
20 years old and then 10 years old. Presently we are just only on the first
slope of decline. We still have not yet entered into the actual cycle. All
this is happening in the first intermediate eon. The eon of abiding is made
up of 20 intermediate eons. So far we are still on the first intermediate
eon. What I have said up to now covers the first four Buddhas. All these
happen in the first intermediate eon of the 20 intermediate eons. In terms
of the graph that shows the cycle of increase and decrease, we are still on
the first decline.
The average lifespan of the human beings in this world will gradually
decline all the way till 30 years, then to 20 years and then only 10 years.
This is said to be a result of the vast accumulation of non-virtue and
negative karma.
Eon of famine is when the average lifespan of the human beings reaches 10
years, and that is when many sentient beings will die due to not finding
food and water.
Eon of sickness is when many sentient beings will die from sicknesses and
diseases.
Eon of weapons is when anything which sentient beings pick up will become a
weapon and they start hacking at one another. Then the world will be
transformed into like a living hell.
The three dedications that are mentioned here are related to the eon of
famine, the eon of sickness and the eon of weapons. We dedicate our roots
of virtue such that:
- during the eon of famine, we transform into food and drink.
- during the eon of sickness, we transform into doctor, medicine and nurse.

- during the eon of weapons, due to our roots of virtue, may all those
weapons transform into flowers.
During the eon of weapons, many sentient beings will be killed but there
will be some who will survive. They become extremely disenchanted with
killing, and then abandon killing. Due to the roots of virtue of abandoning
killing, their lifespan gradually increases. Then overtime sentient beings
will gradually also abandon the other non-virtues. They then live in the
ethical discipline of abandoning the ten non-virtues. As a result of the
roots of virtue, the average lifespan of the human beings gradually
increase to 10 years, 20 years, 30 years and all the way up to 80,000
years.
When the lifespan is on the increase i.e. when the cycle is going up, it is
said that the buddha does not appears because sentient beings are not
likely to generate disenchantment. The Buddha does not come at the time
when the average lifespan is below 10 years, neither the buddha will
descend when the lifespan is more than 80,000 years.
From average lifespan of 10 years, the lifespan starts to increase i.e. it
ascend again and reach 80,000 years. When the average lifespan starts to
decrease, that is when Maitreya Buddha will descend.
