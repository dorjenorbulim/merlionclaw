Student:  Is the mind that is concomitant with mental factor of anger

Khen Rinpoche: It is taught that anger is necessarily non-virtue. Probably
there isn’t any opportunity or instance whereby anger is good or virtuous.
According to the Compendium of Knowledge, the mental factors in the retinue
of the mind of anger would be non-virtuous because they share the similar
entity, similar object of observation and similar aspect. You can say that
all the mental factors that are in the retinue together with anger would
have to be non-virtuous.  According to the Compendium of Knowledge, which
is the higher Abhidharma, anger is a mind of the desire realm and there is
no anger in the form and formless realm. Since anger is necessarily non-
virtuous, therefore in the higher realms (i.e. form and formless realms),
there is no anger. There isn’t any non-virtue in the higher realms, so
there is no anger in the higher realms. This is mentioned in the Treasury
of Knowledge and the Compendium of Knowledge.

[C9] Conscientiousness (bag yod)
Regarding conscientiousness, the Compendium of Knowledge says:
QUESTION: What is conscientiousness?
RESPONSE: It is a cultivation of virtuous qualities within abiding in non-
attachment, non-hatred, non-ignorance, as well as effort, and guards the
mind against contaminations. It has the function of thoroughly completing
and fully establishing all mundane and supramundane success.
Just as it has been said above, it is an awareness that accomplishes virtue
within abiding in effort (joyous perseverance) without coming under the
influence of afflictions, and it guards the mind against contaminations.

The object of conscientiousness can be any virtues.
The aspect of conscientiousness is to guards the mind from the discordant
class or incompatible factors of that virtue.
The function of conscientiousness is that causes the mind to abide in
virtue and causes those virtues to increase.
Conscientiousness is basically protecting virtue and guards the mind from
the incompatible factors of virtue.

[Root text: page 46] Regarding this, [Asanga's] Bodhisattva Grounds
(Bodhisattvabhumi) mentions five types:
(1) conscientiousness with respect to the former
(2) conscientiousness with respect to the latter
(3) conscientiousness with respect to the middle
(4) conscientiousness prior to activity
(5) conscientiousness of concordant application

Perhaps we can call conscientiousness that which cherishes virtue and hold
virtues as very dear.
There are five types of conscientiousness:
1. Conscientiousness with respect to the former – With respect to the
   faults, misdeeds and negativities committed in the past, one generates
   regret and then applies the remedies.
2. Conscientiousness with respect to the latter – With respect to not
   committing non-virtue in the future, one relies on mindfulness and
   introspection to stop engaging the performance of any non-virtuous
   action.
3. Conscientiousness with respect to the middle – In the present, one
   realizes with mindfulness and introspection whenever one is about to
   engage in non-virtue. With conscientiousness, one stops oneself from
   engaging in it.
4. Conscientiousness prior to activity – Before non-virtuous action is
   committed, you already have the mind thinking: “How wonderful it would
   be if I do not engage in non-virtue”.
5. Conscientiousness of concordant application – At all times one think: “I
   will not engage in non-virtue and guard the mind against it”.
The first, conscientiousness with respect to the former, is to do with the
non-virtues that were created in the past.
The second, conscientiousness with respect to the latter, is with respect
to the non-virtue that will be performed in the future.
The third, conscientiousness with respect to the middle, is to do with the
present i.e. restraining.
The fourth, conscientiousness prior to activity, is more of motivation.
The fifth, conscientiousness of concordant application, is also motivation
but extending the motivation to all times i.e. keeping the mind in that
motivation at all times.
Conscientiousness is extremely important because it always protects the
mind in virtue. Once we lose conscientiousness, afflictions creep in almost
immediately. In order to increase whatever virtues and qualities, one has
to depends on conscientiousness.

[C10] Equanimity (btang snyoms)
Regarding equanimity, the Compendium of Knowledge says:
QUESTION: What is equanimity?
RESPONSE: It is a discordance with afflictive states, an evenness of mind,
a dwelling in a natural state, and a spontaneous abiding, within abiding in
non-attachment, non-hatred, non-ignorance, as well as effort. It has the
function of not allowing an opportunity for afflictions.
Just as it has been said above, in dependence on the techniques for
internally setting the mind single-pointedly on an object of observation,
the nine mental abidings are gradually accomplished. When the ninth is
achieved, one no longer needs not strive to use the antidotes to laxity and
excitement and one attains a spontaneous abiding of the mind.
Note: The nine mental abidings are: (1) setting  the  mind,  (2)  continuous
setting, (3) re-setting, (4) close setting,
(5) disciplining, (6) pacifying, (7) thorough  pacifying,  (8)  making  one-
pointed, and (9) setting in equipoise.
In general, there are three types of equanimity:
(1) equanimity of application
(2) equanimity which is feeling
(3) immeasurable equanimity

The objective feature of equanimity can be posited to be the same as the
object for meditative stabilization.
Therefore the object of equanimity is specified to be an imputed thing.
The subjective aspect feature of equanimity is engaging in discriminative
equanimity.
The function of equanimity is that it doesn’t give the afflictions of the
desired realm a chance to manifest.
In the course of developing meditative stabilization/concentration, when
obstacles such as laxity or excitement arise, you have to apply the
antidotes. But if there is no obstacle, for example, there is no laxity
then it is incorrect to apply the antidotes. One should not apply any
antidotes if there is no need for it.
If you do not apply the antidote when there is a need, then it is a fault.
If you apply the antidote when there is no need for it, it is also a fault.
In this context, the equanimity is when the mind is in equilibrium whereby
you neither have to apply the antidotes nor have to not apply the
antidotes.
Equanimity is achieved during the ninth mental abiding. So there is no need
to apply any antidote or there is also no need to not applying any
antidote.
The actual equanimity happens on the ninth mental abiding.  While on the
eighth mental abiding, there is unlikely any laxity and excitement because
the mind is probably freed from that but there are still some danger of it
coming up.
The equanimity that we are talking about here is to achieve on the ninth
mental abiding.
At the eighth mental abiding when one projects the will or intention to
stay in the session and not be affected by laxity or excitement, there is
no laxity or excitement during the session but that comes through effort
i.e. one has to make an effort.
But on the ninth mental abiding, as soon as you place your mind on the
chosen object of observation, the mind is able to abide in that state. It
is free of laxity or excitement without effort. It is natural.
We didn’t look at the six perfections in the Lamrim as we do not have time.
Actually all these are in the Lamrim especially in the section on the
perfection of meditative stabilization which explains:
   1. What are the nine mental abiding?
   2. How does one move from one to the other?
   3. What are the obstacles that are met along the way?
   4. What is the difference between excitement and laxity and the coarse
      and subtle forms of both?
If we do look at the six perfections in the future we will discuss about it
then.
Of the three types of equanimity - the equanimity of application,
equanimity of feeling and immeasurable equanimity - here we are referring
to the equanimity of application that is achieved on the ninth mental
abiding.

[C11] Non-harmfulness (rnam par mi 'tshe ba)
Regarding non-harmfulness, the Compendium of Knowledge says:
QUESTION: What is non-harmfulness?
RESPONSE: It is a mind of compassion and is involved with non-hatred. It
has the function of not inflicting injury.
Just as it has been said above, non-harmfulness is a patience that, lacking
malice, observes suffering sentient beings, thinking, "May they be free of
that [suffering]!"

The object of non-harmfulness is sentient being.
The aspect is compassion.
The function is not inflicting harm.
Khen Rinpoche spoke in English: Is sentient being does not suffer?
What is the problem of saying: “The object is sentient being, the aspect is
compassion and the function is not causing harm?”
The object of observation of great compassion is all sentient beings. So
Buddha also cares for the tenth ground bodhisattvas.
In order to develop non-harmfulness, we have to give up harmfulness.
So the advice given in the vinaya for counteracting harmfulness is:

[Root text: page 49] And on the occasion of giving the instructions, one is
cautioned about the necessity to abide in the four qualities that makes one
a trainee-in-virtue:
    Even when derided, do not deride in return.
    Even when someone gets angry at one, do not get angry in return.
    Even when hit, do not hit back.
    Even when one's faults are exposed, do not expose others' faults.

We don’t harm sentient beings just through physical actions. We can also
harm sentient beings verbally and mentally. Therefore we have to avoid
violence, harming others physically, verbally and mentally. It concludes
with:

[Root text: page 49] Therefore, if the intelligent ones analyze and
understand this well, they will be able to understand that abandoning
harmfulness is the essence of the teachings.

QUALM: With regard to virtue, are the eleven [virtuous mental factors]

RESPONSE: In general, virtues are taught to be of five types:
    (1) virtues by way of entity
    (2) virtues by way of relation
    (3) virtues by way of subsequent relation
    (4) virtues by way of motivation
    (5) virtues by way of ultimacy
1) The eleven explained above – faith and so forth – are specified as being
   virtues by way of entity because they are generated in the entity of
   virtue from their mere establishment without relying on other factors
   such as motivation. Hence, here I have indicated the eleven principal
   virtues.
The eleven virtuous mental factors that we have just finished looking at
are virtuous from their own side; it is mere establishment without
depending on motivation.

2) "Virtues by way of relation" are minds and mental factors that are
   concomitant by way of the five similarities with the eleven – faith and
   so forth.
3) "Virtues by way of subsequent relation" are imprints of virtue.
4) "Virtues by way of motivation" are, for instance, physical and verbal
   actions motivated by faith.
5) "Virtues by way of ultimacy" are explained to be thusnesses, because all
   obscurations will be purified if one meditates within observation of
   thusness; therefore, they are designated as "virtues" but are not actual
   virtues.

Whether talking about thusness (de zhin ni) or emptiness (ton pa ni), they
are talking about the same thing. Thusness and emptiness are not virtues
because thusness and emptiness are uncomposed phenomena and permanent.
Virtues are composed phenomena and impermanent.
“All obscuration will be purified if one meditates within observation of
suchness” - when you focus on suchness, all obscurations will be purified.
Then question will arise: How? Why? Please explain when we observe
suchness, how all obscurations will be purified.

very important to always make aspirational prayer continuously when one is
still up and everything is alright. So it is important that one strives in
virtues. You have to see the point why. When you are still alright and when
nothing is happening, it is better to strives in virtues. That is why it is
very important to remind ourselves that while we are still okay, it is
important to engage in virtues. All of us are the same. When nothing is
wrong then it is fine but when something happens such as when we are sick
and tormented by pain and suffering, it is very difficult to remember
dharma. Even if you do remember dharma and if you want to practice, it is
very difficult because you would be overwhelmed by suffering. This is
something we need to remind ourselves and while we can do it then better do
it; whatever studies, continue with our own practices, as much virtues as
possible, also to be good hearted person. Also keep in mind of everyone
else in our center and in our community because many people have problems;
with varying problems and varying sufferings. So keep everyone in mind, to
pray for them to be freed from their pain and their obstacles and for
everyone to be able to lead their remaining life meaningfully.
