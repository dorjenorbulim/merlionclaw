[B] The five object-ascertaining mental factors (yul nges)
[Root text: page 26] The five object-ascertaining mental factors are:
(1) aspiration
(2) belief
(3) mindfulness
(4) meditative stabilization
(5) wisdom



In order to achieve any goal, one must have an aspiration for it.
Therefore, it is important to understand what exactly aspiration is:
    • What is its entity?
    • What is its object?
    • What is its function?
As mentioned in the previous class, when one has aspiration, one will
likely to put in effort. Joyous perseverance is induced by aspiration. When
one has joyous perseverance, one is likely to achieve one’s desired goal.
           Aspiration ( Joyous perseverance ( Goal
The first object-ascertaining mental factors listed here is aspiration.
Next is the mental factor of belief.
Belief is very important. You may have aspiration and based on that you may
have joyous perseverance. But if you do not have belief, your aspiration
and the joyous perseverance that is induced is changeable. Therefore, it is
important to have stable belief.
Belief is essentially an ascertainment of the object; whatever objects in
question - the thing that you are trying to achieve or the thing that you
want. Belief is an ascertainment; it views the object and hold on to it and
believes that “It is just as I have determined it to be, it is like that.”
With this belief, your joyous perseverance and the aspiration for that
particular goal or object will not change.
Even if you have aspiration and belief and your mind is familiar with a
particular goal or object, but if you lack of mindfulness then it is
difficult to sustain the practice of virtue. For example, it is difficult
to accomplish your goal and seeing your effort come to fruition if you
don’t have mindfulness. That will not happen. So mindfulness is also very
important.
In this context, we are talking about mindfulness of a virtuous object;
which means not forgetting to focus your mind on the virtuous object. As
mentioned in the text, the moment we forget the virtuous object or we
forget to put our focus on something beneficial or something virtue,
immediately afflictions creep in and manifest right away. In order to not
allow that to happen then it is important to be mindful; which means not
forgetting the virtuous object. For this reason, mindfulness is important.
For these three mental factors -aspiration, belief and mindfulness- you
should know:
  -what are their respective objects,
  -what are their respective aspects,
  -what do these knowers thinking of and
  -what are their functions.
By knowing the characteristics of these mental factors you come to see how
important they are and how they are necessities.
You also have to know what the features of meditative stabilization are.
Just like aspiration, belief and mindfulness, when you look at meditative
stabilization, it also has an objective feature and so forth. Meditative
stabilization (also translated as concentration or single-pointedness of
mind) is important. It is not sufficient just to have mindfulness. It is
not sufficient just not forgetting to put your mind on a virtuous object.
If you don’t have single-pointedness of mind, then whatever antidotes you
may have in hand to counteract the afflictions, those antidotes cannot
become effective without single-pointedness of mind. Just by having single-
pointedness of mind alone would not enable anyone to readily destroy the
afflictions. It is on the basis of having a stable concentration or single-
pointedness of mind you are able to develop the special wisdom which is the
antidote to the afflictions.  Therefore the next mental factor is the
mental factor of wisdom.
It is true that when you have single-pointedness of mind, the mind is fully
concentrated. It is possible that the afflictions do not manifest and you
can stay in that meditation framework of single-pointedness of mind for
very long time; even for eons. While you are in that state of mind, your
afflictions do not manifest, but this does not mean that you have removed
the afflictions. They are still there. In order to eradicate the
afflictions from the root, you need wisdom, in particular the wisdom of
individual analysis. On the basis of developing concentration, one comes to
achieve calm-abiding (or meditative serenity). On the basis of calm-abiding
one generates special insight. It is this special insight that acts as the
direct antidote to the afflictions.
[B5] Wisdom (shes rab)
[Root text: page 32] Regarding the entity of wisdom, the Compendium of
Knowledge says:
 QUESTION: What is wisdom?
 RESPONSE: It strongly differentiates the qualities of things that are to
 be examined. It has the function of overcoming doubt.
Just as it has been said above, wisdom is a knower that individually
differentiates the faults and good qualities of things that are to be
examined.
Just like the other mental factors that were described earlier, wisdom also
have their objective features and functional features. The object of wisdom
as stated here in the text:
[Root text: page 32] Objects that are to be examined by wisdom are of three
types: virtuous, non-virtuous, and unspecified.
What is the aspect of wisdom? It distinguishes what you need to abandon and
what you need to cultivate.
What is the function of wisdom? It helps to overcome doubt.
How does one differentiate between that which is to be abandoned and that
which is to be cultivated?
How does one differentiate good qualities and faults?
One relies on the four ways of reasoning.
[Root text: page 32] [Wisdom] individually differentiates the faults and
good qualities of these.
There are four ways of reasoning for differentiating faults and good
qualities:
(1) functional reasoning
(2) reasoning of dependence
(3) logical reasoning
(4) reasoning of nature
We will just look at the reasoning of nature.
In the division of functioning things, there are matter or form,
consciousness and non-associated compositional factors. They are mutual
exclusive. Why? It is just like that. Consciousness is not matter and
matter is not consciousness. They are just separate and they are just
different.
The reasoning of nature is just describing things as they are. It is just
that there is a consciousness and there are matters. They exist separately
and individually on their own. That is nature. It is how it is.
If you look at their respective entities, from where does consciousness
come from and how does matter arise?
-  The current moment of consciousness has to come from the previous moment
of consciousness, which is its substantial cause. It is in dependence on
its substantial cause, which is consciousness.
- Similarly, matter comes into being in dependence on its substantial cause
which is a former moment of matter.
This is how you come to understand the reasoning of dependence. So the
reasoning of dependence is just to highlight the fact that these phenomena
come into being in dependence on their own substantial causes.

    • Consciousness is clear and knowing,
    • Consciousness  is able to illuminate object and able to know object,
    • Matter/form can obstruct another matter/form,
The reason why they are able to perform their respective functions is
because they have arisen from a concordant cause. Consciousness is able to
function in the way that it is supposed to and matter is able to function
in the way it is supposed to.
Here we are trying to see how to use these four ways of reasoning to
examine reality as it is. The reality is such that there is consciousness
and there is matter. Also reality is such that by their nature, they are
just established to be different.  This is the observation of the reality
that satisfies the reasoning of nature (also translated as principle of
nature).  The nature of form and consciousness come into being in
dependence on causes. They can only produce their respective functions
because they come into being in dependence on concordant causes; therefore
they are able to function. We can gain an understanding on all these by
using logic and reasoning and comparing these two phenomena, for example,
through using three possibilities or four possibilities or using a correct
sign to generate an inferential understanding of such phenomenon. The
principle of logical reasoning here is to gain an ascertainment of whatever
phenomenon in question.
We have to know phenomena are like that by nature and they come into being
by causes. They have their own concordant causes and so forth. We have to
understand that they are indeed like that by using reasoning. In order to
understand things, we either perceive them directly or we gain an
inferential cognition of that phenomenon in dependence upon a sign of
either inference through the power of fact or inference through belief.
This is how we can generate the wisdom that differentiates between good
qualities and faults.  By doing through the process of the four reasonings,
we enhance our wisdom.

[C1] Faith (dad pa)
[Root text: page 33][Regarding the entity of faith, the Compendium of
Knowledge says:
QUESTION: What is faith?
RESPONSE: It is a conviction, clarity, and wishing with respect to an
existent that is endowed with excellent qualities and power. It has the
function of acting as a support for aspiration.
Just as it has been said above, faith is a knower that has the aspect of
conviction, clarity, or wishing, and it serves as the direct antidote for
non-faith. When divided, faith is of three types:
(1) clarifying faith,
(2) faith of conviction and
(3) wishing faith.
1. Clarifying faith is a clear mind engendered by seeing the excellent
  qualities of those so endowed, such as the Three Jewels. Why is it called
  "clarifying"? For example, when one places a water-purifying gem in dirty
  water, the murkiness of the water is immediately cleared away. Similarly,
  when this faith is generated, the murkiness of the mind is cleared away,
  whereupon all excellent qualities of realization become suitable to arise
  in one’s continuum.
2. Faith of conviction is the gaining of conviction through contemplating
  the modes of dependent-arising, cause and result, and so forth that are
  taught by the Conqueror.
3. Wishing faith, is, for instance, having contemplated the modes of the
  four noble truths, ascertained true sufferings and true origins as
  objects of abandonment, and true cessations and true paths as objects of
  attainment, and having understood that these can be attained if one makes
  the proper effort, the faith thinking, "I shall definitely obtain them."

Faith is of three types:
   1. Clarifying faith  (also translated as clear faith)
   2. Faith of conviction
   3. Wishing faith (also translated as aspiring faith)
In the definition of faith, it says “with respect to an existent”,
“existent” here is the object of observation of faith of conviction. In
relation to an existent, for example, the law of actions/karma and effect,
actions have consequences. Good action leads to happiness and negative
action leads to suffering. Faith of conviction is having this conviction
that such a thing exists and it is like that. You gain a belief (here not
referring to the mental factor of belief) that there is such thing as karma
and karma works like that and it is infallible. So gaining a belief in this
is essentially faith in conviction.
“Existent that is endowed with excellent qualities” - “excellent quality”
here is the basis of observation of clarifying faith.  Clear faith is the
clear mind that comes about when you see the qualities of the Buddha,
Dharma and Sangha. Clear faith is liking; literally the mind of liking. It
is a very clear mind that it arises when you see the qualities of the
Buddha, Dharma and Sangha. When you see the qualities of the Buddha,
Dharma, Sangha, your mind becomes very clear and develop this liking. When
this happens, at that moment your mind is separated from all the pollutants
such as afflictions and so forth and that allows the mental space to
develop the mind spiritually and make it suitable to cultivate the path.
Just like the example as stated in the text, when chemical is added to
murky water that is not clear and it is to settle the pollutants. When the
pollutants are settled, the water becomes clear. Only when the water is
clear then images can be reflected on the surface of the water. Likewise,
through seeing the qualities of the Three Jewels, the mind is clear of all
mental pollutants which then give us the possibility to develop the
qualities of the Three Jewels within ourselves.  Therefore, the faith that
comes from seeing the qualities becomes very important.
As taught in the section of correctly relying on the virtuous friends in
The Stages of the Path to Enlightenment, Lamrim, the root of training is
developing faith; the faith comes from seeing the qualities. By focusing on
the qualities and seeing the qualities, your mind then become suitable to
pick up and develop those qualities of the gurus in ourselves. You generate
that kind of clear mind from focusing on the qualities.  If you have the
mind of non-faith and think negatively, it blocks the mind from developing
those qualities.
When you have the mind of clear faith in the virtuous friend, this clear,
clean, calm and happy mind becomes easy to accomplish whatever the virtuous
friend says; whatever he says, you will do. The reason why you do not do is
because there is no happy mind. That is why the mind is very tough.
If you have faith in the virtuous friend, then through seeing the qualities

Without that mind then it is very difficult. It is just like that in life

In the definition, [It is a conviction, clarity, and wishing with respect

(1) that which is liking but not faith
(2) that which is faith but not liking
(3) that which is both
(4) that which is neither

[C2] Shame (ngo tsha shes pa)
[Root text: page 36] Regarding shame, the Compendium of Knowledge says:
  QUESTION: What is shame?
  RESPONSE: It is an avoidance of misdeeds on account of oneself. It has
  the function of acting as a support for effective restraint from
  misconduct.
Just as it has been said above, shame is an avoidance of misdeeds on
account of oneself.
What is shame? If we restraint ourselves or stop ourselves from committing
mistakes with our body, speech and mind on account for ourselves, that is
shame.
For example, if we remember “I have taken the pratimoksha, bodhisattva and
tantric vows, and if I were to do this, then it would be wrong of me to
engage in this action”. With this mind, it is shame.
With that we restraint or stop ourselves from committing that action. So
this mind is shame.
Shame is the mind that avoids committing misdeeds on account of oneself.
When you actually put a stop to yourself from committing a particular
misdeed on account of yourself, then that mind is called shame.
Ven Gyurme: In the English connotation, shame is bad but here shame is a
good thing.

[C3] Embarrassment (khrel yod pa)
[Root text: page 36] Regarding embarrassment, the Compendium of Knowledge
says:
  QUESTION: What is embarrassment?
  RESPONSE: It is an avoidance of misdeeds on account of others. It has the
  function of just that [i.e., acting as a support for effective restraint
  from misconduct].
Just as it has been said above, shame is an avoidance of misconduct on
account of others.
Embarrassment is a restraint from non-virtues. Unlike shame, embarrassment
is done on account of others.
For example, if we stop ourselves from engaging in the misdeeds with our
body, speech and mind thinking “If I were to do this, the buddhas and
bodhisattvas will be displeased. When other people see and know about this,
they will also be unhappy and would disagree with this”. Basically on
account of others, embarrassment is a mind that put a stop. It is an
avoidance of misdeeds.
[Root text: page 37] [Discussion of the pair – shame and embarrassment]
What is the difference between shame and embarrassment? Although both of
them are similar in terms of avoiding misconduct, shame is such that, when
one is about to engage in misconduct, one avoids it, thinking, "This is not
something I should do". In the case of embarrassment, when one comes close
to engaging in misconduct, one avoids it on account of others, thinking,
"If others were to despise me [as a result of my
engaging in misconduct], this would not be fitting." One avoids misconduct
due to concern about the displeasure of one's gurus or, teachers – the
principal object [causing] this avoidance. Their function is specified as
"acting as a support for effective restraint from misconduct," because
shame and embarrassment are definitely necessary for restraining misconduct
of the three – body, speech, and mind. If shame and embarrassment are
absent, one will be unable to restrain oneself from any misconduct
whatsoever, for, without fear about the fruition from one's own side, and
lacking concern about the displeasure of one’s gurus, teachers, and so
forth, there will be no way to cease misconduct.
So the difference between shame and embarrassment is:
-If it is an avoidance of misdeeds on account of oneself, it is shame.
-If it is an avoidance of misdeeds on account of others, it is
embarrassment.

  QUESTION: What is non-attachment?
