[Root text: page 53] [D] The Six Root afflictions (rtsa nyon)
The six root afflictions are:
(1) attachment
(2) anger
(3) pride
(4) ignorance
(5) doubt
(6) afflicted views


Of the six root afflictions, we have finished with attachment, anger,
pride, ignorance and doubt.
Attachment is a mind that is desirous of or attached to a particular
object.
Anger is a malice towards sentient beings and sources of sufferings.
Pride is puffing of the mind in dependence upon the view of the transitory
collection.
Ignorance, in accordance with the higher and lower Abhidharma, is a mind
that does not knows its object.
Today we will look at the afflicted views.
[D6] Afflicted views (lta ba nyon mongs can)
Afflicted views are of five types:
(1) view of the transitory collection
(2) view holding to an extreme
(3) conception of a [bad] view as supreme
(4) conception of [bad] ethics and modes of conduct as supreme
(5) wrong view






[D6.1] View of the transitory collection ('jig lta)
[Root text: page 62] Regarding the view of the transitory collection, the
Compendium of Knowledge says:
QUESTION: What is the view of the transitory collection?
RESPONSE: It is any endurance, desire, intelligence, conception, or view
which views the five appropriated aggregates as a real I and mine. It has
the function of acting as a support for all views.
What is the entity of the view of the transitory collection?
Just as it has been said above, it is an afflicted wisdom that observes the
appropriated aggregates and apprehends them to be a real I and mine.
What is the reason for calling this view the "view of the transitory
collection"? It is just as it has been said in the Great Exposition of the
Stages of the Path:
  Regarding this, since the "transitory" is impermanent and a "collection"
  is manifold, the basis which it views is simply phenomena that are
  impermanent and manifold. However, one designates the name "view of the
  transitory collection" in order to indicate that "a permanent and unitary
  person does not exist."
The object of observation of the view of the transitory collection is
essentially our aggregates i.e. one’s aggregates that is with in one’s
continuum.  By observing our contaminated or appropriated aggregates, the
sense of ‘I’ arises and then one apprehends this ‘I’ to be self-sufficient
substantially existent.
When we talk about the ‘I’, the person, the self, obviously the ‘I’, the
person, the self does exist.
The question is: How does the ‘I’, the person, the self exists?
There are many views on the existence of the ‘I’, the person, the self. For
example, is the self permanent or impermanent? Obviously the ‘I’, the
person, the self does exist. I think all philosophical traditions assert
that there is an ‘I’, a person, a self. While all the philosophical systems
agree that an ‘I’ exist but if you look at the vast majority of the non-
Buddhist philosophical systems, while they do assert that there is a self
or an ‘I’,  they conceive the ‘I’ to be permanent, unitary and independent;
essentially a soul. While they assert that there is such a soul, but they
have differing opinion as to whether such a soul or an ‘I’ have a beginning
or have an end. In essence, most of these non-Buddhist philosophical
systems assert that the ‘I’, the person, the self is something that is
different from one’s body and mind. This ‘I’, the person, the self or the
soul is permanent, unitary and independent. While from the time of our
birth up to the present moment our body has undergone numerous changes. But
when we think of ourselves and look at ourselves, we feel that as if we are
the same person since birth until now. We feel that we are the same and not
changing. While our body has undergone a lot of changes since birth until
the present moment, naturally somehow we conceive ourselves as there is no
change and it is the same old me. Based on this concept and feeling, vast
majority of the non-Buddhist philosophical systems has asserted that there
is an I’ or a self that is separate and different from the body and mind.
One’s body and mind may change but the ‘I’ is always there, the same,
permanent, unitary and independent. This is the position of the vast
majority of the non-Buddhist philosophical systems.
The Buddhist’s position is radically different. For the Buddhist, there is
an ‘I’, the person, the self, however, the ‘I’, the person, the self is not
permanent, not unitary and not independent. So the Buddhist’s position is
that such a self or soul that is permanent, unitary and independent does
not exist whatsoever.
In this context when we talk about the lack of self or selflessness, the
self that does not exist is the self or ‘I’ that is self-sufficient
substantially existent. In this context, selflessness refers to the lack of
the self-sufficient substantially existent person. Of course, this self-
sufficient substantially existent person does not exist. Essentially it is
saying that there isn’t such a self whose existence does not depend on the
body and mind. There isn’t such a self that is separate/ different from the
body and mind.
There isn’t a self that is like an owner or the controller of the body and
mind.
There isn’t a self or person that is self-instituting i.e. able to stand on
its own without depending on the body and mind.
Such a kind of self does not exist whatsoever.
How does the person or the ‘I’ exist? The ‘I’ is that which is imputed in
dependence upon any of the five aggregates. So that is what the ‘I’ or the
person is. This is how the ‘I’ exist. There cannot be an ‘I’ that is self-
established, and able to stand on its own feet without depending on the
body and mind. Such a kind of ‘I’ does not exist. So it is selfless.
Summary: What is the view of the transitory collection? The view of the
transitory collection observes on the aggregates- the body and mind.  Based
on observing these body and mind, the sense of ‘I’ arises. The view of the
transitory collection apprehend this ‘I’ and conceive this ‘I’ to be self-
sufficient substantially existent. This is what being called the view of
the transitory collection. This is the meaning of self-sufficient
substantially. When we talk about the self-sufficient substantially
existent person, which does not exist, self-sufficient means able to stand
on its own independent of the body and mind and it can exist on its own
power without depending on the body and mind i.e. something separate from
the body and mind that can act as a controller or owner of the body and
mind. Such a kind of self does not exist.
If you look at most of the non-Buddhist philosophical view, they assert
that the soul or ‘I’ is permanent, unitary and independent.  But according
to the Buddhist, such a kind of soul or self does not exist. This self that
which does not exist becomes selfless.
The Buddhist position is that one can never ever posit or claim that an ‘I’
is permanent, unitary and independent.  The Buddhist can only talk about
the ‘I’ in relation to the body and mind. We cannot talk about an ‘I’ that
is completely separate from the body and mind i.e. no relation with the
body and mind.
When we focus on our contaminated body and mind, the sense of ‘I’ arises
and then we conceive ourselves or ‘I’ to be self-sufficient substantially
existent, this conception is called the view of the transitory collection.
In this context, the view of the transitory collection is apprehending the
‘I’ to be self-sufficient substantially existent.
What is the function of the view of the transitory collection? It has the
function of acting as a support for all views. So the view of the
transitory collection is the basis for arising of all the other
afflictions. Based on holding onto the self-sufficient substantially
existent person ‘I’, then one conceives of ‘I’ and others strongly. From
that, we become attach to the people who are on my side and we get upset or
angry at those who are against me. That is how holding onto the self-
sufficient substantially existent. This is how it induces the other
afflictions.
The view of the transitory collection is essentially can be divided into
two:
   1. Conception of ‘I’
   2. Conception of ‘mine’
When hold on to the ‘I’ as self-sufficient substantially existent, then the
view of the transitory collection is the conception of ‘I’. Based on that,
then we have the conception of ‘mine’.

[Root text: page 62] When this view of the transitory collection is
divided, there are twenty types of view of the transitory collection:
(1) view of forms as a self
(2) view of the self as possessing forms
(3) view of forms as mine
(4) view of the self as abiding in forms
Similarly, due to the divisions into four each with respect to feelings,
discriminations, compositional factors, and consciousness [i.e., the
remaining four of the five aggregates], there are twenty views
[altogether].
[i.e.,
(5) view of feelings as a self
(6) view of the self as possessing feelings
(7) view of feelings as mine
(8) view of the self as abiding in feelings
(9) view of discriminations as a self
(10) view of the self as possessing discriminations
(11) view of discriminations as mine
(12) view of the self as abiding in discriminations
(13) view of compositional factors as a self
(14) view of the self as possessing compositional factors
(15) view of compositional factors as mine
(16) view of the self as abiding in compositional factors
(17) view of consciousness as a self
(18) view of the self as possessing consciousness
(19) view of consciousness as mine
(20) view of the self as abiding in consciousness]
View of forms as a self: while the form aggregate is not the self, one
apprehends that form as self.
While the self does not possess form, here there is a view of the self as
possessing forms. But when we talk about the self as possessing forms, we
are talking about the self possessing forms by its nature.
While the self does not abide in forms there is a view of the self as
abiding in forms. Here abiding in forms refers to abiding in form by its
nature.
While that is about the view of the self as abiding in forms, now we are
talking about forms depending on the self. While the form does not abides
in the ‘I’ or depend on the ‘I’, but here there is a view seeing the form
depending on ‘I’ or abiding on ‘I’ or the self.
We have to look at these four in terms of whether possessing form by its
nature or abiding in form by its nature. The self and the aggregates are
different but they are not essentially different. They are not different in
nature. Likewise, you apply the understanding to what we have just talked
about, for example, the view of the self as possessing forms, it seem that
the self as possessing form by its nature.
Remember in the teaching of selflessness, when we say the self is not truly
existence because it is not truly one with the aggregates or truly
different from the aggregates.
Summary: What is the view of the transitory collection? It is a mind that
apprehend the self or the ‘I’ to be self-sufficient substantially existent,
while it is not.
When condensed, there is the view of the transitory collection that is the
conception of an ‘I’ and that is the conception of mine.
Don’t we all think of ourselves as something essentially different from our
body and mind? We have the feeling that the ‘I’ as something that is of the
different entity from the body and mind; something separate from the body
and mind, something independent from the body and mind and then act like a
controller. Don’t we have this feeling?
When we think of ‘my body’, the ‘I’ that possess the body, we have the
strong feeling that there is something that is separate from the body and
is of different entity from the body. We have the strong feeling of an ‘I’
that is the owner of this body and mind; an owner that is separate from the
body and mind and control the body and mind. We have such a feeling. We do
definitely operate in such a way thinking “I am completely separate from my
body and mind. I am like the owner of the body and mind. An ‘I’ that is
independent of my body and mind. I own my body and mind”. This mind is the
view of the transitory collection and is an afflicted view. It is not
wisdom because it is holding onto something that does not exist and
believing in something that does not exist. While such a mind, the view of
the transitory collection, is given the name intelligence or wisdom, it is
not the correct intelligence.
Student: It was mentioned that the view of the transitory collection
observed the aggregates then the sense of ‘I’ arises. What is the
difference between this aggregates that it observed and the sense of ‘I’
arises?
Does the view of the transitory collection apprehend this ‘I’ to be self-
sufficient substantially existent or does it apprehend the aggregates to be
self-sufficient substantially existent?
Ven Gyurme: Why the view of the transitory collection does not apprehend
the aggregates as self-sufficient substantially existent?
Khen Rinpoche: The self or ‘I’ definitely exist. No doubt about it. It is
not the question whether it does exist or not. The question is how the
self, ‘I’ or the person exists.
The Buddhist’s position is that the self, ‘I’ or the person is that which
is imputed in dependence upon any of the five aggregates.
The Buddhist does not know how to explain a self or person that exists
without depending on the aggregates.
The Buddhist’s position is that the self and the aggregates are one entity;
they are not separate entity.
The Buddhist’s position is that it is wrong to conceive the self and
aggregates as essentially different; just as we conceive a vase and pillar
as different. The vase and pillar are of different entities. If you think
that is how the self and aggregates are related to one another essentially
of different entities, then you are wrong.
Why is the self and aggregates are of one entity? If the self and
aggregates are of different entities, when we eliminate the aggregates one
after another then we should be able to find the self a part from the
aggregates.
If we eliminate form, feeling, discrimination, compositional factors, and
consciousness one after another, at the end if we can find there is an ‘I’,
then we can say the self and the aggregates are of different entities, but
we are not able to find such a self. In reality the self and the aggregates
are of one entity. We will not be able to point to a self that is of
separate entity from the aggregates.
The view of the transitory collection refers to this conception of such an
‘I’ that is completely distinctly different from the aggregates and
separate entity from the aggregates. That does not exist but there is a
mind which believes such an ‘I’ exist. This is called the view of the
transitory collection. The view of the transitory collection apprehend such
an ‘I’, it does not apprehend the aggregates.
The aggregates is the object of observation of this the view of the
transitory collection. Based on observing the aggregates, one conceives of
an ‘I’ and the sense of ‘I’ arises. The view of the transitory collection
apprehending this ‘I’ to be self-sufficient substantially existent;
completely separate from the body and mind and is of different entity from
the body and mind.
The text says: the view of the transitory collection is it is an afflicted
wisdom that observes the appropriated aggregates and apprehends them to be
a real I and mine.
The word seems to be saying that the view of the transitory collection is
apprehending the aggregates to be self-sufficient substantially existent
person. While it seem to be saying that, it is difficult to posit that.
Student: What is the object of engagement of the view of the transitory
collection?
Khen Rinpoche: We do not posit the object of the mode of comprehension for
this mind. For the purpose of debate, if you want to posit that, we don’t
posit the ‘I’ but rather we posit the self-sufficient substantially
existent person ‘I’; which is non-existent. The view of the transitory
collection does not just apprehend the ‘I’, it apprehend the self-
sufficient substantially existent person or ‘I’, which is of non-existent.
In general, we do not posit an object of the mode of comprehension for this
mind. If you want to say: “What is the object of the mode of comprehension
for this mind?”, then we will say that.
Student: For the view of the transitory collection, the object of
observation is the aggregates, whereas the subject aspect would be the self-
sufficient substantially existent?
Khen Rinpoche: The subject aspect is the self-sufficient substantially
existent “I”.

[D6.2] View holding to an extreme (mthar lta)
[Root text: page 64] Regarding the view holding to an extreme, the
Compendium of Knowledge says:
QUESTION: What is the view holding to an extreme?
RESPONSE: It is any endurance, desire, intelligence, conception, or view
which views the five appropriated aggregates as being either permanent or
annihilated. It has the function of hindering definite emergence by means
of the middle path.
Just as it has been said above, it is an afflicted wisdom which observes
the self that is held by the view of the transitory collection and
apprehends it to be either permanent or annihilated. How does it hold [that
self] to be either permanent or annihilated?
The object of observation of this view is the self that is held by the view
of the transitory collection.
Does this mean the self-sufficient substantially existent person or it
means the ‘I’ or self?
If we posit the object of observation to be just the ‘I’, person or self,
the view holding to an extreme is holding onto such an ‘I’, person or self
either as permanent or annihilated.
Annihilated refers to holding on to a self that does not go on to the next
life.
If we assert the object of observation is the conventional self or ‘I’, the
view holding to an extreme is the mind which focuses on the conventional
‘I’ or self and hold on to it as either permanent (unchanging) or
annihilated (something that will not move on to next life).

[Root text: page 64] The Great Exposition of the Stages of the Path says:
  The view holding to an extreme is an afflicted wisdom which, observing
  the self that is held by the view of the transitory collection, views it
  as either being permanent in the sense of being unchanging, or being
  annihilated in the sense of not transmigrating to a future life after
  this life.
Therefore, since this bad view causes one to fall to the extremes of
permanence and annihilation, it is the principal obstacle to progressing on
the middle path free from these extremes.

[D6.3] Conception of a [bad] view as supreme (lta ba mchog 'dzin)
[Root text: page 65] Regarding the conception of a [bad] view as supreme,
the Compendium of Knowledge says:
QUESTION: What is the conception of a [bad] view as supreme?
RESPONSE: It is any endurance, desire, intelligence, conception, or view
which views:
    • views and
    • the locus of the views, that is, the five appropriated aggregates,
to be supreme, to be chief, to be superior, and to be excellent. It has the
function of acting as a support for strongly adhering to bad views.
Just as it has been said above, it is an afflicted wisdom that observes
other bad views and the aggregates, the locus in dependence on which they
arise, and holds them to be supreme.

Conception of a [bad] view as supreme is an afflicted wisdom that observes
other bad views. The other bad views are mentioned in Lamrim Chenmo:
[Root text: page 65] In this context the Great Exposition of the Stages of
the Path says:
The conception of a [bad] view as supreme is an afflicted wisdom which
observes:
• any of the three – the view of the transitory collection, the view
  holding to an extreme or wrong view – and
• the aggregates of the viewer in dependence on which they [i.e., the above
  three views] arise,
and holds them to be supreme.
The conception of a [bad] view as supreme is observing any of these three –
the view of the transitory collection, the view holding to an extreme or
wrong view – and holding them to be supreme.
The object of observation is not just other bad views but also the
aggregates of the persons who has this view and holding them to be supreme.
So there are two conception of the conception of a [bad] view as supreme:
   1. the conception of other view as supreme
   2. the conception of the aggregates of the person holding on to this view
      as supreme
[D6.4] Conception of [bad] ethics and modes of conduct as supreme (tshul
khrims dang brtul zhugs mchog 'dzin)
[Root text: page 65] Regarding the conception of [bad] ethics and modes of
conduct as supreme, the
Compendium of Knowledge says:
QUESTION: What is the conception of [bad] ethics and modes of conduct as
supreme?
RESPONSE: It is any endurance, desire, intelligence, conception, or view,
which views:
 • [bad] ethics and modes of conduct and
 • the locus of the [bad] ethics and modes of conduct, that is, the five
   appropriated aggregates, to be purifying, liberating, and delivering. It
   has the function of acting as a support for fruitless fatigue.
Just as it has been said above, it is an afflicted wisdom that observes:
 • [bad] ethics motivated by bad views,
 • [bad] modes of conduct that prescribe physical and mental activities
   such as wearing animal skins, and
 • the abode of [bad] ethics and modes of conduct – the five appropriated
   aggregates –to be purifying and liberating.
There are people who believe that certain bad forms of ethics are the
causes of liberation, for example adopting certain physical or verbal
conducts which by nature are bad and believing these are the causes of
liberation.  Such a mind that believes in these is the conception of bad
ethics and modes of conduct as supreme.
There are people who really work very hard and doing all sorts of wrong
things thinking that they are doing something wonderful to achieve
liberation but at the end they achieve nothing. These people believe that
such conducts lead to liberation which actually do not. There is also view
that apprehends the aggregates of body and mind of such people to be
superior. That kind of view is considered to be a conception of bad ethics
and modes of conduct as supreme.
I don’t think we can touch on the wrong view today. So please read them on
your own. We will start with the Twenty Secondary Afflictions. Probably we
will be finishing soon.
[D6.5] Wrong view (log lta)
[Root text: page 65] Regarding wrong view, the Compendium of Knowledge
says:
QUESTION: What is wrong view?
RESPONSE: It is any endurance, desire, intelligence, conception, or view
which deprecates cause, result or functionality, and wrongly conceives
existent disintegrating things. It has the functions of severing roots of
virtue, holding tightly to roots of non-virtue, and acting as a support for
engaging in non-virtue and not engaging in virtue.
Just as it has been said above, it is an afflicted wisdom which views the
cause and result of actions, past and future lives, and so forth as non-
existent.
