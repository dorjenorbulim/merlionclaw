Haughtiness is the derivative of attachment.
Rejoicing is not involved with attachment, but it is possible that
rejoicing can lead to attachment. If one doesn’t know how to rejoice, it
can be mixed with attachment, In doing so, there is a danger it will become
haughtiness.
Rejoicing is a mind of liking that arises when focuses on one’s virtues,
whereas haughtiness is a puffed up mind when focus on one’s attributes.
If we rejoice in our virtues and qualities that involve with attachment and
leads to puffing up of one’s mind, then it would not act as support for the
generation and increase of one’s good qualities. It might end up becoming
haughtiness.
Contaminated success refers samsaric perfections or enjoyments. It refers
to those desirable objects that causes the generation of afflictions.

[E10] Harmfulness (rnam par 'tshe ba)
[Root text: page 75] Regarding harmfulness, the Compendium of Knowledge
says:
QUESTION: What is harmfulness?
RESPONSE: It is involved with anger. It is non-benevolence, non-compassion
and non-mercy. It has the function of harming others.
Just as it has been said above, it is a wish, bereft of benevolence, to
harm sentient beings.
Here the synonyms – non-benevolent and so forth [in the citation above] –
are explained in the commentaries as follows:
 • since it is a wish to harm by oneself, it is non-benevolence,
 • since it is a wish to engage others in harming, it is non-compassion,
   and
 • since it is a rejoicing upon seeing or hearing others acting [to harm],
   it is non-mercy.

[E11] Non-shame (ngo tsha med pa)
[Root text: page 76] Regarding non-shame, the Compendium of Knowledge says:
QUESTION: What is non-shame?
RESPONSE: It is a non-avoidance of misdeeds on account of oneself and is
involved with attachment, hatred and ignorance. It has the function of
assisting all [root] afflictions and secondary afflictions.
Just as it has been said above, it is an awareness that does not avoid
misdeed on account of either oneself or the Dharma. For example, if a fully-
ordained monk encounters an intoxicant and avoids a misdeed on account of
himself, thinking, "Drinking this intoxicant is not something I should do,"
then this is shame. The opposite of this is non-shame.
Non-shame is essentially the opposite of shame
The virtuous mental factor of shame is an avoidance of misdeeds on account
of oneself; it is a mind restraining from committing misdeeds on account of
oneself.

[E12] Non-embarrassment (khrel med pa)
[Root text: page 76] Regarding non-embarrassment, the Compendium of
Knowledge says:
QUESTION: What is non-embarrassment?
RESPONSE: It is a non-avoidance of misdeeds on account of others and is
involved with attachment, hatred and ignorance. It has the function of
assisting all [root] afflictions and secondary afflictions.
Just as it has been said above, it is an awareness that does not avoid
faults on account of others. If one, when about to engage in a misdeed,
avoids the misdeed on account of others, thinking, "If the Teacher and the
clairvoyant gods were to become displeased and I were to be despised by
others, this would be unfitting," then this is embarrassment. The opposite
of this is non-embarrassment.

[E13] Lethargy (rmugs pa)
[Root text: page 77] Regarding lethargy, the Compendium of Knowledge says:
QUESTION: What is lethargy?
RESPONSE: It is an unserviceability of mind and is involved with ignorance.
It has the function of assisting all [root] afflictions and secondary
afflictions.
Just as it has been said above, it is a heaviness and an unserviceability
of the body and mind and is a factor of ignorance. Moreover, the Precious
Garland (stanza 5.32ab) says:
      Lethargy is a state of inactivity
      Due to heaviness of body and mind.
The Explanation of the 'Treasury of Knowledge' says:
What is lethargy? It is a heaviness of the body and a heaviness of the
mind. It is an unserviceability of the body and unserviceability of the
mind.
The Great Exposition of the Stages of the Path also explains it in this
way. Its function is specified as "assisting all [root] afflictions and
secondary afflictions," because all afflictions will increase in dependence
upon lethargy. In this vein, the Exhortation to the Exceptional Resolve
(Adhyasayasamcodanasutra) says:
      Phlegm, wind, and bile
      Occur to a great extent in the bodies
      Of those who take joy in sleep and lethargy;
      Their constituents are in disorder.
      For those who take joy in lethargy and sleep,
      Their stomachs are unclean due to a bad diet,
      Their bodies are heavy and their complexions unhealthy,
      Even their speech is slurred.
And,
      Those who take joy in lethargy and sleep
      Are confused and their aspirations for the Dharma degenerate.
      The childish completely degenerate from all excellent qualities,
      Their wholesomeness degenerates, and they proceed to darkness.

[E14] Excitement (rgod pa)
[Root text: page 78] Regarding excitement, the Compendium of Knowledge
says:
QUESTION: What is excitement?
RESPONSE: It is an unpeacefulness of mind that subsequently engages an
attractive cause. It has the function of hindering calm-abiding.
Just as it has been said above, it is an awareness that, upon observing the
attributes of the desire realm previously experienced, mentally scatters
outwards to them and engages them with craving. Regarding this, the
Precious Garland (stanza 5.32cd) says:
      Excitement is an
      Unpeacefulness of the body and mind.
These points are taught in the Great Exposition of the Stages of the Path:
  Its object of observation is a pleasant and attractive object. Its
  [subjective] aspect is an unpeacefulness of the mind that is scattered
  outward and it is involved with attachment. Thus it engages its object
  with the aspect of craving. It has the function of hindering the mind
  from abiding on the object of observation.
Are all outward scatterings of the mind excitement? The author does not
believe it to be so.
QUALM: Are all outward scatterings of the mind excitement?
RESPONSE: I do not hold it to be so. Excitement is involved with
attachment, but the mind is frequently distracted to objects by way of
afflictions other than attachment; the mind even scatters to non-afflicted
virtuous objects of observation. Hence, not all scatterings of the mind are
excitement.
Excitement is a derivative of attachment; it is involved with attachment.
Excitement hinders the mind from staying focused on an object; as it is
said here that it has the function of hindering calm abiding. Excitement is
a mind that is scattered outwards to a desirable object.
Excitement is a mind that is involved with attachment. Excitement is a mind
that is scattered outwards to a desirable object.
What is the difference between excitement and scattering?
Excitement is classified as a secondary affliction therefore it is either
non-virtuous or neutral.
For mental scattering, it can be non-virtuous, neutral, or it can also be
virtuous. The mind is scattered outwards and not necessarily always to a
non-virtuous object, it can also focus on a virtuous object.
There can be a virtuous mental scattering but you will not be able to find
a virtuous excitement.
Excitement is a scattering of the mind due to attachment, whereas mental
scattering does not necessarily occur due to attachment. That being the
case, perhaps we can say excitement is necessarily mental scattering,
whereas if it is mental scattering, it is not necessarily excitement. What
do you think?

[E15] Non-faith (ma dad pa)
[Root text: page 79] Regarding non-faith, the Compendium of Knowledge says:
QUESTION: What is non-faith?
RESPONSE: It is a non-conviction, non-clarity, and non-wishing of the mind
with respect to virtuous phenomena, and is involved with ignorance. It has
the function of acting as a support for laziness.
Just as it has been said above, it is the discordant class of faith and is
involved with ignorance. It is of three types:
(1) non-faith of non-conviction in actions and their results and so forth –
the discordant class of the faith of conviction;
(2) non-faith of non-clarity and a mental dislike with respect to the
possessors of excellent qualities, such as the three jewels – the
discordant class of the faith of clarity; and
(3) non-faith of non-wishing and non-seeking of liberation and so forth –
the discordant class of wishing faith.
Earlier on when we look at the mental factor of faith, we saw that there
are three types of faith; clear faith, faith of conviction and aspiring
faith. Here non-faith is the opposite of these three. For example:
- a mind that is opposite of clear faith such as a mind that does not see
the qualities of the Three Jewels
- a mind that is not convinced in reincarnation, in karma and in the Three
Jewels
- a mind that does not aspire for liberation
Non-faith is involved with ignorance. It has the function of acting as a
support for laziness because faith functions as a support for joyous
perseverance. So if you don’t have faith, i.e. non-faith, then you are not
going to have joyous perseverance. When we don’t have joyous perseverance
that means we are lazy. The antidote of non-faith is faith, so we just have
to try our best to develop faith.

[E16] Laziness (le lo)
[Root text: page 80] Regarding laziness, the Compendium of Knowledge says:
QUESTION: What is laziness?
RESPONSE: It is a non-enthusiasm of the mind engendered by the comfort of
lying down, reclining on one's side, and slouching about, and is involved
with ignorance. It has the function of hindering application to the class
of virtue.
Just as it has been said above, it is a non-enthusiasm of the mind for
virtue due to a habit of lying down and so forth. Laziness causes the
entire class of virtue to degenerate. The Close Placement of Mindfulness
(Saddharmasmrtyupasthana) says:
    The one basis for afflictions
    Is laziness. Who has it?
    Wherever laziness is present,
    All Dharma is absent.
Laziness is the opposite of joyous perseverance. Joyous perseverance is the
antidote to laziness. Laziness is a mind that is unenthusiastic in virtue.
Joyous perseverance is a mind that is enthusiastic about virtue. So in
order for joyous perseverance to arise in our mind, we first must be
convinced of the benefits to engage in virtue. So depending on what virtue,
we should always reflect on the benefits of engaging in that particular
virtue, think about the ripening effects of that virtue and, in particular,
think about the benefits of achieving liberation and enlightenment. When we
see the benefits, we become enthusiastic and wanting to do it. So this is
how joyous perseverance can arise.
The key to develop joyous perseverance is developing faith. Without faith,
then there is no joyous perseverance. As mentioned in one major outline of
the Lamrim, developing faith of conviction in karma is the root of all
happiness. Therefore having developing this conviction in karma is
extremely important for developing joyous perseverance. So with this faith
of conviction in karma, we will be able to cause our virtue to increase.
It is like that in life. Although we do not call every effort we put in
life to be joyous perseverance, but the process is essentially very much
the same. Before willing to work hard in order to achieve something, we
first must be convinced what is in there for us. If we do see the benefits
and that is what we really want, then we will aspire to achieve that goal.
With aspiration then comes the effort. Then we put in effort and work hard
to achieve that goal.
The point is that joyous perseverance is important. We need faith to
develop joyous perseverance. Without joyous perseverance we cannot turn
away laziness.

[E17] Non-conscientiousness (bag med pa)
[Root text: page 80] Regarding non-conscientiousness, the Compendium of
Knowledge says:
QUESTION: What is non-conscientiousness?
RESPONSE: Abiding with attachment, hatred, ignorance as well as laziness,
it is a non-cultivation of virtuous qualities and non-guarding of the mind
against contaminated phenomena. It has the function of acting as a support
for the increase of non-virtue and the decrease of virtue.
Just as it has been said above, it is an awareness that, not guarding the
mind against the hosts of afflictions and misdeeds, gives in to relaxation.

Non-conscientiousness is the opposite of conscientiousness. As mentioned in
the teaching, we have to rely on conscientiousness all the time to guard
the mind. Without conscientiousness and with non-conscientiousness, we
allow the afflictions to arise.

[E18] Forgetfulness (brjed nges pa)
[Root text: page 81] Regarding forgetfulness, the Compendium of Knowledge
says:
QUESTION: What is forgetfulness?
RESPONSE: It is a mindfulness that is concomitant with afflictions. It has
the function of acting as a support for distraction.
Just as it has been said above, it is a knower that is an unclear mind and
forgetful with respect to virtue on account of mindfulness of an object of
observation of afflictions.
Similarly, A Discussion of the Five Aggregates also explains forgetfulness
to be an afflicted mindfulness, whereby an unclear, forgetful, virtuous
mind should be called "forgetfulness." However, since the objects of
observation of afflictions – pleasant and unpleasant characteristics and so
forth – do not arise as objects of awareness [of such a virtuous mind],
perhaps it should not be posited as forgetfulness. Forgetfulness acts as a
support for distraction, because, in dependence on afflicted mindfulness,
the mind will be distracted to an object of observation of the afflictions.
Forgetfulness is listed as a secondary affliction. Therefore, we have to
posit it as a mind that is not clear on virtue and is a mind that forgets
the virtuous object of observation. Here in this context, forgetfulness is
a mind that focus on a non-virtuous object of observation, and through that
becomes unclear with respect to virtuous objects of observation and then
forgets them.
The function of forgetfulness is a support for distraction. In dependence
on afflicted mindfulness i.e. the mind that is focused on a non-virtuous
object of observation, the mind is distracted to that. Because it is
distracted by a non-virtuous object of observation, the mind forgets about
the virtuous object of observation.
Afflicted mindfulness is a mind that is focused on a non-virtuous object
and holds on to it without forgetting it.
So this afflicted mindfulness acts as a condition for the mind to forget
about virtuous object of observation.

[E19] Non-introspection (shes bzhin ma yin pa)
[Root text: page 81] Regarding non-introspection, the Compendium of
Knowledge says:
QUESTION: What is non-introspection?
RESPONSE: A wisdom that is concomitant with afflictions and engages
unknowingly in activities of body, speech, and mind. It has the function of
acting as a support for infractions.
Infraction literally is translated as downfall. In some commentaries, non-
introspection is also said to act as a support for negativities and
downfalls.
Just as it has been said above, it is an afflicted wisdom that engages
unknowingly in activities of the three doors; it is the discordant class of
introspection. That this non-introspection acts as a support for
infractions accords with what is said in Engaging in the Bodhisattva Deeds
(stanza 5.26):
  Even though learned,
  Faithful and diligent in effort,
  Many become tainted by downfalls
  Due to the fault of lacking introspection.
In the teachings, we are advised to be vigilant/ introspection all the
time. So we have to continuously cultivate introspection all the time.
Without introspection, we will not be able to guard our body, speech and
mind against negativities and we will not be able to protect ourselves from
committing downfalls, infraction of the vows and so forth.

[E20] Distraction (rnam par g.yeng ba)
[Root text: page 81] Regarding distraction, the Compendium of Knowledge
says:
QUESTION: What is distraction?
RESPONSE: It is a scattering of the mind and is involved with attachment,
hatred, and ignorance. It has the function of hindering separation from
attachment.
Just as it has been said above, it causes the mind to be distracted from
its object of observation through the force of afflictions. A Discussion of
the Five Aggregates explains it as a scattering of the mind to the five
attributes of the desire realm.
QUALM: Is there no difference between this [distraction] and the excitement
that was explained above?
RESPONSE: Excitement is a scattering to attractive objects previously
familiarized with and is involved with attachment, whereas distraction can
be distracted to any object and be involved with all three poisons. Thus
they are different.
If one is asked “Is there a difference between excitement and distraction?”
one has to say that they are different. For excitement, it is involved with
attachment. Due to attachment, it is an outward scattering of the mind to
an attractive object. Distraction is not necessarily focused on an
attractive object. One can be distracted due to attachment, or anger, or
ignorance, so it is not necessarily involved with attachment.
Distraction here is classified as a secondary affliction. Therefore, in
this context, distraction can involves ignorance, attachment or anger.
When distraction is divided, there are six:
        1) Distraction by way of its very entity
        2) External distraction,
        3) Internal distraction,
        4) Distraction by way of signs,
        5) Distraction by way of unfavorable states,
        6) Distraction by way of attention.
1) Distraction by way of its very entity is explained to be the five sense
   consciousnesses. The reason is that, at the time of being set in
   equipoise on meditative stabilization, if any of the five sense
   consciousnesses are generated, then [the mind] arises from meditative
   stabilization. Thus, they are called "outward distraction of the mind
   away from meditative stabilization."
From this you can come to understand that concentration is not to be
cultivated by the sense consciousness. Concentration is achieved by the
mental consciousness. When the five sense consciousnesses are generated,
you can have distraction. You cannot concentrate if you are distracted.
2) External distraction is, when one is engaged in virtues of the three –
   listening, contemplation and meditation – the outward scattering of the
   mind to the attributes of the desire realm without abiding in them
   [i.e., the virtues]; since it causes scattering to an erroneous object
   of observation without abiding on the virtuous object of observation, it
   is called such.
3) Internal distraction is laxity, excitement, and craving for the taste of
   meditative absorption at the time of being set in equipoise on
   meditative stabilization. What is the reason for calling these "internal
   distraction"? They are so-called because they are the main obstacles for
   cultivating meditative stabilization internally.
4) Distraction by way of signs is to put effort in virtuous training with
   the thought, "How wonderful it would be if another person were to
   develop conviction in me, thinking, 'He has attained a concentration.'"
Essentially this involves with the eight worldly dharmas.
5) Distraction by way of unfavorable states is a puffing up of the mind
   conceiving I and mine when the feeling that one is engaging in virtue
   arises in dependence on the unfavorable states of the view of the
   transitory collection and pride.
Distraction by way of unfavorable states is essentially a puffing up of the
mind i.e. pride.
6) Distraction by way of attention is, for instance,
  • giving up the meditative absorption of the fourth concentration for the
    meditative absorption of the third concentration or below, or
  • thinking, "Giving up engaging in the great vehicle, I shall abide in
    the lesser vehicle."
These types of distraction explained here are taught to be general
enumerations of distraction, but it is not definite that they are actual
secondary afflictions; the first is unspecified and the last one is
included within virtue.
As there are six types of distraction being discussed here, the first is
unspecified and the last one is included within virtue. Unspecified means
not specified; virtuous or non-virtuous.
In this context, distraction is classified as secondary affliction,
therefore it is afflicted. Having said that, not all distractions as
mentioned here are afflicted. The text says that the last one -distraction
by way of attention- is included within virtue.
Since it is taught that the distraction that is an actual secondary
affliction is, for instance, the external distraction and internal
distraction which have been explained here, those of keen intellect need to
analyze this in detail.
[Discussion of the category of secondary afflictions]
In this way, these twenty – from belligerence up to distraction – are
called "secondary afflictions" [Tibetan: nye nyon, literally, near-
afflictions] because they are near to or involved with the root
afflictions. Furthermore, you should understand them individually –
belligerence and resentment being near to anger, and being involved with it
[anger] and so forth. Most of them are clear from the Knowledge passages
that have been previously cited.
The reason why these are called the secondary afflictions is because these
minds are near to the root afflictions and are the derivative of the root
afflictions.
Summary:
Belligerence, resentment, jealousy and spite, [harmfulness] are involved or
derivatives of anger.
Miserliness, deceit, dissimulation, haughtiness and excitement for example
are derivatives of attachment. Concealment, lethargy, non-faith, laziness
are derivatives of ignorance.
Many others such as [non-shame, non-embarrassment, distraction,] non-
introspection, non-conscientiousness, and so forth are derivative of all
the three mental poisons- anger, attachment and ignorance.

fault or mistake to point out or expose the faults with an intention to
help or benefit. If that is done with an intention to benefit, perhaps
there isn’t any fault in pointing out the fault of someone.
