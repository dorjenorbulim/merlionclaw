6Why study minds and mental factors?
z“It is the mental factors that are primarily responsible 
for leading us to all forms of experience. When the mind is under the influence of unwholesome mental 
factors, such as attachment and anger, one is led 
into committing actions that cause experiences of frustration and suffering. But whenever the mind is 
dominated by wholesome factors, such as 
compassion and patience, then any ensuing physical and vocal activity will only result in happiness and 
wellbeing. It is the unwholesome aspects of the mind 
that are the root of our bondage within samsarawhereas it is the wholesome aspects that allow us to 
discover freedom from this unsatisfactory condition.
(Geshe Rabten’s Mind and its Functions) 
7Why study minds and mental factors?
z“Since the principal motivating force behind our lives is the desire to find 
happiness and avoid suffering it is thus e ssential to recognize the psychological 
processes that are at work within ourselves. Only by means of careful 
examination will we be able to realize ex actly what the caus es for our pleasure 
and pain are and subsequently be able to engage in cultivating certain mental 
factors whilst discarding others. But as long as the mind continues to be 
dominated by unwholesome af flictions, then what we basically wish for and 
what we actually do in order to fi nd it, will be completely opposed to one 
another. Whilst wishing for happiness we s hall only be creating the causes for 
further suffering”
z“To persist in trying to m anipulate the external, mate rial world in order to 
achieve happiness and dispel suffering is like being concerned about fighting a 
battle outside, unawar e that the enemy is  dwelling indoors. Without dealing 
directly with our own psychological  make-up, no real happiness will ever 
be found within external situations: we shall always find ourselves facing 
the same problems as the person who does not realize that the enemy 
dwells within his own house. Thus the importance of being aware of the 
way in which the mental factors a ffect our lives cannot be stressed 
enough.” (Geshe Rabten’s Mind and its Functions)

18Main minds and Mental factors
z“The term ‘primary mind’ ( main mind ) denotes the 
totality of a sensory or mental state composed of a 
variety of mental factors. A primary (main) mind is 
like a hand whereas the mental factors are like the 
individual fingers, the palm and so forth.”
(Geshe Rabten, The Mind and its Functions)
19Minds vs. mental factors
z“A primary mind ( main mind ) is defined as a primary cognition 
established by means of its apprehension of the fundamental 
presence of the object. It is not the function of a primary 
mind to be specifically concerned with any aspect of the 
objective field, it is mere consciousness of the data 
presented to it. As we shall see, it is the individual mental 
factors that are responsible for the selection and 
processing of this data .In this respect the relationship of a 
primary mind and its mental fact ors is somewhat similar to an 
overseer and a group of workers. Each worker is solely occupied with his or her own specif ic task whilst the overseer is 
equally conscious of what each worker is doing, without being directly involved in any one of their individual activities.”
(Geshe Rabten, Mind and its Functions)
20Minds vs. Mental factors
Yeshe Gyeltsen: “Regarding the difference between a mind and a mental 
factor, [Maitreya's] Discrimination of the Middle and the Extremes 
says:
That which sees an object is a primary consciousness ( mind).
That which sees its attribute is a mental factor .”
Yeshe Gyeltsen continues:
“That which knows the mere entity of an object is called a mind. 
That which, taking the observation of that very object as its basis, 
engages the object by means of the isolates of other attributes, such 
as its function, is called a mental factor .”

24Five similarities according to Treasury 
of Knowledge 
According to Vasubandhu’s Treasury of Knowledge 
(Abhidharmakosha) , the five similarities between 
main minds and mental factors are:
1. Similar support
2. Similar object of observation
3. Similar [ subjective ] aspect
4. Similar time
5. Similar substance
25Five similarities according to Treasury 
of Knowledge
1.Similar support – since a mental factor  is also supported by 
that sense power upon which th e mind is supported, their 
supports are similar.
2.Similar object of observation – since a mental factor also 
observes that object which the mind observes, their objects of 
observation are similar.
3.Similar [subjective] aspect – since, when a mind is generated 
having an aspect, for instance, blue, its mental factor is also 
generated in that aspect, blue, their [subjective] aspects are 
similar.
4.Similar time – since a mind and its mental factor are 
simultaneous in regard to the three – production, abiding and 
cessation – their times are similar.
26Five similarities according to Treasury 
of Knowledge
5. Similar substance –Just as minds of a similar type are distinct 
substances, likewise, mental factors, such as feelings, of a similar type 
are also distinct substances, therefore their substances are similar. 
(Yeshe Gyeltsen)
(“because a main mind and its accompanying mental factors are 
produced qualified by [being] one type of substantial entity and are not 
produced as different substantial entities” – Purbuchok)
(“they have a similar substance since their basic cognitive character is 
always the same. it never happens that a primary mind is a perception whilst one of its mental factors is a conception, or that a mental factor 
is a mistaken cognition whilst its accompanying primary mind is an 
unmistaken cognition. Their cognitive nature, or substance, is always 
identical.” – Geshe Rabten) 
27Example
Example: eye consciousness seeing red
The primary eye consciousness an d its accompanying mental 
factors have:
1. Similar support – all have a support of the eye sense power .
2. Similar object of observation – all have the object red
3. Similar [subjective] aspect – all arise in the aspect of red 
4. Similar time – the primary eye consciousness and its 
accompanying mental factors arise, abide, and case 
simultaneously
5. Similar substance –the primary eye consciousness and its 
accompanying mental factors are the same substantial entity.
28Five similarities according to 
Compendium of Knowledge 
According to Asanga’s Compendium of Knowledge 
(Abhidharmasamucchaya) , the five similarities 
between main minds and mental factors are:
1. Similar substance
2. Similar object of observation and [ subjective] 
aspect
3. Similar entity
4. Similar time
5. Similar realm and level
29Five similarities according to 
Compendium of Knowledge
(1) Similar substance refers to the following. In the retinue of a 
single main mind, there can ar ise only one mental factor, for 
instance, feeling, of a similar type; two [mental factors] of a 
similar type but of different  substances cannot occur.
(2-3) Similar object of observation and [subjective] aspect and 
similar entity refer to the following.
–[A mind and the mental factors in its retinue] are similar in 
observing one object – the object of observation – and they are 
similar in being afflicted or non-afflicted in terms of the object of 
observation and the [subjective] aspect.
–If a main mind has become afflicted, then the mental factors in its 
retinue also become afflicted, whereas if a main mind has become
uncontaminated, the mental fact ors in its retinue also become 
uncontaminated.
30Five similarities according to 
Compendium of Knowledge
(4) Similar time refers to the following.  A main mind and the 
mental factors in its retinue ar e simultaneous in regard to the 
three – production, abiding and cessation.
(5) Similar realm and level refers to the following. If a main mind 
is a mind of the desire realm, then  it is impossible for a mental 
factor of the form or formless realms to arise in its retinue, and 
it is impossible for a mental factor  of the desire realm to arise in 
the retinue of a mind of the form realm. That realm in which the
main mind is included is also nec essarily the realm in whic h the
mental factor in its retinue is included.

(Geshe Rabten’s Mind and its Functions)

16Karma is definite
zThe Great Exposition of the Stages of the Path says:
–The manner is which actions are definite is as follows. 
Regardless of whether we are ordinary beings or superiors, 
all happiness in the aspect of pleasant feelings –even 
as slight as that produced in dependence on a cool breeze 
when one is born in hell –arises from virtuous actions 
accumulated in the past . Happiness cannot arise from 
non-virtuous action. 
–Also, all suffering in the asp ect of painful feelings –even 
as slight as that produced in the mental continuum of a foe-
destroyer –arises from non-virtue accumulated in the 
past. Suffering cannot arise from a virtuous action. 
17Non-virtue brings suffering,
Virtue brings happiness
(Nagarjuna’s) Precious Garland:
From non-virtues comes all sufferings
And likewise all bad migrations.
From virtue comes all happy migrations
And the happiness within all rebirths.
18Non-virtue brings suffering,
Virtue brings happiness
zTherefore, happiness and suffering neither arise without 
cause nor arise from discordant causes such as the 
fundamental nature, Ishvara, and so forth. Rather, in general, 
happiness and suffering come from virtuous and non-
virtuous actions [respectively ],and even the various 
instances of happiness and suffering arise individually, without
even the slightest mix-up, from the various instances of these 
two types of actions. Hence, gaining certainty regarding this definiteness or infallibility of actions and their results is 
known as “the correct view of all Buddhists” and is praised as the foundation of all wholesome qualities .
(Kachen Yeshe Gyeltsen’s Necklace )

separated from.

nor suffering is that which, when  it arises, neither of the two 
wishes occurs.

34Feelings divided by way of their 
supports
Furthermore, when feelings are divided by way of their 
supports, there are six:
Feeling arising from contact upon the aggregation of:
(1) a visible form, an eye [sense power], and an eye consciousness;
(2) a sound, an ear [sense power], and an ear consciousness;
(3) an odor, a nose [sense power], and a nose consciousness;
(4) a taste, a tongue [sense power], and a tongue consciousness;
(5) a tactile object, a body [sense power], and a body consciousness; and
(6) a phenomenon, a mental [sense power], and a mental consciousness.
Further, these six are each divide d into three – pleasant, sufferin g and neutral – giving eighteen 
types of feeling. 
35Division of feelings by way of object of 
abandonment and antidote
Moreover, when feelings are divided by way of object of abandonment and 
antidote, there are two:
(1) feelings that support adherence and
(2) feelings that support deliverance .
(1) Feelings that support adherence are feelings that are concomitant with the

43Three features of mindfulness

familiarized with.
(2)[Subjective] aspect feature – a non-forgetfulness upon

presently dawn as an object of t he mind, mindfulness will not 
occur.

mind is enhanced in dependenc e on special mindfulness.

53Three types of faith
1. Clarifying faith
Clarifying faith is a clear mind engendered by seeing 
the excellent qualities of those so endowed, such as the Three Jewels.
Why is it called “clarifying”? For example, when one 
places a water-purifying gem in dirty water, the murkiness of the water is immediately cleared away. Similarly, when this faith is generated, the murkiness 
of the mind is cleared away, whereupon all excellent 
qualities of realization become suitable to arise in 
one’s continuum .
54Three types of faith
2. Faith of conviction
Faith of conviction is the gaining of conviction 
through contemplating the modes of 
dependent-arising, cause and result, and so 
forth that are taught by the Conqueror.
55Three types of faith
3. Wishing faith
Wishing faith is, for instance, having contemplated 
the modes of the four noble truths, ascertained true sufferings and true origins as objects of abandonment, and true cessations and true paths as objects of attainment, and having understood that these can be attained if one makes the proper effort, 
the faith thinking, “I shall definitely obtain them.”
56z“Here I have merely identified some 
illustrations of the three types of faith; it is not 
that all [instances] have been exhausted 
here.”
(Necklace)
57Faith
zFaith is a knower that has the aspect of 
conviction, clarity, or wishing, and it serves 
as the direct antidote for non-faith.
(Necklace)
58Faith is the direct antidote for non-faith
1. Clarifying faith eliminates the non-faith that causes 
us to see faults in Dharma teachers, the Three 
Jewels, etc.
2. Faith of conviction eliminates the non-faith that 
causes us to doubt or disbelieve in things that are necessary to make spiritual progress, such as the law of karma.
3. Wishing faith eliminates the non-faith that does not 
aspire to virtuous attainments such as 
enlightenment.
59Liking vs. Faith
zFaith is by entity a virtuous mental factor, 
whereas liking has both virtuous and non-
virtuous factors.
60Four possibilities between liking and 
faith
There are the four possibilit ies between liking and faith:
1. That which is liking but not faith , e.g., liking one's son, one’s 
wife and so forth, and liking sources of misdeeds....
2.That which is faith but not liking , e.g., fear from one's depths 
and faith of conviction regarding the drawbacks of the 
sufferings of cyclic existence.
3.That which is both , e.g., faith from one's depths and liking due 
to contemplating the excellent qualities of the spiritual guide 
and the benefits of wholesome actions and their results.
4.That which is neither , e.g., anger, suffering, and so forth.
61Function of Faith
zFaith has the function of acting as a support for 
aspiration.
zHere, the function of faith is specified as "acting as a support for 
aspiration," because, as expl ained above, the cause of all 
excellent qualities is effort ; in order to generate effort, one 
needs the aspiration that seeks; in order to generate 
aspiration, one needs to see the excellent qualities as well as 
possess the faith of conviction . For this reason, faith is 
praised more than once as t he foundation of all virtuous 
qualities in the scriptures and their commentaries.

46Five types of effort
1. The effort of armoring is the donning of the great 
armor of preceding attitude, a mental enthusiasm 
prior to embarking on a virtuous activity. 
“This is the thought prior to engaging in virtue that is the mind’s taking delight in that activity. It is like putting on great armor in that it affords a willingness to engage in extended activity.”
(Hopkins, Meditation on 
Emptiness)
47Five types of effort
2.The effort of application is a mind that is 
enthusiastic when engaging in practice 
through application. 
“A mental delight while engaging in practice.”
(Hopkins, Meditation on Emptiness )
“The joy found in the practice of Dharma.”
(Rabten, Mind and its Functions )
48Five types of effort
3.The effort of non-inferiority is a generation 
of enthusiasm when engaging in virtue 
without the mind being discouraged, thinking, 
"How could one such as I do this?"
49Five types of effort
4. The effort of irreversibility is an accomplished 
mental enthusiasm such that one cannot be diverted 
by other circumstances at the time of engaging in virtuous activities.
“A fullness of mental delight such that circumstances cannot divert one from engaging in virtuous activity.”
(Hopkins, Meditation on Emptiness )
50Five types of effort
5. The effort of non-complacency is exertion that 
does not just rest complacent with some trifling 
virtue, but seeks higher qualities.
“from the point of view of not letting oneself deteriorate but develop to completion, a state of joy in which one is not contented with just a little virtue.”
(Rabten, Mind and its Functions )
51Furthermore, taking a minute branch of the

52In this vein, the Great Exposition of the Stages of the Path says:
Nowadays, the following appears to happen. Whether it be an

that, thinking, "I have made great progress on the path!" Then, 
when an expert in the essential po ints of the path confronts one
with the yardstick of scripture and reasoning, explaining, "Even
though that is indeed some good  quality, with that alone you 
have not gotten anywhere," understanding dawns, and one 
becomes totally discouraged. Although it is necessary to seek 
higher attributes without being complacent about a portion of 
some good quality and to train in th e infinite objects of trainings, 
the absence of discouragement seems to be very rare.

zThe function of effort is specified as

By making effort, nothing whatsoever,

This I have exhorted them to do.
56Method to generate effort
Thus, since all excellent qualities follow upon effort, the method to 
generate effort is extremely important....Hence it is of utmost 
importance to know the following  in accordance with ...Tsong-
Kha-pa's Great Exposition of the Stages of the Path :
zThe benefits of making effort ;
zThe disadvantages of not doing so ;
zThe way in which it stops its discordant class – the laziness 
of procrastination, the adherence to unwholesome activities, and indolence (or inferiority);
zThe way to practice the favo rable conditions for effort , the 
four powers – the power of aspiration, the power of 
steadfastness, the power of jo y and the power of abandonment.
57Four powers for generating effort
1.Aspiration – “The way to develop aspiration 
is to meditate on how pleasant and 
unpleasant effects arise from virtuous and 
non-virtuous karma respectively.”
(Tsongkhapa, Lamrim Chenmo )
58Four powers for generating effort
2. Steadfastness – “Developing the power of 
steadfastness means that you bring to conclusion, 
without turning back, anything at which you joyously 
persevere. 
“First, do not try to do everything; examine the 
situation carefully. If you see that you can do it, you 
engage in it; whereas if you cannot do it, you do not 
engage in it. You should not even start in the first place things you will do for a while and then discard.”
(Tsongkhapa, Lamrim Chenmo )
59Four powers for generating effort
2. Steadfastness
“The way to practice Dharma with steadiness...is 
(a) to proceed without expecting help from others.
(b) be confident that you have the capability to benefit 
both yourself and others, 
(c) be determined to overcome the delusions and not 
allow them to overcome you.”
(Loden, Path to Enlightenment )
60Four powers for generating effort
3.Joy – “The power of joy means that when 
you first engage in an activity, you do it 
joyfully, and once you have engaged, you 
have a sense of being insatiable in that you 
do not want to discontinue the activity.”
(Tsongkhapa, Lamrim Chenmo )
61Four powers for generating effort
3.Joy– “With the self-confidence that comes from 
aspiration and steadiness you should then engage in 
any task by approaching it with the mental factor of joy. Through being fully immersed in the enjoyment of the task, you will not want to stop, just as a child does not want to stop playing. This is the right 
attitude with which to approach your Dharma 
practice.”
(Loden, Path to Enlightenment)
62Four powers for generating effort
4.Abandonment – “If you become physically or 
mentally fatigued from your perseverance, you must 
rest for a while. Otherwise, you will become exhausted and very disheartened, thereby later preventing your joyous effort. Immediately after you have rested, persevere again, and when you have

13Five types of Virtue
In general, virtues are taught to be of five 
types:
1.virtues by way of entity
2.virtues by way of relation
3.virtues by way of subsequent relation
4.virtues by way of motivation
5.virtues by way of ultimacy
14Five types of Virtue
1. virtues by way of entity (or natural virtue) – The 
eleven (mental factors) explained above – faith and 
so forth – are specified as being virtues by way of entity because they are generated in the entity of 
virtue from their mere establishment without relying on other factors such as motivation . 
Hence, here I have indicated the eleven principal virtues.
15Five types of Virtue
1.virtues by way of entity (or natural virtue)
“These eleven virtuous mental factors are 
called ‘natural virtues’ because they are 
virtuous entities in themselves, without 
depending on consideration of other factors such as motivation and so forth.”
(Hopkins, Meditation on Emptiness)
16Five types of Virtue
2.virtues by way of relation –are minds and 
mental factors that are concomitant by way of 
the five similarities with the eleven – faith and so forth. 
3.virtues by way of subsequent relation –
are imprints of virtue. 
4.virtues by way of motivation – are, for 
instance, physical and verbal actions motivated by faith.
17Five types of Virtue
5.virtues by way of ultimacy (or ultimate 
virtue) – are explained to be thusnesses (or 
emptinesses), because all obscurations will 
be purified if one meditates within 
observation of thusness; 
therefore, they are designated as "virtues" but are not actual virtues.
185. Ultimate virtue
“Ultimate virtue is nirvana, the liberation from cyclic existence. 
Specifically, it is true cessati on, the permanent cessation of 
some or all of the afflictions. Th is is not the usual virtue that we 
talk about because virtue is generally seen to be an 
impermanent phenomenon, a cause that produces a positive 
result and non-virtue is seen to  be a cause that produces a 
negative result. Nirvana is called ultimate virtue because, fromone point of view, cyclic existenc e is ultimate non-virtue and so 
the opposite of that is ultimate  virtue. These descriptions are 
logical because when non-virtuous acts are created they lead to 
the destination of cyclic exist ence and when virtuous acts are 
created they lead to th e destination of nirvana.”
(Geshe Jampa Tegchog) 
19Eight virtues divided from the 
viewpoint of circumstances
When the virtues explained above are divided from 
the viewpoint of circumstances, there is:
1. virtue by way of attainment at birth
2. virtue by way of application
3. virtue by way of activity [directed] in front
4. virtue by way of benefiting
5. virtue by way of bearing
6. virtue by way of acting as an antidote
7. virtue by way of pacification
8. virtue by way of concordant cause
20Eight virtues divided from the 
viewpoint of circumstances
1. virtue by way of attainment at birth –is, for 
instance, the faith that arises through mere rebirth 
due to the imprints from past lives without depending 
on familiarization in this lifetime.
2. virtue by way of application –is, for instance, the 
generation of the wish to attain buddhahood in dependence on the four conditions – (i) relying on a 
virtuous spiritual guide, (ii) listening to the excellent doctrine, (iii) properly taking such to mind, and (iv) 
practicing doctrines that are concordant with the 
attainment of nirvana. 
21Eight virtues divided from the 
viewpoint of circumstances
3. virtue by way of activity [directed] in front –is, for 
instance, actions of making prostrations and making 
offerings within observation of the special field of accumulation. 
4. virtue by way of benefiting –is, for instance, 
actions such as ripening sentient beings by way of the four means of gathering disciples.
5. virtue by way of bearing –is, for instance, special 
wholesome actions which act as the means for 
attaining high status and definite goodness.
22Eight virtues divided from the 
viewpoint of circumstances
6. virtue by way of acting as an antidote –is, for 
instance, virtuous actions that possess the special 
power to directly destroy the objects of abandonment and the discordant class. 
7. virtue by way of pacification –is explained to be, 
for instance, true cessations
8. virtue by way of concordant cause –is, for 
instance, the five clairvoyances and the ten powers that arise though the force of attaining true 
cessations.
23Five types of non-virtue
Similarly, non-virtues are also taught to be of 
five types:
1.non-virtues by way of entity
2.non-virtues by way of relation
3.non-virtues by way of subsequent relation
4.non-virtues by way of motivation
5.non-virtues by way of ultimacy
24Five types of non-virtue
1. non-virtues by way of entity (or natural non-
virtue) –are mostly the [root] afflictions and the 
secondary afflictions. 
2. non-virtues by way of relation –are minds and 
mental factors that are concomitant with those 
afflictions. 
3. non-virtues by way of subsequent relation –are 
imprints of non-virtue. 
4. non-virtues by way of motivation –are the 
physical and verbal actions motivated by those 
afflictions.
25Five types of non-virtue
5.non-virtues by way of ultimacy (or ultimate 
non-virtue) – are [phenomena] included 
within cyclic existence. 
Since these are the sources from which superiors 
generate despondency, they are called "non-virtues 
by way of ultimacy." Nonetheless, it is not definite that everything included in cyclic existence is an actual non-virtue. 
26Five types of non-virtue
5. non-virtues by way of ultimacy (or ultimate non-
virtue) – The state of cyclic existence itself. 
“Cyclic existence is ultimate  non-virtue because it is 
a state where the sufferings of birth, sickness, old 
age, and death occur continuously. Although we call 
cyclic existence ultimate non-virtue, the usual meaning of non-virtue is a mental event. But cyclic existence is not a mental factor, so it is a nominal 
non-virtue (not an actual non-virtue).”
(Khensur Jampa Tegchog)

zIt is an afflicted wisdom which views the cause and 
result of actions, past and future lives, and so forth as non-existent. 
(Necklace)

44Wrong view
zA wrong view “is an afflicted state of 
intelligence that denies the existence of 
something which in fact exists.”
(Geshe Rabten, Mind and its Functions)
45Wrong view
zIt is only a wrong view in this context if it 
denies the existence of an object that is 
necessary to understand to attain liberation 
or enlightenment. 
46Functions of wrong view
It has the functions of 
zsevering roots of virtue, 
zholding tightly to roots of non-virtue, and 
zacting as a support for engaging in non-virtue 
and not engaging in virtue.
47Four types of wrong view
When wrong view is divided, there are four:
(1) wrong view that deprecates causes ,
(2) wrong view that deprecates results ,
(3) wrong view that deprecates functionality , and
(4) wrong view that deprecates existent things .
48Wrong view
(1) The wrong view that deprecates causes is the view that 
good behavior and bad behavior and so forth do not exist.
(2) The wrong view that deprecates results is the view that the 
fruition of virtuous actions  and misdeeds does not exist.
(3) The wrong view that deprecates functionality is the view 
that one's parents, past and future  worlds [i.e., rebirths] and so 
forth do not exist.
(4) The wrong view that deprecates existent things is the view 
that the attainment of foe-des troyer and so forth do not exist.
49The most serious wrong views
zAlthough in general there are many wrong 
views, since the wrong view which 
deprecates actions and their results, past 
and future worlds [i.e., rebirths], and so 
forth severs all roots of virtue, it is taught 
to be the most serious among all wrong views.
50Summary of wrong view
zIt is an afflicted wisdom which views the cause and result of actions, 
past and future lives, and so forth as non-existent. It denies the 
existence of an object that is necessary to understand to attain
liberation or enlightenment. 
zIt is any endurance, desire, intelligence, conception, or view. There are four types: wrong views that: (1) deprecate causes , (2) deprecate  
results , (3) deprecate functionality , and (4) deprecate existent 
things .
zIt has the functions of severing roots of virtue, holding tightly to roots 
of non-virtue, and acting as a supp ort for engaging in non-virtue and 
not engaging in virtue.
zThe most serious among all wrong views are those that sever all roots of virtue, that which denies actions and their results (karma), past and 
future worlds [i.e., rebirths], and so forth.
51Condensing the five afflicted views
Furthermore, when the five types of views are 
condensed, they can be subsumed into two

(1) views that superimpose
(2) views that deprecate

29Five types of wrong livelihood

toward others, feigning good qualities one does not have.
(2) Flattery is to speak smooth words in conformity with the minds 
of others for the sake of gain and honor.
(3) Hinting is to first flatter others and then praise their 
possessions out of an attachment to gain.
(4) Extortion is to speak about the faults  of great miserliness and 
the like for the sake of gain.
(5) Seeking gain from gain is to seek gain by praising in the 
presence of another what one ha s previously obtained, saying, 
"Previously so-and-so gave me such-and-such."

51Review of faith
zFaith is a conviction, clarity, or wishing with respect to an 
existent that is endowed with excellent qualities and power.
zIt has the function of acting as a support for aspiration.
Three types of faith
1. Clarifying faith eliminates the non-faith that causes us to s ee faults in Dharma 
teachers, the Three Jewels, etc.
2. Faith of conviction eliminates the non-faith th at causes us to doubt or 
disbelieve in things that are necessary to make spirit ual progress, such as the 
law of karma.
3. Wishing faith eliminates the non-faith that does not aspire to virtuous 
attainments such as enlightenment.
52Three types of non-faith
1.non-faith of non-conviction in actions and their 
results and so forth – the discordant class of the faith 
of conviction ;
2.non-faith of non-clarity and a mental dislike with 
respect to the possessors of excellent qualities, such 
as the Three Jewels – the discordant class of 
clarifying faith; and
3.non-faith of non-wishing and non-seeking of 
liberation and so forth – the discordant class of 
wishing faith.
53Function of non-faith
zIt has the function of acting as a support for 
laziness . (Asanga, Compendium of Knowledge)

laziness and of causing the power of faith 
to decrease .(Geshe Rabten, Mind and its Functions)
54Non-faith

such as the law of actions and their results – non-
faith is the complete opposite of faith. 
zIn addition it makes one disbelieve, disrespect and have no desire for that which is positive, thus severing the roots of any wholesome development .”
(Geshe Rabten, Mind and its Functions)
55Non-faith
zNon-faith makes our mind like a dry seed 
from which no sprouts of spiritual realization 
can grow. However much we study Dharma, 
if we lack faith we shall never gain any 
realizations. 
zTherefore, to gain results from our Dharma study we must improve our faith in Dharma 
and in our Teachers.

60Six types of distraction
When distraction is divided, there are six:
(1) distraction by way of its very entity
(2) external distraction,
(3) internal distraction,
(4) distraction by way of signs,
(5) distraction by way of unfavorable states,
(6) distraction by way of attention.
61Six types of distraction
(1) Distraction by way of its very entity is explained to be the 
five sense consciousnesses. The r eason is that, at the time of 
being set in equipoise on meditati ve stabilization, if any of the 
five sense consciousnesses are generated, then [the mind] 
arises from meditative stabilization. Thus, they are called 
"outward distraction of the mind away from meditative 
stabilization."
(2) External distraction is, when one is engaged in virtues of the 
three – listening, contemplation and meditation – the outward 
scattering of the mind to the attributes of the desire realm 
without abiding in them [i.e., the virtues]; since it causes scattering to an erroneous object of observation without abiding
on the virtuous object of observation, it is called such.
62Six types of distraction
(3) Internal distraction is laxity, excitement, and 
craving for the taste of meditative absorption at the 
time of being set in equipoise on meditative 
stabilization. What is the reason for calling these 
"internal distraction"? They are so-called because they are the main obstacles for cultivating meditative 
stabilization internally.
(4) Distraction by way of signs is to put effort in 
virtuous training with the thought, "How wonderful it would be if another person were to develop 
conviction in me, thinking, 'He has attained a concentration.'"
63Six types of distraction
(5) Distraction by way of unfavorable states is a 
puffing up of the mind conceiving I and mine when 
the feeling that one is engaging in virtue arises in 
dependence on the unfavorable states of the view of 
the transitory collection and pride.
(6) Distraction by way of attention is, for instance,
zgiving up the meditative absorption of the fourth 
concentration for the meditative absorption of the 
third concentration or below, or
zthinking, "Giving up engaging in the great vehicle, I 
shall abide in the lesser vehicle."
64Six types of distraction
zThese types of distraction explained here are taught 
to be general enumerations of distraction , but it is 
not definite that they are actual secondary 
afflictions ; the first is unspecified and the last one is 
included within virtue. 
zSince it is taught that the distraction that is an 
actual secondary affliction is , for instance, the 
external distraction and internal distraction which 
have been explained here, those of keen intellect need to analyze this in detail.

29Sleep
zSleep is a subtle mind. When a subtle mind 
becomes manifest, gross minds such as 
sense consciousnesses necessarily become 
non-manifest, which is why sleep has the 
function of gathering the sense 
consciousnesses inwards. 
30Sleep
zIt is a withdrawal of the mind in dependence 
upon sleep, its cause, with respect to:
–virtue, non-virtue and the unspecified,
–the timely and the untimely,
–and the appropriate and the inappropriate,
zand is involved with ignorance. 
(Asanga, Compendium of Knowledge)
31Sleep
zHere, regarding " the timely and the untimely ", it is just as it 
has been said by the Protector Nagarjuna [in his Letter to a 
Friend]:
Sensible ones! Occupy your selves the entire daytime and
Also the first and last parts of the night.
Sleep with mindfulness between these;
Then even the time of sleep will not be wasted.
zJust as it has been said above, the middle watch of the night 
is the time for sleep , whereas the first and the last watches of 
the night, as well as daytime, not  being the time for sleep, are
times for making effort in the application of virtue. 
32Sleep
zRegarding "the appropriate and the 
inappropriate " [in the citation above], 
sleeping during the middle watch of the night out of the wish to engage in virtue by developing the elements of the body is appropriate , whereas sleep motivated by 
afflictions, even during the middle watch of the night, is inappropriate . Hence it is taught 
for the sake of understanding this. 
33Function of sleep
zIt has the function of acting as a support for 
failing to perform actions .
zIts function is specified as "failing to perform 
actions" because, since sleep is of two types 
– the virtuous and the non-virtuous – the non-virtuous type, afflicted sleep, causes 
virtuous activities to degenerate .

37Three types of regret
Regret is of three types:
1.virtuous –e.g., regret for past misdeeds.
2.non-virtuous –e.g., regret for having created 
merit.
3.unspecified –e.g., regret for something that 
neither benefited nor harmed others, such as manual 
work.
38Regret
z"The timely and the untimely, and the appropriate and the 
inappropriate" [in the citation above] means that generating 
regret when something is rectifiable is appropriate , 
whereas regret when it is not is inappropriate . 
zMoreover, appropriateness of regret when it is rectifiable refers 
to, for instance, the necessity to  confess misdeeds with regret 
before the fruition has been es tablished in the present. 
zNot rectifiable refers to, for instance, rebirth in a bad migration 
due to fruition having been established; since having being 
born blind, physically disabled,  and so forth are occasions 
where the fruition has been estab lished, they are irreversible. 
39Regret
zIf we regret a non-virtuous  karma before the principal 
karmic result occurs we can purify it, but regret while 
experiencing a result cannot purify the cause . 
–For example, one who has been reborn in hell cannot change that 
situation by regretting the non-virtuous action that caused it. A 
blind man cannot reverse his condition by regretting the karmic 
cause for his blindness.  
zBuddha said that a principal ka rmic result that has ripened 
cannot be altered, there are no  exceptions. This may confuse 
those who think that any heavy negative karma can be purified, 
but such purification can only occu r if it is before the 
principal karmic result has ar isen. All negative karmas can 
be purified before they ripen.
(Khensur Jampa Tegchog)

49Investigation and analysis
z“Investigation being a consciousness with a ‘rough 
aspect’ means that it is always actively involved in 
work, whereas analysis has a fine or clear aspect 
because it holds and remains upon a specific object that has been already thought about and well ascertained. 
zSome texts say that investigation is a way of 
ascertaining objects in a gross manner and analysis
is a way of ascertaining objects through precise 
checking.”
(Khensur Jampa Tegchog)
50Function of investigation and analysis
zTheir function is to act as a support for abiding in 
contact and not abiding in contact. 
zThe function of investigation and analysis is 
specified as " acting as a support for abiding in contact and not abiding in contact," because investigation and analysis are each of two types –the virtuous and the non-virtuous.
51Function of investigation and analysis
zActing as a support for abiding in contact with 
happiness – investigation and analysis belonging to 
the class of virtue act to produce pleasant results, 
thus "acting as a support for abiding in contact" with 
happiness, and
zActing as a support for not abiding in contact 
with happiness – investigation and analysis 
belonging to the class of non-virtue act to produce 
unpleasant results, thus "acting as a support for not 
abiding in contact" with happiness.
52Virtuous, non-virtuous and unspecified 
investigation and analysis
Moreover,
zInvestigation and fine analysis of the meaning of selflessness 
with the thought to definitely em erge from cyclic existence, and
so forth belong to the class of virtue ,
zInvestigation and fine analysis of the modes of pleasant or 
unpleasant objects motivated by attachment, anger, and so 
forth belong to the class of non-virtue, and
zAnalysis and investigation of wo rk, behavior and so forth that 
are neither virtuous nor non-virtuous minds are unspecified .
53Discussion of the category of 
changeable mental factors
Why are these four – sleep, regret, investigation, and 
analysis – called "changeable"?
zThey are called "changeable" because they become 
virtuous, non-virtuous, or unspecified through the 
force of being concomitant with the motivating 
thought.
zAfflicted sleep, regret, investigation, and analysis 
obscure the entire class of virtue in general, and especially the three trainings [in ethics, meditative 
stabilization, and wisdom].

To the prison of [cyclic] ex istence since beginningless time,

In the wilderness covered by th ickets of [cyclic] existence,

58A summary of the salient points and 
their application to practice
It is just as it has been said in the King of

Although I have explained the good doctrine,
If you do not put it into practice correctly after having 
heard it,
Then you are like a sick person who clutches a bag 
of medicines,
But is unable to cure his sickness.
59A summary of the salient points and 
their application to practice
The Exhortation to the Exceptional Resolve 
(Adhyasayasamcodanasutra ) says:
The bark of the sugarcane has  no essence whatsoever;
The taste that is the obj ect of joy dwells inside.
Through eating the bark,
The delicious taste of th e sugarcane cannot be gained.
Thus, blabbering away is like the bark and
Contemplation of the m eaning is like the taste.
Therefore, abandon joy in blabbering,
Always be conscientious and contemplate the meaning.
60A summary of the salient points and 
their application to practice
zHence, through becoming skilled in the 
classifications of virtue and non-virtue as explained above, at the time of endeavoring single-pointedly in the practice of the stages of the path to 
enlightenment, repeatedly examine your own 
continuum in order to recognize the root and secondary afflictions that have been explained above, and if these afflictions arise, having 
recognized them, immediately overcome them 
with the antidotes. This is a crucial point.
61A summary of the salient points and 
their application to practice
zAs soon as an affliction arises in your 
continuum, having forcefully recognized it, thinking, "This is it! It has arisen!" you must examine which causes and conditions produced it, and what the object of observation from which it arose is, and then, 
regarding it as a fault, hold it as an enemy 
and immediately overcome it with the antidote. 
62A summary of the salient points and 
their application to practice
zWhen we come under the influence of an 
affliction, we do not know which affliction it is, 
and even if we do know, due to our feeble 
effort and antidote, they recur. 
zHowever, if we were to generate the power of the antidote, we will definitely be able to 
abandon them. 
63A summary of the salient points and 
their application to practice
The Foremost Omniscient [Tsong-kha-pa] says [in his 
Great Exposition of the Stages of the Path ]:
zHaving recognized the afflictions, contemplate their 
drawbacks and the benefits of being separated from 
them and then plant the spies of mindfulness and introspection. Whenever any affliction sprouts forth, 
again and again, you must nip it in the bud. Further, 
as soon as any affliction stirs in your continuum, you must regard it as an enemy and combat it. 
Otherwise, if it were to emerge first, and you 
acquiesce and foster it with improper attention, then, without any resistance, you will only succumb to it.
