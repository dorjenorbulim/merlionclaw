                             Attaining Serenity
The stages in which the mental states develop
  1. The actual stages in which the mental states develop
  2. The process of achieving them with the six forces
  3. How the four attentions are involved in this
1. The actual stages in which the mental states develop
Today we will look at achieving calm abiding through the nine mental states
as explained in Maitreya’s Ornament for the Mahayana Sutras.
In order to achieve single-pointed concentration, it is extremely important
to first rely on the preconditions for developing calm abiding. This was
explained earlier. Gathering and relying on these preconditions for
developing calm abiding is indispensable, and is very important for
developing calm abiding.
The preconditions are having little desire, having contentment and
completely giving up the hustle and bustle of life, giving up many
activities, have pure ethical discipline and so forth. It is mentioned in
the teachings that keeping a pure ethical discipline is an extremely
important precondition for developing concentration. With regards to
keeping pure ethical discipline, at the minimum, one must be intentionally
refraining or restraining one’s body and speech i.e. restraining from
committing non-virtues with one’s body and speech. That is the minimum.
The first of the nine mental states is translated as the mental placement
in the LRCM. Perhaps you could say settling the mind. What happen on this
first stage is that you are settling your mind or placing your mind on the
object of meditation.
The first of the nine mental states is called a mental placement or
settling the mind. This is achieved with the power of hearing.
There are six powers that are needed for developing concentration as we
moved along the nine mental states. Of the six powers, the first is the
power of hearing.
Before you can settle your mind on an object of observation, you first must
hear the instructions for doing so from another person. You must be
introduced to the kinds of objects that you can settle your mind upon and
so forth. Then through hearing and understanding the instructions, you
place or settle your mind on that chosen object of meditation.
Here it is said that through the power of hearing (i.e. based on your
hearing about the instructions and about the object of meditation), you
place your mind on that object of meditation i.e. you settle your mind on
that object of meditation.
Now it is not the time to read your text or notes. Put that down. Now it is
the time to focus and try to understand what is being said. For that, it
will make some difference.
The first mental state is called mental placement or settling the mind.
This is achieved through the power of hearing. You hear the instructions
and the kinds of objects of meditation and so forth. You decide on an
object of meditation and then you place your mind exactly according to what
you have heard, i.e. according to the instructions. So through the power of
hearing, you try to settle your mind on that object.
At this initial level when you are trying to settle your mind/place your
mind on an object of observation, there must be tight focus. At this
initial level, we are talking about forcibly engaging or attempting to
tighten your mind’s focus i.e. attempt to settle your mind on the object of
meditation.
It is said in the teachings that at this initial level, placing your
mind/settling your mind on an object of observation is challenging. It is
difficult. At this initial level, you are only able to place your mind on
the object of meditation for a short while.
The analogy used in the teaching is of how the bees move from one flower to
another. The bees only land and stay there for a short time before moving
on to another object. So that is an analogy of one’s attempt at settling
one’s mind.
On this first mental state, which is called the mental placement/settling
the mind, one is only able to settle the mind on the object for very short
period of time. At this initial level, the mental placement/settling the
mind on the chosen object of meditation is achieved through the power of
hearing.
On this level, there is a need for tight focus. Of the four mental
engagements or the four mental attentions, at this initial level it is
called tight focus or forcibly engaging.
[LRCM Vol 3, page 73] 1. Mental placement: This entails thoroughly
withdrawing your attention from all outside objects and directing it
inwardly to the object of meditation. Maitreya's Ornament for the Mahayana
Sutras states: "After you have directed your attention to the object of
meditation...."
On the second mental state, one is able to settle the mind on the object of
meditation for a relatively longer period of time. This happens on the
second mental state. Therefore the second mental state is called continuous
placement or continuous setting.
On the second mental state, one’s ability to set the mind on the object of
meditation for a longer period of time does not come through the power of
hearing. It comes through the power of reflection. So the second mental
state of continuous placement/continuous setting is achieved through the
power of reflections; not through the power of hearing.
Here, through the power of reflection (i.e. through constantly thinking and
reflecting about the object of meditation) by putting your focus on the
object of meditation, you are able to set the mind on the object of
meditation and place it there for a longer period of time. As such, on the
second mental state the quality of the mindfulness that the person has is
better than the mindfulness that was present on the first state.
This is saying that the quality of mindfulness that comes about through the
power of reflection is better than the quality of the mindfulness that
comes from the power of hearing.
[LRCM Vol 3, page 74] 2. Continuous placement: Your attention that was
initially directed to the object of meditation does not stray elsewhere,
but is continuously set upon the object of meditation. The Ornament for the
Mahayana Sutras: "Its continuity is not distracted."
The third mental state is called patched placement or resetting.
Just like the first mental state, on the second mental state there is also
the need for tight focus.
Of the four mental engagements or the four mental attentions, the first one
is called forcibly engaging or the mental attention of tight focus, and is
required on the first two mental states.
On the first mental state, the mind is just barely able to abide on the
chosen object of meditation. It does not abide for a long time as it is
kind of distracted.
On the second mental state, one is able to abide, place or set the mind for
relatively longer periods of time. In that sense, there is this continuity
of the mind placement or setting on the object.
On the third mental state, the mind is pretty much abides on the object of
meditation for a lot of time. So on the third mental state, when your mind
is pretty much placed on the object of meditation, there is the great
danger of excitement and laxity arising. In order to notice their arising,
you need very strong mindfulness. You need such strong mindfulness in order
to notice the arising of excitement and laxity. So immediately upon their
arising, you must then apply the antidote to cut them off.  Therefore,
there is a need for strong mindfulness. As such, the third mental stage
which is called patched placement or resetting is achieved through the
power of mindfulness.
On the third mental state, strong mindfulness is required to notice the
distractions. When the mind indeed gets distracted and if you have strong
mindfulness at this level, you are able to place your mind’s attention back
on the object of meditation.
So what mindfulness does is non-forgetfulness of the object of meditation.
If you have strong mindfulness, even if you get distracted, you can
immediately notice it and put the attention back on the object of
meditation. Hence, it is called patched placement or resetting.
You are resetting your mind’s attention on the object of meditation. Such a
powerful mindfulness which occurs on the third mental state is a continuity
of the mindfulness that comes about on the second mental state.
On the second mental state, through the power of continuous reflection, one
develops mindfulness. This mindfulness continues and then on the third
mental state, you are seeking to make it strong.
On the third mental state, if the mind comes under the influence of
excitement or laxity, then the mind becomes distracted. And you are
supposed to reset the mind’s attention on the object of meditation. So it
is call resetting or patched placement.
[LRCM Vol 3, page 74] 3. Patched placement: If your attention is drawn away
by forgetfulness and distracted outward, you recognize this and again fix
it upon the object of meditation. The Ornament for the Mahayana Sutras:
"Swiftly recognizing distraction, it is patched up again."
The fourth mental state is called close placement or close setting. This is
also achieved through the power of mindfulness. So third and the fourth
mental states are achieved through the power of mindfulness. However, there
is a difference between these two states.
The third mental state, which is called patched placement or resetting,
through the power of strong mindfulness you reset your mind’s attention on
the object of meditation when it is distracted. That is achieved through
the power of strong mindfulness.
The fourth mental state involves strong mindfulness as well. The difference
is that on the fourth mental state, you are depending or relying on the
power of strong mindfulness to keep the mind from being distracted in the
first place.
As I have said the third mental state, which is called patched placement or
resetting, depends on powerful mindfulness to reset your mind if it is
distracted.
On the fourth mental state, it depends on mindfulness to set your mind on
the object without being distracted i.e. not letting it become distracted.
The ability that you gained on the fourth mental state is a result of the
strong mindfulness that was developed on the third mental state.
So on the third mental state, your mindfulness is being developed because
every time you noticed that your mind is distracted, you immediately pull
the attention back. When you get distracted again, you pull it back again.
So through the cultivation of the mindfulness of the third mental state,
this gives rise to a more powerful mindfulness which occurs on the fourth
mental state. With that powerful mindfulness on the fourth mental state,
you are able to just put the mind on the object without letting it be
distracted.
[LRCM Vol 3, page 74]  4. Close placement: Kamalasila's first Stages of
Meditation comments that with the previous mental state you recognize
distraction and eliminate it; with this mental state you have eliminated
distraction and with effort place your attention upon the object of
meditation. Ratnakarasanti's Instructions for the Perfection of Wisdom
asserts that your attention, which is by nature expansive, is repeatedly
drawn in and refined, establishing ever greater stability. This is in
accord with the statement [from the Ornament for the Mahayana Sutras]: "The
wise withdraw their attention inward to ever greater levels." Asanga's
Sravaka Levels explains that first mindfulness is applied, and your
attention does not stray outside. As the force of mindfulness develops,
forgetfulness does not create outward distraction

The fifth mental state is called taming or disciplining.
On the fourth mental state, which is called close placement/close setting,
through the force of powerful mindfulness you are able to keep your
attention on the object of meditation without any distraction. But due to
such tight focus, there is a danger that the mind becomes slack and then
there is a danger of laxity arising.
Therefore, on the fifth mental state, which is called taming or
disciplining, there is a need to uplift the mood of the mind and there is a
need to cultivate delight in the mind. One does that by reflecting on the
qualities of concentration.
The fifth mental state, taming or disciplining, is achieved through the
power of vigilance (or the power of introspection). As I mentioned earlier,
due to the danger of the mind becoming slack and then coming under the
influence of laxity due to very tight focus on the fourth mental state,
therefore there is a need to uplift the mood of the mind; literally to make
the mind happy is to cultivates delight. One does that through thinking
about the qualities of concentration. What you delight of possesses
concentration and so forth. And in doing so, you generate delight and
uplift the mood of the mind. This is achieved through the power of
vigilance.
The third and the fourth mental states are achieved through the power of
mindfulness, i.e. through very strong mindfulness.
The fifth and the sixth mental states are developed through the power of
vigilance. So on the fifth and sixth mental states one develops a very
strong vigilance. As we had seen in the earlier lessons, powerful vigilance
arises from powerful mindfulness.
As mentioned earlier, due to this very tight focus on the object of
meditation, then there is the danger of the mind becoming slack and laxity
arising. You need to stop this on the fifth mental state. This is done
through the power of vigilance (power of introspection). It is only with
vigilance you are able to notice that there is laxity in the mind. And
through detecting it, immediately you have to do something about it.
So when the mind is lax, there is slackness of the mind, you need to uplift
the mind and you need to cultivate delight in the mind. One of the ways to
do so is to reflect on the qualities of concentration.
The fifth mental state is called taming or disciplining.

[LRCM Vol 3, page 74]  5. Taming: Reflecting upon the advantages of
concentration, you take delight in concentration.
The Ornament for the Mahayana Sutras: "Then, because you see the
advantages, your mind is tamed in concentration." The Sravaka Levels
comments that if your mind is distracted by the signs of any of the five
sensory objects of visual form and so on, of the three mental poisons
[attachment, hostility, and ignorance], or of a man or a woman, you regard
these ten signs as disadvantageous from the outset and do not let them
scatter your mind.

The sixth mental state is called pacification.
On the fifth mental state, through the power of vigilance you notice the
presence of slackness of the mind. When you notice there is laxity, you
need to uplift the mind. Then you need to generate delight i.e. have to
have delight for concentration. In doing that, one works at eliminating
laxity.
When one achieves that, one moves on to the sixth mental state. On the
sixth mental state, the likelihood of laxity arising is minimal and perhaps
laxity may not even arise.
Due to the uplifting the mind on the fifth mental state, then on the sixth
mental stage, although the opportunity for laxity to arise is pretty much
non-existent, nevertheless due to uplifting the mind, then there is also
the danger of the mind becoming distracted. There is also the danger of
excitement. One needs to recognize these dangers with vigilance. The way to
deal with such potential distractions on the sixth mental stage is to bring
to mind and to reflect on the faults of distractions. So, on the sixth
mental stage, through reflecting on the faults of distractions and so
forth, one pacifies the mind that does not take delight in concentration.
On the fifth mental state, there is a danger of laxity arising.
On the sixth mental state, there is a danger of excitement and distractions
arising.
In order to recognize their arising and to deal with them, you need a very
strong vigilance.
Hence, the fifth and the sixth mental states are achieved through the power
of vigilance.

[LRCM Vol 3, page 74]  6. Pacification: Regarding distraction as a fault,
you quell any dislike for concentration.
The Ornament for the Mahayana Sutras: "Because you see the faults of
distraction, you quell dislike for the meditation."  The Sravaka Levels
asserts that if your attention is disturbed by thoughts such as those
concerning sensory objects and by secondary afflictions such as
obstructions involving attraction to the sensory, you regard these from the
beginning as disadvantageous, and do not allow your attention to be drawn
to your thoughts and secondary afflictions.
The seventh mental state is called complete pacification.
The seventh and the eighth mental states are achieved through the power of
effort.
On the seventh mental state, laxity does not arise but there is still the
danger of excitement arising. There is still the danger of scattering.
However, these two are easily pacified i.e. easily removed with just a
little bit of effort.
Of the four mental engagements or the four mental attentions, the mental
engagements of the third to the seventh mental states are called
interruptedly engaging.
From the third to the seventh mental states, the mind is not able to abide
on the object of meditation completely all the time. The placement or the
setting of the mind on the object of meditation is interrupted every now
and then by laxity and excitement. Hence, the mental engagement during
these states is called interrupted engagement.

[LRCM Vol 3, page 74]  7.  Complete pacification:
This entails the fine pacification of the occurrence of attachment,
melancholy, lethargy, sleepiness, etc. The Ornament for the Mahayana
Sutras: "As soon as attachment, melancholy, etc. arise, they are pacified."
The Sravaka Levels says that if the thoughts and secondary afflictions
mentioned earlier arise as a result of forgetfulness, you do not submit to
all that appear, but eliminate them.

The eighth mental state is literally called making one pointed, and as a
noun you can say one-pointed attention.
On the eighth mental state one’s concentration will not be interrupted by
either laxity or excitement.
With effort, one is able to place one’s attention on one’s mind on the
object continuously without being interrupted by laxity and excitement.  It
is said that on the eighth mental state, just by relying a little bit on
mindfully and vigilance, one is able to place the attention on the object
of meditation without any interruption from laxity and excitement.

[LRCM Vol 3, page 75]  8.  One-pointed attention:
This entails exerting effort so that you engage the object of meditation
effortlessly. The Ornament for the Mahayana Sutras: "Then one endowed with
restraint and enthusiasm applies remedies for the obstacles to his or her
mind and naturally achieves the ninth mental state." This is to be
understood from the statement in the Sravaka Levels: "By means of
application you have no hindrance, and, since you continuously establish a
flow of concentration, you make a single channel." Another term applied to
the eighth mental state is "single channeling," the meaning of which is
easily understood.

The ninth mental state is called setting in equipoise.
When one on the ninth mental state, it is as if one is having control over
the mind.
On the eighth mental state with the application of a little bit of effort
and with a little mindfulness and vigilance, one is able to set one’s mind
on the object of meditation without being interrupted by laxity and
excitement.
On the ninth mental state, the mind spontaneously settles and remains on
the object of meditation without effort.
Of the four mental engagements, the third one is called uninterruptedly
engaging, is involved with the eighth mental state. It is called
uninterruptedly engaging because one’s engagement of the object of
meditation is uninterrupted by laxity and excitement.
Then you have the ninth mental state setting in equipoise. Of the six
powers, the ninth mental state is achieved through the power of thorough or
complete familiarity. Here the word “acquaintance” is too weak. So, it is
the power of thorough familiarity.
Of the four mental engagements, the mental engagement called spontaneously
engaging is involved with the ninth mental state because the mind is
spontaneously abiding on the object without any effort.
On the ninth mental state, one’s concentration is completely freed of
laxity and excitement i.e. it will not be affected by any laxity and
excitement. One’s mind is spontaneously just settled on the object. Just by
turning one’s attention to the object of meditation, the mind just
naturally spontaneously settles. One evens no need to work or to strive for
it. Hence it also does not need for mindfulness and vigilance here.
Laxity and excitement does not arise on the eighth mental state.
Nevertheless, on the eighth mental state, one still needs to apply
mindfulness and vigilance whereas on the ninth mental state there is no
need for any mindfulness or vigilance. These two mental states are similar
in that both are not affected by laxity or excitement. But on the eighth
mental state, there is the need for mindfulness and vigilance whereas on
the ninth mental state, there is no need for mindfulness and vigilance as
the mind just spontaneously settles on the object of meditation by just
turning the attention of the mind to the object.
When you reach the ninth mental state, it is as if you have gained control
or masterly over your mind but you have not achieved calm abiding yet.
Is the mind of such a person who has reached the ninth mental state the
mind of the desire realm or the mind of the form realm? It is a mind of the
desire realm.

[LRCM Vol 3, page 75]  9. Balanced placement:
According to Kamalasila's Stages of Meditation, this refers to the
equanimity that occurs when your mind becomes balanced; Ratnakarasanti's
Instructions for the Perfection of Wisdom says this refers to spontaneous,
natural attention and the attainment of independence as a result of
familiarity with single channeling. The Ornament for the Mahayana Sutras
states: "There is non-application due to familiarity with that." The
Sravaka Levels says your mind is "concentrated," and the meaning of this is
clearly stated in the same text:
   As a consequence of dedication, familiarization, and frequent practice,
   you reach the path of both spontaneous and natural attention. With no
   application and with spontaneity, your mind enters into a flow of
   concentration that is without distraction. In this way it is
   concentrated.
The names of the nine mental states are in accord with the lines in
Kamalasila's first Stages of Meditation: "This path of meditative serenity
is explained in the Perfection of Wisdom sutras and so on...."

What is the dividing line between achieving calm abiding and not achieving
calm abiding?
One has not achieved calm abiding yet even though you are abiding on the
ninth mental state. If the mind, the concentration is conjoined or
qualified by the bliss of pliancy, then that is calm abiding. Otherwise, it
is not calm abiding.
You must know that there are two forms of pliancy:
   1. Mental pliancy
   2. Physical pliancy
There is also the bliss of pliancy. There is the bliss of physical pliancy
and the bliss of mental pliancy.
Mental pliancy is the antidote to the dysfunctional tendency of the mind.
Physical pliancy is the antidote to the dysfunctional tendency of the body.

Of the two types of pliancy, it is mental pliancy arises first, and then
follows by physical pliancy.
Then that is followed with the arising of the bliss of physical pliancy.
Then you have the bliss of mental pliancy. This is the order of the stage
of their arising.
Mental pliancy ( physical pliancy ( bliss of physical pliancy ( bliss of
mental pliancy
So, you have mental pliancy, physical pliancy, the bliss of physical
pliancy and then the bliss of mental pliancy. When the bliss of mental
pliancy arises initially, it arises strongly. It arises with a force and
such it makes difficult for it to go hand in hand together with
concentration.
When the force of that bliss of mental pliancy gradually subsides, when it
becomes kind of balance and equal measure with concentration,  that is when
calm abiding is achieved initially.

[LRCM Vol 3, page 75] (b) The process of achieving them with the six forces
There are six forces: the force of hearing, the force of reflection, the
force of mindfulness, the force of vigilance, the force of enthusiasm, and
the force of thorough acquaintance. The method of accomplishing the mental
states with these forces is as follows:
1. With the force of hearing, you accomplish mental placement. The reason
for this is that due to following the instructions that you have merely
heard from someone else about focusing on the object of meditation, at
first you simply fix your attention upon the object. But this is not a case
of familiarity due to your own repeated reflection.
2. With the force of reflection, you accomplish the mental state of
continual placement; for, as a consequence of the practice of repeatedly
reflecting on the continuation of the initial fixation of attention upon
the object of meditation, for the first time you achieve the ability to
maintain a little continuity.
3. With the force of mindfulness, you accomplish the mental states of
patched placement and of close placement; for, in the case of patched
placement, when your attention is distracted away from the object of
meditation, you are mindful of the previous object of meditation and your
attention is drawn back in; and in the case of close placement, you
generate the power of mindfulness from the beginning, and this prevents
your attention from being distracted away from the object of meditation.
4. With the force of vigilance, you accomplish the mental states of taming
and of pacification; for, with vigilance you recognize the faults of being
scattered toward thoughts and the signs of the secondary afflictions, and
by regarding them as faults, you do not let scattering toward these two
occur.
5. With the force of enthusiasm, you accomplish the mental states of
complete pacification and of one-pointed attention; for, by striving to
eliminate even subtle thoughts and secondary afflictions, you do not submit
to them; and by so doing, laxity, excitement, etc. are unable to interfere
with your concentration, and you achieve continuous concentration.
6. With the force of acquaintance, you accomplish the mental state of
balanced placement; for, with the force of great familiarity with the
above, you develop effortless, natural concentration.
These accord exactly with the intended meaning of Asanga's Sravaka Levels;
so, although there are alternative explanations, do not rely on them.

[LRCM Vol 3, page 78] How the four attentions are involved in this
The Sravaka Levels states:
With respect to these nine mental states, know that there are four types of
attention:
 (1) tight focus,
 (2) intermittent focus,
 (3) uninterrupted focus, and
 (4) spontaneous focus.
Now in the first two mental states of mental placement and correct mental
placement [i.e., continuous placement], there is the attention of tight
focus.
In the next five mental states of withdrawn mental placement [i.e., patched
placement], close placement, taming, pacification, and complete
pacification, there is the attention of intermittent focus.
In the eighth mental state of single channeling [i.e., one-pointed
attention], there is the attention of uninterrupted focus.
In the ninth state of concentrated awareness [i.e., balanced placement],
there is the attention of effortless [i.e., spontaneous] focus.
During the first two mental states the attention must be strenuously tight,
so this is tight focus. Then during the phases of the next five mental
states there is interference by laxity and excitement and you are unable to
maintain long meditation sessions; so this is intermittent focus.  Then
since in the eighth mental state you are able to sustain long meditation
sessions without interference by laxity and excitement, there is
uninterrupted focus. Then since in the ninth mental state there are no
interruptions and no need for continuous exertion, you apply the attention
of effortless focus.
