Student: In each of the ten grounds, there is certain perfection such as
perfection of generosity, perfection of ethics, perfection of patience and
so forth. It is stated that there are very special kind of qualities and
practices associated with each of these perfections. Are we going through
them later on? I also want to know why certain perfection is practiced in
each ground. Are the bodhisattvas in meditative equipoise? Are they not
practicing all these perfections? Why they are compartmentalized in each
ground? Or they just focus on that perfection on that ground and at the
same time they are still doing all the other perfections as well?
Answer: The discussion on the perfections will come later on when we look
at the deeds of the bodhisattvas as part of the path of the person of great
capacity in the Lamrim literature.
There is a perfection associated with each of the ten grounds, but it is
not that the bodhisattvas only practice generosity on the 1st ground, and
not doing the rest. The bodhisattvas on the 1st ground practice all the
perfections. It is just that their practice on the perfection of generosity
on the 1st ground becomes very developed, very special. The bodhisattvas’
practice of the perfection of generosity exceeds their practice of ethics
while on the 1st ground. When the bodhisattva moves on to the 2nd ground,
they achieved the perfection of ethics. On the 2nd ground, their practices
of ethics become very much developed, such that their practice of ethics
was not as developed when he was on the 1st ground. While they were on the
1st ground, the bodhisattvas do practice of ethics, but it is on the 2nd
ground that their practice of ethics becomes very developed and so forth.
When the bodhisattva reaches the 6th ground, their practice of wisdom on
that 6th ground becomes very developed and very special.
Let’s take the bodhisattva superior’s practice of the perfection of wisdom.
From the 1st to the 6th ground, the bodhisattva enters into meditative
equipoise when he meditates on emptiness.  Of course, in the meditative
equipoise he realizes emptiness directly. While on the 1st to the 5th
ground, the bodhisattva does not have the ability to enter into meditative
equipoise on emptiness and then arise from the meditative equipoise in an
instant.  An instant is like one finger-snap. On the 1st to the 5th
grounds, the bodhisattva cannot enter at will into meditative equipoise and
arise from that meditative equipoise in such a short time as a finger-snap;
i.e. he cannot in and out, in and out. But on the 6th ground, the
bodhisattva achieves the special practice of wisdom, the perfection of
wisdom, such that in an instant, he can enters into meditative equipoise
and in another instant he can arise from that meditative equipoise.
This is the explanation of how the bodhisattvas’ practice of wisdom is
extremely developed or special when they have achieved the 6th ground. So
on the 6th ground, in an instant, they can enter into meditative equipoise
and in another instant, and they can arise from their meditative equipoise.
For them, it is easy, whereas before they achieved the 6th ground, the
bodhisattvas find it difficult.
Basically the reason why there is a perfection that is related to each of
the ten grounds is to show that on those respective grounds the
bodhisattvas gain a very special kind of practice related to the perfection
of that ground. It is not that prior to the 1st ground, the bodhisattvas
are not practicing generosity.

As we have seen in the previous module that all the four Buddhist tenets
hold the view of selflessness to be something very important to understand
and to realize. All four Buddhist tenets are the same in asserting that one
must realize selflessness.
Why do all the Buddhist tenets hold the view of selflessness to be very
important?
Because all Buddhist tenets propound or assert that the root of samsara,
the root of all our problems, lies in our ignorance. Although the four
Buddhist tenets do differ as to what exactly this ignorance is, but they
all assert that it is ignorance that is the root of cyclic existence.
All Buddhist tenets assert that it is ignorance that is the root of all the
undesirable things, all the undesirable outcomes, all sufferings. All the
four Buddhist tenets assert that the sole antidote to this ignorance is to
realize selflessness. It is only the wisdom realizing selflessness that can
destroy our apprehension of a self which is the root of all the problems.
Therefore, realizing selflessness is extremely important.
Like what I have mentioned in the previous lesson, when we come to
understand for ourselves in our heart that the root of all the problems lie
in our ego grasping - the apprehension of a self- and on that basis, we
come to understand that the sole antidote and the only solution to this is
the wisdom realizing selflessness.
When you come to understand that our founder, Buddha Shakyamuni, has
revealed such a path of selflessness, and when you put all these together
and see for yourself, only then, you will develop this unparalleled faith
in the Buddha. On the basis of this, then you can see how different our
founder Buddha is, how he is in his class of his own, completely in a
different league compared to the founders of other religious or
philosophical systems. Only then, you will be able to really differentiate
between the Buddha and the founders of other traditions, philosophical
views and religions. If you look around, all the philosophies and religious
beliefs that are found in this world, it is only in Buddhism that the view
of selflessness is taught. There is no other religious tradition that talks
about no-self.
This is the reason why the Buddha has talked about the benefits of even
reading a sutra or a treatise that has selflessness as it subject matter.
The Buddha talked about the great benefits of even just listening, reading,
writing or explaining this to others. If you think about why there are such
benefits, it boils down to what we have just discussed - the view of
selflessness is the only antidote to the root of all our problems – the
apprehension of a self.
There are different quotations from the sutras that talked about the
benefits of listening to the teachings on emptiness. There is so much
merits that one accumulates just by listening to the teaching. On top of
that, if one reflects on the meaning on what one has heard, then the merit
that one accumulates is even greater than just listening to the teaching.
On top of that, if one is able to meditate or familiarize oneself with what
one has already ascertained from reflections, then of course the merits
that is derived from meditation on emptiness is even much greater.
The great Indian master Aryadeva says: “At the time of small merits, for
those who have small merits, with regards to this dharma, the teaching on
selflessness, one does not generate profound doubt. However, even if one
merely generates doubt, that doubt will tear apart cyclic existence”.
The doubt that the great Indian master Aryadeva was referring to is the
doubt that tends towards the fact.
Remember from previous module on Lorig, there are different kinds of doubt:
the doubt tending towards the factual? We are not even talking about having
an understanding or realization of emptiness, even you just start to
entertain doubt about emptiness, the doubt that tends towards factual, even
that can start to shake your entire samsara.
While there are all these benefits to be gained from learning and
reflecting on selflessness, and it is important to do this, one must
remember to do all these with the correct motivation. Having the right
motivation is extremely important. If you think about it, the main reason
why the Buddha gave all these different kinds of teaching, such as the
teaching on emptiness and so forth is to help us sentient beings to achieve
liberation from samsara, and to achieve full enlightenment. In order to
achieve liberation from samsara and to achieve full enlightenment, we have
to destroy our ignorance with the view of selflessness. Without the view of
selflessness, without understanding and realizing selflessness, there is no
way to destroy the afflictions. These are the methods for us to tame and to
destroy our afflictions. Therefore, we should keep this as the motivation
whenever we have the opportunity to learn, or even just to read, for
example, the Heart of the Perfection of Wisdom Sutra. The motivation should
be to be able to gain the understanding and realization of selflessness, so
that one can destroy the afflictions. In so doing, one achieves liberation
from samsara and full enlightenment. So it is extremely important to have a
pure motivation and a very extensive and big mind when studying such thing.

The Buddha taught selflessness in order to give us the antidote to destroy
our ignorance -an apprehension of a self- so that we can gain liberation
from suffering and full enlightenment. That was the Buddha’s main purpose.
Likewise, when we educate ourselves on emptiness, we learn, reflect, listen
and so forth, and it is extremely important to do so with a correct
motivation. The motivation has to be wanting to destroy our afflictions
with the view of selflessness so we can achieve liberation from samsara and
achieve full enlightenment. So it is important to have correct motivation
and big goal. If you think small, your motivation is small, your mind is
small, your perspective is small, and the result would be likewise small.
The actual sutra starts with stating the title or the name of the sutra.
The title of the sutra stated in Sanskrit is called the Bhagavati
Prajnaparamita Hridaya.
In many of the great treaties and in the Tibetan translation of the sutras,
the title of the text is usually first stated in Sanskrit. There are
different reasons for doing so. One of the reasons for doing so is that the
Buddha taught, for example, this Heart of the Perfection of Wisdom Sutra,
in Sanskrit language. So by expressing the title of the sutra in Sanskrit,
it is kind of like expressing the preciousness of this particular language,
Sanskrit.
In short, by expressing the title in Sanskrit, it places an imprint in the
readers’ mind to be able to know Sanskrit in the future and it is also a
cause for receiving blessing in the mind.
The title in Sanskrit is Bhagavati Prajnaparamita Hridaya.
When translated in Tibetan, Bhagavati is bcom ldan da.
In English, it is the Destroyer, fully endowed, gone beyond.
If you think of it in terms of gender, generally, Bhagavan is male, and
Bhagavati is female.

Paramita is perfection, literally it means gone to the other side.
One can look at gone to the other side from the perspective of the act of
going or from the perspective of the act of having arrived on the other
side.
 • If you look at gone to the other side from the perspective of the act of
   going, this is from the perspective of a learner, someone who is
   travelling towards full enlightenment.
 • If you look at gone to the other side from the perspective of the act
   having arrived, it means you are talking about someone who is a Buddha.
There is a discussion about whether perfection does exist on the buddha
ground or not.  Some say it does exist.
On the learner’s path, there is a discussion as to whether the perfections
exist or not.
From the perspectives of the CMWS, the conclusion is that you could say

If you look at the presentation of the paths and grounds, the perfections

There are different etymological divisions of the perfections of wisdom.
   1. The natural perfection of wisdom
   2. The textual perfection of wisdom
   3. The path perfection of wisdom
   4. The resultant perfection of wisdom
The natural perfection of wisdom is the emptiness of the mind.
You may have heard of the term ‘natural nirvana.’ You may also have heard
of the Tathagathagarbha (Tathagatha Essence). These are basically talking
about the emptiness of the mind.
The natural perfection of wisdom, refers to the emptiness of the mind, is
not the actual perfection of wisdom. It is just a nominal or an
etymologically division of the perfection of wisdom. The natural perfection
of wisdom while it is an etymological or nominal division of perfection of
wisdom, it is not an actual perfection of wisdom.
The text that has emptiness as its main subject matter is called the
textual perfection of wisdom.
The exalted knowers in the continuum of bodhisattva superiors are the path
perfection of wisdom. The path perfection of wisdom is an actual perfection
of wisdom.
The resultant perfection of wisdom is the omniscience wisdom.

Historically, after the Buddha passed away into paranirvana, it is said
that three councils were convened to compile the Buddha’s teachings. To
compile means to write it down from memory.
When we talk about the compilation of the Buddha’s teachings, there was the
compilation of the Hinayana teachings, but then we also have to talk about
the Mahayana teachings. That is a bit complicated.
What is commonly known now in the world about the compilation of the
Buddha’s teachings is it was an event compiling the Hinayana teachings, or
what is commonly known as the Pali Canon. The first compilation was done
one year after the Buddha passed away into paranirvana and the King
Ajatashatru acted as the sponsor. Many arhats came together to compile the
teachings, the primary ones being Ananda, Mahakashyapa and Upali. Ananda
was responsible for compiling the sutra pitaka, Mahakashyapa was
responsible for compiling the abhidharma pitaka and Upali was responsible
for compiling the vinaya pitaka. So that was the first council, the first
compilation of the Buddha’s teachings. The second compilation happened
about a hundred years after the Buddha passed away into paranirvana. The
main sponsor for the second compilation of the Buddha’s teachings was King
Ashoka.

who did it?

How the three scriptural collections are differentiated?
I think I have mentioned this quite a while ago.
 • The main subject matter of the vinaya pitaka is the higher training in
   ethics.
 • The main subject matter of the sutra pitaka is the higher training in
   concentration.
 • The main subject matter of the abhidharma pitaka is the higher training
   in wisdom.
The ancient translators of the past devised a system in order to help the
reader to identify which scriptural collection does the particular text
fall into. The distinction is made based on the object of homage.
 • If a text belongs to the vinaya pitaka, at the beginning of the
   translation of that particular text, you would find the homage being
   paid to the Omniscient One, “I prostrate to the Omniscient One”.
 • If the translated text belonged to the sutra pitaka, the homage will be
   paid to the buddhas and bodhisattvas.  So at the beginning of the text
   you will see this sentence “I prostrate to all the buddhas and
   bodhisattvas”.
 • If a text belonged to the abhidharma pitaka, then the beginning homage
   is paid to Manjughosha, “I prostrate to Manjughosha”.

The Tibetan translation of the word “Bhagavan” is dcom ldan das.
“dcom” means to destroy. As a noun then it is “destroyer” because the
Buddha is someone who has destroyed the afflictions and the knowledge
obscurations.
“ldan” literally means to possess. The Buddha is someone who has destroyed
all the unfavorable opposing factors, which in this case are the
afflictions together with the knowledge obscurations.  The Buddha is
endowed with or possesses all the good qualities.
‘das” literally means to pass beyond or to go beyond. But the original
Sanskrit word Bhagavan is not there literally but the meaning is implied.
Therefore, in the Tibetan translation “das” is there, which means to pass
beyond or to go beyond.
“To go beyond” means the Buddha is beyond the extreme of samsara and
nirvana. So the Buddha has gone beyond the extreme of samsara and extreme
of nirvana.
“das” can also mean the Buddha is superior to ordinary or to worldly gods.
Therefore, the Bhagavan refers to the perfect teacher.
