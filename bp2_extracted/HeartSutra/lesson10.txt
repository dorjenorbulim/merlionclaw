Student: Using the example of the very first uninterrupted path, how does
the uninterrupted path work to completely uproot the obscurations of that
particular path?
Ven Gyurme (summarized): How does a particular uninterrupted path remove
the afflictions of the respective level?
Answer:  The uninterrupted path of the Mahayana Path of Seeing is said to
be the direct antidote against the intellectually acquired afflictions and
its seeds. When we talk about intellectually acquired afflictions, perhaps
there are different varieties. So how does one uninterrupted path in one
meditative equipoise act as an antidote to these varieties of afflictions?
The answer could be this. Think about the practice of patience. If one has
a stable or good practice of patience, one will not just be patient to one
person and not the other, especially when the practice of patience
culminates in the Perfection of Patience. So, the person who has achieved
the Perfection of Patience is someone who has conquered anger in relation
to every single person. It is not that he has to practice with one enemy,
and then goes to the next enemy and so forth. It is not like that. In the
“Engaging in the Bodhisattva Deeds”, Shantideva, the great bodhisattva,
mentions that one could imagine the whole world is covered with thorns. If
one has to walk, it is not practical and probably not possible to eliminate
every single thorn before one start walking. However, if one put on a pair
of shoes, one can walk anywhere because one can just step over the thorns
and walk.
The power behind the 1st uninterrupted path of the Mahayana Path of Seeing,
which acts as an antidote to the intellectually acquired afflictions, does
not just happen suddenly. That power has been built up already starting
from the beginning of the Path of Preparation. There are four stages of the
Path of Preparation: heat, peak, forbearance and supreme mundane qualities.
On those paths, that bodhisattva is working hard in order to gain the power
or strength of the mind to overcome the manifest conception of true
existence. As he progresses through the Path of Preparation, the power of
his mind becomes stronger and more powerful as he goes along. As such, even
the manifest conception of true existence that is suppressed along these
four stages, increasingly becomes more subtle. Then comes the time when
that bodhisattva’s perception of emptiness becomes direct, that is when he
gains a very clear appearance of emptiness and sees emptiness directly
without any intervening mental image. That is the time where the mind
becomes powerful enough to destroy the intellectually acquired afflictions
and its seeds.
When this bodhisattva realizes emptiness directly, that realization is
completely free of all dualistic appearances. All dualistic appearances
subsided in the perspective of that wisdom. When this bodhisattva newly
attained the Path of Seeing, i.e. when he achieves the path of release of
the 1st ground, at that time he has completely abandoned the intellectually
acquired afflictions and its seeds. The uninterrupted path is the one that
does the abandoning. So this uninterrupted path is said to be the mind that
has the power to destroy the intellectually acquired afflictions.
The question is about the uninterrupted path of the Path of Seeing. This
particular uninterrupted path is the wisdom directly perceiving emptiness.
This wisdom realizing emptiness realizes one specific emptiness or one
emptiness related to a specific basis or realizes the emptiness that
pervades all phenomena.
We could only say that this is what happens on the path of release. In this
case the intellectually acquired afflictions are abandoned. About the
uninterrupted path, what else could we say about it other than it being a
mind powerful enough to destroy the intellectually acquired afflictions? As
to exactly what it is, it is not possible to explain because it is
something completely beyond the ordinary scope of mind. (It is like the
question being raised previously) what is the sign when one achieved the
path of release. It is not possible to really talk about it. I would think
that you will not be able to tell from looking at the external demeanor of
that person who is in meditative equipoise.
In the teaching, it gives an analogy to describe the uninterrupted path and
the path of release.
The uninterrupted path is like expelling the thief from the house.
The path of release is like having to close the door on him.
I guess all of us consider the Buddha’s teachings to be very sacred and
precious.
The Buddha’s teachings consist of the scriptural dharma and the
realizational dharma.
Of the two, the more important one is the realizational dharma.
But to have this realizational dharma, you first must depend on the
scriptural dharma. We have discussed on this before. Of all the
realizational dharma, the supreme one is said to be the view of the
ultimate nature of reality, i.e. having the correct view of reality or
emptiness. Having said this, you have to figure out for yourself why it is
so. It is said that of all the many realizational dharma, the supreme or
the highest is the wisdom realizing emptiness. You have to see for
ourselves, and you have to know the reason. You need to gain an
ascertainment that the mind realizing emptiness is the supreme or the
highest of all realizational dharma. In order to reach that kind of
ascertainment, you have to analyze over and over and over again.
We have talked about the different scriptural dharma. Remember that there
was an explanation of the benefit of studying, learning, reflecting and
meditating on the perfection of wisdom? In one sutra, it is stated that the
merit of learning, reflecting, meditating, and practicing the perfection of
wisdom far surpasses the merit of someone who fills the billion-fold world
system with the seven kinds of jewels and offer all of that to the buddhas.
 We have to think about this benefit over and over again.  Of course, in
order to understand the benefit, we first have to see the point why
hearing, reflecting and meditating on the perfection of wisdom has such
benefit. Ultimately, when we hear, study, and reflect on the teaching on
the perfection of wisdom, we are also learning about dependent origination/
dependent arising. By understanding that there are these benefits,
therefore the importance of learning, hearing, and reflecting on the
perfection of wisdom and then practicing it later, the effort will then
come. That aspiration and effort will come to want to actually study,
learn, analyze, and then meditate on the perfection of wisdom.
Whenever we have the opportunity to read or recite The Heart of the
Perfection of Wisdom Sutra, we have to think, reflect and meditate on it,
and we should remember why we are doing this. The motivation has got to be
about destroying our ignorance and the apprehension of a self. The Buddha
gave such a teaching in order to enable sentient beings like us to destroy
our ignorance and the apprehension of a self. With that in mind, when we
recite, reflect, learn or meditate on The Heart of the Perfection of Wisdom
Sutra conjoined with the thought that I am doing this in order to achieve
full enlightenment for all living beings, then the benefit will be very
extensive. So always carry a virtuous motivation and whenever we recite or
think about The Heart of the Perfection of Wisdom Sutra, we could also
think “I am doing this to plant imprint of emptiness in the mind”.
The Buddha spoke about the amazing benefits of hearing, reflecting and
meditating on emptiness. Just by hearing, learning, and studying on the
teachings on emptiness on the perfection of wisdom, we accumulate so much
merit. On top of just accumulating the data, if we process the data by
critically analyzing and thinking about it, then of course that merit will
supersede the merit that we get from just hearing the teachings on
emptiness. Then base on our critical analysis and thinking, whatever
knowledge or the meaning of emptiness that we have ascertained by depending
on mindfulness and introspection, then that merit would supersede the merit
that comes from just analyzing or reflecting on the perfection of wisdom.
We know the threefold activity of hearing, reflection and meditation. Let’s
say we are doing it and the object here is the perfection of wisdom, these
three activities do plant imprints on the mind, but there is a difference
in the quality and the stability of the imprints that are planted on the
mind.
The imprint that is planted on the mind through hearing alone is not long
lasting and is not stable.
The imprint that is planted on the mind through reflection produces certain
knowledge and this imprint comparatively is more stable and last a longer
time.
But the most important imprint that is planted on the mind is through
repeatedly familiarizing our minds with whatever ascertainment or
conviction we have gained over and over again.
Of the imprints that are planted by these three activities, it would be the
imprint that is planted on the mind through familiarizing, which we call it
meditation, that moves from life to life and that gives hope in the future
lives.
Studying, learning, and hearing is important, without which, there is
nothing to think about. If there is nothing to think and analyze, then
there is nothing to familiarize ourselves with at the end of the day. On
the basis of the data that is accumulated from hearing, and if we have to
work on through processing and analyzing them and then familiarize the mind
over and over again with whatever knowledge, understanding or conviction
that one has gained. That is where we need to put in the effort.  Please
think about the benefits and the merit that you can accumulate from
learning, reflecting and familiarizing or meditating on the perfection of
wisdom.
The sutra starts with the prologue.
Thus did I hear at one time. The Bhagavan was dwelling on Mass of Vultures
Mountain in Rajagriha together with a great community of monks and a great
community of bodhisattvas. At that time, the Bhagavan was absorbed in the
concentration on the categories of phenomena called “Profound Perception.”
When you recite this particular section of the sutra, you can bring to mind
that the Buddha is a special being. You can think about the qualities of
his exalted body, exalted speech and exalted mind. For it is this special
being who gave this very special teaching on the perfection of wisdom at
this particular time to this particular congregation, which is said to be a
perfect congregation, at a perfect place.
So, this is the idea that should come to your mind when you recite this
prologue. At this time, you bring to mind the Buddha and generate devotion.

Next, Shariputra is going to ask his questions to Avalokiteshvara.

upon the very practice of the profound perfection of wisdom and beheld
those five aggregates also as empty of inherent nature. Then, through the
power of Buddha, the venerable Shariputra said this to the bodhisattva
mahasattva arya Avalokiteshvara: “How should any son of the lineage train

At this point in time it is not so much about just focusing on Shariputra,
but you can imagine that you are the one who is asking the question. You

least you should have some queries.

Then Avalokiteshvara replied:
He said that and the bodhisattva mahasattva arya Avalokiteshvara said this
to the venerable Sharadvatiputra. “Shariputra, any son of the lineage or
daughter of the lineage who wishes to practice the activity of the profound
perfection of wisdom should look upon it like this, correctly and
repeatedly beholding those five aggregates also as empty of inherent

“correctly and repeatedly beholding those five aggregates also…” – here
“behold” is ta war in Tibetan which literally means to view (but not with
your eyes) those five aggregates also as empty of inherent nature.
Here the five aggregates are qualified by “also” - this means that not only
the “I” that is imputed in dependent on the basis of designation - the
aggregates- empty of existing inherently, the aggregates themselves also
are empty of existing inherently.
Having said that, what does it mean when we say the “I” is empty of
existing inherently?
What does it mean when we say the aggregates are empty of existing
inherently?
We have to reflect on this:  “I” is empty, aggregates are empty, empty of
what?
It is empty of inherent existence.
“correctly and repeatedly beholding those five aggregates also as empty of
inherent nature” is the brief explanation. The next few paragraphs are the
expanded explanation, which start with:
“Form is empty. Emptiness is form. Emptiness is not other than form; form
is also not other than emptiness. In the same way, feeling, discrimination,
compositional factors, and consciousness are empty.
In the commentary, this is said to be the training of the perfection of
wisdom in the Path of Accumulation and Path of Preparation.
It starts with the form aggregates: “Form is empty. Emptiness is form” but
it would be easier if we use “I”, the person or the self as the subject.
Rather than starting with form, we could reflect on: “I” is empty.
Emptiness is “I”. Emptiness is not other than “I”; “I” is also not other
than emptiness.
It is reflecting on the four features on the basis of “I”. It is said to be
easier than if we were to start with phenomena other than the “I”. After
you have done that, perhaps you could move on to the aggregates, which we
can start with the aggregate of form; “Form is empty. Emptiness is form.”
and so forth. After you have finished with form then you can move on to the
next aggregate. That is why it says; “In the same way, feeling…”, you
reflect on feeling, discrimination, compositional factors and then
consciousness.
We start with the self, the person, “I”; “I” is empty. Emptiness is ‘I’.
We recollect our past experiences of the sense of ‘I’ that we have. We
could bring to mind an experience we had of the sense of ‘I’ when we were
feeling very depressed, very down, feeling very useless and hopeless. That
is when we feel that the ‘I’ is so low. That is this sense of ‘I’ that feel
depressed. There are also times whereby we feel this ‘I’ is so elevated
especially during the time when there is manifest pride, the sense of ‘I’
is so overwhelming. Try to recollect that experience of those sense of ‘I’,
then analyze the ‘I’ that we grasp at during those times. Using the ‘I’ to
reflect on how that ‘I’ is empty and how emptiness is ‘I’.  If we can do
this meditation, it helps to pacify the very disturbed mind.
At the point when it says ‘I’ is empty, what we have to reflect or realize
is that those strong sense of ‘I’ that we have recollected, whether low or
high, at the end of the day what we have to understand is how this ‘I’
which we grasp at does not exist from the side of the body and mind. There
is nothing in the body and mind or in the collection of the body and mind
where you can point to such an ‘I’. So, the ‘I’ does not exist from the
side of the aggregates.
How do we think about ourselves when we have this sense of ‘I, the person?
How does it appear? It obviously appears.  There is an appearance of
ourselves to us and we believe that it exists in certain way. How it
appears for all of us is that the ‘I’ appears to exist from the side of the
body and mind. In simple English, it means the ‘I’ appears to be the body
and mind. From the body and mind, the ‘I’ is somewhere there, inside,
outside, basically somewhere there. Not only is there such an appearance,
we basically believe that is how the ‘I’ exist. We assent completely to the
appearance. We grasp and apprehend the ‘I’ to be like that. It is true, and
it is somewhere inside, it is there from the side of the body and mind.
What we have to understand and realize for ourselves is that the ‘I’ does
not exist from the side of the aggregates. The ‘I’ does not exist on the
aggregates.
We can come to understand that there is no ‘I’ on the aggregates, there is
no ‘I’ from the side of the aggregates. We can come to understand this by
depending on analogies. There are different analogies. Do you remember the
analogies of mistakenly of seeing a coil of rope as snake? There is an
appearance of a snake on the coil of rope, there is an appearance of a
snake from the side of the coil of rope under certain weather condition.
But the reality is that, when you look for the snake on the coil of rope,
you will not be able to find snake on the rope. You will not able to find
snake among the parts that make up the rope. There are different parts of
the rope and you will not be able to find a snake. The individual parts are
not the rope, and the collection of the whole coil of rope is not the
snake. So, exactly in the same way, the ‘I’ cannot be found on the
aggregates, the ‘I’ cannot be found among the aggregates, the ‘I’ does not
exist from the side of the aggregates. This is an example or an analogy to
help us to understand that the ‘I’ or self does not exist from the side of
the aggregates, which is the basis of designation.
We are trying to discover how the ‘I’ does not exist inherently. Whether we
say ‘I’ exist inherently or ‘I’ exist from its own side, that is the point.
We are trying to understand and see for ourselves how there is no ‘I’
existing from the side of the aggregates although there is such an
appearance and belief, but in reality is there is no ‘I’ from the side of
the aggregates. That is what we are trying to find out. So, using the
analogy is one way to help us to understand that. Another ways to
understand this is seeing how things that are dependently originated, based
on causes and conditions, are dependent, i.e., not independent and not
inherently existent. The Buddha said this in the sutras, and Nagarjuna also
says this. Therefore there is statement; “Those that are produced from
causes and conditions are not produced”.  What it means is that those that
are produced from causes and conditions are not inherently produced.
On the Path of Preparation, the realization of emptiness on the Path of
Preparation is not a direct realization. In that the mind realizing
emptiness does not see emptiness directly. Rather it still realizes
emptiness but it does so through an intervening image; technically it is
called the meaning generality of emptiness. It is only on the Path of
Seeing that the individual can realize emptiness directly without any
intervening meaning generality. The training on the perfection of wisdom on
the Path of Seeing is indicated by the next paragraph:
Shariputra, likewise, all phenomena are emptiness; without characteristic;
unproduced, unceased; stainless, not without stain; not deficient, not
fulfilled.
Here it talks about the eight characteristics or the eight features of
emptiness.
“all phenomena are emptiness” - this is talking about the nature or entity
of all phenomena. The ultimate nature of all phenomena is emptiness. On top
of the nature of all phenomena being empty of inherently existence, the
causes of phenomena themselves do not exist inherently. The effects are
produced but there are not inherently produced. There is no inherent
production. In short, not only the ultimate nature of phenomena is
emptiness but the causes and effects themselves are also not existing
inherently.
Here it is talking about the thinking of the emptiness of inherently
existing entities, causes and results.
Shariputra, therefore, in emptiness there is no form, no feeling, no
discrimination, no compositional factors, no consciousness;
This indicates the emptiness of inherently existence of the five
aggregates. Five aggregates basically do not exist inherently. So, it is
showing their emptiness.
no eye, no ear, no nose, no tongue, no body, no mind; no visual form, no
sound, no odor, no taste, no object of touch, and no phenomenon
This is explaining the emptiness of the twelve sources.
There is no eye element and so on up to and including no mind element and
no mental consciousness element.
This is showing the emptiness of the eighteen constituents/ elements. What
manifestly shown is the six sense powers and the six consciousnesses. What
is implied in the meaning? The six objects are also implied in the meaning.

This whole paragraph here is talking about the training of the perfection

I was asked about what is “bodhisattva mahasattva”?
“Bodhisattva” - “bodhi” refers to enlightenment and the great
enlightenment.
So, bodhisattva is an aspirant to enlightenment. Someone who aspires is an
aspirant, so aspirant to enlightenment. What this person is aspiring to is
the dharmakaya which is what he sees as something that needs to be
obtained. The object that needs to be achieved or obtained is the truth
body- the dharmakaya. So, from that perspective he is a bodhisattva because
he is aspired to that.
It is kind of explaining, not the definition but the play of the word
“bodhisattva”.
“Mahasattva” - because he aspires to full enlightenment for the benefits of
all other sentient beings, therefore he is a mahasattva.
“Sattva” is loosely translated as being. So, he is a great being, he is a
great mahasattva. He is a mahasattva because he aspires to that
enlightenment for others.
It is kind of looking at the entomology on the words “bodhisattva” and
“mahasattva.”
• Because he aspires to the dharmakaya in order to accomplish his own
  purpose, he is a bodhisattva.
• Because he aspires to this enlightenment also to accomplish the welfare
  of others, he is called a mahasattva.
