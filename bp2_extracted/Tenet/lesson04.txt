In general, how do we know whether something exists or not? If the
particular object in question is suitable to be realized or known by valid
cognizer then that object is an existent. That is what certified or
determined whether something exists or not. If it can be realized by a
valid cognizer then it is an existent.
According to the first of the four Buddhist tenets, GES, existents/things
are divided into two:
   1. Permanent things
   2. Impermanent things
It is only the Proponents of the Great Exposition who assert permanent
things. The other proponents of the Buddhist tenets do not assert permanent
things. The GES’ understanding of things is pretty much similar to the rest
of the other Buddhist tenets. How do they define a thing?
The definition of thing is that which is able to perform a function.
In that sense, the definition is the same. But the proponents of GES go one
step further by saying that permanent things can perform function as well.
The illustrations of permanent things according to GES are given at the
bottom of page 2:
Examples of permanent things are uncompounded space, analytical cessations,
and non-analytical cessations.
According to GES, uncompounded space is an illustration of permanent thing.
Uncompounded space is permanent because it doesn’t undergo momentary
change. According to this school, uncompounded space is also a thing.
A thing is that which is able to perform a function, so according to the
GES, the uncompounded space performs the function of allowing movement to
take place because there is space. So this uncompounded space performs a
function of allowing things to be moved within.
Analytical cessations are permanent cessations or abandonment any of the
afflictions that comes through the force of the correct antidote; which is
the wisdom directly perceiving selflessness.
In this school, the analytical cessations are things.
You must remember the definition of things: that which is able to perform a
function.
According to GES, analytical cessations are permanent things; which mean
they are also able to perform a function. Why? Analytical cessations
perform the function of ceasing or stopping the afflictions; i.e. once the
afflictions are abandoned they are prevented from occurring again. That is
the function of analytical cessations.
Non-analytical cessations are also ceasing or stopping of something. But
this factor of abandonment/cessation does not come about through the force
of an antidote, but rather comes about through the temporal absence of the
condition for the production of that particular phenomenon.
The horn of a goat doesn’t grow on our head because of the absence of the
causes for its production.
The fact that the horn of a goat will not grow on our head is not due to
the force of an antidote that ensure its complete non-production or
whatsoever. It is just because, for now, of the absence of the causes for
its production on our heads. So that factor of the non-production of the
goat horn on our head is the non-analytical cessations.
There are also temporary abandonments or cessations of afflictions due to
achieving the concentration of the form and formless realm through
cultivating and meditating on the aspect of coarseness: by looking at the
level beneath it as coarse and by looking at the next level as more fine.
When one’s mind is at the concentration of the form and formless realm, one
abandons the afflictions temporary at the respective levels. That temporary
abandonment of the afflictions is not due to the force of the antidotes but
due to an absence of the condition for the afflictions to manifest or
arise.
My purpose of explaining this to you is for you to understand why only this
Buddhist tenet asserts that there are permanent things. As long as you
understand why and where they are coming from, then I think that is fine
and is enough.
According to the Proponents of the Great Exposition, all phenomena are
substantially established. Why do they have this world view? Because for
them, when they look at things or events or anything that exist, there must
be something from the side of the object i.e. there is substance behind
every phenomenon. That is why we talk about substantiality. According to
this school, without this substantiality from the side of the object then
things cannot exist. Therefore for them, anything that exists is
necessarily substantially established. This is the world view of the GES;
anything that exist, it is substantially established.
The next tenet, SS, the one above that, MOS, and the one above that, AMWS,
all of them do not assert that all phenomena are substantially established.
For the SS, MOS and AWMS, to be substantially established means that the
phenomenon in question must first be a composed phenomenon or impermanent
phenomenon; whereas in the GES, everything is necessarily substantially
established.
Do you see the progression of the views? Here, the first school, GES, for
them everything, whether permanent or impermanent, every phenomena is
necessarily substantially established.
Then what about the next school, the SS, then the MOS, then the AMWS?
For the next three schools, SS, MOS and AMWS, not everything is
substantially established; only impermanent/ composed phenomena are
substantially established.
For the highest school, the CMWS, it does not matter whether it is an
impermanent phenomenon or a permanent phenomenon, all phenomena are not
substantially established.
This should become clearer and clearer as we move along.
Earlier on, in this school we saw how object of knowledge or existent, are
divided into permanent and impermanent things.
Another way of dividing object of knowledge or existent is into
conventional truths and ultimate truths.
Another way of dividing things is into:
 1. conventional truths and
 2. ultimate truths.
The definition of the conventional truth is as Vashubandhu’s  Treasury of
knowledge, (Skt. Abidharmakosha)
The definition of a conventional truth is: a phenomenon such that an
awareness apprehending it is cancelled if it is broken up or mentally
separated into its individual parts.
According to GES, an illustration of the conventional truth is a vase. Why
a vase is a conventional truth?
A vase is made up of many parts and many factors/causes coming together to
make a vase. When the causes and conditions come together then a vase is
established. In that sense, its existence is imputed. If the vase is
destroyed, smashed or broken, then the valid cognizer apprehending that
vase ceases to exist.
A vase is not an enduring thing. It is not there all the time. In that
sense, it is not an ultimate i.e. is not real - the truth. Therefore, the
vase is conventional truth. The vase is an imputed existent.
According to this school, conventional truth and imputed existent are
synonymous.
Whether you call it a conventional truth or imputed existent, it has the
same meaning. It is good to understand the meaning of conventional in this
context and the reason why it is an imputed existent.
The vase is an existent that comes about because of the gathering of many
factors. It has to be something that is a flat-base bulbous.
Any other phenomenon whose mode of existence is different from the
definition of the conventional truth is an ultimate truth; as the
Abidharmakosha said; “Other exist as ultimate truth”.
There are many examples given in the text to illustrate the conventional
truths. One example is a mala. Assuming a mala has one hundred and eight
beads. It is only when all the one hundred and eight beads come together
and is strung together then you have a mind apprehending a mala (rosary).
If you were to start to remove each and every single beads, at the end what
you are left is just a string and the mind apprehending the rosary will be
cancelled.
Another illustration of a conventional truth of this school is a forest. We
do not call a single tree as forest. It is only when there are many trees
located together in a specific geographical location then we call it a
forest. Likewise, an army is made up of many soldiers. We don’t call one
soldier as an army. Army is composed of many soldiers. When you eliminate
all the trees or all the soldiers then at the end what is left is not a
forest and is not an army. Then the mind that apprehending forest or the
mind that apprehending army does not exist anymore.
The point is that you have to understand the GES’s understanding of the
conventional truths and ultimate truths. The illustrations are meant to
help.
The definition of an ultimate truth is: A phenomenon such that an  awareness
apprehending it is not cancelled if it is broken up  or  mentally  separated
into its individual parts.
Illustrations [of ultimate truths] are, for example, directionally  partless
particles, temporally partless [moments of] consciousness, and  uncompounded
phenomena.
An illustration of the ultimate truth is a directionally partless particle;
the smallest possible particle in existent. According to this school, the
smallest particle is directionally partless and you cannot make it any
smaller. To them this thing is the smallest thing that you can point to and
it cannot go any smaller. Therefore it is real and it is an ultimate truth.

This directionally partless particle is the smallest possible thing in
existence. According to them, you cannot do anything to it and you cannot
destroy it. It is always be there. Therefore for them, it is an ultimate
truth.
Nobody can do anything to destroy this directional partless particle. That
means it will always exist. If it exists then necessarily there will be a
valid cognizer apprehending it. The mind apprehending it cannot be
cancelled in any way.  So going by the definition of the ultimate truths,
this makes the directionally partless particle an ultimate truth.
In this school, as opposed to an imputed existent, an ultimate truth is the
same as saying substantially existent.
Other illustrations of an ultimate truth are temporally partless moments of
consciousness, and uncompounded phenomena. The reason why they are ultimate
truth is similar to what I have explained.
Uncompounded space is permanent, it would never undergo change, and it is
stable. As it is always there, then there will be a mind apprehending
uncompounded space cannot be cancelled. So it is the truth. It is real and
it is true.

If you remember our earlier discussion on what defines a proponent of
Buddhist tenets, a proponent of Buddhist tenets is a Buddhist who accepts
the four seals. The four seals are the four things that attest that a
particular doctrine is Buddhist. The four seals are:
   1) All composed phenomena are impermanent.
   2) All contaminated things are miserable
   3) All phenomena are empty and selfless
   4) Nirvana is peace
All proponent of Buddhist tenets assert that there isn’t a self that is
permanent, unitary and independent.  So in interpreting the third seal “All
phenomena are empty and selfless, most scholars would say that statement is
talking about the emptiness or the lack of a permanent, unitary and
independent self.
While there are many scholars who assert the third seal “All phenomena are
empty and selfless” is referring to the lack of permanent, unitary,
independent self, there are some who assert that the third seal has to be
understood in terms of the lack of a self-sufficient, substantially
existent person.
The reason why there is this differing interpretation of the third seal has
got to do with the subdivisions of the GES. Under GES, there are four main
schools which when further divided, there are eighteen subdivisions.
Seventeen out of the eighteen divisions assert the selflessness of person.
One of the subdivisions known as the follower of Vatsiputra, asserts a self
of person.
In some sutra, the Buddha actually said in some occasions that there is a
self of a person. Therefore, there are some followers who felt the need to
assert a self of person. These particular followers assert that there is a
self-sufficient substantially existent person. So the Vatsiputra assert a
self of a person in that the self or the person is self-sufficient
substantially existent. Nevertheless, this Vatsiputran do not assert that
there is a self that is permanent, unitary and independent.
Following from the assertion of these Vatsiputran, some scholars therefore
said that Vatsiputran are not Buddhist from the perspective of the view.
Therefore some scholars assert that these Vatsiputran are not Buddhist
because their view is not Buddhist as they assert a self-sufficient
substantially existent person.
However there are other scholars assert that Vatsiputran are Buddhist even
from the world view because they don’t assert a self that is permanent,
unitary and independent.
If you understand the third seal “All phenomena are empty and selfless” to
mean that there isn’t a permanent, unitary and independent self, then these
Vatsiputran fit nicely, so they are Buddhist.
Therefore we say that proponents of Buddhist tenets is someone who asserts
the four seal and the third seal “All phenomena are empty and selfless” has
to be understood as saying that the permanent, unitary and independent self
doesn’t exist.
As to what is a self of a person versus the selflessness of person, and in
this context, what exactly does it mean by a self that is permanent,
unitary and independent, what exactly is a selflessness of person, what is
the meaning of the emptiness of a self-sufficient substantially existent

• All things are necessarily substantially established, but  they  are  not
  necessarily substantially existent.
• If something is substantially existent, this does not necessitate that it
  is a substance, since impermanence is substantially existent  but  not  a
  substance. Rather, to be substantially existent means (in accordance with
  the definition of an ultimate truth given below) that when the object  is
  either broken physically or mentally divided into parts, the awareness of
  that object is not cancelled.
• Ultimate truths and substantial existents are mutually inclusive.
• Conventional truths and imputed existents are mutually inclusive
• The definition of a conventional truth is: a phenomenon which is such
  that when it is broken up or mentally separated into individual parts,
  the awareness apprehending that object is cancelled.
Illustrations of conventional truths:
A clay pot -  if  a  clay  pot  is  broken  with  a  hammer,  the  awareness
apprehending that object as a clay pot is cancelled
-A rosary  -  if  the  beads  of  a  rosary  are  separated,  the  awareness
apprehending that object as a rosary is cancelled.
• The definition of an ultimate truth is: a phenomenon which is such that
  when it is broken up or mentally separated into individual parts, the
  consciousness apprehending that object is not cancelled.
Illustrations of ultimate truths are directionally partless particles,
temporally partless moments of consciousness, and uncompounded space.
• In this system, the smallest units of matter are asserted to be
  directionally partless, but this does not mean that they are partless in
  general; even the smallest particle has factors relating to its
  production, duration, and cessation and factors relating to the
  production of effects, and so forth.
• Similarly, the smallest temporal unit of consciousness is temporally
  partless but is not partless in general; for example, one instant of an
  eye consciousness can have many parts that apprehend the various colors
  of a tile floor.
