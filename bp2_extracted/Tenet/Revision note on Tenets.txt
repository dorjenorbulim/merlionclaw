Reasons for studying tenets:  To understand the subtle meaning of dependent
arising.
As mentioned in Lama Tsongkhapa’s Three Principles Aspects of the Path:
  [9] Without the wisdom realizing ultimate reality,
  Even though you have generated renunciation and the mind of enlightenment

  You cannot cut the root cause of circling.
  Therefore, attempt the method to realize dependent arising.
Lama Tsongkhapa says: Without understanding the subtle meaning of dependent
arising we cannot cut the root of our suffering in samsara.
In the study of tenets, one will be looking at the assertions of the four
Buddhist tenets. Each has their own presentations of selflessness. As one
progresses up the hierarchy, one will come to understand how the
explanations of selflessness become increasingly subtle.
Etymology for tenet - in Tibetan: grub mtha - an established conclusion.
-grub (established) means a thesis that is decided upon and established in
reliance upon scriptural and/or reasoning from the perspective of one’s own
mind. Even though from one's own perspective such a decision is correct,
nevertheless, this does not mean that this is true in reality.
-mtha (conclusion) - means one have concluded, “It is liked that and
nothing else.”
Therefore, tenet is a thesis that is decided upon and established in
reliance upon scripture and/ or reasoning and, from the perspective of
one's mind, will not be forsaken for something else.

What exactly is a person who propounds Buddhist tenets?
Person who possesses three distinguishing characteristics:
1) Teacher –they have a teacher who has extinguished all faults and
completed all good qualities.
2) Teachings – their teachings are not harmful to any sentient being.
3) View –they assert the view that the self is empty of being permanent,
unitary and independent.
Definition: A person who propounds Buddhist tenets: is a person propounding
tenets who accepts the Three Jewels to be the final objects of refuge and
who does not assert any objects of refuge other than them.
Another way to define a person who propounds Buddhist tenets: A person who
asserts the Four Seals.

Detailed explanations of the Four Seals
1. All compounded phenomena are impermanent
“Compounded” indicates the characteristic of the phenomenon, whose
existence comes into being in dependence on the coming together of various
causes and conditions. Therefore, the phenomenon is subjected to change. So
if the causes and conditions for the coming together of any phenomenon do
not gather, then this particular phenomenon will not arise.
Likewise, for the phenomenon to go out of existence, the causes and
conditions for its destruction/ going out of existence must also gather.
Without which, that phenomenon will continue to exist.
‘Impermanent' indicates that compounded phenomena undergo momentary change
at each single moment of time.

2. All contaminated things are suffering
Phenomena can be divided into:
   1. Contaminated phenomena
   2. Uncontaminated phenomena
“Contaminated” refers to the afflictions.
“Contaminated phenomenon” is a phenomenon that is under the influence of
the afflictions. Such a contaminated phenomenon is miserable.
Uncontaminated phenomena are not suffering/ not miserable.
Can contaminated phenomena, which are suffering, be eliminated?
This important question leads us to the third seal - “All phenomena are
empty and selfless”.

3. All phenomena are empty and selfless
As long as a phenomenon arises through the influence of the afflictions, it
is miserable.
Where does this anger and attachment arise from?
If we do analyze, anger and attachment arises from an apprehension of a
self.
Can this apprehension of a self be eliminated?
Two factors to consider whether such an apprehension of a 'self' can be
eliminated:
1. Does the 'self', that is apprehended by one's erroneous mind, exists or
not?
2. Is there any 'antidote' to such an apprehension of a 'self'?
Through analysis, one should realize that an apprehension of a 'self' is an
erroneous way of apprehension.
Such a mind is a wrong consciousness; which is a knower that engages in its
object erroneously.
While in reality, such a 'self' does not exists, this apprehension of a
'self' believes that such a 'self' exists.
Since such an apprehension is a wrong consciousness in reality, therefore,
there must be another mind whose mode of apprehension is the complete
opposite of such an apprehension.
Such a mind is the wisdom that realizes selflessness; which is a valid
cognizer.
It is a valid mind because, this wisdom realizing selflessness realizes
that there is no such 'self' in reality.

4. Nirvana is peace
When one is able to eradicate the apprehension of 'self' (ego-grasping),
which is the root of all of one's problems and sufferings, then sufferings
and problems will not arise anymore, there is liberation from all
sufferings. Then one will have a peaceful state/ nirvana instead.
Therefore, nirvana is not established from outside of oneself, it is
realized right within the basis of one's mind.
Since all phenomena are empty, therefore nirvana is peace.
All phenomena are selfless, what is the self that does not exist?
‘Selfless’ means that it is absent of a particular 'self', which refers to
a permanent, unitary and independent 'self'.
All Buddhist tenets accept that such a 'self', a permanent, unitary and
independent self, also known as soul, does not exist whatsoever
• Non-Buddhist tenet systems are not asserting such a 'self' without
  intellectual analysis nor asserting it with mere belief. They do have
  their own reasons for such a conclusion.
• Among them, there are some non-Buddhist also assert reincarnation [like
  the Buddhist]. Therefore, they also assert something that transmigrates
  from life to life. Obviously, they would not assert that it is the body
  that transmigrates, as this is very clear that it is left behind.
• Permanent: They assert the self or soul that transmigrates from life to
  life must be something that is completely non-dependent on and unrelated
  to the body. The self is also unchanging in the sense of being the same
  'soul' that transmigrates from life to life. Therefore, they assert the
  'self' as permanent in the sense of not changing.
• Unitary: They assert the 'self' as unitary/ single in the sense of not
  being constituted by parts. Therefore, is partless.
• Independent: They assert the ‘self’ is independent of all causes and
  conditions
• Conclusion: The reason that these non-Buddhist systems assert a 'self'
  that is unrelated to the body is because they do not know how to posit a
  self that is of the same entity with the body. Therefore, they assert the
  'self' has to be permanent, unitary and independent.
How to identify a Buddhist proponent of tenets and the reasons for this:
• A proponent of Buddhist tenet is a Buddhist who accepts the four seals
  which attest that any particular doctrine is Buddhist.
• In the third seal: All phenomena are selfless. All proponents of Buddhist
  tenets accept that there is not a 'self' that is permanent, unitary and
  independent.
• Most scholars accept that the third seal refers to the emptiness/ lack of
  permanent, unitary and independent 'self'.
• However, some scholars understood this as the emptiness/ lack of self-
  sufficient, substantially existent person.
• The difference in the two interpretations of the third seal has to do
  with the subdivisions of the GES which is divided into four main schools.
  When this is further divided, there are eighteen.
• Seventeen out of these sub-schools assert selflessness of persons,
  however, some texts state that the Vasiputriyans assert a self of person.
  This is because in some sutras, Buddha actually did assert a self of
  person. Due to this, the Vasiputriyans understood this literally and
  thereby assert a self of person, thus asserting that a self-sufficient,
  substantially existent person exists.
• Nevertheless, all Buddhist tenets, including the Vasiputriyans, do not
  assert a self that is permanent, unitary and independent.
• Therefore, if one were to understand the third seal to mean an emptiness
  of a permanent, unitary and independent self, then this would also
  include the Vasiputriyans as Buddhist proponents of tenets.
• Therefore, a proponent of Buddhist tenets is one who asserts the four
  seals, with the understanding that the third seal to mean that a
  permanent, unitary and independent self does not exist.

What are self-knowers and other-knowers?
• Consciousnesses is clear and knowing, and it can be analyzed in terms of
  other-knowers and self-knowers:
• Within a single collection of consciousness, there is a part of the
  consciousness that knows an object other than itself (other-knower) while
  there is another part of the consciousness that knows itself (self-
  knower).
• Self-knower is that which has the aspect of an apprehender, is free from
  conceptuality, and is non-mistaken.
• For example, an eye consciousness apprehending blue: within a single
  collection of this mind.
• For self-knower, there is a part of it that is knows itself.
• An other-knower is a consciousness that engages an object other than an
  internal consciousness. It is a consciousness directed outward.
• For other-knower, there is also another part of this same mind to which
  the object 'blue' is appearing clearly and to which this eye
  consciousness is cognizant of.
• GES do not assert self-knowers. They do not accept that the consciousness
  has the ability to see itself clearly, to be able to apprehend itself.
  They assert that consciousnesses are only other-knowers.

What is an external object?
An external object is a coarse object that is formed through the
aggregations of subtle atoms and particles.
These form a coarse object which is then suitable to be apprehended by a
sense power, which becomes a condition for the production a sense
consciousness that apprehends the coarse object.
GES asserts truly established external objects; i.e. external objects to
exist, and the external objects to be truly established.
Therefore, they assert that an external object has to exist before the
consciousness apprehending it can be generated.
What is 'truly established'?
They assert objects to be truly established because anything that exists,
all external phenomena, has an independent substance [substantial entity]
right there. Its existence has nothing to do with the mind imputing it.
This is because they cannot explain how the mind can have a role in the
existence of an object. They are unable to posit that it is the
characteristic of phenomena that can exist by being imputed by
consciousness.

There are three divisions (made mainly due to geographical locations): –
1. Proponents of the Great Exposition from Kashmir,
2. Proponents of the Great Exposition from the Western Region (Skt.
Aparantakas) and
3. Proponents of the Great Exposition from the Central Region (Skt.
Magadhas).

3 Etymology
Why are they called ‘Proponents of Particulars’?
They are called such because they propound tenets through following
[Vasumitra’s] Great Exposition of Particulars (Skt. Mahavibhasa) or,
alternatively, they are called Proponents of Particulars because they
propound the three times to be particulars [i.e., instances] of substances.
• A set of teachings called 'Seven Treatises of Knowledge', which are not
  the actual speech of Buddha but are composed by his chief disciples,
  Arhats Shariputra and Mudgalyayana. GES take these teachings to be the
  words of Buddha.
• Vasumitra's Great Exposition of Particulars, which comprises one hundred
  thousand verses, is essentially a commentary on the Seven Treatises of
  Knowledge. Vasubandhu condensed the essential meanings of the Great
  Exposition of Particulars, and composed the Treasury of Knowledge
  (Abhidharmakośa).
• So the reasons why GES are called 'Proponents of Particulars' is because
  they follow the Great Exposition of Particulars.
• Another reason they are called such is because they assert the three
  times to be [instances of substantial entities]:
• Composed phenomena have the factors of production, abiding and
  disintegration. For them, these factors are related to the three times,
  which are instances of substantial entities.
What 'instances of substantial entities' mean?
Example: a golden cup is essentially made of gold, which can be melted and
shaped into a plate, which can again be melted and shaped into a statue.
So the entity of the golden cup has changed into a golden plate. The entity
of the golden plate later changed into a statue. While these changes take
place, its substantial entity/ substance of being gold has not changed.
What 'three times to be instances of substantial entities' mean?
Example: the three times of a sprout: a past sprout, a present sprout and a
future sprout.
• The sprout has changed in the course of these three times, each is not
  the other.
• Even though that is the case, a past sprout, a present sprout and a
  future sprout is nevertheless a sprout, the substantial entity of it
  being a sprout has not changed.
 1) A future sprout is posited at the time of the seed [and thus exists at
 the time of the seed].
 2) When the seed germinates, a [present] sprout arises.
 3) When the [present] sprout matures and becomes a tree which bears fruit,
 the [present] sprout ceases to exist and thus becomes and exists as a past
 sprout. At the time of the fruit there is no more [present] sprout.
• Even though the entity or nature of the sprout has changed, its
  substantial entity as a sprout has not.
• To the GES, the substance [substantial entity] of a sprout exists in all
  three times.
Example: A young girl can be said as a future mother. In the future, she
becomes a [present] mother when she conceives. When she passes away, she
becomes a past mother.
• Just like the substance/ [substantial entity] of a sprout which exists in
  the past, present and future, likewise, the substantial entity of a
  mother exists in the past, present and future as well.
• Therefore, one who propounds the three times to be particulars of
  substance [substantial entity] is a proponent of Particulars.
Example: GES maintain that:
- a pot exists even at the time of the past of a pot
- a pot exists even at the time of the future of a pot
Yesterday’s pot exists today as a past pot. The past of a thing occurs
after its present existence, i.e. after its present existence has passed
Tomorrow’s pot exists today as a future pot. The future of a thing occurs
before its present existence, i.e. when its present existence is yet to be.
Today’s pot exists as a present pot today

4 Way of asserting objects
The definition of thing is: that which is able to perform a function.
Thing, existent, and object of knowledge are equivalent.
There are two types of things:
  1. permanent things and
  2. impermanent things.

• In general, to know whether something exists or not, that object is
  necessarily suitable to be realized by a valid cognizer. This is how an
  object is verified as an existent.
• According to GES, existent is divided into two: permanent things and
  impermanent things.
• Only GES assert permanent things. All other Buddhist tenets do not assert
  permanent phenomena as things.
• GES, in the same manner as all other Buddhist tenets, assert things that
  which is able to perform a function.
• However, GES go further to assert that permanent things can perform
  functions as well.
➢ Uncompounded space is permanent because it does not undergo momentary
  change. According to GES, it is also a thing, because it is able to
  perform the function of allowing movement to occur within it.
➢ Analytical cessations are permanent cessations/ abandonment of any
  afflictions through the force of the wisdom directly realizing
  selflessness. GES assert analytical cessations to be a thing, able to
  perform the function of ceasing the afflictions. Once the affliction is
  abandoned, it is prevented from ever occurring again.
➢ Non-analytical cessations are also factors of abandonment/ cessations.
  However, these abandonments do not occur through the force of the
  ultimate antidote of the wisdom realizing selflessness, rather, these
  occur through the temporal absence of the conditions for the production
  of the phenomena. For example: the horns of a goat will not grow on the
  head of a human, due to the absence of the causes for their production.
  This is not due to the force of an antidote that ensures its complete non-
  production, but merely because, for now, there is an absence for the
  causes for its production on human heads.
➢ Another example of a non-analytical cessation: temporal abandonment/
  cessation of afflictions due to attaining the concentrations of the form
  and formless realms through cultivating the mundane path bearing aspects
  of calmness of higher realms and coarseness of the realm below it. When
  one's mind attains the respective concentrations of these realms, one has
  abandoned the afflictions temporarily. Such temporal abandonment of
  afflictions is not due to the force of the antidote of the wisdom
  realizing selflessness], but merely due to an absence of the conditions
  for the afflictions to manifest.

Why a vase is a conventional truth?
• A vase is conventional (Tib. kundzob) because its existence depends on
  the aggregation of many factors.
• When these factors come together, a causally concordant phenomenon comes
  into being. Since that is the case, it is therefore true, established and
  a truth. For this reason, a vase is a conventional truth.
• When one conceives a vase, one is essentially doing so in terms of its
  shape. Therefore, if it is broken up, the shape that makes it a vase is
  no more. The mind which conceives of it as a vase is cancelled or ceases.
• Example of a 'mala' comprising 108 beads: it is only when all the beads
  are strung together that there would be a mind apprehending rosary. When
  one removes each one of them, towards the end, one would be left with
  merely the string. Therefore, the mind apprehending the rosary would be
  cancelled.
• Example of  'a forest': one does not call a lone tree a 'forest'.
  However, when many trees are located together in a geographic location,
  one would then call it a 'forest'.
• Example: An army is made up of many soldiers. One does not call a lone
  soldier an 'army'.
• When one conceptually eliminates the trees or the soldiers, at the end,
  what is left is no longer a forest nor an army. The mind apprehending
  forest or army does not exist anymore.
Subtler example: Water in a cup: According to the Buddhist presentation of
physical reality, water is essentially an aggregation of eight different
particles – particles of earth, water, fire and wind; together with form,
smell, taste and touch. If one were to individually isolate these
particles, conceptually removing its smell, taste, tactility and so on, at
the end, one will find there is no water left. Then the mind apprehending
water as a conventional truth is cancelled.
Directionally partless particles essentially refer to the smallest
[indivisible] evolute of form, which is the basic building block of matter.
As it is the smallest possible form, it cannot be physically destroyed, nor
can one even conceptually break it down further into smaller forms. It is
autonomous by itself without the need to depend on anything else. Since it
does not depend on other factors, it is not conventional. Thus for them, it
is not a conventional truth but an ultimate truth.
For consciousness, there are temporally partless moments of consciousness:
the consciousness existing within the smallest moment of time is also an
ultimate truth, as it cannot be mentally broken down further into smaller
moments of consciousnesses.
Uncompounded space is permanent, not undergoing change, due to it being
never destroyed, therefore it is an ultimate truth.
• An ultimate truth is not necessarily permanent.
• A conventional truth is not necessarily impermanent.
 Are directionally partless particle impermanent or permanent?
• The time it takes for a healthy person to snap a finger is asserted by
  GES to be divided into 120 parts. A single part is the smallest unit of
  time. This smallest unit of time is asserted to be an ultimate truth, any
  other units that are greater than this unit is a conventional truth.
• Therefore, this shows that, even though the smallest unit of time is an
  ultimate truth, it is still undergoing momentary change, as this specific
  unit does not last longer than its time, it is impermanent.
• Likewise, the pastness of this particular unit of time, due to it having
  ceased to exist, is a conventional truth, nevertheless, since it no
  longer undergoes momentary change, it is permanent.
• Even though a generality pervades its instances, however, a generality is
  not necessarily its instances
• The mind apprehending a directionally partless particle can never be
  cancelled. This is because it is the smallest matter which can never be
  destroyed by anything. Since it cannot be destroyed, then the mind
  apprehending it also cannot be cancelled. Therefore, this makes the
  directionally partless particle an ultimate truth.
One way to divide an existence is into conventional and ultimate truths.
However, if it is an existent, is it necessarily one these two truths?
Not necessarily. This is because, if an existent is an ultimate truth, then
it follows that it is also a conventional truth. If that is the case, then
this would incur two fallacies:
a) There would be a common locus between an ultimate truth and a
conventional truth, or,
b) an existent would be both an ultimate truth and a conventional truth.]
Difference between established base and ultimate truth:
• If it is an established base, it is necessarily an existent,
  substantially established, [phenomena, object, thing, object of
  knowledge, truly established, substantial entity, substance.]
• However, whatever is an established base/ substantially established/
  truly established, is not necessarily substantially existent/ ultimately
  established/ substantially existent [which are ultimate truths].
• It is asserted that conventional truths are not ultimately established,
  although they are truly established, because GES asserts that all things
  are necessarily truly established.
General and specific relationships of 'Established' and 'Existence':
• In general, 'established' and 'existence' are synonymous, especially from
  the perspective of the highest school.
• However, when these terms are affixed to 'substantial' in the GES, then
  substantially established and substantially existent do not mean the
  same.
    ➢ All things are necessarily substantially established, but they are
      not necessarily substantially existent.
    ➢ So if something is substantially existent, this does not necessitate
      that it is a substance, since impermanence is substantially existent
      but not a substance.
- substantially established is an established base
- substantially existent  and ultimate truth is mutually inclusive

If the mind apprehending the phenomena cannot be cancelled when the object
is either broken physically or mentally divided into parts, the object is
substantially existent, is an ultimate truth.
• In general, substantially established and substantially existent mean the
  same, from the perspective of the highest school
• For GES, the phrase term 'truly established' and 'truly existence' has to
  be differentiated.

Comparisons of the four schools on what is 'substantially established':
• According to GES, all phenomena are substantially established. So,
  whatever that exist are necessarily substantially established, because
  they assert a substance/ substantial entity from the side of the object.
  Without such a substantially established phenomenon/ established base,
  objects cannot exist. So everything is substantially established.
• However, SS, MOS and AMWS do not assert that all phenomena to be
  substantially established, because they assert that substantially
  established phenomenon must only be a composed/ impermanent phenomenon.
• To CMWS, all phenomena [whether they are permanent or impermanent] are
  not substantially established.
Comparison of true existents and false existents:
• For, all phenomena are necessarily substantially established, truly
  established, real, because, for a phenomenon to exist, it is not
  sufficient to merely have a consciousness imputing it. The object must
  autonomously exist from its own side. If there is no real substance or
  substantiality right within the object, then merely having the mind
  imputing it cannot cause it to exist.
• While all phenomena are truly established, however, this does not mean
  that all phenomena are true/ truly existent. For a phenomenon to be truly
  existent, the mind apprehending it can never be cancelled, this means,
  only ultimate truths are true. Therefore, uncompounded space is true,
  since the mind apprehending it can never be cancelled. Since that is the
  case, conventional truths are false [falsely existent]. Therefore, water
  for example, is false, since the mind apprehending it can be cancelled.
• For GES, they are unable to posit that things and events can exist as
  merely imputed by the mind. Therefore, whatever that can exist,
  necessarily has to be truly established/ substantially established
  [existing from its own side].

As a proponent of Buddhist tenets, why there is this assertion that the
permanent, unitary and independent self does not exist?
• The self, I or person  that wishes to be happy and avoid suffering does
  exists. To attempts to investigate into its exact nature, the four
  Buddhist tenets each have its own explanations to what exactly it is.
• Particularly, for those who assert past and future lives and believe in
  reincarnation, it is even more important to discover what exactly is the
  self, I or person that moves on from life to life, that accumulates karma
  and experiences its results.
• Those non-Buddhists who assert reincarnation also have to account for the
  self, I or person that moves from life to life. Many among them concluded
  that the self, I or person is permanent, unitary and independent.
• However, Buddhist refutes such a notion of a permanent, unitary and
  independent person.
Non-Buddhists' Mode of apprehension of the permanent, unitary and
independent person:
For the non-Buddhists who assert reincarnation and a self, I or person, it
must be:
1) permanent, unitary and independent,
2) being completely distinct/ separate/ unrelated and an independent entity
from the body and mind /aggregates.
This is because, they do not how to relate the self, I or person to the
body and mind and unable to posit a self, I or person that is dependent on
and of the same entity with the body and mind.
All proponents of Buddhist tenets unanimously assert that a permanent,
unitary and independent person does not exist whatsoever, and there cannot
be a self, I or person that is not dependent/ of a different entity from
the body and mind.
5. Way of asserting object -possessors (done in Lorig module)
To GES, illustration of a person - mere collection of the mental and
physical aggregates that are its basis of imputation.
‘Illustration of a person” refers to what is found upon analyzing what a
person is. GES hold that there is no substantially existent person assert
that the mere collection of body and mind is a person.

6. Way of asserting selflessness
• Subtle selflessness and subtle selflessness of persons are asserted to be
  mutually inclusive.
    • All selflessness are either of persons or of phenomena. Since GES do
      not assert a selflessness of phenomena, for them a subtle selflessness
      is necessarily a selflessness of persons.
• A selflessness of phenomena is not accepted because they hold that all
  established bases (i.e. body, mind, house) have a self of phenomena [that
  is, they are truly exist and have a difference of entity of subject and
  object].
• Followers of Vatsiputra, being a proponent of Buddhist tenets,
  -accept a selflessness of person that is a person being empty of being
  permanent, unitary, and independent.
  -do not accept a selflessness of person that is a person being empty of
  being self-sufficient substantially existent because they accept a self-
  sufficient substantially existent self that is inexpressible even in
  terms of being one entity with or a different entity from the aggregates,
  and being permanent or impermanent.
• Therefore, Followers of Vatsiputra,
  - do not assert a permanent, unitary and independent self
  - assert a self-sufficient substantially existent person
• Reason: Because they do not know how to posit a person or I that is of
  the same entity as the body and mind as taught by the Buddha. Buddha said
  that there isn’t an I that is of different entity from the body and mind.
• For them, if the I or person is of the same entity with body and mind,
  they have the difficulty of explaining reincarnation. They do not know
  how to explain how the I can move from life to life.
• So they say that self-sufficient substantially existent person exist and
  accept a self-sufficient substantially existent self that is
  inexpressible even in terms of being one entity with or a different
  entity from the aggregates, and being permanent or impermanent.
• Vasiputriyans, is the only proponents of Buddhist tenets assert the self-
  sufficient substantially existent person, whereas other sub-divisions of
  the GES and the proponents of other tenets (SS, MOS and MWS) assert that
  the self-sufficient substantially existent person does not exist
  whatsoever.
• All Buddhist tenets similarly in asserting the non-existence of the
  permanent, unitary and independent self.

The fallacies of a permanent, unitary and independent person':
• What the non-Buddhists refer to as their 'soul' or 'atman' essentially
  refers to a permanent, unitary and independent self; which is in contrast
  to what Buddha taught. Such a 'soul’ or self, I or person does not exist
  whatsoever.
• According to the GES, MOS & AMWS:
  - the non-existence of such a permanent, unitary and independent person
  is the coarse selflessness of person
  - the non-existence of a self-sufficient substantially existent person is
  the subtle selflessness of person
• The CMWS has a different presentation on what is the coarse and subtle
  selflessness of person.
• The fallacies of a permanent self: If one asserts a self, I or person
  that is permanent, it would mean that it does not undergo momentary
  change. If that is the case, then how does one posit a self, I or person
  that experiences happiness and suffering, as well as achieving liberation
  and enlightenment?
• The fallacies of an independent self: If one asserts a self, I or person
  that is independent, it would mean that it is independent of causes and
  conditions. If that is the case, then how does one posit a self, I or
  person that exists that is independent of causes and conditions? For
  Buddhists, it is extremely difficult to account for a self, I or person
  that is independent of causes and conditions.
• The fallacies of an unitary self: If one asserts a self, I or person that
  is unitary, it would mean that it is partless. This would mean that it
  pervades all phenomena.
• According to the GES, directionally partless particles and temporally
  partless moments of consciousnesses exist. Therefore, what would be the
  problem of asserting a partless 'self'?
• GES assert that the self, I or person is truly established, an ultimate
  truth. It is not a phenomenon such that an awareness apprehending it is
  cancelled if it is broken up or mentally separated into its individual
  parts. Therefore, the self, I or person is not a conventional truth but
  is an ultimate truth/ substantially existent.
• There is an 'I' that came from the previous lives, in fact, from time
  without beginning up to now; there is also an 'I' that will move on from
  this life to the next. Therefore, such an 'I' is truly established. Such
  a self, I or person would probably be an ultimate truth as well. The
  self, I or person is an ultimate truth in this system; nevertheless, one
  cannot say that it is partless.
How the ‘I’ exist and the way the ‘I’ appear
• In reality, the self, I or person does not exist for even the smallest
  unit of time, for every moment it is already different.  The 'I' of
  yesterday has already ceased to exist/ has disintegrated. In this sense,
  the 'I' has changed. This is mere coarse impermanence; it is not even
  subtle impermanence. However, the way one sees oneself, one has a very
  strong believe that the 'I' of yesterday and the 'I' of today is exactly
  the same person, nothing has changed. Therefore, this is the appearance
  of an unchanging ‘I’, which is so real.
• If one can come to understand that the self, I or person is changing each
  moment, down to the smallest unit of time, that it does not exist even
  for a moment, then this constitutes an understanding of subtle
  impermanence.
• Such an understanding can really be an enormous remedy to counteract
  one's afflictions of the three mental poisons.

What is a self-sufficient substantially existent person?
• The non-existence of a permanent, unitary and independent self is the
  coarse selflessness of persons.
• At a deeper level, there is the subtle selflessness of person, which is
  the non-existence of a self-sufficient substantially existent person.
• With the exception of CMWS, the rest of the Buddhist tenets, from GES to
  AMWS analyze the 'I' on the basis that it exists from its own side.
• Looking at one's own experiences, one has the idea of oneself as a
  person/ I and one has the believe that “I am the possessor of the
  aggregates,” “I own my body and mind, I am the utilizer of my
  aggregates.”
• One's view of one's self, I or person in relation to one's body and mind
  is similar to the relation between a businessman and the goods he owns:
• The self, I or person is like the businessman, the goods that he trades
  in is like the body and mind.
• Such a believe in the self, I or person being the owner of the body and
  mind is innate and not taught, thus it arises naturally.
• Example of how the 'I' is like the owner of one's body: When one is ill
  with pain sees another healthy person free of pain, one can have this
  idea: “How nice it would be if I can have that person's body!”
• One does not have the wish to exchange the self, I or person, but just to
  exchange the body as if one owns it.
• This shows that the self, I or person is like the owner of the body, just
  like a businessman who can do whatever he wishes with the goods he owns.
• Example of how the 'I' is like the owner of one's mind: In the same way,
  one innately feels that one owns one's mind. If one is not so intelligent
  or wise see another person who is very intelligent, one may think, “How
  wonderful it is if I had his wisdom!” One does not think “If I can have
  that person self, I or person”, but instead to exchange them with one's
  mind if it is possible. Such a wish is innate, not taught.
• In this case, the self, I or person does not seem to be dependent on the
  body and mind whatsoever. Rather, one has the idea that one's body and
  mind is dependent on one's self, I or person, and the 'I' becomes like
  the owner.
• One therefore has an innate believe that the self, I or person is the
  owner/ possessor of one's body and mind, not dependent on one's body and
  mind. Such a believe is an apprehension of a self-sufficient
  substantially existent person.
• Therefore, the permanent, unitary and independent self does not exist.
  Likewise, the self, I or person being the owner/possessor of the body and
  mind also does not exist.
• Permanent here has the connotation that the I or self is completely
  unrelated to the body and mind i.e. there is no relationship.
Does one need to abandon the intellectually acquired apprehension of a self-
sufficient substantially existent person before abandoning the innate
apprehension of a self-sufficient, substantially existent person?
Yes, one need to. Afflictions can be divided into:
      - intellectually acquired afflictions
      - innate afflictions.
In the case of the apprehension of a self-sufficient substantially existent
person, there are two forms:
    - intellectually acquired form
    - innate forms.
• The object of abandonment to abandon these two types of apprehension is
  the same. If this is the case, then why we need to abandon the
  intellectually acquired one before we can abandon the innate one?
• This is because the modes of apprehension of these two minds are
  nevertheless different:
  1) The mode of apprehension of the intellectually acquired self-
  sufficient substantially existent person is essentially similar to the
  mode of apprehension of the permanent, unitary and independent person:
  the mode of this mind apprehending its object is to apprehend the self-
  sufficient person to be a separate entity from the aggregates.
  2) The mode of apprehension of the innate self-sufficient substantially
  existent person: the mode of this mind apprehending its object is not to
  apprehend at the self-sufficient person to be a separate entity from the
  aggregate, but as the owner/ controller of its aggregates.

Nirvana with remainder and Nirvana without remainder
 • All three enlightenments consist in a nirvana with remainder and a
   nirvana without remainder.
 • A nirvana with remainder is attained in a bodily support produced by
   karma and afflictions. Here “remainder” is that very bodily support
   produced by karma and afflictions.
 • When hearer, solitary realizer, or Buddha die, the continua of the body
   and mind are  severed and they attain a nirvana without remainder.
 • Hearer and solitary realizer foe-destroyers who possess a nirvana with
   remainder have abandoned all the afflictive obscurations but not the non-
   afflictive obscurations, yet when they die the non-afflictive
   obscurations no longer exist because their support, the mind, no longer
   exists. Thus, at the time of a nirvana without remainder, foe-destroyers
   have abandoned the non-afflictive obscurations but not by the power of
   an antidote.
What is the boundary of the generation of the path and the absence of the
path?
The moment a fully qualified renunciation is developed in the mental
continuum, a path is generated.
However, a proponent of any of the four Buddhist tenets need not be someone
who has generated renunciation in his mind.

Self-knower
An eye consciousness apprehending blue has:
    - Blue as its object of observation
    - Blue is that which is apprehended by consciousness
So the eye consciousness apprehending blue has the aspect of the
apprehended
According to the SS, this eye consciousness apprehending blue has two
aspects:
 ➢ One aspect of it that apprehends an object other than itself - in this
   case is blue.
   So it has the aspect of the apprehended
 ➢ Another aspect of it being able to experience itself. It has the entity
   of clarity and knowing.
   This is a self-knower, which has the aspect of the apprehender.
The reason for SS and other tenets for asserting self-knower is memory;
there must be something within the consciousness that can experience itself
[eye consciousness that apprehended blue].
-In the case of an eye consciousness apprehending blue, there must be a
part of this consciousness that experiences itself, is aware of itself.
-After having experienced 'blue', it is possible, at a later time, to
remember having seen 'blue'.
-SS assert that the only reason that one is able to remember having seen
'blue' is due to having experienced 'blue' previously. Therefore, without a
consciousness having actually experienced an object, one would not be able
to remember that object later on.
- There must be an experiencer of that eye consciousness apprehending blue.
That experiencer is called a self-knower.
- Logical reasoning:-
    - The subject: eye consciousness apprehending blue
    - The predicate: has an experiencer
    - The reason: because at a later time, it is possible to have a memory
      of this eye consciousness apprehending blue.
 • Those who assert self-knowers are SS, MOS and AMWS.
 • Those who do not assert self-knower are GES and CMWS.

The meaning of external objects
• SS assert partless particle, which is the basis of aggregation.
• Through the aggregation of these directionally partless particles, a
  gross object is formed.
• This then becomes the object that is apprehended by a sense
  consciousness.
According to SS:
• First, there must be an object, a gross object or form.
• Only then can you have a sense consciousness apprehending it.
• So the object comes before the subject apprehending it.
To the SS, this is how a sense consciousness apprehending form is
generated. That means sense consciousness is generated after the form
itself has come into existence.
MOS differ greatly in this respect.
• The MOS assert form but they do not accept external form. They refute
  external objects as asserted by the SS.
➢ The MOS assert form but they do not assert that there is a form that is
  outside the mind.
➢ The form is none other than that which is of the same entity with the
  mind.
➢ MOS do not assert external form because that is of a different entity
  from the mind.
➢ MOS propounds all phenomena are in the nature of mind.
➢ To the MOS, form is none other than the factor of appearance that
  appears to that consciousness as form.
Example: There is an eye consciousness apprehending blue:
The subject ( the eye consciousness
The object  (  blue
Both are produced in dependence on the ripening of karmic imprints that are
the substantial causes for the production of the object and the subject.
One part of this imprint produces the eye consciousness apprehending blue.
Another part of this imprint produces the object blue.
For this reason, both object and subject are empty of being different
entities.
This means that the object (blue) and the subject (eye consciousness)
apprehending blue are produced simultaneously.
Therefore, the emptiness of object and subject as different entities is the
selflessness of phenomena.
Examples of how forms are mere entities of mind:
1) Appearances in a dream: In a dream, there are all kinds of appearances,
but those appearances in the dream are not outside of the mind. They are
not separate from the dream consciousness. They are essentially mind too.
2) Appearance of two moons:  While  in  reality  there  is  only  one  moon,
nevertheless there is the appearance of  two  moons  to  some  people.  That
appearance  is  not  something  that  exists  outside  of  the  mind.   That
appearance is essentially an appearance to the mind.
3) Different appearances on the same base of liquid in a cup to different
beings:
-To humans, the liquid appears through the force of karma as water. That
appearance of water is just the factor of appearance to the mind. There is
no an external water out there that is of a different entity from the mind.

-To the hungry ghost, the same cup of liquid, they do not have the
appearance of water due to their karma. What they see is pus.
-To the gods, the same cup of liquid appears as nectar
-To the hell beings, the same cup of liquid appears as molten iron due to
different imprints.
The MOS uses this example to prove that there are no external objects that
are of a different or separate entity from the mind. Due to different
imprints, different beings see different things. For humans, that water in
a cup is a mere factor of appearance to the mind, there is no 'external
water' out there.
4) Different appearances on different bases: We see our own home, body and
clothes as attractive and pleasant. Therefore one has an appearance of
pleasant house, body and possessions. To the MOS, there really isn’t the
nice house that is appearing to us. It does not exist externally as a
different entity from the mind. When the gods look at the house or the body
that we have, what they see is disgusting to them.

That is due to the ripening of the latencies of common actions.
Latencies of common and uncommon actions - when human beings being able to

Everything is in the nature of mind
MOS assert that form and the eye consciousness apprehending form are
produced simultaneously; not serially.
That means both the object that is apprehended and the subject apprehending
it comes into being at the same time.
The MOS does not assert any object that is separate from the mind.
Everything is in the nature of mind.
1. How does one posit an object that is apprehended by the consciousness?
Reply: An illusionist conjuring an illusory elephant. Although there is an
appearance of an elephant, there is no real elephant out there. This
appearance of an elephant is just a factor of appearance to the mind.
• Nevertheless when this illusory elephant is conjured up, there can be a
  consciousness apprehending it. In this case it is an eye consciousness
  apprehending the illusory elephant.
• What exactly then is that illusory elephant?
• It is just an appearance to the mind.
• Although it is an appearance to the mind, nevertheless an eye
  consciousness apprehending it is being generated.
2. If external objects do not exist, then how do main minds and mental
factors arise?
Reply: Although there is a mirage appearing as water, there is no real
water out there, other than what is appearing to the mind.
3. How does one account for the appearances of external objects?
Reply: Although there is no external object, but due to the mind being
polluted by the latencies of ignorance, this mistake of an appearance of an
external object to the mind appear to exist.
For example, the appearance of 'falling hairs’ (floaters), while in reality
there are no hairs falling, there can still be such an appearance.
While in reality there are no external objects, this does not mean that
there can be no appearances of external objects. Such appearances occur due
to mistaken consciousnesses.

The Three Natures
MOS assert the basis in term of the two truths:
   1. Ultimate truth
   2. Conventional truth
In the classification of phenomena, MOS assert the basis by way of the
three natures:
   1. Other-powered natures
   2. Thoroughly established natures
   3. Imputational natures
Other-powered nature
Other-powered natures refer to those phenomena that are under the control
or influence of their own causes and conditions. In that sense, they are
other-powered.
Other-powered nature refers to composed phenomena. Why are composed
phenomena other-powered natures?
   1. In dependence on causality
   2. In dependence on time
Note:  Other-powered  nature,  composed  phenomena,  impermanent  phenomena,
functioning things are mutually inclusive
Composed phenomena cannot come into being by depending on  themselves.  They
are not self-powered. Composed phenomena are created. They come  into  being
by depending on causes and conditions. Therefore they are other-powered.
Other-powered natures refer to  phenomena  that  undergo  momentary  change,
i.e., phenomena that change from moment to moment. This  change  is  due  to
the cause that brought about  that  particular  phenomenon.  Impermanent  or
composed phenomena are under the power  of  the  causes  that  brought  them
about.
1. In dependence on causality – Composed phenomena cannot  come  into  being
by depending on themselves. They are not self-powered.
Composed phenomena are created. They come into being by depending on  causes
and conditions. Therefore they are other-powered.
Due to such assertions of Other-powered nature, some proponents of MOS
assert the collections of 8 consciousnesses:
      5 sense + mental consciousnesses + afflicted consciousness + mind-
basis-of-all.
The mind-basis-of-all is the repository or holder of all the imprints that
are deposited there.
Through the awakening of the imprints that are planted on the mind-basis-of-
all, composed phenomena come into existence. This is how composed phenomena
are other-powered

it cannot exist beyond the very first moment that they come into being.
Composed phenomena can remain only for as long as that first moment when
they come into being. They disintegrate in the very next moment.
Composed phenomena do not have the power to remain for more than one
moment. Therefore they are other-powered. So, other-powered natures refer
to composed phenomena. They are under the power or the control of their own
causes.
Two divisions of other-powered natures:
   1. Pure other-powered natures
   2. Impure other-powered natures
1. Pure other-powered natures refer to the wisdom of Superiors subsequent
to meditative equipoise and the major and minor marks of a Buddha.
2. Impure other-powered natures - refer to the contaminated appropriated
physical and mental aggregates, i.e., our contaminated existents that are
under the control of their causes, karma and afflictions.

Imputational nature
Imputational natures are those phenomena that are imputed by the conceptual
consciousness apprehending them. So, imputational natures are those
phenomena that are established as merely imputed by conceptuality.
Illustration - Uncompounded space is an imputational nature because
uncompounded space does not exist by way of its own character. It is just
the factor of superimposition by conceptuality.
Imputational natures are of two types:
   1. existent
   2. non-existent
Imputational natures that are existents refer to all permanent phenomena
other than emptiness.
Example -uncompounded space.
Imputational natures that are non-existents such as external objects. For
MOS, external objects do not exist. An external object is an imputation
through the force of conceptuality.
What is the purpose of the presentation of the three natures -other-powered
natures, thoroughly established natures, and imputational natures -in the
MOS?
The purpose of presenting the three natures is to arrive at an
understanding of emptiness, the thoroughly established nature. The whole
point of arriving at the presentation of the ultimate nature of reality
–emptiness-is because it is the antidote to an apprehension of the self.
    • A thoroughly established nature is an other-powered nature’s being
      empty of its imputational nature.
    • Other-powered natures such as our contaminated body and mind are empty
      of imputational natures.
    • The emptiness that is an other-powered nature’s emptiness of an
      imputational nature is the thoroughly established nature.

Object of negation
➢ The object of negation is the object that exists by way of its own
  character as the basis for the applying the term “object” to the
  conceptual consciousness apprehending the object.
➢ Example: The object blue. Blue exists by way of its own character as the
  basis for applying the term “blue” to the conceptual consciousness
  apprehending blue. This is the object of negation.
➢ Blue appears to the conceptual consciousness to be the basis for
  applying the term “blue.” This blue appears to exist naturally from its
  own side, by way of its own character as the basis for applying the term
  “blue” to the conceptual consciousness apprehending blue without depending
  on the conceptual consciousness apprehending blue. This is the object of
  negation.

The difference between existing from its own side and existing by way of
its own character from the perspective of the MOS.

- Existing from its own side means that when you search for the imputed

The mind-basis-of-all
Mind-basis-of-all is a repository of all the latencies of karma.
Latencies of all kinds of actions are deposited on the mind-basis-of-all.
It is through the ripening of these latencies that there is the appearance
of phenomena.
In order for the six consciousnesses to be produced, there must be an
uncommon empowering condition, i.e., their respective sense powers. It is
only by depending on their respective sense power that a consciousness is
generated. That means the consciousness is generated temporarily and that
it does not exist all the time.
According to the True Aspectarians, because such a consciousness does not
exist all the time, therefore this consciousness cannot be the
consciousness that receives all the latencies of the karma that one have
accumulated.
The repository of all the imprints of our karma must be something that is
stable and enduring at all times. Otherwise it is difficult to account for
karma. Therefore the True Aspectarians think that there must be a
consciousness:
1) that is other than these six consciousnesses
2) that does not depend upon a sense power that is its own uncommon
   empowering condition.  Such a consciousness is called the mind-basis-of-
   all.
The reason they assert the mind-basis-of-all is to account for the need for
a stable and enduring repository for all the imprints of the karma.
The mind-basis-of-all
    ➢ is an awareness to which an object appears and is not ascertained.
    ➢ does not realize its object.
    ➢ neither virtuous nor non-virtuous, i.e., it is unspecified.
Why is the mind-basis-of-all not virtuous?
If it is virtuous, it is very difficult to account for the mind-basis-of-
all being a repository of imprints of non-virtuous actions.
If the mind-basis-of-all is asserted to be non-virtuous, then it is
difficult to explain how the mind-basis-of-all can act as a repository of
imprints of virtuous actions.

basis-of-all and apprehends it to be a self-sufficient substantially

What does the afflicted mentality observe? It observes the mind-basis-of-

The afflicted mentality apprehends the mind-basis-of-all to be a self-

The four kayas
The Mahayana teachings explain enlightenment or buddhahood in terms of the
four kayas or four bodies.
In general, there are the two bodies:
   1. Truth Body (the embodiment of truth)
   2. Form Body (the embodiment of form).
The Truth Body (dharmakaya) has two divisions:
  -the Wisdom Truth Body
  -the Nature Truth Body
The Form Body (rupakaya) can be divided as:
-  the Complete Enjoyment Body (sambhogakaya)
-  the Emanation Body (nirmanakaya)
Such a presentation of the four kayas or the four bodies is not found in
the Hinayana teachings.
What is the wisdom truth body?
It is the mind of a buddha that directly perceives all phenomena as they
are.

What is the nature truth body?
According to the MOS, the nature truth body is the emptiness of the mind of
a buddha superior existing by way of its own character as the basis for
applying the term, “mind of a buddha superior.”

The basis of the presentation of the four kayas is made on the premise that
the continuum of awareness of a buddha is not severed after a buddha passes
beyond sorrow without remainder because you cannot talk about the four
kayas if a buddha disappears.

One first achieves enlightenment in the pure land called Akanishta in the
aspect of the complete enjoyment body. This complete enjoyment body is the
basis of emanation from which various emanations are sent out to help
sentient beings. This complete enjoyment body lasts forever until samsara
is emptied.

The complete enjoyment body possesses five certainties:
   1. Certainty of place: it abides only in Akanishta.
   2. Certainty of retinue:  its retinue consists of only bodhisattva
      superiors.
   3. Certainty of Dharma: it teaches only the Mahayana Dharma.
   4. Certainty of time: it lasts forever until samsara is emptied.
   5. Certainty of the body: it is beautified or ornamented by the 32 signs
      and the 80 exemplifications.

The Mind Only True Aspectarians’ assertion of the three final vehicles
The True Aspectarians assert that there are three final vehicles because
they assert that sentient beings, since beginningless time, are of three
different lineages or dispositions and thus have three different
aspirations, and thus have three different ways of practicing, and thus
will attain three different results.
The True Aspectarians have to assert that there are three final vehicles
because:
• they assert that when a hearer arhat passes beyond sorrow without
  remainder, his continuum of awareness is severed. When the continuum of
  awareness is severed, there is nothing beyond that. One dies as a hearer
  arhat.
• they assert that when solitary realiser arhats pass beyond sorrow without
  remainder, their continua of awareness are also severed. They die as
  solitary realiser arhats.
• they assert there are the buddhas.
Another reason why the True Aspectarians assert that there are three final
vehicles has to do with sentient beings being of different dispositions.
They assert the mind-basis-of-all. Predispositions are deposited on this
mind-basis-of-all so the mind-basis-of-all is the repository of these
predispositions or imprints.
According to the True Aspectarians, the mind-basis-of-all is truly
established. There is an imprint that is stored in the mind-basis-of-all
that predisposes that person to be a hearer. That imprint makes it definite
that the person will be a hearer. When that person enters the path, he
enters the hearers’ path. That is the path he will travel on. He will
achieve the hearers’ enlightenment and become a hearer foe destroyer.
Likewise, it is the same with the imprint that predisposes someone to be a
solitary realiser or to be a bodhisattva who aspires to be a buddha. These
imprints are stored in the mind-basis-of-all.
These imprints predispose that person to be of a certain lineage.

Definite and indefinite lineages
What is the hearer’s lineage?
The hearer’s lineage refers to the lineage or the potential that enables
one to achieve the hearer’s enlightenment.
There are:
1) the definite hearer’s lineage
2) the indefinite hearer’s lineage
Definite hearer’s lineage refers to those sentient beings who definitely
enter the hearer’s path, travel on it to the end, and achieve the hearer’s
enlightenment, i.e., become hearer foe destroyers or arhats.
Indefinite hearer’s lineage are those who may initially aspire to the
hearer’s enlightenment, wanting to become a hearer foe destroyer. They
enter the hearer’s path from the path of accumulation, progressing to the
hearer’s path of preparation. Along the way, they may meet with a virtuous
friend, a guru of the solitary realiser’s path or even a Mahayana guru.
They then switch their path. Let’s say that this person who initially
aspired to the hearer’s enlightenment switches path and enters the solitary
realiser’s path. In doing so, he travels that path to the end and becomes a
solitary realiser arhat. Such a person is considered to be of an indefinite
hearer’s lineage.
There are also those who are of definite solitary realiser’s lineage and
indefinite solitary realiser’s lineage.
There are also those who are of definite Mahayana lineage and indefinite
Mahayana lineage.
According to the True Aspectarians, there are two types of arhats:
   1. Those who are definitely going to pass beyond sorrow without
      remainder. In so doing, their continua of awareness are severed. That
      means they will remain as arhats and die as arhats.
   2. Those who, after having achieved arhatship and before passing beyond
      sorrow without remainder, enter the Mahayana path.  In so doing, they
      will become a buddha in the future.
False Aspectarians:
• Do not assert that the whole continuum of awareness is severed at the
  time of passing beyond sorrow without remainder
• Assert that only that part of the continuum of awareness that is included
  in true sufferings and true origins is severed and the mere awareness goes
  on to the buddha ground, i.e., mere awareness is not severed at the time
  of passing beyond sorrow without remainder. That mere awareness goes on to
  enlightenment.
• For that reason, the False Aspectarians assert one final vehicle.

Definitive and interpretative teachings
According to GES and SS, the proponents of Hinayana tenets, assert that the
Buddha’s words are to be taken literally. Therefore everything the Buddha
said is true.
Mahayana tenets such as the MOS assert that the Buddha’s teachings must be
classified into
   1) teachings that require interpretation
   2) teachings of definitive meaning
Hinayana do not accept that the Mahayana teachings are the teachings of the
Buddha. To them, whatever the Buddha says can be taken literally so they
must take the teachings literally.
Mahayana teachings cannot fit their mind and do not make sense to them. To
them these teachings are not true and cannot be taught by the Buddha.
Therefore they conclude that the Mahayana teachings are not taught by the
Buddha.
The MOS divides the scriptures into two categories:
   1. scriptures that are suitable to be taken literally
   2. scriptures that are not suitable to be taken literally
The MOS says that all the teachings of the Buddha are not necessarily
suitable to be taken literally.
There were three turnings of the Wheel of Dharma, the MOS accept that:
• the scriptures from the first turning and the second turning of the Wheel
  of Dharma were taught by Buddha but they do not accept that these
  teachings are suitable to be taken literally
• the teachings or the sutras from the third turning of the Wheel of Dharma
  are suitable to be taken literally
For example, the Perfection of Wisdom Sutras belong to the second turning
of the Wheel of Dharma. The MOS accepts that these statements “Form does
not exist inherently” or “Form is not produced inherently” were made by the
Buddha but cannot be taken literally.
“Form is not produced inherently” cannot be taken literally and requires
interpretation. This statement means that form is not produced from a cause
that is of the same nature as that form. Form and the cause of form are of
separate natures. The cause of form is a separate nature from form, so form
is not produced from a cause that is of the same nature as form.
In the Mahayana view, all the Buddha scriptures or teachings cannot be
taken literally. Therefore there is a division of the scriptures into:
• sutras of interpretative meaning
• sutras of definitive meaning
Buddha gave teachings according to the interests and dispositions of his
disciples to fit their minds. Because of that, the words that were used by
the Buddha and what the Buddha said are not necessarily the final truth or
even necessarily true. They were not necessarily indicative of the final
intent of the Buddha.
The Mahayana view is that the Buddha taught to benefit sentient beings and
his teachings accorded with the dispositions of sentient beings.

Assertions of the AMWS
AMWS asserts that phenomena exist by way of their own character. Although
things do not have an atom of true existence, phenomena do exist by way of
their own character.
The AMWS asserts that, if a phenomenon exists, it necessarily:
• exists by way of its own character,
• exists inherently, and
• exists from its own side.
According to the AMWS, if someone were to say that a phenomenon does not
exist by way of its own character, does not exist from its own side, and
does not exist inherently, such a phenomenon does not exist.
AMWS cannot accept that phenomena exist merely by being imputed by mind
because there must be something that exists from the side of the object.
For them, if a phenomenon exists as merely imputed by mind, then one can
make up anything with one’s mind.
If everything is merely imputed by mind, AMWS do not know how to explain
existence and non-existence. To them, there must be something coming from
the side of the object.
Exist from its own side/exist inherently/exist by way of its own character
With the exception of the CMWS, AMWS, MOS, SS, and GES assert that
phenomena must
 (1) exist from their own side
 (2) exist inherently
 (3) exist by way of their own character.
This is because, when you look for the object, you must be able to find it.

This is what is meant by things “existing from their own side,” when you
search for the imputed object, you can point to something that is the
object.
Only CMWS asserts that the imputed object, when sought, is not findable.
That means you cannot point to the thing that is the object when you look
for it.
True Existence
Proponents of the Middle Way, both AMWS and CMWS, assert that phenomena do
not truly exist. There is not even a single atom of true existence. There
is no true existence, no true establishment. Phenomena are not truly
existent, not truly established.
How then does the AMWS explain the non-existence of true existence?
Conventional phenomena are not truly existent. If conventional phenomena
are truly existent, they have to exist in the perspective of a valid
cognizer or ultimate awareness.
Ultimate awareness refers to the meditative equipoise of a superior that is
the wisdom directly perceiving emptiness.
If conventional phenomena are indeed truly existent, this means that they
not only appear as truly existent—they appear in the way they exist—but
they will have to exist in the perspective of the wisdom directly
perceiving emptiness. However conventional phenomena do not exist in the
perspective of the wisdom directly perceiving emptiness. Therefore, this
means that there are no truly existent conventional phenomena.
Established by way of its own uncommon mode of existence without being
posited through the force of appearing to a non-defective awareness
• According to the AMWS, the meaning of (1) true existence, (2) true
  establishment, and (3) ultimate establishment is: “Established by way of
  its own uncommon mode of existence without being posited through the force
  of appearing to a non-defective awareness.”
• This is the self of phenomena
• This is the object of negation.
Not established by way of its own uncommon mode of existence without being
posited through the force of appearing to a non-defective awareness is the
meaning of not truly existent, not truly established, and not ultimately
established.
Non-defective awareness is a valid cognizer

Existing by way of its own character means that when you search for the

to something that is the imputed object.

True existence (according thought he AMWS)
AMWS interpretation on true existence is based on the quotation in the
Descent into Lanka Sutra, the teaching by the Buddha: “All phenomena exist
through appearing to the consciousness. All phenomena exist conventionally
and all phenomena do not exist ultimately.”
“All phenomena exist conventionally” - All phenomena exist conventionally
because all phenomena are posited to exist through the force of the
phenomena appearing to a consciousness. This is the reason why all
phenomena exist conventionally and not ultimately.
• For, if a phenomenon can exist without being posited through the force of
  appearing to a consciousness, then that phenomenon would be ultimately
  existent.
• According to the AMWS, if a phenomenon is established by way of its own
  uncommon mode of existence without being posited through the force of
  appearing to a non-defective awareness, that phenomenon exists ultimately.
  That phenomenon is truly existent.
• There is no phenomenon that you can point to that is established by way
  of its own uncommon mode of existence without being posited through the
  force of appearing to a non-defective awareness. Therefore phenomena do
  not exist ultimately or truly exist. All phenomena have to exist
  conventionally.

How do things exist according to the AMWS?
In order for something to be considered as an existent, it has to be
something that is appearing to a mind.
All phenomena are posited because they appear to a mind.
• There is also existence from the side of the object.

Therefore the AMWS do not accept that phenomena are established by way of
their own uncommon mode of existence without being posited through the
force of appearing to a non-defective awareness.

According to the AMWS, this particular mind has to be a non-defective
awareness.
Non-defective awareness means there is nothing wrong with that particular
mind existing by way of its own character. The factor of it existing by way
of its own character is non-defective because it cannot be harmed by a
reason.
• Existence by way of its own character, (2) existence from its own side,

Valid cognition
Two types of awarenesses:
   1. valid cognizers
   2. non-valid cognizers
The valid cognizer has two divisions:
   1. direct valid cognizers
   2. inferential valid cognizers
The CMWS define a valid cognizer differently from the other tenets. To
them, as long as the mind realizes its object and it is infallible with
respect to the object, it is a valid cognizer.
Unlike the schools below it who all insist that a valid cognizer has to be
a newly incontrovertible knower (“Newly” refers to the first moment of
cognition), the CMWS say that is unnecessary. To them, a valid cognition
need not be the first moment of realization of the object. For that reason,
subsequent cognizers are considered to be valid cognizers as well. (In
lorig, a subsequent cognizer is a knower that realizes what has already
been realized and is not a valid cognizer)
To what constitutes a valid cognizer, the assertions of the CMWS and the

valid cognizer.

• is an incontrovertible knower with respect to its object of the  mode  of

Why is the first moment of an inferential cognizer realizing sound to be

realizing emptiness in the continua of sentient beings. It is a mental
direct perceiver and it is non-mistaken.
Yogic direct perceivers
A yogic direct perceiver can be either mistaken or non-mistaken.
Yogic direct perceivers are generated in dependence upon their uncommon
empowering condition, a concentration that is a union of calm abiding and
special insight. Same assertion as the lower tenets.
With the concentration that is a union of calm abiding and special insight
as its uncommon empowering condition, there are three types of yogic direct
perceivers depending on the object it realizes:
• yogic direct perceivers realizing subtle impermanence
• yogic direct perceivers realizing coarse selflessness
• yogic direct perceivers realizing subtle selflessness

Inferential cognizers
There are four types:
   1. inferential cognizers through the power of the fact,
   2. inferential cognizers through renown,
   3. inferential cognizers through an example,
   4. inferential cognizers through belief.
Inferential cognizer through renown and inferential cognizer through an
example are included in inferential cognizer through the power of the fact

Inferential cognizer realizing sound to be impermanent:
    • is an inferential cognizer.
    • is a valid cognizer.
    • is a conceptual consciousness.
    • is a mistaken consciousness.
    • is mistaken with respect to its determined object.
➢ According to the lower tenets, all conceptual consciousnesses are
  necessarily mistaken, because they are mistaken with respect to their
  appearing object that is a meaning generality.
➢ For the CMWS, what is the sign that shows that this dream consciousness
  realizes its object?
➢ A dream consciousness that has the appearance of a dream elephant is a
  mistaken consciousness but that dream consciousness is realizing
  something. What it realizes is the dream elephant appearing as an
  elephant.
➢ So the CMWS way of asserting consciousness is different in many ways
  from the lower tenets.
Is an inferential cognizer realizing impermanent sound a wrong
consciousness?
It is a wrong consciousness because it erroneously engages in its object of
the mode of apprehension.

   - true existence - upon the basis of a person is the subtle selflessness

   - true existence - upon the basis of the aggregates and so forth is  the

• The two subtle conceptions of self  are  differentiated  by  way  of  the

  - observing the basis - the person - and  apprehending  it  to  be  truly

  - observing the basis of imputation – the aggregates and so forth  -  and
  apprehending it to be truly existent is posited as the subtle  conception
  of a self of phenomena
According to the CMWS, all phenomena are merely imputed by thought.
The word “merely” negates establishment by  way  of  its  own  character  or
existence from its own side.
Illustration: Mistaking a coil of rope to be a snake under  certain  weather
conditions and yet it is not completely dark. From the side of the  coil  of
rope, you will not be able to find a snake on the rope. There  is  no  snake
on the rope. The coil of rope appearing to be a  real  snake  is  merely  an
imputation by the mind.
How the “I” exists
 “I” is merely imputed in dependence upon the aggregates that is  the  bases
of designation.
How does the “I” appear to our mind?
The “I” appears to exist from the side of the aggregates.  We  believe  that
there is an “I” within the aggregates,  that  the  “I”  is  the  aggregates,
coming from the side of  the  aggregates,  and  is  not  merely  imputed  by
thought. This is how the “I” appears to exist to us.
Is this how the “I” actually exists in reality?
• If the “I” exists from the side of the aggregates, then  when  we  search
  for it among the individual aggregates, we should be  able  to  point  to
  something that is the “I.” But the “I” is not findable upon analysis.
• The “I” is not the aggregates and cannot be found on the  aggregates.  Is
  there an “I” that is separate from and is not the  aggregates?  When  you
  eliminate the aggregates one by one, you will not be able to find an  “I”
  there in the end.  One  cannot  find  the  self  or  the  person  on  the
  aggregates or among the aggregates. Neither one is able to find  the  “I”
  nor self that is separate or apart from the aggregates.
• The “I” is none other than that which is  merely  imputed  in  dependence
  upon the aggregates. Like the “I,” the person, the  aggregates  are  also
  merely imputed by mind.

Object of negation
According to the CMWS, everything that exists is necessarily merely  imputed
by thought. There  isn’t  anything  that  can  exist  without  being  merely
imputed by thought. So, anything that is  said  to  exist  and  yet  is  not
merely imputed by thought becomes the object of negation. This  is  what  is
meant by self-establishment.
• If there is a person who can exist without being merely imputed  by  mind,
  existing from his own side, that is the self of persons.
• If there are phenomena other than persons that can exist  from  their  own
  side without being merely imputed by mind, that is the self of  phenomena.


• The  non-existence  of  a  person  existing  from  its  own  side  is  the
  selflessness of persons.
• The non-existence of phenomena other than persons existing from their  own
  side is the selflessness of phenomena.
According to the CMWS, the two selflessnesses (selflessness of persons or
the selflessness of phenomena) are not differentiated by the object of
negation.  Here the object of negation is true existence or inherent
existence.
• The non-existence of the object of negation in relation  to  a  person  is
  the selflessness of persons.
• The non-existence of the object of negation in relation  to  phenomena  is
  the selflessness of phenomena.

Distinguishing between existence and inherent existence
As we do not have any understanding of the ultimate nature of reality, the
emptiness of phenomena, whatever appears to us appears as truly existent,
inherently existent, and existing from its own side. We think this is how
things exist.
The “I” cannot be found
In his Precious Garland, Nagarjuna said that we should look for the “I” on
the basis of designation. In this case, the basis of designation is the
aggregates - the body and mind.
-is the “I” is among the aggregates?
-is the “I” the earth, water, fire, or wind?
-is the “I” the consciousness?
-is the “I” the collection of all of these together?
When we think of the “I,” what naturally comes to mind is:
- the “I” appears to exist from its own side,
- the “I” appears to exist from the side of the aggregates
- an “I” somewhere either on the body or the mind
- an “I” both the body and mind
- an “I” both the collection of all these things
This is how the “I” appears to us. Not only that.  We believe that the “I”
is probably like that.
Does the “I” exist in the way it appears?”
Although it appears in such a way, will we be able to find the “I” among
the basis of designation?
Nagarjuna concluded by saying that when you look for the “I”, you would not
be able to point to the body, or the mind, or anything else for that
matter.
But the “I’ exists
 • Although the “I” appears to exist inherently, appears to exist from its
   own side, and appears to be findable among the bases of designation -
   the aggregates (the body and mind) - when you look for it, you will not
   be able to find such an “I.”
 • The fact that such an inherently existent “I” cannot be found when we
   search for it shows that the “I” does not exist inherently. Instead what
   you find is the emptiness of the inherently existent “I.”
 • Although you are unable to find the “I,” that does not mean that the “I”
   is non-existent. That is incorrect. Obviously the “I” exists. Definitely
   there is a self, a person, or “I” who wishes to be happy and who does
   not want to suffer. Although you are unable to find the “I,” that does
   not mean that you have found the non-existence of the “I.”
How does the “I” exist?
The “I” exists, but the “I” is:
 • not existing on or among the bases of designation,
 • not the body or mind, and
 • not the collection of the body and mind.
The “I” cannot be found on the basis of designation yet the “I” exists.
The “I” does not exist inherently.
The “I” is none other than that which is merely imputed or posited by the
mind in dependence upon the aggregates, the bases of designation. There is
nothing more to the “I” than that.
According to the CMWS, it is easier to gain an initial comprehension of the
emptiness of inherent existence when we think about it on the basis of the
“I” or person.
So first we should reflect on the emptiness of inherent existence on the
basis of the “I” rather than thinking about the emptiness of inherent
existence of phenomena other than the “I.”
With the exception of the CMWS, the AMWS and those below it assert that if
phenomena exist, they must exist inherently. Otherwise they cannot exist.
They assert that things exist inherently, are findable, and definitely, you
will be able to point to something that you can say is the object.

The person is none other than that which is merely imputed in dependence

Dependent origination
According to the MOS, everything is in the nature of the mind. So
everything that exists arises due to the awakening of predispositions that
produce the appearances of particular objects.
According to the MWS, the main reason why we all can become buddhas is
because our minds are not truly existent. Therefore they can change.
Everything that exists exist as dependently originated. All phenomena are
dependently originated, including the mind. For that reason, all phenomena
including the mind do not exist truly or do not exist inherently.
Therefore, when causes and conditions come together, change can be
effected.
Afflictive obscurations
 • The conception of a coarse self of persons is the conception of a self-
   sufficient substantially existent person.
 • The conception of a subtle self of persons is the conception of a truly
   existent person.
 • These two conceptions—the conception of a self-sufficient substantially
   existent person and the conception of a truly existent person together
   with their seeds that they plant on the mind together with the
   afflictions that arise from those seeds and conceptions—are posited to
   be afflictive obscurations.
 • The afflictive obscurations hinder the attainment of liberation from
   samsara.
 • So, in order to achieve liberation, one must abandon them.
Afflictive obscurations:
-The conception of a truly existent person
-The conception of the aggregates as truly existent or the conception of a
phenomenon other than the person to be truly existent
The conception of a self of persons and the conception of a self of
phenomena are both afflictive obscurations.
These two conceptions are not differentiated by their object of negation.
Both these conceptions have to be abandoned for liberation to be achieved.
So, in order to achieve liberation, one must directly realise the
selflessness of persons and the selflessness of phenomena.

Obscurations to knowledge
Obscurations to knowledge are the latencies placed on the mind by the
conception of true existence.
Not only that, all the mistaken dualistic appearances that arise due to the
force of those latencies are also posited to be the obscurations to
knowledge.
They mainly hinder the attainment of omniscience or full enlightenment.
Therefore in order to achieve omniscience, one must abandon the
obscurations to knowledge.
The hearers and solitary realisers do not abandon the obscurations to
knowledge nor are they capable of abandoning them.
On the Mahayana path, the abandoning of the obscurations to knowledge does
not begin till after the eighth bodhisattva ground is achieved. Only then
does the abandoning of the obscurations to knowledge start.
Objects of Abandonment
The persons of all three vehicles do not have different objects of
meditation.
They differ with respect to their main objects of abandonment.
There are differences with respect to their main objects of abandonment
because:
- hearers and solitary realizers take the two conceptions of a self
together with their seeds as their main objects of abandonment
-bodhisattvas take the latencies of those conceptions as their main objects
of abandonment
Nirvana without remainder and nirvana with remainder
• The nirvana that is in the continuum of a hearer or solitary realiser
  arhat is the factor of abandonment of the afflictive obscuration, i.e.,
  the mind’s emptiness of true existence. This suchness of the mind in the
  continuum of an arhat is posited to be nirvana or the sorrowless state.
• According to the CMWS, one achieves the nirvana without remainder first
  followed subsequently by the achievement of nirvana with remainder.
• Nirvana without remainder is the factor of abandonment of the afflictive
  obscurations in the continuum of a superior in meditative equipoise. So
  when the hearer or solitary realiser arhat is in the meditative equipoise
  directly perceiving emptiness, what he has achieved in his mind is a
  thorough abandonment of the afflictive obscurations..
Why is it called a nirvana without remainder?
 • When that arhat is in meditative equipoise directly perceiving
   emptiness, in that state of realization, all dualistic appearances have
   completely subsided. This means that no dualistic appearances remain.
 • When that arhat arises from meditative equipoise and enters post-
   equipoise, he still has dualistic appearance. He also has the appearance
   of truly existent phenomena.
 • This arhat has already abandoned all afflictive obscurations and
   achieved nirvana yet, in post-equipoise, he has all these mistaken
   dualistic appearances. Therefore when the arhat is in post-equipoise,
   that state is called nirvana with remainder.

 For those of the Great Vehicle lineage who are definite in that lineage
 from the very beginning, the complete abandonment of the afflictive
 obscurations occurs simultaneously with the attainment of the eighth
 ground, and the [complete] abandonment of the obscurations to knowledge
 occurs simultaneously with the actualizing of the four bodies
