   Lesson No: 57                           Date: 22nd May 2012


We had talked about assuming the  aspirational  mind  generation  through  a
ritual. There are two ways of doing this, either (1) in the  presence  of  a
master or (2) in the presence of a representation of  a  buddha  such  as  a
buddha statue.

Whichever option we choose:
 • In the beginning, one has to go for refuge in the Mahayana way.
 • One needs to accumulate merit and purify the obscurations  by  doing  the
   seven-limb practice.
 •  One  has  to  sustain  the  relevant  meditations  either  through   the
   instructions of the sevenfold cause and effect  or  exchanging  self  and
   others.
 • By sustaining the  meditations  well,  you  should  be  able  to  develop
   heartfelt love and compassion.
 • On the basis of having developed these feelings  well,  you  develop  the
   mind generation through a ritual. At that time, think, “For  the  benefit
   of all sentient beings who do not have happiness and who are suffering, I
   will achieve enlightenment for them.” You generate this  thought  through
   the means of the ritual.

This is generating the aspirational mind generation that does not come  with
the commitment. That can be done by  anyone.  If  this  is  done  through  a
ritual, it is said that there are immeasurable benefits. If  developing  the
mind generation through a ritual is done well at the beginning of  the  day,
as the motivation for the  entire  day,  it  is  said  there  will  be  many
benefits.

When we develop this mind generation well  in  the  beginning  of  the  day,
whatever we do during that day will become a cause for us  to  achieve  full
enlightenment. It makes a difference to that day when you are able  to  this
well. It also makes a difference to your mind. Over  time  you  become  more
virtuous, good-hearted, accumulate more merit and  leave  stronger  imprints
in your mind. When you do  this  well,  you  will  be  able  to  assume  the
aspirational mind generation that comes with the commitment.  When  you  are
able to do that, there will come a day when you will also be able  to  adopt
the engaged bodhisattva vows.

When you are able to generate the aspirational mind  generation  well  right
from the very beginning, you generate the aspirational mind generation  that
comes with the commitment. Then comes the day when you are able to take  the
actual bodhisattva vows. By seeing all the benefits, you  will  be  able  to
train in the bodhisattva practices and, sooner or later, you  will  be  able
to achieve full enlightenment without much difficulty.

What is the aspirational mind generation that comes with the commitment?  On
the basis of generating the thought, “I want to achieve  full  enlightenment
for the benefit of all  sentient  beings,”  on  top  of  that,  you  make  a
commitment to never ever give  up  this  thought  until  you  have  achieved
enlightenment. That is generating the aspirational mind generation with  the
commitment.

Having made that commitment, it is  important  that  you  ensure  that  this
promise never degenerates. So you have to train  in  the  causes  that  will
ensure that this aspirational mind generation that was  generated  does  not
degenerate in this life. You also train in the causes that ensure  you  will
not be separated from it in future lives.

You have to train in the causes that will ensure that the aspirational  mind
generation that you have generated does not degenerate  in  this  life.   As
mentioned in the text, there are four trainings to be done  after  you  have
generated the aspirational mind generation with the commitment:

    2B4B-2B3B-2C2B-1A  [Deceiving the abbot, master, guru, or those worthy
    of offerings] (Page 161)


First black dharma: Consciously  deceiving  the  abbot,  master,  guru,  and
those worthy of offerings
Antidote: The first white dharma i.e., abandoning lying to any living  being
even in jest or even for the sake of our life.


    2B4B-2B3B-2C2B-2A  [Abandoning lying knowingly] (Page 162)


When  you  are  able  to  train  well  in  the  first  white  dharma,  i.e.,
consciously forsaking lying to any living beings, you will be able  to  stop
committing the first black dharma of  consciously  misleading  or  deceiving
one’s abbot, master, guru, and those  worthy  of  offerings.  You  will  not
deceive or lie to anyone.


    2B4B-2B3B-2C2B-1B  [Causing others to regret virtues that they do not
    regret] (Page 161)





Second black dharma: Causing  someone  who  is  cultivating  virtue  without
regret to regret what he has done.
Antidote: The fourth white dharma that is causing someone whom you  want  to
mature, instead of letting them enter the Hinayana, you are able to  connect
them to the Mahayana path

           2B4B-2B3B-2C2B-2D      [Ripening sentient beings] (Page 163)

When you are able to ripen someone by helping that person to connect to  the
Mahayana, at the same time, you will not have the thought of wanting him  to
regret his virtuous actions.

For the first black dharma, i.e., deceiving one’s abbot, master,  guru,  and
those worthy of offerings, it does not  matter  whether  they  are  actually
deceived or not. The very fact that  you  have  tried  to  deceive  them  is
sufficient to make it a black dharma.

In the same way, for the second black dharma, i.e., causing someone  who  is
cultivating virtue to  regret  doing  so,  it  is  said  that  it  makes  no
difference whether that person actually feels regret or  not.  As  long  you
have done something with the intention to cause that person  to  regret  his
virtuous actions, it becomes a black dharma.


         2  Clearing up doubts

As regards [/As for] these two, it is the same [/it is the same] whether  or
not [the person] is deceived and whether or not  [the  person]  has  regrets
(Page 161).

    2B4B-2B3B-2C2B-1C  [Saying that which is not praise and so forth [?
    Lit. indescribable] about a sentient being who has entered the
    Mahayana] (Page 161)


Third black dharma:  Saying  something  unpleasant  motivated  by  anger  to
beings who has generated the mind generation
Antidote: The third white dharma  that  is  developing  the  idea  that  all
bodhisattvas are the Buddha.





    2B4B-2B3B-2C2B-2C  [Generating the discrimination that bodhisattvas are
    like the Teacher and praising them] (Page 163)


The objects of the third white dharma are all bodhisattvas. We are  supposed
to think that all bodhisattvas are similar  to  the  Buddha  and  we  should
proclaim whatever good qualities they may have. It is difficult to  say  who
is and who is not a bodhisattva and where they reside. When you are able  to
train in pure appearance, by regarding everyone as a  buddha,  that  becomes
the antidote to the third black dharma.





         3  The way to express the qualities (Page 163)


With respect to the bodhisattvas, you are supposed to promote the  qualities
that they actually possess. As the text says, you are  supposed  to  express
their good qualities  in  the  four  directions  when  there  are  listeners
around. Otherwise, it becomes a fault. However, when there are no  listeners
around, you do not incur a fault when you do not praise the  good  qualities
they possess.

The essence of training in this third white dharma is to  express  the  good
qualities actually possessed by  any  sentient  being.  When  you  see  they
possess these qualities, you are supposed to proclaim them.





         2  How [there is] a need to guard against this (Page 163)


There  is  a  great  danger  of  speaking  disparagingly  to  a  bodhisattva
motivated by anger. That is a cause for going to the lower  realms.  If  you
are about to generate a realisation, it is said that your  realisation  will
be postponed when you speak disparagingly, out of hatred, to a  bodhisattva.
As I mentioned before, it is difficult to say  who  is  and  who  is  not  a
bodhisattva. If you should speak disparagingly to a bodhisattva, then  there
are these consequences.

When you are able to adopt the practice of not criticising  others  and  not
pointing out their faults, motivated by anger, and, at the  same  time,  you
are able to praise the qualities that they possess, since you  are  supposed
to proclaim the qualities of the bodhisattvas,  that  would  be  very  good.
This third white dharma is particularly important for  developing  the  pure
appearance of seeing everyone as a buddha.


    2B4B-2B3B-2C2B-1D  [An activity done out of deceit and dissimulation[?*-
     sgyo dang gyos nye bar- intention?] b  but without the extraordinary
    attitude]


Fourth black dharma: Without any sincerity, deceiving, or misleading  others
through deceit and dissimulation.
Antidote: The second white dharma  is  not  deceiving  sentient  beings  and
being honest with them.

 • What is deceit? It is pretending to have and the  wish  to  exhibit  good
   qualities that you do not possess.
 • Dissimulation (also translated as misrepresentation  or  guile)  is  also
   done to mislead and deceive others  through  hiding  and  not  exhibiting
   one’s faults.

Those who have generated the aspirational mind generation with a  commitment
have to abandon the four black dharmas. The way to do so is by applying  the
four white dharmas. This  is  the  way  to  ensure  that  you  will  not  be
separated from the mind generation in your future lives.





         B  The statement that by abandoning pride and the like the mind
         generation will not to be abandoned (Page 164)


It is also said that, in order to ensure that the mind generation  does  not
degenerate in future lives,  you  have  to  give  up  pride,  jealousy,  and
miserliness. You should cultivate joy when you see others doing well.


         C   If all actions and conduct are done conjoined with the mind
         generation, one will not be separated from the mind of
         enlightenment in all lives (Page 164)


By training in such a  way,  it  will  help  you  to  ensure  that  whatever
aspirational mind generation that has arisen will  not  degenerate  and  you
will not be separated from it in future lives. You must try to  conjoin  all
your activities with this motivation of the mind generation and  you  should
always dedicate your merit towards increasing this mind generation.

Remember the outline for how to assume the mind generation  by  means  of  a
ritual has three parts:

         1  Receiving that which has not yet been received
         2  Guarding that which has been received without degenerating it
         3  The method for restoring it if it has been degenerated (Page

Third: If you transgress the trainings, excluding the two of giving  up  the

Question from Khen Rinpoche: What is the meaning of this paragraph?  Why  do
we need to exclude the precepts of (1) giving up the aspiring mind  and  (2)
not mentally abandoning sentient beings?

Student: Because this constitutes the  definition  of  the  mind  generation
that comes with the two aspirations of achieving buddhahood for the  welfare
of others. When we give up these two aspirations, that  are  the  definition
of  aspirational  mind  generation,  we  are   actually   giving   up    our
aspirational mind generation.

When we do not give up these two aspirations, then  whatever  transgressions
we commit are misdeeds that we can repair  by  applying  the  four  opponent
powers. They do not constitute a total giving up or losing  our  bodhisattva
vow from the root. But if we were to give up the two  aspirations,  we  have
to  retake  the  vows  from  the  beginning  because  we  do  not  have  the
bodhisattva vows anymore.

Khen Rinpoche: OK. I think I got something out of  that.  Does  anyone  else
get something from what he said? Come up and talk.

We  are  talking  about  someone  who  has  adopted  the  aspirational  mind
generation with a commitment. When this person  gives  up  the  aspirational
mind generation or mentally abandons any living beings,  then  there  is  no
aspirational mind generation anymore. It is gone.

With reference  to  this  person  who  has  assumed  the  aspirational  mind
generation  with  a  commitment,  when  he  breaks  the  precepts  with  the
exception of those two that were mentioned -  mentally  abandoning  sentient
beings and giving up the thought of wanting  to  achieve  enlightenment  for
their welfare - it is only a fault and it does not  become  a  transgression
of the bodhisattva vows. It is not a bodhisattva downfall.

This is because this person has yet to take the bodhisattva vows[1]. He  has
only assumed the aspirational mind generation with a  commitment.  In  order
to  incur  a  bodhisattva  downfall,  the  person  has  to  be  holding  the
bodhisattva vows. What does this  person  then  need  to  do  since  he  has
incurred a fault by breaking the precepts? He has  to  confess  by  applying
the four opponent powers.

We have now completed the aspirational mind generation.

First: Although, having thus generated the mind [of enlightenment],  you  do

We are now looking at the reasons why  we  must  learn  the  training  after
having developed the  mind  generation.  After  having  developed  the  mind
generation, one has to learn  the  training  of  the  bodhisattvas,  without
which one will not be able to achieve enlightenment.  For  someone  who  has
developed the  mind  generation,  his  main  goal  is  enlightenment.  Since
enlightenment cannot be achieved  without  learning  the  trainings  of  the
bodhisattvas, this person must learn those trainings.

That is one reason why the person who  has  developed  the  mind  generation
must learn the trainings of the bodhisattvas. Another reason is
the need to discipline one’s own mind.


         2  First of all taming oneself, since others will not be tamed
         without taming oneself

Also [?] [Kamalashila says] in the First Stages of Meditation:

{195} A bodhisattva who has generated the mind, understands that  he  cannot
tame others without taming himself, and thoroughly applies  himself  to  the
practice of generosity and so  forth.  Without  them  enlightenment  is  not

The main job of the bodhisattvas is to benefit others. That is done  through

trainings of the perfections, without which they would not be able  to  tame
others.


    2B4B-2B3B-3B Demonstration that buddhahood is not achieved by training
    in method or wisdom alone (Page 165)


This outline refers to the need to train in an unmistaken and complete  path
in order to achieve enlightenment.





    2B4B-2B3B-3B1      How to train in the unmistaken and complete method

         A  The need to be unmistaken concerning the complete path

Second: In the Middle Stages  of  Meditation  [Kamalashila]  says:  One  who
engages in [/takes up?] the method for  accomplishing  buddhahood  needs  an
unmistaken method[?], for, however hard you try, a mistaken  path  will  not
lead to the [desired] result, just as pulling [a cow] by its  horn,  if  you
want milk. If, although faultless, it is incomplete,  the  result  will  not
come about despite your effort, just as [/in the  same  way]aa  sprout  will
not grow if either the seed, water,  earth,  and  the  like  are  incomplete
(Page 165).

When we enter a mistaken path, or even if the  path  is  unmistaken  but  is
incomplete, then no matter how hard we try, it will be  very  difficult  for
us  to  achieve  full  enlightenment.  Therefore,  we  must  engage  in  the
practices of a path that is not only unmistaken but must also  be  complete.
That is why it is very important to engage in the  threefold  activities  of
hearing, reflecting, and meditating on the teachings of the Buddha in  order
to gain a complete understanding of the entire path that is  unmistaken  and
complete.

         B  Identifying the complete path

Well then, what are the complete and unmistaken causes  and  conditions?  In
Vairocana’s Manifest Enlightenment (Vairocana’s Abhisambodhi) it says:

         Oh, Lord of the Secret, the exalted wisdom  of  omnisicence  arises
         from the root that is compassion. It arises from the cause that  is
         the mind of enlightenment. It is consummated by method [?].


In this regard  “compassion”  has  already  been  explained.  “The  mind  of
enlightenment”  is  the  two:  the  conventional  and   ultimate   mind   of
enlightenment. “Method” is fully perfected generosity  and  so  forth.  This
was explained by the great trailblazer Kamalashila (Page 165).

Enlightenment has compassion as its root. The cause of enlightenment is  the
mind generation: the conventional and the ultimate mind generation:
 • For the conventional mind generation,  we  have  already  looked  at  the
   Mahayana mind generation.
 • The ultimate mind generation refers to the direct perception of emptiness
   in the continuum of the arya Mahayanist.
In order to train for enlightenment, we must train in the path that  unifies
both method and wisdom. It is not sufficient to train in  method  or  wisdom
alone.

Then the text brings up a wrong conception (or misconception).


    2B4B-2B3B-3B2      Refutation of wrong conceptions about this

         A  Wrong modes of conception
         B  Their refutation

    2B4B-2B3B-3B2A     Wrong modes of conception

         1  Wrong modes of apprehension

One such mistaken conception regarding the path is held up  by  the  Chinese
Hashang and others who say: “As long as they are discursive  thoughts  –  no
need to talk about it if they are bad conceptions,  but  even  if  they  are
good conceptions – they will bind us in cyclic existence.  Therefore,  their
results cannot transcend cyclic existence, just as one can be  bound  [/tied
down] with fetters of gold or ropes, {196} and just as both white and  black
clouds obscure the sky, and just as pain is produced regardless  of  whether
one is bitten by a white or a black dog. That is why just equipoise  without
conceptions  about  anything  is  the  path[?]  to  buddhahood.  Generosity,
ethical discipline,[/ethics] and the like were only taught for  the  welfare
[/benefit]of  dull  [?blun  po]  beings  incapable  of  meditating  on  this
definitive meaning. Therefore,  having  found  the  definitive  meaning,  to
engage in[?] those conducts is like a king  becoming  a  commoner  or  like,
having found the elephant, looking for its footprints” (Pages 165 – 166).

The misconception is this:  You  shouldn’t  think.  All  thoughts  are  bad,
whether virtuous or non-virtuous. This is because once you  start  thinking,
your mind will be  disturbed.  When  your  mind  is  disturbed,  you  cannot
achieve enlightenment. You need to achieve enlightenment without  your  mind
being disturbed.

Therefore, the way to do this is to focus on nothingness and to  place  your
mind in a state that is without  any  thoughts.  By  doing  this,  you  will
achieve enlightenment. Therefore it is not necessary  to  train  extensively
in ethics, giving, and so forth.

Khen Rinpoche: Sound good, doesn’t it? This  is  the  same  as  the  saying,
ignorance is bliss. Sometimes that is good. You don’t think about all  these
problems and you have inner peace. But is this true? Does it fit your mind?


    2B4B-2B3B-3B2B-1   How scholars refute the worst of[/the most
    misguided] wrong [/perverted] views

The statement by them that “All the factors[?] of method are not  a  genuine
path  to  buddhahood”  is  a  grave  deprecation,  and,  by   refuting   the
application of the essence of the  Jina’s  teachings  –  selflessness  –  by
means of the wisdom of individual investigation, they abandon  the  mode  of
the highest meaning [erring] far away  from  it.  Although  Kamalashila  has
refuted them well with stainless scriptural  passages  and  reasonings,  and
although he has presented in detail the  good  path  that  pleases  [?]  the
jinas… (Page 166)

Ha-shang’s view actually deprecates the method and  wisdom  aspects  of  the
path.  The  wisdom  aspect  of  the  path,  i.e.,   the   wisdom   realising
selflessness, is the very heart-essence  of  the  entire  teachings  of  the
Buddha. In order to develop this wisdom, one must first engage in  analysis.
Without  analysis,  there  is  no  way  to  develop  the  wisdom   realising
selflessness.

If we were to adopt Ha-shang’s view of stopping all  thoughts,  how  are  we
going to analyse? Without analysis, how  is  it  possible  to  develop  this
wisdom  realising  selflessness?  Ha-shang’s  assertions  destroy  both  the
method and wisdom aspects of the path. By doing so, how is it then  possible
to achieve full enlightenment? His wrong views were eliminated by the  great
Kamalasila.

Ha-shang’s idea is this: when we talk about the  mode  of  abidance  or  the
ultimate reality of phenomena, it is true  that  that  ultimate  reality  is
something very difficult to describe. It is likened to  nothingness.  It  is
indescribable and you cannot compare it to anything else. That is  the  mode
of abidance of all phenomena. That is the ultimate nature of reality.

That being the case, Ha-shang’s view is that since ultimate reality is  like
that, then from the very onset  when  you  begin  to  meditate,  you  should
meditate on nothingness. Therefore Ha-shang’s view is that there is no  need
to think or analyse. You simply meditate on nothingness,  which  is  similar
to what you are trying to achieve, that  indescribable  realisation  of  the
nature of reality.
There is another view that believes that prior to realising  emptiness,  you
do need to engage in analysis. You need discerning wisdom to do  this.   But
once you have realised emptiness, you should remain in meditative  equipoise
on emptiness and you don’t have to employ discerning wisdom anymore.

These assertions are all  wrong.  We  will  see  how  these  assertions  are
refuted in the next lesson.

Question:  Someone  taking  on  the  aspirational  mind  generation  with  a
commitment and observing the four white and four black dharmas, that  person
would necessarily have taken refuge before, presumably with the five  refuge
precepts? The reason I am asking  this  is  because  the  very  first  white
dharma is avoiding lying and deceiving your abbot and so  forth.  I  thought
our refuge vow of not lying would already have taken care of that.

The second question is whether there is a  particular  significance  to  the
sequence in which the black and white dharmas are presented?

Answer: I  am  not  sure  if  there  is  a  reason  behind  this  particular
arrangement of the four black and white dharmas.

As for the refuge vow, when one takes the  refuge  vow,  this  doesn’t  mean
that that person is necessarily taking on any or all the lay precepts.  Even
if one takes the precept of not lying at the time  of  taking  refuge,  that
precept of not lying does not refer to just any form of lying. That  precept
of not lying is referring to not lying about abilities or realisations  that
normal human beings do not have so it is very specific.  Whereas  here,  not
lying among the four white dharmas mentions not lying even in  jest.  It  is
more difficult as it is more extensive.

I have been telling you that you have to read the text. You really  have  to
push yourself a bit and try to do this.  When  you  come  to  class  without
reading the text, we will have to read everything in class. This  will  take
forever. We will not be able to finish. When you don’t  read  before  coming
to class, I don’t think there will be much benefit.

So, as much as possible, please read the text. When you have any  qualms  or
questions, please note them down. Then when I give  you  an  opportunity  to
ask questions in class, you should be ready. I will give the chance  to  ask
question in class. If there are like ten people waiting  to  ask  questions,
that is the sign that people are reading the text. So far, there is no  sign
of this. Of course when we read the text there will always  be  things  that
we do not understand. That is why you ask and that  is  how  questions  come
about.
