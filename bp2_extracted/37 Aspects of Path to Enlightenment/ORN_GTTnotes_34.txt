EXPLANATION There are another three:  (44) application of separation by the maras, the maras causing a rift between the Dharma and the person, by coming to him in the guise of a trainee-in-virtue and saying, “The Mothers occur in my sutra scriptural basket, they do not occur in the extensive, medium, and brief Perfection of Wisdom Sutras,”  (45) establishing the counterfeit Mothers, teaching that meditating on ugliness and the like is meditating on the meaning of the mode of abidance, and  (46) generation of joy towards an improper teacher, the object of faith, producing faith in the maras (who have come in the guise of buddhas) as buddhas.  If the master and the student were to get together by way of being free from these faults which have been explained, the proper way of relying upon a spiritual guide, the root of the path, would will occur. Hence, the way to rely upon a spiritual guide is also indicated by implication. Indeed, having thought that the extensive explanation would be easy to understand, [Maitreya] did not include it in the explicit indication of the root text. This has been taught by the Acarya.  Here there is a discussion about maras. It is possible for maras to come in the guise of trainees-in-virtue, for example in the guise of a monk, a buddha, or a teacher. There is a saying, “There are maras where there is the Dharma.” There are two types of maras: outer and inner. The main one to be aware of is the inner mara. Of course, due to outer circumstances, the inner mara has the opportunity to manifest. Sometimes due to circumstances, the outer maras manifest, and as a result of that, the inner maras also increase. However, the main thing is the inner maras. If we can control the inner maras, the outer maras do not have such influence over us. Due to the inner maras, we have been experiencing suffering since beginningless time. The outer maras are not always present; they only occur occasionally.   In the last paragraph [in the middle of p. 393], it talks about how “if the master and the student were to get together by way of being free from these faults which have been explained, the proper way of relying upon a spiritual guide, the root of the path would occur.” It says “the way to rely upon a spiritual guide is also indicated by implication.” We have to be very careful with respect to the way we rely on a spiritual guide. The way in which we learn about the Dharma is through relying on a spiritual guide; without relying on a spiritual guide, it would be difficult for us to learn the Dharma. For this reason, it is said that the teacher, the spiritual guide, is the root of the path. We have to be very careful when we rely on a spiritual guide, because if we make a mistake [in this

 3 relationship], it is easy for non-faith to arise, and if non-faith arises, we create a lot of negativity. We really need to check our attitudes; it depends very much on whether we know how to rely on a spiritual guide.   At this time, we have obtained a precious human rebirth—a human body—and have met the Dharma; we have met very precious teachings that reveal to us how to attain the state of full enlightenment in one life in this very body. If we cannot attain buddhahood in this life, we have the opportunity to attain the state of liberation, or foe-destroyer; at the minimum, we should be able to prevent a bad rebirth, such as a hell rebirth, in the next life. If, having been reborn as a human being and having met the Dharma, we continue creating the causes to be reborn in the hell realms, this is really a huge shame. If, in our next life, we were to be reborn in the hell realms, the very fact that we have obtained a human body and met the teachings of the buddha would not been of benefit at all. In general, it is important to contemplate the possibility of attaining full enlightenment and liberation. However, we should also try to ensure that [at least] we will not be reborn in the hell realms in the next life. If we cannot even ensure this, what chance is there for us to attain enlightenment or liberation?   It is said that among all practices, the practice of patience is supreme. We practice with the goal of attaining happiness. In particular, we should be able to practice patience toward our own teacher. The teacher is the embodiment of kindness; the teacher knows the Dharma and teaches us the Dharma for the sake of our happiness. If we cannot see our teacher as being kind to us and as being a friend to us, there is hardly a chance to practice patience with regard to other people.   We need to be careful when meeting with Dharma teachers. If I talk about myself, I teach you the Dharma. Besides the formal teachings, I don’t meet you much, and when I meet you, generally you are very respectful and polite to me, addressing me as “Geshe-la,” and so on. This kind of respect that you show to me, a geshe, should also be extended to other Dharma teachers, because Dharma teachers do not have to be geshes; those who teach the Dharma can be laypeople, monks and nuns, and Westerners, so you should extend respect to them as well. I have seen this phenomenon where people treat Tibetan lamas and geshes with great respect, but when they meet Western Dharma teachers, they do not treat them with respect because these Westerners are neither lamas nor geshes. But slowly, there will be Western geshes and lamas!   It is important to treat any teacher with whom we have a Dharma relationship respectfully. If a Dharma relationship has been created, you should treat this teacher with respect—this teacher does not have to be a lama or a Tibetan geshe—so we should be careful with these relationships. Sometimes I see people who are very respectful towards lamas with whom they have no Dharma relationship, but this is strange! It is good to respect lamas, but this peculiarity that I observe is that people respect, as teachers, those who are called “lamas” but with whom they have no Dharma relationship, and yet do not treat, for example European Dharma teachers, with whom they have a Dharma relationship, as teachers; this is strange. As mentioned before, it does not mean that if the person is not a Tibetan lama or geshe, then this person who explains the Dharma should not be treated as a teacher. This expression “entity attribute” is used by this “other system.” In our system, we do not use this expression; we use “entitiness characteristic.” Likewise, the “functional attribute” is used by this other system. In our system, we say “functional characteristic.”   What you should understand from this passage here is that according to this other system of the past acaryas, the “very valid cognition which affirms the three—the characteristic, the object of characterization, and the basis of characterization—is ‘the characterization.’” How about the basis of characterization? In our system, if it is a knowledge characteristic, it is pervaded by being a characteristic; if it is a distinguishing characteristic, it is pervaded by being a characteristic; if it is a functional characteristic, it is pervaded by being a characteristic; and if it is an entitiness characteristic, it is pervaded by being a characteristic. The fourteen unspecified views  In this section there is an explanation of the “fourteen unspecified views.” What is the meaning of this expression? There were some non-Buddhists who posed questions to the Buddha. Since they were not suitable as vessels for [the teaching on the] selflessness of persons, the Buddha did not reply to their questions, so the views expressed in their questions are known as the fourteen unspecified views.   The first set of four views includes the views of the self of persons and the world as permanent, impermanent, both, and neither. These are views of non-Buddhist sages who developed clairvoyance through meditative stabilization. They were able to see many past lives, and could see that the god Brahma existed for a very long time, and thus they concluded that the self of persons is permanent. They also viewed the world as having been formed a long time ago, and since it still exists, they concluded that it is permanent. At the same time, they could see that the world is undergoing disintegration, and concluded that the world is also impermanent. There is also the view that the self of persons is impermanent; they could see that certain gods exist in a permanent way, until those gods meet with their companions, and when they have these companions, they die after some time, and so they concluded that these gods are impermanent, and thus that the self of persons is impermanent. Thus there are different kinds of combinations: some hold that the self of persons is permanent, some hold that it is impermanent, some hold that it is both, and some hold that it is neither. The same goes for the world: some hold that the world is permanent, some that it is impermanent, some that it is both, and some that it is neither.  The next four views are related to the possession of a limit, meaning a limit of the world: these are views of possession of a limit, non-possession of a limit, both, and neither. Again, this is related to those with clairvoyance, who conclude that the world has a limit, has no limit, has both, or has neither. Why do some conclude that the world possesses a limit? They look in a “horizontal way,” i.e. at the horizon, and see that there is a limit, so they conclude that there is a limit. Others, when

 52 When it says: “Having eliminated superimpositions by way of hearing and thinking…” understand that just eliminating such superimpositions by hearing and thinking will not cause one to be in the partial concordance with liberation. On top of eliminating the superimpositions by hearing and thinking, one needs to produce an experience. The text says, “after that, by having practiced them, an experience with respect to all the clear realizations will be produced.” Only after one has produced this experience is one considered to have entered the partial concordance with liberation.   We talk about this experience being produced, but what is the criterion for producing it? The text says “Through observing all sentient beings, one spontaneously engages in compassion bearing the aspect of desiring them to be utterly released from suffering….” This refers to the criterion of having produced the experience of great compassion. In other words, one produces this wish that sentient beings be free from suffering, like the compassion a mother would have for her only child. If one can produce such an experience, one has the experience of [great] compassion. If a mother has only one child, she would have very strong compassion for the child. If one can produce an experience of compassion of this degree of intensity with respect to all sentient beings, then one is considered to have elicited the experience of great compassion.   The text says, “Inferring from this, one should understand the boundaries where one has elicited experience with respect to other paths.” This means that if you want to know the criteria for eliciting experience with regard to other paths, you should infer them from this passage. In other words, if one is able to produce a spontaneous, effortless experience, one has elicited experience with respect to that particular path. If a path needs to be created with effort (i.e. if it is contrived), then one has not elicited the experience; however, if it is uncontrived, then one has elicited the experience with respect to that path.    In the second paragraph, the criterion for having produced an experience with respect to emptiness is described. “With regard to the meaning of emptiness, one familiarizes oneself with it by way of a wisdom arisen from contemplation. Due to the imprint of that, when one views these things, they appear to the mind like the reflection of a face in a mirror…” Here an example is given: when one views phenomena, “they appear to the mind like the reflection of a face in a mirror.” If there is a reflection of a face in a mirror, and if one were to blow on it, then the mirror would steam up and one would not be able to see the reflection; it would be as if the reflection had disappeared. Of course, in this case of a reflection of a face in a mirror, we are using the eye consciousness to perceive it; analogously, when we view phenomena with the mental consciousness, if they appear like a reflection of a face in a mirror, such that if one were to blow on this physical reflection in the mirror, the reflection would vanish, then one is said to have elicited the experience of the meditation of emptiness with a wisdom arisen from contemplation.   In general, it is said that if you realize that the reflection of a face in a mirror is not established truly in the way that it appears [in the perspective of an eye consciousness], this does not mean that you have realized emptiness. However, if you realize that phenomena are like a reflection of a face in a mirror with respect to, or in the perspective of, the mental consciousness, it is said that you have elicited the experience of emptiness. The point here has to do with whether you have this realization [in terms of the way things appear] to the eye consciousness or the mental consciousness. If you realize that the reflection of the face in the mirror does not exist truly as it appears in the perspective of an eye consciousness, then of course you have not realized emptiness. But if you are able to realize this [in terms of the way things appear] to a mental consciousness, then it is said that you have realized emptiness.
