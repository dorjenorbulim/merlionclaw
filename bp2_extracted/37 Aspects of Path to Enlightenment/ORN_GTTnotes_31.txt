As I explained a few sessions ago, the exalted wisdom of the Mahayana path of seeing is divided into various types: an exalted wisdom of meditative equipoise of the path of seeing, an exalted wisdom of subsequent attainment of the path of seeing, and an exalted wisdom of the path of seeing that is neither meditative equipoise nor subsequent attainment. The first of these, the exalted wisdom of meditative equipoise of the path of seeing, is sub-divided into three: an exalted wisdom of the uninterrupted path of the path of seeing, an exalted wisdom of the liberated path of the path of seeing, and an exalted wisdom of meditative equipoise of the path of seeing that is neither an uninterrupted path nor a liberated path. Here, the subject is the path of seeing of bodhisattvas, i.e. the Mahayana path of seeing, and the reasons mentioned in some of these statements are “because it is an exalted wisdom directly realizing that all phenomena lack true existence,” or “…directly realizing freedom from elaborations,” and this means it should be an uncontaminated exalted wisdom. According to the general divisions of the Mahayana path of seeing, however, there is, for example, the exalted wisdom of subsequent attainment, which is not uncontaminated.   Student: Is a direct realization uncontaminated, while the others are contaminated?  Geshe-la: If we have a direct exalted wisdom, i.e. an exalted wisdom that is a direct perception, it is necessarily uncontaminated, and the others, for example the exalted wisdom of subsequent attainment, are contaminated.   Student: If the meaning of contaminated is conceptual and uncontaminated is non-conceptual, then would an eye consciousness of an arya bodhisattva during subsequent attainment be uncontaminated, since it is non-conceptual?  Geshe-la: When we use the terms “contaminated” and “uncontaminated,” they only apply to mental consciousnesses, not to sense consciousnesses. Sense consciousnesses are pervaded by being direct cognizers and pervaded by being non-conceptual, but we do not use the terms “contaminated” and “uncontaminated” with respect to sense consciousnesses. A bodhisattva’s eye consciousness, in fact, is a mistaken consciousness, because to such a bodhisattva’s eye consciousness phenomena, such as forms, appear as external objects.   Student: Even on the pure grounds, the eighth, ninth, and tenth grounds? Geshe-la: Yes, there is the appearance of forms as external objects to the eye consciousness even on the pure grounds. If we explain this from the point of view of the Consequence school, we would say that there is the appearance of true existence to, for example, the eye consciousness. In the Consequence school, from the point of view of the appearance of true existence to the eye consciousness, we say that the eye consciousness is a mistaken consciousness; we would not say that because there is the appearance of external objects to the eye consciousness, the eye consciousness is mistaken. This is because in the Consequence school, external objects are asserted.  Student: The direct realization of emptiness is the antidote to the afflictive obscurations and knowledge obscurations, so whatever we say here is referring to that direct realization of emptiness, regardless of whatever words we use.  5  In the first two of the four noble truths, the way in which we are tormented by suffering and the way in which we circle in samsara are explained. By understanding the first two noble truths, we understand the faults of samsara, and thus we are able to generate the wish to separate from sufferings. Explaining true sufferings by way of specific and general characteristics COMMENTARY The aspects of (1) impermanent, (2) suffering, (3) empty, (4) selfless, {23} and (5) the fifth, the entitiness that is an aspect lacking characteristics; in that way, the aspects of true sufferings are five.   EXPLANATION The subject—the five appropriated aggregates— • (1) are impermanent because they disintegrate from the point of view of continuity as well as from the point of view of an instant of time; • (2) are suffering because they are objects to be discarded that do not concord with the aryas; • (3) are empty because they are devoid of a self who is an agent that is a different object; • (4) are the aspect of selfless because they are phenomena that are not established as the entity of a self of persons; and • (5) are the fifth, the entitiness that is an aspect lacking characteristics, because they are an aspect that is empty of a truly existent entity ultimately.  In that way the aspects of true sufferings are five. A bodhisattva’s exalted wisdom that directly realizes those aspects is an aspect of the knower of paths.  This outline says “Explaining true sufferings by way of specific and general characteristics.” The specific characteristics are the first four of these five: the characteristics of being impermanent, suffering, empty, and selfless. Why? Because each of these has their uncommon reasons. For example, for the first, it says that the five appropriated aggregates are impermanent because they disintegrate from the point of view of continuity as well as from the point of view of an instant of time; so there is this specific reason for their being impermanent. Likewise, for the second, they are suffering because they are objects to be discarded that do not concord with the aryas; and likewise for the third and fourth: there are uncommon reasons stating why they are empty and selfless. The fifth is a general characteristic. If we look at the reason—“because they are an aspect that is empty of a truly existent entity ultimately”—this reason, the emptiness of true existence ultimately, is a pervasive characteristic, and is thus a general characteristic. In fact, the first four characteristics—being impermanent, suffering, empty, and the aspect of selfless—are all also empty of true existence.   Impermanence  The first of the five points is: the five appropriated aggregates are impermanent because they disintegrate from the point of view of continuity, as well as from the point of view of an instant of time. You have to examine this reason extensively by thinking about how there is disintegration from moment to moment, as well as from the point of view of continuity. This continuity of the aggregates is not constant; it is subject to change. Besides thinking about impermanence from the point of view of continuity and from the point of view of an instant of time, you can think about it from the point of view of these aggregates being produced, being a product, being able to perform a function, and so on. There is much to think about from the point of view of impermanence. It is important to contemplate the reasons for impermanence carefully. Of course if you are able to realize impermanence, that is excellent, but even if you can attain an intellectual understanding, that is very good. You must therefore apply this reasoning to yourself. Just thinking about the impermanence of the aggregates in a general way is not beneficial; you must think about your own aggregates: how you have the five appropriated aggregates in yourself, and how they disintegrate in

Milarepa once had a conversation with a scholar who was skilled in philosophy, and in using logic to prove that sound is permanent, [by saying, for example,] “the subject, sound, is impermanent because of being a product.” Milarepa said that this scholar still grasped at sound as permanent, although he was skilled in terms of logic, and thus such logic was meaningless. Sometimes we see ads for beauty creams, where a beautiful woman says that after using these creams she looks ten years younger. When you see these media representations, you can develop wrong ideas; if you contemplate impermanence properly, you can eliminate such wrong conceptions.   In India, there were some students who were protesting against a rise in salary of their teachers, because if the teachers’ salaries increased, their fees would increase. During this, police came, asking “Why are you protesting? The raise is justified, because the cost of living is increasing; if the salaries do not increase, they will have problems paying their bills.” They said, “You should not protest against this; you should protest against the increase, for example, in the price of face creams.” This is just a sidetrack, but the point is that whatever we study must be applied to ourselves; not “the aggregates out there.” What we learn must be applied to ourselves in order to be relevant and beneficial for our practice. Sometimes we come across topics that seem very remote from ourselves, such as how high-level bodhisattvas train in meditative stabilization, improve their dexterity in meditation, and so on. You may feel, “What’s the point of studying this? It is not relevant to me.” We can apply such descriptions to ourselves, for instance, thinking about what we do in our own practice of meditative stabilization; do we make effort, or do we waste our time? Bodhisattvas are able to attain high levels because of going through hardships. Through this, we can encourage ourselves to make effort and to not be lazy, but to do something about our own practice so that we can eventually attain that state. We should think, “If I do nothing, how can I attain that state? I should not waste my time; I should do something. If bodhisattvas at these high levels need to train in meditative stabilization, it goes without saying that I need to train in it.”    Suffering   The second aspect is: “The subject—the five appropriated aggregates—are suffering because they are objects to be discarded that do not concord with the aryas.” We say that these aggregates are under the influence of karma and afflictions; therefore, they are objects to be discarded that do not concord with the aryas. We say these aggregates are suffering because of being under the influence of karma and afflictions. This is one aspect of true sufferings. We need to repeatedly contemplate this. If you understand that the reason for the aggregates being suffering is because of being under the influence of karma and afflictions, you understand that without becoming free from karma and afflictions, there is no way to be free of suffering.   In the lam rim texts, there are descriptions of many types of suffering; it is important to think about these carefully. We can also see that in the world around us, there are many types of suffering; we can also see many forms of suffering on the Internet. Thinking about these various types of suffering carefully, we can generate a powerful mind wishing to be free of suffering. Based on these descriptions in lam rim texts, we understand the various types of suffering. We should try to gain an experience by looking around and seeing how people in the world are tormented by suffering. We should think: “They have no wish to suffer, yet they are tormented by suffering. I am under the influence of karma and afflictions, and I have no choice with respect to taking rebirth; I am not completely free to choose my rebirth. What if, in my future life, I am reborn as one of these suffering people? What will I do if I take such a rebirth? For example, if my body is composed of half-wood and half-flesh (like the example I gave before), what will I do?”

Aspect of cessation COMMENTARY The entitiness that is the aspect of cessation by ceasing the things that are (1) the inner, (2) the outer, and (3) both is of the three aspects of the emptinesses of: (1) the inner, (2) the outer, and (3) both.  EXPLANATION The subject—the entitiness that is the aspect of cessation included within a separation in which the seeds of the knowledge obscurations have been abandoned—is of the three aspects of cessations that are the emptinesses of: (1) the inner, (2) the outer, and (3) both, because it is a separation that is distinguished by ceasing adherence to the things that are (1) the inner, (2) the outer, and (3) both as truly existent.  Here we are talking about true cessations. In general, there are many levels of true cessations. For example, on the path to enlightenment, one abandons the objects to be abandoned by the path of meditation, and we talk about nine levels of these objects of abandonment. Corresponding to each of these nine levels, there is a true cessation. However, here we are talking about true cessations in general, without specifying a specific level. Bear this in mind as we are going through this passage; we are not talking about specific true cessations; we are talking about true cessations in general.   In the passage we just went through, the emptinesses of the inner, the outer, and both are mentioned. To remind you, when we say the emptiness of the inner, this is the emptiness of true existence of the eye primary consciousness, ear primary consciousness, etc.—the six primary consciousnesses. The emptiness of the outer is the emptiness of true existence of forms, sounds, odors, and so forth. The emptiness of both is the emptiness of true existence of the sense powers, such as the eye sense power. These sense powers are “inner” relative to forms, etc., but “outer” in relation to the eye consciousness, and so forth; thus they are said to be “both inner and outer.”   The syllogism above says, “the entitiness that is the aspect of cessation included within a separation in which the seeds of the knowledge obscurations have been abandoned—is of the three aspects of cessations…” So this aspect of cessation is of the three aspects of cessation which are these three emptinesses, and the reason given is “because it is a separation that is distinguished by ceasing adherence to the things that are (1) the inner, (2) the outer, and (3) both as truly existent.” This is saying that this aspect of cessation is of the three aspects of the three emptinesses because it is a separation that has ceased adherence to the things that are (1) the inner, (2) the outer, and (3) both as truly existent. These three things are the bases of the three emptinesses.

 12  These three types of emptiness are indicated as aspects of true cessations. Does this mean that emptiness is true cessation, or that true cessation is emptiness? The answer is “no.” If emptiness is not true cessation and true cessation is not emptiness, what is the reason for indicating that the three types of emptiness are aspects of true cessations here? What do we mean by true cessations here? It refers to the cessation of adherence to the things that are inner, outer, and both as truly existent. Through ceasing adherence to these things as truly existent, one establishes the emptiness of the inner, the outer, and both; for this reason, these three types of emptiness are indicated as aspects of true cessations.   The adherence to things that are inner, outer, and both as truly existent are adventitious stains. By ceasing this adherence, one eliminates the adventitious stains. By eliminating these stains, one also purifies the stains that are of the nature, or the natural stains. Through the process of eliminating the adventitious stains, the nature of the mind becomes more and more clear; the natural purity of the mind becomes more and more pure. For the adventitious stains, we posit the conception of true existence; these obscure the natural purity of the mind. There are many levels of adventitious stains, since there are many levels of the conception of true existence—gross, subtle, and so forth. By removing these adventitious stains, which obscure the seeing of the nature of the mind, one is able to see the nature of the mind more and more clearly, and in that way we say that the purity of the mind becomes more and more pure. Of course, the stains have not entered the nature of the mind, but the purity of the mind is obscured by stains. As much as we are able to remove these stains, that much the obscurations obscuring the nature of the mind are eliminated. When we talk about true cessations, this is the essential point to understand.   Using your intelligence, you should think about this aspect of true cessations in an extensive way, and by doing so you may come up with other ways of understanding the true cessations that we are discussing. Of course, when you apply your own reasoning to this topic, not all of it is correct; you may have some mistaken ideas. But that is not a problem, because nobody is completely right from the beginning—it is through this process of contemplating and applying your own intelligence that you arrive at a correct conclusion, and it is in this way that you improve your understanding. It is important to contemplate these points; if you don’t think about them at all, your mind does not improve. You need to train in thinking and applying your intelligence, and in that way, you will be able to improve. However, you must be careful! If you think too much, the winds in your body may go out of balance and it can be disturbing. Watch your physical condition, and do the appropriate amount of contemplation. Close placement of mindfulness on phenomena Each of these four close placements of mindfulness is to be meditated on, or cultivated, by examining the specific characteristics and the general characteristics. What are the specific characteristics and the general characteristics? They are described as follows [starting on the bottom of page 361]: “the body being unclean, feelings being suffering, minds being impermanent, and phenomena being selfless.” For the general characteristics, it says “impermanence, suffering, emptiness, selflessness, and emptiness of true existence.”   The definition of a close placement of mindfulness has not been stated here, but Jetsun Chokyi Gyeltsen’s General Meaning says it is “an exalted knower of one who has entered the path that is included within either mindfulness or wisdom that, through examining the specific and general characteristics of each of the four—body, feeling, minds, and phenomena—meditates on them.” We have mentioned these various characteristics, such as the body being unclean, etc. It is not enough just to know these characteristics—to know that the body is unclean, that feelings are suffering, and so on—one must remember them again and again. We must analyze them with wisdom and not forget them, but remember them, hold them with mindfulness, and meditate on them again and again.   Close placement of mindfulness on the body   As stated in the text [p. 362, second paragraph], “By closely placing oneself through mindfulness on the body, one will engage true sufferings, because having understood that the body is in the nature of pervasive compounded suffering through that, one will generate the wish to be free from it.” By closely placing oneself through mindfulness on the body, one understands that the body is in the nature of pervasive compounded suffering. If one does not do this repeatedly, one will instead grasp, or adhere to the body; one will not be able to remember that the body has the nature of pervasive compounded suffering. Due to adherence to the body, one creates negativities; due to creating negativities, one will again and again take on an unclean body that is in the nature of pervasive compounded suffering.   Here, we have the phrase “one will generate the wish to be free from the body.” Does this mean that we neglect our body, and don’t take care of it? This is not the meaning. To generate the wish to be free from the body means that through understanding that this body is in the nature of suffering, we try to separate from the karma and afflictions that cause this body. By thinking about the faults of suffering, we try to destroy the karma and afflictions that bring about this suffering. By destroying the karma and afflictions that result in this body, we are able to put a stop to the process of taking on this kind of suffering body again and again. You need to understand the meaning of this phrase correctly; wishing to be separated from the body means not wanting to take on a body again and again, forever. We have already taken rebirth in this contaminated, unclean body, but we

 29 should take care of it and make use of it, while at the same time we should generate the wish not to be born in such a body in the future.   There may be some of us who, due to our attitude and mental strength, think that with the body of this life we cannot attain the state of a foe-destroyer or a buddha. This body that we have is actually a body in which the outer and inner conditions are ideal for Dharma practice. Even though we may not be able to attain liberation or enlightenment in this life, at least we should think: “I must use this human support to obtain a good support in the next life; with a good human support in the next life, may I work to attain liberation and enlightenment.” Of course, this body is contaminated, unclean and so on, but temporarily, it is useful; we should generate the wish to be free from this kind of body in the long run. Although we may not be able to attain liberation and enlightenment with this body, it is useful while we have it; we have perfect conditions for creating merit and for receiving a good human support in the next life. It is important to contemplate how the body is unclean and in the nature of suffering; by repeatedly contemplating these points, we can reduce our grasping and attachment to this body.   Regardless of what kind of meditation we do, mindfulness is crucial. If we do not have mindfulness, how can there be meditation? Mindfulness is important in meditation, but it is also important in our daily lives in general. This can also be translated as “memory.” We should take care to train in mindfulness, or memory, because it is important in our daily activities. For instance, Ngawang and Sonam, [two Tibetan men living in Pomaia] who frequently visit my house, often leave their things, such as their mobile phones, behind, and when they are reminded, they say “yes, yes,” but then they keep forgetting. This absent-mindedness is not a good sign at all; it is a sign of unstable mindfulness. One needs to train in having stable mindfulness. This is important for all kinds of activities, and of course for meditation. The mindfulness I am talking about here is not grasping. There may be strong grasping to the self, as a result of which you grasp to your possessions and you don’t forget them!   Related to this point is the practice of conscientiousness; we need to be conscientious with respect to our belongings and with respect to public property. At the Institute, we use things such as the cups and plates in the kitchen, and we need to be conscientious when we use them. Lacking conscientiousness is a kind of negativity. However, you need to differentiate conscientiousness from miserliness. Some people say that you are being miserly when you are trying to be conscientious, but mindfulness and conscientiousness are different. The second sentence in that paragraph [p. 362] says: “By closely placing oneself through mindfulness on feelings, one will engage true origins, because, having seen that craving is induced by feelings, one will generate the wish to abandon craving.” This refers to contaminated feelings of happiness and suffering, not to feelings in general. How does this work? When we have a happiness feeling, this induces craving that does not wish to be separated from the happiness feeling; when we have a suffering feeling, this induces craving that wishes to be separated from the suffering feeling. So we see that craving is induced by contaminated feelings, and having seen that, we generate the wish to abandon craving.   Close placement of mindfulness on the mind  Moving on to mind, the text says: “By closely placing oneself through mindfulness on minds, one will engage true cessations, because, having realized that the mind (that is free from a self) is produced and ceases momentarily through that, one will not have the fear that the self will be severed, so one will generate the wish to actualize cessations.” One understands that the mind is impermanent, and subject to momentary disintegration. This mind transforms over time, and it can transform for better or worse. In any case, the mind abides, and the person who is supported on this mind also abides. Although there is disintegration, it is not as though the mind and the person go out of existence. So when one realizes that, one will not have the fear that the self or person will be severed, so one will generate the wish to actualize cessations.   The existence of past and future lives is established by the fact that the mind has a continuous nature; it is not severed. By understanding the continuous nature of the mind, one will not be afraid that the self, or person, who is supported on this mind, will be severed, and one develops the wish to actualize cessations. In Dharmakirti’s text, Pramanavartika, which is a commentary to Dignaga’s Compendium of Valid Cognition, the existence of past and future lives is established by examining the nature of the mind. If one is interested in proving the existence of past and future lives, one needs to examine the mind. There are many reasonings in that text that prove past and future lives, but there is no need to go through them now.   Student: How does one actually meditate on the characteristic of the body being unclean? Is it like generating the concept that the body is unclean, and repeating it in our mind, or is there some other way to do it? Geshe-la: The way to meditate on the uncleanness of the body is to first analyze it with wisdom: is it the case that the body is unclean? You examine, and when you arrive at a conclusion you hold it with mindfulness and do not forget it. Thus mindfulness is preceded by analysis. As I mentioned in a previous session, it is difficult to meditate on the unattractiveness of the body. If there is an ugly body, we naturally feel repulsed; but if the body is attractive, it is difficult to see it as unattractive, so in that case we must analyze repeatedly with wisdom to understand this.
