Commentary COMMENTARY On all occasions—the first, second, and third attentions to belief which engaged in the perfection of wisdom, with the characteristic of the qualities which are seen in accordance with how they are believed—{433} respectively higher and higher by nine types individually, cause manifest joy and are asserted to be praised, eulogized, and lauded.  Thus these praises and so forth bear the characteristic of realizing facts just as they are in reality; they are not the entity of flattery.  EXPLANATION They are asserted to be praised, eulogized, and lauded. On which occasions? On all occasions—the first, second, and third attentions to belief which engaged with the aspect of conviction in the object of belief, the perfection of wisdom, with the characteristic of the qualities which are seen in accordance with how the Mothers are believed to be the source of the three welfares—by also causing manifest joy and making praise and so forth by means of the later ones being superior, respectively higher and higher compared to the former ones by nine types individually.  The subject—thus these praises and so forth—are not the entity of ingratiatory flattery not abiding in accordance with fact, because the praises and so forth are made motivated by that bearing the characteristic of realizing facts just as they are in reality according to how the excellent qualities exist.  I have explained the meaning of this passage last week. Just to clarify this passage, we are talking about praises, eulogies, and laudations, which are to be correlated with the belief of one’s welfare, of both welfares, and of others’ welfares respectively. One’s welfare refers to the truth body, both welfares to the complete enjoyment body, and others’ welfare to the emanation body. Actualization of the truth body is wonderful or amazing, so it is praised. Actualization of the complete enjoyment body is even more amazing, because besides benefitting oneself—the element of one’s welfare, it is also able to benefit other sentient beings—the other element of others’ welfare; so it is even more worthy of praise. Actualizing the emanation body is even more amazing, because it is able to benefit many sentient beings with pure karma. In that way, the praises become stronger and stronger with respect to these various states of buddhahood. Each of these—praises, eulogies, and laudations—has nine divisions, resulting in twenty-seven divisions in total. These praises, eulogies, and laudations are not in the entity of flattery, because they express facts as they really are.  5  In this section, we are looking at dedication—not just of one’s own roots of virtue, but also those of others—towards unsurpassable enlightenment; in this way, these roots of virtue are transformed into a branch of great enlightenment.   Let’s suppose someone creates virtue: we see that, and we rejoice in our minds, thinking “This is very good.” In dependence on this rejoicing in the virtues created by others, we accumulate virtue as well. It is said that if this other person is of a lower realization than us, we create greater virtue than this other person; if the person is equal in realization to us, we create equal virtue through our rejoicing; if the person has a higher realization than us, by our rejoicing we create half of the virtue that this person has created. This also applies to dedication: if we dedicate someone’s virtue, we also create virtue. If this person is of a lower realization than us, we create greater virtue through our dedication; if this person is equal in realization to us, we create equal virtue; if the person has a higher realization than us, we create half of the virtue that this person has created.  This practice of rejoicing is very profitable. When someone creates virtue and we rejoice, we create great waves of virtue without having to experience hardship. Instead, if we become jealous, we accumulate non-virtue.   Anger is very negative. It is said that there is no negativity worse than anger. Anger is sometimes compared to fire: although we may start with a small flame, it can become a big blaze; likewise, small anger can destroy a huge amount of merit. It is said that when one makes a long prostration, one accumulates a lot of virtue. If we consider the support who is a pure fully ordained monk doing a single full-length prostration, he accumulates the amount of merit necessary for taking as many rebirths as a wheel-turning king as there are the number of particles covered by his body, from between the ground below him until the foundation of the earth mandala. Even if you hold a handful of earth, the particles contained there are very difficult to enumerate. So just think: when this person makes a full-length prostration, how many particles are there that lie below his body all the way down to this foundation of the earth mandala? Many.   You may then think that this monk may attain enlightenment very quickly, but this is not necessarily so. Why? Because in the second instant, he may become angry and destroy those merits. It is said that one instant of anger can destroy the accumulation of merit of 100 eons. It may not be able to damage the accumulation of wisdom, but it is able to damage the accumulation of merit of 100 eons. This last statement is made by considering the case of getting angry with a bodhisattva.   How do we know who is a bodhisattva? It is difficult for us to identify a bodhisattva. An example is given: let’s say there is a big forest fire, after which everything is burned down. If we walk there afterwards, we need to be careful, because although the fire has died down, there are hot embers that can burn us. Similarly, we do not know who bodhisattvas are. There are some beings who look very pathetic, so we may treat them badly, but they may be bodhisattvas. With those who look very impressive, strong, and famous, we may not treat them badly. But, with those who look poor, we must be careful, because bodhisattvas do not wear “labels” identifying them as bodhisattvas.  We need to be careful because we never know who the other person is; the internal qualities cannot be seen on the outside.  There is a story that came from Tibet. Langdol Lama Rinpoche belonged to Choden Rinpoche’s kangtsen, or section in the monastery. Langdol Lama Rinpoche dressed very poorly, ate poor-quality food, and was very thin. Questions and answers  Student: On page 280, the response says, “There is no fault, because, although that very path of seeing that acts to pass through…” Can Geshe-la explain what it means that it does not realize the sixteen aspects? Geshe-la: “The path of seeing that acts to pass through” mentioned here refers to the path of seeing directly realizing emptiness. Such a path of seeing that acts to pass through neither realizes the sixteen aspects directly nor indirectly. However, in the continuum of this person there is a realization of the sixteen aspects directly. Moreover, such a mind directly realizing the sixteen aspects in this person’s continuum is conjoined with the direct realization of emptiness.   Student: What does it mean that dedication requires a substance of dedication, while prayer does not? Geshe-la: If you pray, “May all sentient beings be healthy, free from sickness, happy,” and so forth, these are not dedications; these are prayers. However, if you add this qualification, “In dependence on my virtue from having practiced generosity, ethics, etc., may all sentient beings be happy, may they attain liberation, buddhahood, etc.,” then this is both a dedication and a prayer.  Student: So what is the substance? Geshe-la: The substance is not a material substance; it is virtue. Making a connection COMMENTARY The things which have been well dedicated in that way, due to being objects to be increased, the third, the so-called “attention to rejoicing”:  EXPLANATION The things that are virtues of oneself and others which have been well dedicated towards unsurpassable enlightenment in that way, due to being objects to be increased higher and higher, the third, the so-called “attention to rejoicing”:  In general, when we create virtue, it is important to dedicate. If we do not dedicate the virtue we have created, it is uncertain that it will ripen into a result. Rejoicing is like a car driver [who directs the car to move to a particular destination, in that it directs the virtue to ripen into a particular result]. By the act of rejoicing, this virtue becomes more powerful. Sometimes we may have the thought, “I need to perform a great virtue,” and when we are unable to create a great virtue, we think “I should not do it at all.” We shouldn’t think that creating small virtue is a waste of time; in fact, regardless of the kind of virtue we create, what is most important is a pure motivation. You may spend a lot of time reciting prayers or creating other virtues such as generosity. We must be sure the motivation for these activities is pure. If we make recitations, give food, etc., without a pure 
Masters Program: Ornament Week 27: November 10-14, 2008  
 8 motivation, even if others think we are performing great virtue, it cannot be considered a great virtue, because it is polluted.   Let us compare two situations: (1) the entire earth being filled with hearer and solitary realizer foe-destroyers, each of them meditating on emptiness for 100 eons; (2) a bodhisattva meditating on emptiness for one instant. It is said that the bodhisattva will create greater merit than all those foe-destroyers. This is because of the power of the motivation of the bodhisattva; the bodhisattva meditates on emptiness for the benefit of all sentient beings, while the foe-destroyers are motivated by the wish for their own welfare, so the bodhisattva creates greater merit.   Regardless of whether we perform a small or great virtue, we must check that the motivation is pure. If we find the motivation is not pure, we must try to make it pure. The quality of the virtue is contingent on the purity of our motivation. We have to habituate ourselves with such a pure motivation, because if we do not try to develop a good motivation while reciting prayers, etc., we will develop bad habits that will be hard to get rid of. In the explanation under the root text 3.6cd (bottom of page 322), it says: “That follows, because it [an awareness of conventionalities] does not know that forms and so forth are conventional truths.” What is the meaning of this expression “conventional truth”? Let’s look at the etymology. “Conventional” (Tib. kun rdzob) literally means “all-obscuring.” This refers to ignorance, the conception of true existence, because this ignorance obscures the seeing of the mode of abidance of phenomena.  We talk about “conventional truth,” which is literally “truth for the all-obscuring.” An example of a conventional truth is a vase: a vase is a falsity in general, but it is a truth for the ignorance that is a conception of true existence. For this reason, it is a conventional truth, or a truth for the all-obscuring. When one realizes that forms and so forth are conventional truths, or truths for the all-obscuring, then one realizes that forms and so forth are falsities. Thus one realizes that the mode of abidance and the mode of appearance of forms and so forth are discordant, and therefore one realizes ultimate truth.
