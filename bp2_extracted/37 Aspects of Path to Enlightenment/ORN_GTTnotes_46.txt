Translator: Sometimes in the text, we see the expression “enjoyment body,” and sometimes “complete enjoyment body.” Geshe-la says that both expressions mean exactly the same thing; “enjoyment body” is an abbreviation of “complete enjoyment body.”  Here, we see the expression “signs and exemplifications.” What is the difference between these? A sign is that which characterizes a person who possesses it as an excellent being. An exemplification is that which makes known to others that the person who possesses it is an excellent being. In the presentation here, we will see the signs explained first, then the exemplifications. This is because the signs are principal in relation to the exemplifications, and the exemplifications are like members of the retinue of the signs. To understand what we mean by the signs being principal and the exemplifications being like their retinues, we can read through the text. After we have gone through the thirty-two signs and eighty exemplifications, we will see why they are described in this manner.   Explanation: Ornament of the Essence, p. 589:

EXPLANATION The subject—this form body residing in only Akanishta, the entitiness of the thirty-two signs and eighty exemplifications—is the complete enjoyment body of the Buddha Bhagavan, because it is an exalted body possessing the five certainties, utilizing the very unblemished joy and uncontaminated happiness of the enjoyment of the Mahayana together with its retinue, the great bodhisattvas residing on the ten grounds, and so forth. {368}   This passage is actually quite clear. There is the expression, “utilizing the very unblemished joy…” What is meant by “unblemished”? If we discuss what the blemishes are, there are two types: they refer to (1) natural misdeeds and (2) stipulated misdeeds. When we talk about natural misdeeds, they refer to negativities that are committed regardless of whether one is ordained or lay, such as killing. Both the ordained and laypeople are capable of committing natural misdeeds. Ordained people can also create the stipulated negativities; laypeople who do not hold any vows can create natural misdeeds, but cannot create stipulated misdeeds.   Student: What about laypeople who do hold vows? Geshe-la: Laypeople who do hold vows can also create stipulated misdeeds of contradicting, or transgressing, those vows that they are holding.   For example, if those who are ordained do not wear the lower robe properly, they create a stipulated misdeed. Let us suppose an ordained person engages in killing, e.g. an animal; [this person creates both a natural misdeed and a stipulated misdeed. The natural] negativity that is created can be purified by engaging in the four opponent powers of purification. [However,] if this ordained person

 5  In colloquial Tibetan, “lama” refers to someone who is a reincarnated person or teacher. The Sanskrit for this word is “guru,” which should be understood as someone from whom you hear or learn the Dharma. You may know the saying, “Vajradhara asserted that the guru is a buddha.” When you hear such a statement, you may think this is self-praise [on the part of the guru]. Vajradhara proved that the guru is buddha in this manner: the trainees do not have the fortune to perceive the complete enjoyment body, nor to perceive the supreme emanation body; the Buddha is supposed to be enacting the welfare of trainees, and the guru is enacting the welfare of trainees, so if the guru is not the buddha, then who is the buddha?   Among the explainers of Dharma, there are buddhas, bodhisattva aryas, ordinary bodhisattvas, foe-destroyers, hearer aryas, and ordinary people like me. His Holiness the Dalai Lama explains that viewing the guru as buddha is a training in attitude from the side of the student. For example, Serlingpa was a teacher of Atisha, and was said to be on the [Mahayana] path of accumulation. In terms of view, he was said to be lower than Atisha, who was on the [Mahayana] path of preparation at that time. Serlingpa was supposed to be a Vaibhashika at the time that Atisha took him as his teacher, and Atisha was a Middle Wayer.   It is difficult for every guru to be a buddha. If the guru turns out to be an ordinary person like oneself, one must be very careful not to see faults. It is very difficult to have a guru who is completely faultless, and from the side of the student, it is also difficult to have pure karma such that one does not see faults, so one must be very careful when relying upon someone as guru.   It seems that one or two generations ago, some lamas, or teachers, had amazing abilities, such as clairvoyance, emanation, and so forth, and it seems that the reincarnations of these lamas nowadays have deteriorated and lack these wonderful qualities that they used to have. The way in which a teacher manifests depends on the merits of the students; if the students’ merits are poor, the manifestation of the teacher will correspondingly be poor. If we perceive a teacher as poor [in qualities], we have to be careful.   When one attains buddhahood, one displays [various types of] bodies to benefit the trainees. The complete enjoyment body can be seen only by highly realized trainees; for disciples without that good fortune, emanation bodies must be manifested; for those who do not even have the good fortune to perceive the emanation bodies, even grosser bodies must be manifested, and [for this reason,] the buddhas must manifest even in the form of ordinary beings. [In this way,] the exalted activities of the buddhas become more and more coarse due to the poor fortune of the trainees.   It is difficult for us to tell whether the other side, i.e. the teacher, is an emanation body or not. From the side of the students, it is important to have a good attitude; if we have positive attitudes, we profit and do not create negativities. Suppose a teacher like Marpa existed now—would he have any students? Perhaps all of his students would escape! If a teacher like Marpa were here at the Institute, the word would spread that there was a terrible lama at Istituto Lama Tzong Khapa! If there were a teacher like Marpa, but no disciples like Milarepa, the Institute would become deserted and close down! The four means of gathering disciples are the causes for attaining the third sign. The four means of gathering disciples are (i) generosity, (ii) speaking pleasantly, (iii) enacting the welfare, and (iv) behaving accordingly with what one has taught. Generosity refers to giving whatever the trainees need. Speaking pleasantly refers to not using harsh speech. Did Marpa practice speaking pleasantly? That is an exception. In the perspective of Milarepa, whatever Marpa said was very pleasant. The third [means of gathering disciples]—enacting the welfare, or behaving in accordance with the world—means that even though one may have high realizations, one behaves in accordance with the needs of the disciples. This means that one behaves in a way that accords with the trainees; for example, although one has very high realizations, one does not behave in an aloof way that prevents the trainees from being attracted to oneself. This is something we do when we want to communicate with children: we speak like them, laugh like them, etc. If we behave completely in an adult manner, they will not listen to us. To communicate effectively, sometimes we behave as though we are children. The last one is easy to understand.

What should we do about this? Geshe-la: It is precisely because it is difficult to satisfy those who beg for wealth and possessions that the perfection of generosity is difficult to attain. Otherwise, giving would be easy. If the giving satisfies the beggar, then this is the best and wonderful, but it is not easy to do. One needs to train gradually in order to be able to do so. As one’s practice of generosity increases, one will have difficulty finding recipients of one’s generosity. Why do I say that? Due to one’s practice, one’s merit increases, and due to one’s abundant merit, one is reborn in very pleasant situations, where there is little poverty, etc., where there are no beggars who need things; thus one has fewer opportunities to practice generosity. This is why bodhisattvas pray to be reborn in the Southern Continent (this continent), in order to be able to practice generosity. This is the same with patience: as you gradually increase your practice of patience and become better trained, it becomes more difficult to find people with whom you can practice patience. This is why Atisha brought a very bad tempered person with him to Tibet. What does it mean to “set” trainees on a certain level? In accordance with the interests and dispositions of trainees, one has to explain the Dharma. One starts by explaining, for instance, the path of the small scope: how to abandon the ten non-virtues and cultivate the ten virtues; then one goes on to explain the middle scope, etc. In brief, one has to explain the Dharma in such a way that the trainees are able to generate the excellent qualities of realizations, such as the realizations of renunciation, bodhicitta, and the correct view. This is the meaning of “setting the trainees” on the various levels of the path. Other than that, it does not have the meaning of literally setting or placing them in the realizations. As a result of the afflictions becoming stronger, one’s non-virtues increase and one produces more negative karma. Because of the strong negative karma created, the experiences due to the fruition of that karma also arrive more quickly. From the conception of true existence, the conception of self arises, and from that conception, various afflictions arise. Related to this, we have the tendency to criticize or think badly of others; it happens very naturally. When you have this kind of tendency, merely thinking like that does not harm the other party, but this kind of habit is self-destructive. One harms oneself by engaging in these thoughts. By indulging in such critical thoughts, our mental attitudes and well-being deteriorate. It may be that by thinking badly of others, one will follow up with an action. Thinking badly about others may not harm others, but it does harm ourselves. Because of our long-term habituation with this tendency, it is difficult to get rid of. It is worth trying [to get rid of it], by thinking about the benefits of abandoning such negative thought patterns; if one is convinced about the benefits, then one will put effort into practice, in order to work on such attitudes. In this way, we have gone through the explanation based on the root text.   I mentioned patience just now. I would like to emphasize that if one is angry, this is an obstacle to generating realizations. If one has the quality of patience, one has the basis of generating the entire path. It is said, “Among all meditations, the meditation on the practice of patience is supreme.” Anger is the worst affliction. If one does not have patience, one cannot generate even a single excellent quality of realization; therefore, it is very important for us to train in patience. Cultivating patience does not simply mean not showing anger; one may be angry, but not show it, and instead try to suppress their anger. It can happen that after [doing this for] a long time, one cannot bear it any more, and the anger explodes, and one does a drastic, harmful action. One may even bottle up this negative energy and develop a heart disease; one may fall ill due as a result of not having the right method to work with anger.   As I have mentioned before, meditation does not always need to involve a pleasant object. Cultivating patience involves contemplating the shortcomings of anger: does anger have disadvantages or advantages? Through this type of contemplation, one should become convinced of the faults of anger. It is important for you to do this kind of analysis; without it, you might believe that anger has advantages. For example, if someone annoys you and you become angry and tell them off, and then they say, “I’m sorry,” you may think that anger has benefits, because it helped you to get rid of this annoying person. It is important to analyze carefully, to see whether or not anger has benefits. There are many situations we can deal with properly without getting angry.   We should work with our mind so that we can deal with situations without getting angry; in that way, we can gradually decrease our anger. For example, if we are in a bad mood, someone may ask, “Where are you going?” to which we respond angrily, “None of your business.” Of course, it is possible for us to respond without anger. During a weekend course, when I was explaining the practice of patience, a person in the audience said, “I can practice patience with people other than my family members; with my family members, it is difficult.” I said, “In that case, you are a person who does not wish to practice patience, because you say that you can’t practice patience with people who are close to you, while you can practice patience with people who are not close to you; that means you are able to practice patience when there’s no need to practice patience, but you can’t practice patience when there is a need to practice patience!” So when it comes to practicing patience, don’t think about practicing it with people who are far away—start with the people around you. If you are able to practice patience with each other, that is a wonderful start; if you do this, then you can practice patience with regard to everybody else.
