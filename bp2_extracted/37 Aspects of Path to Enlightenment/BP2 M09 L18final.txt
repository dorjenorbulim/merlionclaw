With regard to what I have said earlier:
 • The complete  aspects  application  is  cultivated  for  the  purpose  of
   acquiring mastery.
 • The peak application is an application that has achieved mastery over the
   cultivation of the compendium of the aspects of the three knowers.
This explanation was just for the purpose of helping you to  get  some  idea
of what these two applications are.

Look at the  definition  of  the  complete  aspects  application.  It  is  a
bodhisattva’s yoga conjoined with the wisdom  cultivating  a  compendium  of
the aspects of the three exalted knowers.

If you look at its boundary,  where  does  it  exist?  It  exists  from  the
Mahayana path of accumulation through the end of continuum (of the  sentient
being).

Your second and third examples are incorrect because you said that once  you
have achieved peak application, there is no  complete  aspects  application.
That  cannot  be  because  complete  aspects  application  exists  from  the
Mahayana path of accumulation till the end of the continuum  of  a  sentient
being. This means it also exists on the path of preparation.  It  is  taught
that if it is a path included in the  continuum  of  a  bodhisattva,  it  is
necessarily a complete aspects application.  So  a  bodhisattva  necessarily
possesses the wisdom that cultivates a compendium  of  the  aspects  of  the
three exalted knowers.

Peak application is a wisdom  that  cultivates  the  aspects  of  the  three
exalted knowers but its cultivation is more exalted than what  was  done  on
the path of accumulation.  This is because when one  achieves  the  path  of
preparation, one achieves the peak application that is the wisdom  that  has
achieved mastery over the cultivation of the aspects of  the  three  exalted
knowers. As such, a peak  application  is  necessarily  a  complete  aspects
application but a complete aspects application is  not  necessarily  a  peak
application.

SERIAL APPLICATION

The definition of serial application is a bodhisattva’s yoga conjoined  with
the wisdom that  serially  cultivates  the  aspects  of  the  three  exalted
knowers for the sake of attaining steadiness with respect to the aspects  of
the three exalted knowers.

Its boundary is from the Mahayana path of accumulation up to just  prior  to
the end of the continuum of the sentient  being.  I  have  mentioned  before
that the difference between serial application and momentary application  is
based on whether one has achieved steadiness with respect to the aspects  of
the three exalted knowers.

The bodhisattvas cultivate the aspects of the three exalted  knowers  either
through cultivating a  compendium  of  the  aspects  of  the  three  exalted
knowers or cultivating the aspects of the three  exalted  knowers  serially.
Both are done from the Mahayana path of accumulation.

Why do the bodhisattvas cultivate a compendium of the aspects of  the  three
exalted knowers from the Mahayana path of accumulation? They do it  for  the
purpose of achieving mastery in their cultivation.

Why do bodhisattvas cultivate serially the  aspects  of  the  three  exalted
knowers from the Mahayana path of accumulation? They do it for  the  purpose
of achieving steadiness with respect to the aspects  of  the  three  exalted
knowers.

Although the bodhisattvas on the path  of  accumulation  cultivate  serially
the aspects of the three exalted knowers, when  they  achieve  the  path  of
preparation, i.e. when they  acquire  the  peak  application,  their  serial
cultivation of the aspects of the three exalted  knowers  becomes  definite,
i.e., definitely, they are able to do so in the correct order. This  is  not
necessarily so on the Mahayana path of accumulation.

On the path of preparation, the path of seeing and the path  of  meditation,
in any one meditation  session,  the  bodhisattvas  cultivate  serially  the
aspects of the three  exalted  knowers  for  the  sake  of  achieving  these
aspects of the three exalted knowers. They  cultivate  them  serially  in  a
definite order without missing anything.

The bodhisattvas cultivate the serial application from the Mahayana path  of
accumulation up to just prior to the end of the continuum. This  means  that
at the end of the continuum, there is no serial  application.  So  there  is
serial application up to and just before the achievement of  the  wisdom  at
the end of the continuum.

From the Mahayana path of preparation up to just prior to  the  end  of  the
continuum, the bodhisattva cultivates serially  the  aspects  of  the  three
exalted knowers for the sake of achieving steadiness  with  respect  to  the
aspects of the three exalted knowers. In any  one  meditation  session,  the
bodhisattva cultivates serially without missing out any of the  173  aspects
of the three  exalted  knowers,  and  does  so  in  their  proper  order.  A
bodhisattva achieves steadiness with respect to the  serial  cultivation  of
the aspects of the three exalted knowers at the end of the continuum.

Khen Rinpoche: Are you following? Are you lost? No? OK.

When she achieves the wisdom at the end of the continuum,  that  bodhisattva
has achieved steadiness with  respect  to  the  serial  cultivation  of  the
aspects of the three exalted knowers.  Prior  to  that,  from  the  path  of
accumulation up to just prior to the end of the continuum,  the  bodhisattva
cultivates serially the aspects of the three  exalted  knowers.  This  means
the cultivation of all the 173 aspects of  the  three  exalted  knowers  are
done serially in their proper order, without missing out anything.  Then  at
the end of the continuum, the bodhisattva achieves steadiness  with  respect
to the serial cultivation of the aspects of the three exalted knowers.

These questions will then arise:  Why  does  the  bodhisattva  only  achieve
steadiness with respect to the serial cultivation  of  the  aspects  of  the
three exalted knowers at that time? Why  is  this  steadiness  not  achieved
prior to the end of the continuum? What is the meaning  of  having  achieved
steadiness with respect to the serial cultivation  of  the  aspects  of  the
three exalted knowers?

When you snap your fingers, the duration of that finger snap can be  divided
into many parts. A single part of that finger snap is the  shortest  instant
of a complete action. Here, we are  just  talking  about  an  instant  of  a
complete action, a finger snap. This is not the shortest moment of time  but
even within this short moment of time, the bodhisattva at  the  end  of  the
continuum can bring to mind any uncontaminated  phenomena.  The  bodhisattva
brings to mind one uncontaminated phenomenon  and  all  other  instances  of
uncontaminated phenomena that  are  of  a  similar  type.  She  is  able  to
manifest all of them in her mind in that single moment of a finger snap.

In other words, when one achieves the wisdom at the end  of  the  continuum,
whatever phenomenon is brought to mind, all other phenomena that  are  of  a
similar type are also brought to mind at  the  same  time  immediately.  The
bodhisattva has the capacity to do this.

Here we are trying to understand the meaning of  achieving  steadiness.  For
example, at the end of the continuum,  a  bodhisattva  brings  to  mind  the
emptiness of any phenomenon that is included within  a  thoroughly  purified
class of phenomena. When he brings to mind the  emptiness  of  a  thoroughly
purified phenomenon, the emptiness of all other phenomena that  are  in  the
thoroughly purified class of phenomena manifests as  well.  The  bodhisattva
is able to do all this within the duration of a finger snap. At the  end  of
the continuum, the bodhisattva is able to do this because  of  her  thorough
familiarization with the cultivation of the aspects  of  the  three  exalted
knowers. This started from the path of accumulation so it  is  a  result  of
such thorough familiarization.

When the bodhisattva achieves the momentary application at the  end  of  the
continuum of the sentient being, he achieves a steadiness  with  respect  to
the aspects of the  three  exalted  knowers.  This  steadiness  is  achieved
because of the serial cultivation that started from  the  Mahayana  path  of
accumulation. The bodhisattva has cultivated serially all  the  173  aspects
of the three exalted knowers over a long period  of  time.  Because  of  his
thorough familiarization with them, at the  end  of  the  continuum  of  the
sentient beings, within a short moment of a finger snap, the bodhisattva  is
able to meditate on all these 173 aspects.  This  has  to  happen.  If  this
doesn’t happen, how can we be enlightened? When we are enlightened, we  will
see all phenomena directly as they are. All this must be achieved  prior  to
enlightenment.

With regard to getting an idea of what the eight categories are,  you  study
their definitions and boundaries. If you can get a rough idea of  what  they
are, then within a short period of time, say, five minutes, you will  be  to
understand and explain this is this, and that is that. This  can  happen  if
you are familiar with the material, having thought about it  over  and  over
again.

The definition of momentary application is a bodhisattva’s final  yoga  that
has attained steadiness with respect to the aspects  of  the  three  exalted
knowers.

Its boundary lies only at the end of the continuum of the sentient being.

Serial application is presented in Chapter Six  of  the  Ornament  of  Clear
Realization and momentary application is explained in Chapter Seven.

Answer: In general, with regard to the three  exalted  knowers,  first,  one
would have to generate the knower of the bases, then  the  knower  of  paths
followed by the exalted knower of all aspects.

In terms of the two selflessnesses, firstly, the selflessness of persons  is
realized. This is where you have the explanation of  the  knower  of  bases.
After that, the selflessness of phenomena  is  realized.  According  to  the
AMWS, there are coarse phenomena and subtle phenomena.

When we talk about the selflessness of phenomena, we talk about  the  knower
of paths. Since first, one has to meditate in order to cultivate the  knower
of the bases followed by the knower of  paths,  I  guess  in  terms  of  the
serial cultivation of the aspects of the  three  exalted  knowers,  firstly,
one cultivates serially the 27 aspects of the  knower  of  bases.  Then  one
goes on to meditate on the 36 aspects of the knower of the paths.

It is the same as the practice of the lam-rim. Firstly,  one  must  meditate
on the path that is shared with the persons of small  capacity.  Only  after
that does one go on to meditate on the path that is shared with the  persons
of medium capacity.  On the basis of that, then one can  start  to  meditate
on the path of the persons of great capacity. The  order  is  definite.  One
cannot jump about. Likewise, when  we  talk  about  the  three  knowers,  we
cultivate the knower of bases, then the knower of paths and after  that,  we
go on to meditate on the aspects of the exalted knower of all aspects.

My personal thought on  this  is  that  when  one  meditates  on  a  subject
serially, in the proper order, through the familiarity of  doing  that  over
time,  there  comes  a  time  when  one  can  meditate  on   all   of   them
simultaneously. This can only come about because of one’s  habituation  with
a pattern.

I wonder if at the time of practising to achieve steadiness,  one  meditates
on the 173 aspects but not in the proper order,  i.e.,  you  start  with  A.
Then you meditate on Z the next day and then D on  another  day.  I  am  not
sure whether you will be able to meditate on the 173 aspects  altogether  in
the end. Even if you are able to do that, I  guess  it  will  be  much  more
difficult because the mixed-up sequence will come out together.

Question: What is the meaning of ‘conjoined’?

Khen Rinpoche: Does anybody have an idea of the meaning of ‘conjoined’?

Let’s take the bodhisattva superior’s practice of generosity as an  example.
His mental attitude is one of generosity and that  is  helped  or  conjoined
with the wisdom directly realizing  emptiness.  If  it  is  a  path  in  the
continuum of a bodhisattva superior, it is necessarily  conjoined  with  the
wisdom directly realizing emptiness.

When  the  bodhisattva  superior  practises  generosity,  his  practice   of
generosity is accompanied by the wisdom directly realizing  emptiness.  When
this bodhisattva superior practises generosity, he will not be  affected  by
the tendency to hold on to either the gift or himself, as the giver,  to  be
truly  existent.  His  practice  of  generosity  is  done  within  the  non-
objectification of the three spheres.

We are not saying that the bodhisattva superior’s  generous  attitude,  that
mind  of  generosity,  realizes  emptiness   directly.   Rather   when   the
bodhisattva  is  practising  generosity,  that   mind   is   manifest.   The
bodhisattva’s experience is unlike our own. When we give, we  strongly  hold
on to the giver, the gift and the action itself as truly existent.  In  that
respect, the bodhisattva superiors are not like us because in  their  mental
continua, they have the wisdom directly realizing emptiness.

Likewise,  if  it  is  a  path,  it  is  necessarily  conjoined   with   the
determination  to  be  free,  i.e.,  renunciation.  Let’s  say  someone  who
possesses renunciation in his mind practices generosity. Since he  possesses
renunciation in his mind, his  practice  of  generosity  is  conjoined  with
renunciation. Whatever he does, including the  practice  of  generosity,  is
never done for the happiness of this life.

If it is a bodhisattva path, it  is  necessarily  conjoined  with  the  mind
generation (bodhicitta).  This means  that  whatever  the  bodhisattvas  do,
naturally they do everything for the  sake  of  others.  Their  actions  are
never mixed with self-cherishing because whatever they do  is  conjoined  or
accompanied by bodhicitta.

Whatever recitations and practices that we do, they always start with  going
for refuge to the Three Jewels followed by  the  generation  of  bodhicitta.
Why? It is said that it is  very  important  to  adjust  and  set  a  proper
motivation in the beginning. That is why the  motivation  is  so  important.
Whatever we do, our practice will be affected by the type of  motivation  we
have. In other words, our  actions  and  thoughts  are  conjoined  with  our
motivation.  Then those actions would have their own results.

Likewise, when we are  learning,  listening  to,  reading  or  studying  the
Dharma, the motivation is also important. Depending on the  motivation  that
is conjoined with our Dharma  activity,  for  example,  our  motivation  for
learning or listening to the Dharma is to subdue the mind, then whatever  we
do, it will contribute towards subduing our mind.

It is not just being conjoined at the beginning. Then  when  we  finish  our
motivation and we enter into  the  actual  activity,  we  forget  completely
about the motivation. This is not what is meant.  To  be  conjoined  with  a
motivation means to be helped by it all the time. This means  that  whatever
number of hours we spend reading, studying or listening to  the  Dharma,  it
is helped by the motivation, “I am doing this to subdue  my  mind.”  Because
it is helped by this motivation, then whatever we learn, every  single  word
we read or hear will become a cause  in  helping  us  to  subdue  our  mind.
Likewise, when we ask or answer questions  or  we  receive  answers  to  our
questions, it is the same thing. Whether they become a  cause  for  subduing
the mind depends on whether they are helped by that motivation.

To say that an action is helped by the motivation, i.e.,  conjoined  with  a
thought, both the holder of the thought  and  the  help  must  be  together.
Otherwise, it cannot be said to be conjoined.  Conjoined  means  two  things
must come together. Something cannot be conjoined with itself. So let’s  say
when you are studying or listening to the  Dharma,  that  activity  must  be
helped by the motivation. Therefore, the motivation must  be  there  at  the
same time together with the activity.

Our usual idea of setting the motivation is that it is only related  to  the
beginning of the action. This is what we think. Even then, we  also  do  not
do it well. Generally, we set our motivation in the beginning, “I  have  set
my motivation.” But after that, we  do  something  else.  This  is  why  our
practice doesn’t get anywhere. As much  as  possible,  we  should  keep  our
motivation in mind.

Khen Rinpoche: I think the meaning of “conjoined” is something like that.
