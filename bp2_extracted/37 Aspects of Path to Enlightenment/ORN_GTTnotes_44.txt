The reason is because, since a person who has attained the clear realization of the peak, having taken cognizance of the meanings—(i) the aspects of the three exalted knowers that have been indicated in the first three chapters that are realized individually and (ii) the aspects of the three exalted knowers that have been indicated in the [fourth] chapter on [the manifest complete realization of] all aspects, from the aspects through to the characteristics, [that are realized] together—in accordance with the sequence of the modes of production, thoroughly cultivates [them], it has been indicated for the sake of that. Why is the serial application explained after having explained “the clear realization of the peak,” or the peak application? The reason for explaining the serial application after having explained the peak application is so that one is able to cultivate the aspects of the three exalted knowers for the sake of attaining stability.  3 three exalted knowers progressively. Why does one cultivate these aspects progressively? It is for the sake of attaining stability in the aspects of the three exalted knowers. We say that the aspects of the three exalted knowers are cultivated progressively for the sake of attaining stability in the aspects of the three exalted knowers. What does “attaining stability in the aspects of the three exalted knowers” mean? It means that when one cultivates a single aspect, one does not need to specifically cultivate the other aspects; one would engage in the cultivation of the other aspects without having to do so specifically.   Through the force of having cultivated the aspects of the three exalted knowers progressively, upon attaining the instantaneous application, when one cultivates the aspects upon a single basis, then, without having to cultivate the aspects with respect to other bases, one understands them. This is the power of having attained stability in the aspects of the three exalted knowers through the force of having progressively cultivated the aspects.   The application in complete aspects exists on the Mahayana path of accumulation. The serial application also exists on the Mahayana path of accumulation. This means that on the Mahayana path of accumulation, there is cultivation of the aspects progressively, and also cultivation of the aspects through gathering them together. Between attaining control over and attaining stability in the aspects of three exalted knowers, the attainment of stability is probably more difficult. The attainment of stability and control depends on the amount of training that one does: as much as one can train in attaining stability and control, that much the mind becomes developed to attain these qualities.   When we encounter difficult points in the course of our studies, if we think about them, try to understand them, reflect upon them, etc., they will become easier to understand, and with time they will become very easy. If, instead, we just leave them without making effort to understand them, they will remain difficult. There is a saying that originated from past high lamas, such as Kyabje Trijang Rinpoche, Kyabje Song Rinpoche, and Kyabje Ling Rinpoche: “The instructions remain in the mouths of others, and the understanding remains in the text.” This means that when we hear instructions from others, and if we fail to think about them and to try to put them into practice, basically they remain in others’ mouths; we are unable to adopt them and do something with them. The same goes for points that are explained in the text—when we read points that are explained in the text, if we do not contemplate them and understand them, they remain in the text. This is why we have this saying. Sometimes some monks in the monasteries have the attitude, thinking, “If I read the text, I will know this, I will know that.” This seems to trivialize the process of learning, because in fact the text has existed since a long time ago, and the fact that the text exists is not good enough for us to know something; we have to make effort to contemplate what is written in the text and to integrate the knowledge into our own continua. If the very fact that the knowledge can be found in the text were sufficient, then we would not have to study! What are the thirteen? [Those cultivations are] conjoined with (1-6) the six perfections, from generosity through to wisdom, (7-12) the six recollections remembering the Buddha and so forth, and (13) the wisdom realizing all phenomena as entitinesses lacking the thing ultimately.  Sutra says, “Bhagavan, a great bodhisattva practices serially, trains serially, and with diligence performs serially with respect to all, the entitinesses lacking the thing.”   Someone’s interpretation that the cultivation of these thirteen topics progressively is the mode of cultivation of the serial contradicts the Acarya’s text, because in the commentary below where the summarized meanings have been explained as six1, he explained [the mode of cultivation of the serial] to be the cultivation of the three exalted knowers progressively. Although “the meanings that are realized individually and together” [mentioned] in the section on making a connection of this [point above] are cultivated progressively, it is very clear that (i) the indication of the aspects of the three exalted knowers individually in the first three chapters, and the occasions of training in the aspects of the three exalted knowers and not completing [the training] slightly are [the significances of] “individually” and (ii) the indication of the 173 aspects of the three exalted knowers completely in the [fourth chapter on the manifest complete realization of] all aspects is the significance of “together;” cultivation of those [173 aspects] completely is the meaning of cultivation through gathering them together. {313} One must definitely assert that the cultivation of all the aspects of the three exalted knowers without exception in a single series having definite sequence, like a horse galloping, is the mode of cultivation of the serial.  Further, by means of the cultivation in that manner being conjoined with the thirteen topics, (i) cultivation on the occasion of the path of accumulation is serial activity, (ii) cultivation on the occasion of [partial concordance with] definite discrimination is serial training, and (iii) cultivation on the occasions of the path of seeing and the path of meditation is serial achieving. The mode of acting to be conjoined with the thirteen topics is to be just understood as the mode of being conjoined with the realization [of phenomena] as entitinesses lacking the thing, and one trains through: (a) not being separated from the recollection of the Three Jewels, the support; (b) recollecting ethics that is characterized as the turning away from discordant classes; (c) recollecting giving that is characterized as the engaging in virtue; and (d) recollecting the gods and the gurus as witnesses; one also practices all of those through being conjoined with the six perfections.  5 one recollects the gods as witnesses, it means that one practices in an honest way. One does not have the slightest deceitfulness in one’s practice; one practices in a very sincere manner. One does not hold what other people say or think in the highest regard; rather, one places the buddha aryas as witnesses, e.g., holding Shakyamuni Buddha as witness to one’s practice. Someone may think, “There is nobody watching. I can do whatever I want; I don’t have to be cautious,” and then when this person comes to a monastery and sees buddha images, statues, thangkas, etc., they think, “I should behave myself,” etc. This is not a correct attitude; one should have the awareness, or recollection, of the buddha aryas as witnesses all the time. One places the buddha aryas as the witnesses for whatever qualities or actions that one has or does not have, and one is satisfied, contented, with that.   We are surrounded by different kinds of people, some of whom say [various] things about us; sometimes people say things when they are unhappy, out of jealousy, etc. The main thing is that we should be careful about our behavior. What others say is not so important; as long as our behavior is not mistaken, there is no need to pay too much attention to what other people say.   In this passage, it talks about “cultivation that is conjoined with the thirteen topics.” What does it mean to be conjoined with the thirteen topics? Let me give you a simple example. Let’s say in the morning, after we wake up, we wash our mouths, teeth, faces, etc., and then set a good motivation for our activities. In the day, we may engage in various activities, such as studying the text, talking to others, cooking food, cleaning, etc. If we are able to keep the motivation that we set up in the morning throughout these activities without forgetting it, we can say that all of our activities are conjoined with this good motivation, and this motivation is able to benefit all that we do during the day until we go to bed at night. Let’s say in the morning one sets up the altruistic intention to benefit others. When one is conjoined with this motivation and does not forget it, if there is the opportunity to harm others, one will not engage in harming others; one remembers, “I had this motivation in the morning. I’m going to benefit others.” In that way, one’s motivation at the beginning of the day is able to benefit one’s activity during the day.   Among the thirteen topics, there is the perfection of generosity, and later in the list, there is the recollection of giving. There is likewise the perfection of ethics, and later in the list, the recollection of ethics. Is the perfection of generosity the same as the recollection of giving, and is the perfection of ethics the same as the recollection of ethics? If the perfection of generosity were the same as the recollection of giving and the perfection of ethics were the same as the recollection of ethics, there would be a fault of repetition in the list of thirteen, so to get rid of the repetition, we would need to throw out two, to reduce the list to eleven items. Is there a difference between the perfection of generosity and the recollection of giving? Is there a difference between the perfection of ethics and the recollection of ethics? In the second paragraph of the explanation [p. 553], it asks, “What are the twelve?” The first six involve the six perfections. One should undertake cultivation by means of being conjoined with the conduct of the six perfections. It is important for the six perfections to be present when, e.g., practicing the perfection of generosity. If, when practicing the perfection of generosity, it is not conjoined with the six perfections in their entirety, the practice of the perfection of generosity does not become perfect. This is because, if the discordant classes are present, one will be tainted by many faulty states of mind such as pride, jealousy, miserliness, impatience, etc. By making sure that one’s practice is conjoined with the conduct of the six perfections [in their entirety], one becomes free from the faults of miserliness, immorality, impatience, laziness, distractedness, and corrupt wisdom, and in that way one’s practice becomes perfect.   We see a qualm in this explanation [middle of p. 553]: someone asks, “In order to complete the ten grounds, does one not need the ten perfections?” [In other words,] why is it that only the six perfections are stated here? The response is that, because the last four perfections from among the ten perfections are included within the perfection of wisdom, there is no fault. It is said that the four perfections of (1) skillful means, (2) prayer, (3) power, and (4) exalted wisdom are included in the perfection of wisdom. What is the mode of inclusion within the perfection of wisdom? Does this mean that these four perfections are the perfection of wisdom? What does it mean to be included in the perfection of wisdom?   Let us take the example of the perfection of skillful means. How is it included in the perfection of wisdom? One example of skillful means is to rejoice in others’ virtues, instead of becoming jealous. In order to do that, one needs to rely on wisdom; through relying on wisdom, one is able to rejoice in others’ virtues, instead of becoming jealous. In that sense, we can say that the perfection of skillful means is included in the perfection of wisdom. Being able to rejoice in others’ virtues is actually very intelligent behavior because, through this slight action of rejoicing, which does not require much hardship, one can accumulate very powerful merit. The ability to do this depends on wisdom; this activity definitely involves intelligence of the mind. It is said that this action is reliant, or contingent, on wisdom. The next perfection is the perfection of prayer. Prayer here does not mean simply uttering a prayer. Prayer is followed up by practice. It does not simply mean merely making prayers and not putting anything into action, or not trying to accomplish anything. For this kind of prayer—i.e., prayer that is followed up by action—to come about, it is contingent on wisdom. The next perfection has to do with power. Power has the ability to not be overcome by its discordant classes; this ability definitely depends on wisdom. The last of the ten perfections is the perfection of exalted wisdom. There are many kinds of exalted wisdom, but in this context, the main exalted wisdom is the exalted wisdom of varieties, or conventionalities. This is not a wisdom realizing emptiness, but it definitely depends on wisdom. Based on these reasons explained, these four perfections—skillful means, prayer, power, and exalted wisdom—can be considered to be included in the perfection of wisdom.

[The text (middle of p. 553) says,] “The way of acting to be conjoined with the excellent attitudes [of] the six recollections is as follows.” The first is recollection of the Buddha. How does one perform recollection of the Buddha? One does so by means of the causes. What are these causes? [The three types of causes are:]  1. the close placements of mindfulness and so forth—this indicates the partial concordance with definite discrimination;  2. the cultivation of the aspects of the branches of enlightenment—this indicates the path of seeing; and  3. the eight branches of aryas’ paths—this indicates the path of meditation. From the expression, “recollection of the Buddha,” you might think, “I have to think of, for example, the mental excellent qualities of the buddha—how the buddha has great compassion, is very kind, etc.” From the description here, you can see that recollection of the Buddha is not just thinking about the qualities of the Buddha; it covers more than that. For example, we can think, “I want to attain the state of buddha, so I must create the causes. What are its causes? They are the close placements of mindfulness, the branches of enlightenment, and the eight branches of aryas’ paths.” The perfect way to recollect the Buddha is to recollect the actual causes of attaining the state of a buddha. As explained here, one recollects the close placement of mindfulness and so forth.   Likewise, recollection of the Dharma [point eight] is not limited to thinking, “Dharma is very holy and powerful. I go for refuge to the Dharma.” One has to (i) recall, or remember, virtuous activities and engage in, or adopt, them; (ii) remember non-virtues and discard them; and (iii) remember unspecified phenomena and leave them in equanimity. To engage in this recollection of the Dharma means to correctly practice the Dharma.   Recollection of the Sangha [point nine] means that one cultivates without being separated from recollection of the bodhisattva arya irreversible sangha as assistants. Someone who is at the stage of the serial application is able to practice this kind of recollection. For us, it is difficult to recognize who the bodhisattva arya irreversible sangha are. In any case, it is important for us to recall the excellent qualities of the bodhisattva arya irreversible sangha and then to try to attain their qualities; even if we can practice partially their qualities, it would be good.   In relation to this point, we can remind ourselves that we should keep company with people whose behavior is concordant with the Dharma. If we have many afflictions and our minds are out of control—i.e. we cannot have good control over our own minds—and we hang around negative people who engage in non-virtues, there is a danger of becoming even more negative and becoming like them, so it is important to try to keep company with people whose behavior is concordant with the Dharma. If we don’t even have control over our minds, we must be very careful with the kind of companions we mix with; if we have good control over our minds, there is no danger, even if we hang around very negative people, because, through our behavior such as our speech, we might be able to influence such people. When we try to help people who are negative, there is no need to directly say, “I’m a Buddhist. This is what I believe. This is good for you…” We can speak in a manner that is able to benefit them, without using any kind of so-called “Buddhist jargon.” Whether or not we are able to transform them does not matter. The main thing is that we try to influence them in a positive manner. When we explain things [to others], we try to provide valid reasons that other people can understand, so that we are not just preaching or saying things that we expect others to accept blindly. Recollection of giving [point eleven] involves recollection of the benefits of giving. Recollection of the gods [point twelve] involves recollecting the buddha aryas as witnesses. Making a connection by stating the relationship COMMENTARY Because of thoroughly cultivating, that is, greatly familiarizing, with the clear realization of the serial, since one cultivates those by way of a single instant, there is the manifest complete enlightenment of a single instant; further, that, since there are four types by way of the characteristics:  EXPLANATION After the serial, it is appropriate to explain the manifest complete enlightenment of a single instant, since—when one has progressively thoroughly cultivated, that is, excellently culminated the familiarization with, the aspects of the three exalted knowers, the clear realization of the serial—one is able to cultivate those by way of a single instant. The reason for that is because of having greatly familiarized with them for many countless eons. The subject—further, that—is of four, since there are four types by way of the characteristics in which one entity has been differentiated by way of isolates.  Here we have “making a connection.” Why is the instantaneous application explained after having explained the serial application? It is because the instantaneous application arises after the serial application. On the occasion of serial application, one progressively cultivates the aspects of the three exalted knowers. Because the modern texts are in the form of books, there is greater danger of putting them under one’s arm, [which is not respectful]; it is best if you can hold your books in front of your chest when you carry them. My teacher, Kensur Losang Wangchuk, used to talk about how the people in Kham had this practice of carrying a pouch containing an image, like an image of Tara or the Buddha, and how this was actually not a good practice because this image would be in a low position [i.e. hanging by their side, near their waist]. Also, nowadays we see people wearing pendants around their neck with images of His Holiness the Dalai Lama, Tara, Buddha Shakyamuni, etc. From the point of view of motivation, the people who wear these pendants may have a good intention and motivation, but the fact that it is at the chest level means that it is in a low position, so from that point of view such a practice is not so perfect; it is somewhat flawed.
