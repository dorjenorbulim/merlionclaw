 1  Masters Program: Ornament for Clear Realization Commentary by Geshe Tenzin Tenphel (week 32)   Monday afternoon, 26 January 2009  [Geshe-la gave practical advice about the need for rules and discipline in a monastery, watching the mind and not creating negative karma, etc.]  We have gone through the four close placements of mindfulness, which are very relevant for our present practice. Whatever we go through in the text should be contemplated. You should apply reasoning to try to understand the material well, and also read the lam rim and other texts. Actually, there is nothing amazing about being able to understand the text. It is important that whatever is understood is put into practice; otherwise, without putting into practice what one has learned, one just gathers a lot of intellectual knowledge, causing one’s pride to increase and one’s excellent qualities to decrease. Therefore, we must put into practice what we have learned.   During a teaching by His Holiness the Dalai Lama in Dharamsala, he mentioned that there are some people who have studied very well, have become geshes, and are skilled in debate, but who do not use their knowledge for practice by integrating it into their practice and with the lam rim, the stages of the path to enlightenment. After they have attained the geshe degree, they return home, and learn the practices of phowa (transference of consciousness), or chod (severing the ego). His Holiness said this is very strange because, having studied these great texts, one has studied the lam rim, and yet one goes on and does such practices, ignoring the practice of lam rim. Of course, he is not saying that phowa and chod are not good practices. However, when you have studied these great texts, it means that you know what the path to enlightenment is, so it is strange just to practice phowa or chod; these are good practices, but they are not so necessary, or essential, if one practices the path to enlightenment. In fact, there is no better phowa or chod practice than the practice of bodhicitta and the view of emptiness; if you have bodhicitta and the view of emptiness, you have the best phowa and chod practices.   Student: In the practice of chod, you break down the body and offer it, and you meditate on the three principal aspects of the path—renunciation, bodhicitta, and emptiness—so how can you say it is not a perfect practice of the lam rim? Geshe-la: Of course, if one does phowa and chod practices well, they are excellent practices, but this situation I’m pointing out is that there are some people who simply engage in phowa and chod practices without doing them properly. I am saying that there isn’t any practice that is superior to bodhicitta and the view of emptiness. I have an uncle who did chod practice from when he was seventeen, doing it very intensively and fulfilling various requirements, such as doing it in 108 various cemeteries, or charnel grounds, which are not just any charnel grounds; each of these charnel grounds has to have a minimum number of human corpses. He also practiced at 108 springs. After he completed those, he decided to go to Drepung monastery to study Buddhist philosophy. This uncle later became a geshe at Drepung and went to a mountain near Drepung, where there was a hermitage, and he became the “Ne Lama,” a special position of the hermitage. Every year, around Losar, he would perform long-life practices for His Holiness the Dalai Lama at Norbulingka, His Holiness’s summer palace. In 1985, he arrived in Dharamsala, and had an audience with His Holiness the Dalai Lama, who recognized him and said “You must do this long-life practice for me again!”

Four correct abandonings COMMENTARY  After that, because joyous effort with regard to engagement will arise, regarding the paths that arise from exertion: non-virtue and virtue, that which has been generated and that which has not been generated, respectively the joyous effort that is the cause of: (1) correct abandonment, (2) non-generation, (3) increase, and (4) generation, the four aspects of the correct abandonings.  EXPLANATION Having explained the close placements of mindfulness, after these, regarding the paths that arise from exertion, there are the four aspects of the correct abandonings. There is a reason for indicating them in such an order, because the joyous effort that is enthusiasm for discarding the objects of abandonment and adopting the antidotes with regard to engagement in discarding and adoption with respect to the four truths will arise.  Correct abandoning has four aspects, because there are four that are in the entity of the joyous effort that is the cause of: (1-2) correct abandonment of non-virtue, the thoroughly afflicted class which has been generated, and non-generation of that which has not been generated and (3-4) increase and generation, respectively, of virtue, the completely pure class, which has been generated and which has not been generated. {32}   Further, it has been taught in sutra that, for the sake of (i) abandoning non-virtuous qualities that have been generated, and not generating those that have not been generated, and (ii) increasing the virtuous qualities that have been generated, and generating those that have not been generated, one generates aspiration, makes effort, initiates joyous effort, strongly holds the mind, and sets the mind. “Aspiration” refers to the foundation for joyous effort. “Effort” refers to joyous effort that pays attention to the reasons for calm abiding, strong holding, and equanimity. “Initiating joyous effort” refers to initiating joyous effort in generating the antidotes that act to eliminate laxity and excitement. For the sake of understanding (i) the gaining of the four correct abandonings that have a special potency and (ii) the results of cultivating the close placements of mindfulness, the four correct abandonings are correlated with the middling level of the path of accumulation. In the first paragraph of the explanation [p. 363], we see the reason for explaining the four correct abandonings after having explained the four close placements of mindfulness. After contemplating the four close placements of mindfulness on the body, feelings, minds, and phenomena, one understands that true sufferings and true origins are to be discarded, and that true cessations and true paths are to be adopted. By understanding this, one generates joyous effort that is enthusiasm for discarding the objects of abandonment and adopting the antidotes with respect to the four noble truths. Because joyous effort will arise, the four abandonings are explained after the close placements of mindfulness. What are these four abandonings? 1. Abandoning non-virtue that has been generated 2. Not generating non-virtue that has not been generated 3. Increasing virtue that has been generated 4. Generating virtue that has not been generated If we have contemplated well the four close placements of mindfulness, we will understand the purpose of the four abandonings; without having understood the four close placements of mindfulness, we will not understand what the purpose of the four abandonings is. This is something we can definitely put into practice in our lives: (1) if non-virtue has been generated, we must apply some method to abandon it; (2) if non-virtue has not been generated, we must apply methods to not generate it in the future; (3) if we have virtue that has been generated, we should try to increase it; (4) if virtue has not been generated, we should try to generate it.   One point related to this: regarding non-virtues we have created, we have created many negativities since beginningless time, and it is not wise to ignore them, leaving them as they are; we must engage in a method to counteract them. If we do not do confession, purification, and restraint from repeating them, these negativities will not be purified, and they will definitely issue forth their results. Therefore it is very important for us to contemplate the nature of the ten non-virtues and to think about what kind of results they will bring, and on the basis of that, generate a very strong mind of regret for the negativities we have performed. Then we can do confession and practice restraint. You can engage in any kind of confession practice that you find time for, such as the confession to the thirty-five buddhas, the confession of downfalls, the recitation of Vajrasattva’s 100-syllable mantra, and so forth.   We need to be careful about negative states of mind, which does not [just] mean a mind that wants to kill someone! We generate many seemingly lesser negative states of mind, such as ill-will or malice, a mind wishing to harm the person, and covetousness. We should be careful and catch ourselves when such states of mind arise. We need to contemplate the faults of these negative states of mind; if we do not check them, there is the danger of engaging in a physical activity to harm someone; even if we do not do that, there is the danger that we speak in a nasty way to other people. These negative states of mind can thus potentially lead to great, heavy negativities, so when we catch ourselves thinking in a negative way, we must apply a method to reduce these harmful attitudes, remembering that these kinds of mind are non-virtuous; otherwise they can lead to actual activities of body and speech to harm others. Even if they do not lead to these activities, we still accumulate a negative action of the mind due to this negative mind.  4 unhappy; as it gets stronger, you accumulate more non-virtues. To give you an example, let us say we generate a mind wishing to harm someone: when we have this mind, we are unhappy, and if we see the other person being happy, we become jealous. If this person then tries to speak to us, we ignore them and we remain unhappy.   I’ve given this example of the negativity of speaking to others in a nasty way, but there are many other forms of negativities. For example, there is this microphone here [in front of me]: in order to raise it, you have to unscrew the knob. If you are careless and try to move it without unscrewing it, you will break it; thus there is the negativity of non-conscientiousness: due to your carelessness, you damaged public property. Then the Institute needs to buy a new one, and there are expenses involved. This is one negativity. Because the Institute needs to pay for the repair or replacement, you also accumulate the karma of stealing, since you caused the damage but the Institute pays for it. If you are a monk or nun, then there is danger for your vows. This is just one example: there is much public property here, such as the cushions, tables, etc., so we need to be conscientious when using them. The point is not to be miserly, but to be conscientious. By contemplating these points well, not only do you protect the Institute’s property or community property, but you also protect your vows.   There is an example in a sutra: a person was going for a walk, and was playing. He flung a stone, which hit someone and killed him. We have to be careful about what we do. It is possible that we go for a walk, and we just throw a stone for fun, or take grass and play with it. If we throw a stone, this may not kill a human being, but it could kill an insect or an animal, so there is negativity. If we actually do kill a person, for a fully ordained monk this constitutes a defeat, which is the end of your gelong “career.” You do not have the wish to kill someone, but there is negligence with respect to others’ lives—you just don’t care, and you commit a defeat. If you are a novice monk, you do not commit an actual defeat, but you commit a similitude of a defeat, whereupon your “career” is finished.   For those who are ordained, there are two possible negativities: natural negativities and negativities that are transgressions of vows. Laypeople may just have one level of negativity: the natural negativity, which they can possibly commit. In case you commit a natural negativity, this can be purified with the four opponent powers, which serve as an antidote. For the other type, a negativity due to transgression of vows that were stipulated by the Buddha, if it is committed, it must be purified through ceremonies such as the sojong [confession] ceremony. We need to be very careful. With respect to the negativity of killing, this is not so easy to perform; although there is the danger of performing it, it is not so great. In comparison, the negativity of stealing is easier to commit; there is greater danger of committing the act of stealing. Stealing with respect to community property can be easily committed; we thus need to be very careful when we use community or public property.   Without talking about public property, we also have to be careful about private property. You may borrow something, such as a pen, a book, a rosary, etc., from a friend, and hold onto that for four or five months, after which you think “I think my friend has forgotten about this, so let me just keep it and make it my own property.” Although you think in this way, the person from whom you borrowed it may not have forgotten, and thinks “My friend has borrowed this from me, and hasn’t returned it.” If you are fully ordained, and you think from your side “This is already mine,” you thus commit a defeat; if you are a novice monk or nun, you commit a similitude of a defeat. If you are a layperson who has not taken vows, you commit a natural negativity; if you have the lay vow not to steal, you also commit the negativity of transgressing the vow of not stealing on top of the natural negativity of stealing. Since there is this great danger that we commit stealing easily, we must thus be careful about not stealing, or more literally not “taking what is not given.” There is a Tibetan saying: if you give something to someone, you can even give a large gift, such as a horse or an elephant, but if you borrow even a needle or thread, you must return it. The point here is that there is a difference between giving and borrowing; if you give something, it does not need to be returned, but if you borrow, you must give back what you borrow.

Why do we say that we have probably done every possible kind of negativity that there is? Through examining our behavior in this life, we can see that we knowingly engage in negativities—i.e. we know that something is negative, but we do it anyway—and afterwards we don’t engage in confession and proper restraint, so from this we can deduce that we have committed every type of negativity. This paragraph has to do with a sutra explanation. Aspiration is the cause of joyous effort. When one has aspiration, this leads to the generation of joyous effort. In the passage “‘Effort’ refers to joyous effort that pays attention to the reasons for calm abiding, strong holding…,” “strong holding” refers to the holding of the mind. Basically, this joyous effort that pays attention to the reasons for calm abiding, strong holding of the mind, and equanimity is the “effort” that is referred to in the sutra just after aspiration.   This effort, referring to the joyous effort that pays attention to the reasons for calm abiding, strongly holding the mind, and equanimity, is thus of three types: • joyous effort that pays attention to the reasons for calm abiding;  • joyous effort that pays attention to the reasons for strong holding of the mind; and  • joyous effort that pays attention to the reasons for equanimity.  A bodhisattva tries to practice a union of calm abiding and special insight. If, from between these two, special insight becomes predominant, then the bodhisattva needs to contemplate, or pay attention to, the reasons for calm abiding. By doing so, the bodhisattva will reduce the factor of special insight; therefore, calm abiding and special insight will become balanced. If, from between the two, calm abiding and special insight, calm abiding becomes predominant, the bodhisattva needs to pay attention to the reasons for strongly holding the mind. If the two factors of calm abiding and special insight are equally strong, the bodhisattva pays attention to the reasons for equanimity and leaves calm abiding and special insight as they are. Since we talk about the union of calm abiding and special insight, if either becomes stronger than the other, this would not allow for a union of calm abiding and special insight. Thus we have covered the first two points, aspiration and effort.   The text then says: “‘Initiating joyous effort’ refers to initiating joyous effort in generating the antidotes that act to eliminate laxity and excitement.” The main antidotes to laxity and excitement are explained by the last two points [mentioned above: “strongly holds the mind, and sets the mind”]. The antidote to laxity is discussed here: “When there is laxity within the mind, as an antidote to that, one holds an uplifted mind through an attention that has become lucid, such as the contemplation on the excellent qualities of the buddhas.” One can contemplate the excellent qualities of the buddha to uplift the mind. Other examples of antidotes to laxity would be contemplating the precious human rebirth with its freedoms and endowments, or contemplating how one has attained the great fortune to study the Dharma and the great texts. In this way, one attempts to uplift the mind. Four aspects of the legs of magical emanation COMMENTARY Because possessing joyous effort makes the mind serviceable, regarding the paths that thoroughly train in meditative stabilization: the meditative stabilizations of (1) aspiration, (2) joyous effort, (3) intention, and (4) analysis are the four aspects of the legs of magical emanation possessing the applications that abandon. {33}   EXPLANATION The paths that thoroughly train1 in meditative stabilization are the four aspects of the legs of magical emanation. The reason for explaining them after the correct abandonings is because possessing joyous effort in discarding the objects of abandonment and adopting the antidotes makes the mind serviceable through meditative stabilization. There are four legs of magical emanation, because, as antidotes to the five faults, there are meditative stabilizations possessing the eight applications that abandon [the five faults]: the meditative stabilizations of (1) contact with a single-pointed mind in dependence on the meditative stabilization of aspiration, (2) contact with a single-pointed mind in dependence on joyous effort, (3) contact with a single-pointed mind in dependence on that attained from birth [due to] having previously cultivated meditative stabilization, the meditative stabilization of intention, and (4) contact with a single-pointed mind in dependence on discernment of phenomena that have been taught by others, the meditative stabilization of analysis.  The eight applications that abandon [the five faults] are: (1) aspiration, (2) exertion, (3) faith, (4) pliancy, (5) mindfulness, (6) introspection, (7) intention, and (8) equanimity. Paths of abiding happily in the present life COMMENTARY With respect to the paths abiding happily in the present life, regarding the nature of the path that abides in accordance with liberation, there are four aspects of the formless absorptions; and regarding the nature of the path that abides in peace, there is one aspect that ceases discrimination and feeling. In that way, there are five aspects.  EXPLANATION With respect to the paths in which the body and mind abide happily in this present life, there are five aspects. There are four formless liberations and one liberation of cessation in that way. Regarding the nature of the path that acts as the cause for a path of liberation and abides in accordance with uncontaminated liberation without being polluted by the afflictions of its own level, there are four formless liberations, because they are objects that are freed from the obscurations to relishing the taste of their own levels. This in turn is because there are four aspects of the formless absorptions included within the actual [absorptions].  Regarding this, although bodhisattvas enter into absorption deliberately to abide happily themselves in that life explicitly for the sake of others’ welfare, the object of intent, {42} they never actualized the fruitions of these [absorptions], (i) because bodhisattva aryas have fully exhausted the branches for taking rebirth in cyclic existence through the force of the contaminated karma of the formless realm, and (ii) because the formless realm is not a place for subduing sentient beings.   Regarding the nature of the path that abides in peace by having pacified coarse discrimination and feeling, the one liberation that ceases discrimination and feeling is actualized for the sake of becoming free from the obscurations to ceasing discrimination and feeling. Regarding this, hearer and solitary realizer aryas enter the absorption only for the sake of abiding happily in the present life.   There are five paths of abiding happily in the present life: • (1-4) Four liberations of the formless absorptions (the “four formless liberations”) • (5) The absorption of cessation  Here [in the last two paragraphs of this section] we find the distinction between bodhisattvas and hearer and solitary realizer aryas from the point of view of entering these absorptions. It says “Regarding this, although bodhisattvas enter into absorption deliberately to abide happily themselves in that life explicitly for the sake of others’ welfare, the object of intent, they never actualize the fruitions of these [absorptions]…” This means that bodhisattvas practice these paths of abiding in happiness in this present life—they practice these paths in which the body and mind abide happily in this present life—but they do so for others’ welfare; they do not practice such absorptions because of hoping for the fruitional results of these absorptions. In contrast, hearers and 
Masters Program: Ornament Week 32: January 26-30, 2009  
 29 solitary realizer aryas enter these not for the sake of others, as is said in the text: “Regarding this, hearer and solitary realizer aryas enter the absorption only for the sake of abiding happily in the present life.” These paths of abiding happily in the present life are such that in the continuum of the person who practices them, the mind is not polluted by afflictions. As a result of the mind not being polluted by afflictions, one’s body and mind are able to abide in happiness in the present life.   This is something we can understand through our experience: when the mind is polluted by afflictions, we experience unhappiness. If we are not able to subdue the afflictions, then this mental unhappiness causes the winds in our body to go out of balance; the winds either become too strong or too weak. As a result of this, the body becomes disturbed. Within our body, there are various kinds of elements. The body is composed of earth, water, fire, and wind elements; when the mind becomes disturbed, these elements go out of balance, and it is possible for us to fall ill. For instance, if the fire element is strong, we will experience heat illness; if the wind is strong, a cold illness. If the mind is out of balance, this will also affect our body. When the mind is disturbed, this will disturb the body, and of course if our body is unwell, this will shorten our lifespan.   The fifth of these paths of abiding in happiness in the present life is the liberation of cessation. This refers to a state in which coarse feeling and discrimination are ceased with respect to the mental consciousness. These four formless liberations and the liberation of cessation are all paths. Supramundane paths COMMENTARY  Regarding the supramundane paths, there are nine: the four concentrations, the formless absorptions and the absorption of cessation.   EXPLANATION Regarding the supramundane paths, there are nine serial absorptions, because there are nine: the four concentrations, the four formless absorptions, and the absorption of cessation that are in the entity of supramundane paths. The eight liberations can be generated into the entity of supramundane paths of bodhisattva aryas, while the absorption of cessation is posited as a supramundane path since it is attained through the force of a supramundane path.  Regarding the explanation of the expression “serial absorptions,” they are called such because one enters absorption into each of the later ones after having entered into the earlier ones; later on, when one has attained mastery over them, one enters into them also in a leaping manner. {43}   Other great trail-blazers, having accepted the meaning of the Sutra on the Ten Grounds (Dashabhumisutra), assert that the absorption of cessation is an exalted wisdom of meditative equipoise that is placed in equipoise on dharmata, but it is clear that both Acarya Haribhadra and Arya Vimuktisena assert that it is an attribute that is a non-associated compositional factor, in which the six collections of coarse [primary consciousnesses] along with their retinues have been stopped; someone’s assertion that it is the [state of] having stopped the seven collections upon the mind-basis-of-all is unreasonable, because in Haribhadra’s Great Commentary, merely the citation from Descent from Lanka Sutra, “The eight primary consciousnesses,” has been explained. Besides, there is not even a hint of explanation that there is a mind-basis-of all that is a different entity from the six consciousnesses. I shall explain the other meanings of the absorption of cessation elsewhere.   Let us examine the first sentence: “Regarding the supramundane paths, there are nine serial absorptions, because there are nine: the four concentrations, the four formless absorptions, and the 
Masters Program: Ornament Week 32: January 26-30, 2009  
 30 absorption of cessation that are in the entity of supramundane paths.” Note that this is actually just a terminological explanation, because the absorption of cessation is not a supramundane path. The absorption of cessation cannot be a supramundane path; it cannot be transformed into the entity of a supramundane path, but it is posited as a supramundane path because it is attained through the force of a supramundane path. In contrast, the liberation of cessation, which was explained in the previous section, can be transformed into the entity of a supramundane path.   Here we see the phrase “nine serial absorptions.” In fact, a person who has attained any of these nine serial absorptions is pervaded by having attained all eight concentrations and formless absorptions. The details regarding these nine serial absorptions will come in the fifth chapter, where there is an explanation of dependent arising. There is an explanation of different modes of the serial absorptions, some requiring alternation of the absorption of cessation, some not requiring this alternation of the absorption of cessation.    In the second paragraph, the etymology of this term, serial absorption, is explained: “they are called such because one enters absorption into each of the later ones after having entered into the earlier ones”—so these absorptions are attained in a serial manner—“later on, when one has attained mastery over them, one enters into them also in a leaping manner.” In other words, when one has attained mastery over them, one can skip some absorptions, e.g. one can enter the first concentration, skip the second, enter the third, skip the fourth, and enter into the absorption of Infinite Space. This is just an example; there are many ways to do this in a leaping manner, such as in a forward order, in a reverse order, etc. In this system, the first eight of these supramundane paths are in the entity of supramundane paths, but the ninth—the absorption of cessation—is not a supramundane path.   There are different views presented on what an absorption of cessation is: “Other great trail-blazers, having accepted the meaning of the Sutra on the Ten Grounds (Dashabhumisutra), assert that the absorption of cessation is an exalted wisdom of meditative equipoise that is placed in equipoise on dharmata…” This is the view of the Consequence school, which says that the absorption of cessation is an exalted wisdom of meditative equipoise on emptiness. It then says, “but it is clear that both Acarya Haribhadra and Arya Vimuktisena assert that it is an attribute that is a non-associated compositional factor, in which the six collections of coarse [primary consciousnesses] along with their retinues have been stopped”. This presents the Middle Way Autonomy school’s position; they say the absorption of cessation is a non-associated compositional factor.   We can posit the definition of an absorption of cessation according to Jetsun Chokyi Gyeltsen: a non-associated compositional factor that is an absorption in an arya’s continuum abiding in a class distinguished by having ceased the coarse concomitants that are objects to be ceased with respect to the subtle mental primary consciousness, the basis of cessation. This is the definition according to the Autonomy school. It is then said that “someone’s assertion that it is the [state of] having stopped the seven collections upon the mind-basis-of-all is unreasonable…” This is according to a Mind-Only proponent, who asserts a mind-basis-of-all; this assertion is said to be unreasonable, “because in Haribhadra’s commentary…”  Explanation: Ornament of the Essence, p. 370: 2B2C-2A1B-1A1B-3B2A-5.
