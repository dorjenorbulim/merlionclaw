If that were the case, it follows that it would not even be a mere excellent quality, {333} because it would neither be a final excellent quality nor a temporal excellent quality that is not that. If [you say that] the former [part of the] sign [i.e., that it is not a final excellent quality] were not established, then your position that it is not a buddha would degenerate. If that is accepted, if it were is not the excellent quality of final abandonment, then it would be similar in also not being the excellent quality of final realization.  OBJECTOR: The two form bodies are mere other-appearances of the trainees; they do not exist with respect to the self-appearance of buddhas and they are not included within the continua of buddhas themselves.  RESPONSE: Saying that is a very grave misdeed, because it follows that a form body of a buddha does not exist at all because it is not directly seen by an omniscient knower. We have already read this passage [in the last session], so let us look at the meaning. The first paragraph [bottom of p. 565] says, “Asserting that the nature body of a buddha is not a fully qualified buddha is a negativity greater than other negativities because it deprecates the exalted body possessing the two purities. If that were the case, it follows that it would not even be a mere excellent quality, because it would neither be a final excellent quality nor a temporal excellent quality that is not that. If [you say that] the former [part of the] sign…” The sign has two parts: “it would neither be (1) a final excellent quality nor (2) a temporal excellent quality that is not that.” The former part of this sign is that it is not a final excellent quality. If you say that “it is not a final excellent quality” is not established, “then your position that it is not a buddha would degenerate.” Saying, “If that is accepted,” “that” refers to the consequence above, i.e. “if that were the case, it follows that it would not even be a mere excellent quality.” So “If that is accepted” means “if you accept that it is not even a mere excellent quality;” [then it continues:] “if it were not the excellent quality of final abandonment, then it would be similar in also not being the excellent quality of final realization.” Why do we have this statement there? When you talk about an excellent quality, there are two types: the excellent quality of final abandonment and the excellent quality of final realization. If it were not a mere excellent quality, it would be neither an excellent quality of final abandonment nor an excellent quality of final realization. It is possible to have someone who actually makes this kind of assertion. The response to this is that this is erroneous speech propounding actions and results, in which actions that are done are wasted and actions that are not done are met with. In other words, someone cultivates generosity, morality, etc., but the results of that cultivation are ripened in another person. According to what the objector says, the final results are merely included in the continuum of a sentient being; instead of being included in the continuum of the buddha who has attained the results, the results are included in the continuum of some sentient being. This says that although one makes effort in cultivation, the actions of cultivation are wasted, and the results are experienced by someone who has done nothing in terms of cultivation. EXPLANATION The subject—the nature body of the Subduer—is distinguished by three attributes, because it is an exalted body that (i) has attained the twenty-one sets of uncontaminated qualities, the close placements of mindfulness and so forth: the attribute of possession; and possesses the two: (ii) purity in all ways in being free from the two obscurations along with their latencies: the attribute of separation, and (iii) purity in bearing the characteristics of being empty of true existence, the nature of those uncontaminated exalted wisdoms: the attribute of entity. {336}   Sutra says, “Subhuti, the uncontaminated qualities are like dreams.  5 are completely pure in all ways. This unique characteristic, that which is the nature of all of those, the absence of characteristic, is to be understood as the tathagata, foe-destroyer, perfectly complete buddha.”  Here we are looking at the nature body. Recall that the definition of a nature body is: a final sphere possessing the two purities. There are two types of nature body: (i) a nature body that is a factor of purity with respect to inherent existence [or inherent stains], and (ii) a nature body that is a factor of purity with respect to adventitious stains. Note that when we talk about the nature body that is a factor of purity with respect to inherent existence in this system, this refers to a nature body that is a factor of purity with respect to true existence; in other words, purity with respect to its nature being free from true existence. We have to interpret, or understand, this expression in the correct way in this system. In the Consequence school, there is no ambiguity; this expression can be taken as it is.   This paragraph [bottom of p. 567] points out that the nature body is distinguished by three attributes: 1. the attribute of possession 2. the attribute of separation  3. the attribute of entity  The attribute of possession refers to the possession of the twenty-one sets of uncontaminated exalted wisdoms, which [include] the close placements of mindfulness and so forth. The attribute of separation refers to purity in all ways in being free from the two obscurations along with their latencies. The attribute of entity refers to purity in bearing the characteristics of being empty of true existence, which is the nature of those uncontaminated exalted wisdoms. It is distinguished by (1) the attribute of possession: being uncontaminated through the entitiness of supramundane dharmadhatu, the nature of uncontaminated exalted wisdom, the close placements of mindfulness and so forth. (3) The attribute of entity: the entitiness of lack of production ultimately is this, because it is the entitiness of the nature that is the mode of abidance of those which are the uncontaminated exalted wisdoms that are of the characteristic of being devoid of nature ultimately.   It is not to be newly produced by causes, since it is an object that is not contrived by causes and conditions. It is not attained by any method, a mere mundane path and so forth, because it is attained through a supramundane path; since it is not a mere mundane path and [is not] created by conditions, it is attained through (i) being ornamented by extensive method and (ii) the realization of all phenomena as being free from elaborations by the illusion-like consciousness that directly realizes emptiness.  Regarding the attribute of possession, there are twenty-one sets of uncontaminated exalted wisdom. These include the thirty-seven harmonies with enlightenment. When we talk about the thirty-seven harmonies with enlightenment, are these supramundane? In general, they do not have to be supramundane, but when you talk about the thirty-seven harmonies with enlightenment included in the buddha ground, they are supramundane. Perhaps, at the beginning, one has the thirty-seven harmonies with enlightenment that are not supramundane—e.g. the four close placements of mindfulness that are not supramundane—but through cultivation, they become culminated in the nature, or entitiness, of the supramundane. If you read the description in the explanation [middle of p. 568], it says, “the attribute of possession: being uncontaminated through the entitiness of supramundane dharmadhatu, the nature of uncontaminated exalted wisdom, the close placements of mindfulness and so forth.” This refers to the twenty-one sets of uncontaminated exalted wisdom.   The second attribute is the attribute of separation: “being pure and devoid in all ways in being free from the two obscurations along with their latencies.” Why are the obscurations suitable to be utterly extinguished by antidotes? The reason is that “stains are adventitious.”   Regarding the third point, the attribute of entity, “the entitiness of lack of production ultimately is this, because it is the entitiness of the nature that is the mode of abidance of those which are the uncontaminated exalted wisdoms that are of the characteristic of being devoid of nature ultimately.” In brief, the nature body is empty of true existence; this is the attribute of entity.   In the next paragraph, it says, “It is not to be newly produced by causes, since it is an object that is not contrived by causes and conditions.” It is pacified from the beginning. The nature body is emptiness of true existence [of a buddha’s mind]. This emptiness of true existence is not something that is newly produced by causes; as soon as the exalted wisdom truth body comes into existence, the nature body comes into existence. The next sentence, “It is not attained by any method,” means that the nature body is attained, but not attained by any method; it is attained through a supramundane path. The text continues, “since it is not a mere mundane path and [is not] created by conditions, it is attained through (i) being ornamented by extensive method and (ii) the realization of all phenomena as being free from elaborations by the illusion-like consciousness that directly realizes emptiness.” In other words, it is attained through extensive method and profound wisdom. Although not different in entity from that, those three are presented as different, because, although not different from that in entity, they are posited and distinguished by different beliefs in accordance with how they appear dissimilarly as real conventionalities.  There is a purpose for positing them as three different [exalted bodies], because “They are presented differently as the direct objects of utilization of only buddhas mutually among themselves, and as the objects of utilization of the bodhisattvas, hearers, and so forth” has been indicated.   They are not different entities from the nature body, because it occurs in the system of the great trailblazers, “That called ‘emptiness’ does not exist whatsoever [as being] other from consciousness. Why? Voidness of true existence is asserted as merely not a different entity from the basis of emptiness, that which is devoid of true existence.” Those who assert that the two form bodies are not able to perform functions in the way they appear [and] are unreal conventionalities are simply separated from the fortune of seeing these stainless commentaries.   The subject says “the remaining three exalted bodies that are property-possessors.” What does this “remaining three exalted bodies” refer to? They are the complete enjoyment body, the emanation body, and the exalted wisdom truth body. Other than the nature body, we have these three remaining exalted bodies. These remaining three exalted bodies are not different entities from the nature body. Although they are not different entities from the nature body, they are posited, or presented, as different from the nature body. There is a purpose for positing them as three different exalted bodies:  • the exalted wisdom truth body is presented as the direct object of utilization of only buddhas mutually among themselves;  • the complete enjoyment body is posited as the object of utilization of bodhisattva aryas and those above; and • the supreme emanation body is posited as the object of utilization of sentient beings with pure karma.  Although they are not different in entity from the nature body, these remaining three exalted bodies are posited as three different exalted bodies, “because ‘They are presented differently as the direct objects of utilization of only buddhas mutually among themselves, and as the objects of utilization of the bodhisattvas, hearers, and so forth’ has been indicated.” “Bodhisattvas” here refers to bodhisattva aryas and so forth, i.e. bodhisattvas who have attained a ground and above. “Hearers and so forth” should be understood as hearers with pure karma, and so forth.   What do we mean by “pure karma,” as in “ordinary beings with pure karma” who are able to see, or perceive, supreme emanation bodies? When we talk about ordinary beings with pure karma, it does not mean that in general their karma is pure; this refers to them having pure karma from the point of view of their karma being free from obscurations that prevent the supreme emanation bodies from appearing to them, so it does not mean that their karma is pure in every way. What exactly is this pure karma? It is difficult to pin it down in a detailed manner. We would say that there are many birds and deer, etc., who can perceive the supreme emanation body. Would you say that they have pure karma? It is difficult to answer.  Student: How do we know that these animals can perceive supreme emanation bodies?

And because it is produced and arises in each instant, why is it also described as “permanent?”  Here, we have objections regarding pervasion and permanence. On p. 584, there are the five attributes: the third is [that the tathagatas’ exalted knower of wishes is asserted to be] “unobstructed exalted wisdom with respect to all objects of knowledge.” In other words, the exalted wisdom pervades all objects of knowledge. Questions and answers  Student: What does it mean to say that the buddha directly appears to sentient beings—what kind of buddha body appears, and do the sentient beings understand that this is an actual buddha body?  Geshe-la: To answer the second question: if a buddha directly appears to a person, that person will understand that a buddha is appearing. [As for the first question,] for a buddha to directly appear to a person, the signs and exemplifications of the buddha must appear. If a buddha appears without the signs and exemplifications to a person, then it is not said that a buddha directly appears to this person. In other words, when we say that a buddha enacts the welfare of sentient beings by directly appearing to them, this means that a buddha appears in the form of being ornamented by the signs and exemplifications, and enacts the welfare of sentient beings. It is possible that even though a buddha directly abides in front of a person, that person fails to perceive the buddha as ornamented by the signs and exemplifications. This person can perceive the buddha in various ways: as an ordinary being, as a bodhisattva, and so forth.
