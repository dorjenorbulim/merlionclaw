Lineage abiding in the nature in our own Middle Way system  It is that which is suitable to transform into a truth body; it also is the dharmata that is the emptiness of true existence of sentient beings’ minds. The truth body which it transforms into is the very pure nature that is devoid of all stains. However it is not taught that the pure nature of sentient beings, having transformed, changes into a buddha’s meditative equipoise.   The existence of a potential upon sentient beings’ minds such that it is suitable for one to separate from stains and suitable for one to generate aryas’ realizations is established by reasoning. That [potential] itself is deposited by the condition of hearing and is to be nourished by the condition of hearing. Therefore, the “lineage that arose from perfect adoption” is just that which is suitable to transform into a form body when its transformation is culminated. It is what is posited to be the lineage abiding in the nature by the Cittamatrins. Acarya Arya [Vimuktisena] asserts that [it] is the imputed the lineage abiding in the nature, is imputed, while the and the fully qualified developmental lineage. is fully qualified. {244}   “Our system” is the Middle Way Autonomy system. In this system, lineage is of two types: lineage abiding in the nature and developmental lineage. The developmental lineage is also known as the “lineage that arises from perfect adoption due to conditions.” What is the lineage abiding in the nature according to our system? “It is that which is suitable to transform into a truth body; it also is the dharmata that is the emptiness of true existence of sentient beings’ minds.” The developmental lineage is described (in the second paragraph) as a potential deposited by the conditions of hearing, contemplation, and meditation, and is also nourished by the same conditions. Such a lineage is suitable to transform into a form body.   When we hear the Dharma, it is important not to practice “contentment.” Sometimes it is said that we should have little desire and should practice contentment, but when it comes to listening to the Dharma, we should not have little desire and should not practice contentment. We can see that some people can understand Dharma very quickly, while some of us find it very difficult to understand. This difference is due to our imprints from the past. Some of us have very few imprints, and as a result we find it difficult to understand the material now, whereas some of some of us have many imprints from the past and are able to understand the material quickly. If you find the material difficult to understand, you shouldn’t give up; you should think, “This is because I haven’t heard the Dharma very much. If I listen and study, I will deposit imprints and in the future it will be easier to understand.” Then, when Maitreya Buddha comes, we will be able to gain realizations of, for instance, the four noble truths, upon hearing even a single stanza that he teaches. When Shakyamuni Buddha was alive, many people became aryas when he was giving teachings.

He then spoke about how it is important to study, and that before 1959, Sera Je monks were even forbidden to take many initiations, as it would interfere with their studies—because they would have to recite many sadhanas and thus would have less time to study.]   “The truth body which it transforms into is the very pure nature that is devoid of all stains” refers to the nature body. The passage, “However, it is not taught that the pure nature of sentient beings, having transformed, changes into a buddha’s meditative equipoise” means it is not that the lineage abiding in the nature, having transformed, becomes a buddha’s meditative equipoise. “The existence of a potential upon sentient beings’ minds such that it is suitable for one to separate from stains and suitable for one to generate aryas’ realizations is established by reasoning;” this means that if you think about these points, you will be able to establish them.   How do we establish by reasoning that one can separate from stains? The stains have not entered the nature of the mind, and are incidental, or adventitious. If they were part of the mind, i.e. in the sense that they have entered the nature of the mind, then whenever the mind was manifest, the stains would be manifest; however, this is not something we observe. Also, if the stains were in the nature of the mind, we would have to extinguish the mind in order to extinguish the stains. Also, the stains can be separated from, because they are erroneous states of mind that lack a valid support. Thus they can be eliminated; one can separate from them. This is simply a rough explanation; you need to engage in extensive contemplation on this.   This potential is deposited by the conditions of hearing, contemplation, and meditation, and is nourished by those conditions. The development lineage, the “lineage that arose from perfect adoption,” is just that which is suitable to transform into a buddha’s form body. According to the Cittamatrins, this is the lineage abiding in the nature. However, according to Arya Vimuktisena, such a lineage arising from perfect adoption is not the actual lineage abiding in the nature; according to our system, this is the imputed lineage abiding in the nature and is the fully qualified developmental lineage.  3  Because hatred for the Mahayana Dharma is the cause of those bearing wrong view and craving [not attaining enlightenment for a long time], the teaching that “Those bearing wrong view and craving bear the property of never attaining nirvana” is taught with the intention with respect to other times for the sake of overcoming hatred for the Mahayana Dharma. Never ever becoming pure is not fitting, because, with the intention that that which is suitable to be pure exists in all sentient beings without distinction, the Bhagavan taught that:  Although beginningless, possessing an end,  The bearer of the property of permanent purity by nature, Is not seen, concealed by a cover on the outside since beginningless time, Just like a concealed golden statue.  The meaning of this passage is that those who bear wrong views and craving will not attain enlightenment for a long time. Although it is said they will never attain nirvana or enlightenment, this is not actually the case. They will not attain it quickly; they will only attain it after a very long time. The Bhagavan taught that such a lineage abiding in the nature is beginningless, but that it does have an end. When the stains have been completely eliminated, it comes to an end.  4 C. Damage to the literal It is unreasonable that sentient beings will never purify stains, (i) because the lineage that is pure by nature exists in all sentient beings, and (ii) because the buddhas’ exalted activities engage all sentient beings. For, with even rebirth as a fortunate one who attains the state of high status being the buddhas’ exalted activity, it is impossible for there to be a sentient being who has never attained high status even once.  “Damage to the literal” means damage to the literal presentation in the sutra. The sutra citation says, “Those bearing wrong view and craving bear the property of never attaining nirvana.” This cannot be literally accepted, therefore there is “damage to the literal.” The main reason is that the buddhas’ exalted activities engage all sentient beings, and since that is the case, all sentient beings will be caused to attain nirvana.   Explanation: Ornament of the Essence, pp. 171-2:   There are other extreme positions: • because even though the stains will be purified, the stains in the continuum of some sentient beings are permanent; • because even though they are impermanent, the method for destroying them is not possible; • because even though it is possible, nobody knows the method; • because even though someone knows it, there is some sentient being for whom it is not possible to generate [a mind] seeking it; • because even though someone knows it, a person who cares for [sentient beings] with mercy is not possible; • because it is not possible for some inferior sentient beings to generate disillusionment due to suffering; • even though it may be generated, he will not become an object of exalted compassion of the buddhas, and so forth.  You should know that there is no sentient being for whom (i) stains are not suitable to be abandoned, and (ii) antidotes are not suitable to be generated, from among the reasonings refuting these extremes.  These extreme positions contain many wrong assertions. The first view is not correct, because stains in the continua of sentient beings are impermanent, not permanent. The second view is wrong because there is a method for destroying those stains. The third is wrong because there is someone who knows the method. The fourth is wrong, because sentient beings can generate the mind seeking the method. The fifth is wrong because there is a person who cares for sentient beings with mercy. The sixth is wrong because sentient beings can generate disillusionment due to suffering. The seventh is wrong because all sentient beings are objects of the exalted compassion of the buddhas; they are cared for by the buddhas. There is no sentient being for whom the stains cannot be abandoned and in whom the antidotes cannot be generated. Dispelling objection [Haribhadra’s] Great Commentary says:   QUALM: What precedes the generated mind [of enlightenment], the partial concordances with liberation and definite discrimination, and so forth is that [lineage]. Hence, is lineage not to be explained only first? Why is it explained in this manner?  RESPONSE: That is true. However, that is the sequence of the meaning. Having explained the result first, the cause is indicated later, whereby this is the sequence of explanation. Hence there is no fault.   The meaning of that is as follows. Having first awakened the lineage, after that, one generates the mind [of enlightenment]; that is the sequence of the cause and result meaning. When the generated mind [of enlightenment], the partial concordance with liberation, and so forth are generated in one’s continuum, one will be able to realize the existence of lineage in one’s continuum, whereby the explanation here is made by means of that sequence.  This objection says: before generating the mind of enlightenment, the partial concordance with definite discrimination, and so on, one has the lineage, so lineage should be explained first. Why, then, is it explained after bodhicitta and the path of preparation? The response is that this is true—lineage exists before generated mind, the partial concordances with liberation, and the partial concordances with definite discrimination—that is the sequence of the meaning from the point of view of cause and effect. Here, however, is the sequence of explanation; therefore, there is no fault. From the point of view of cause and effect, first there is lineage, followed by bodhicitta, and so on; here, in the sequence of explanation, the result is explained first, and the cause later. The manner of leading accordingly by way of lineage The method for examining the interests of the trainees at the beginning is as follows. Just as it has been explained in Hearer’s Grounds, having caused them to hear the scriptural baskets of the Hinayana and Mahayana, when leading them to their meaning, one will realize the level of their faculty. Since lineage is distinguished by the accumulations [of merit and exalted wisdom] and the purification [of negativities and obscurations], one leads them by way of the stages of the path of the three—the beings of small, middling, and great capacities. Here, there is an explanation on how we can examine the interests of particular trainees. First one has to cause trainees to hear the teachings of the Hinayana and Mahayana; in dependence on this, one comes to know their faculties. If they belong to the Mahayana lineage, they will have joy in meditating on great compassion, engaging in extensive generosity, guarding extensive morality, in cultivating patience, and so on. One will thus lead them to generate bodhicitta. Even before attaining bodhicitta, they are said to be “bodhisattvas who have not generated the mind [of enlightenment],” but of course they are not bodhisattvas yet.   If they are of the Mahayana lineage, they will not enter the great scope immediately; they first need to generate the attitudes of the small and medium scopes. On that basis, they can generate the attitude seeking others’ benefit and bodhicitta. The meaning of a definitive meaning sutra in this system is: a sutra is definitive if it takes emptiness as its main explicit subject matter and is literally acceptable. What is a sutra requiring interpretation? There are two possibilities: a sutra that either (1) indicates conventional truths as its main explicit subject matter, or (2) is not literally acceptable.   In the definition of a definitive meaning sutra, in the Autonomy school we need to add the qualification “and is literally acceptable.” Examples of definitive meaning sutras are the three Perfection of Wisdom sutras: the extensive, medium, and brief. There are sutras that take emptiness as their main subject matter but are not literally acceptable, such as the Heart Sutra. According to the Autonomy School, this sutra is not literally acceptable; the phrase, “[Avalokiteshvara] beheld those five aggregates also as empty of inherent existence,” is not literally acceptable. A literally acceptable statement would be: “[Avalokiteshvara] beheld those five aggregates also as empty of inherent existence ultimately.”  A sutra of interpretable meaning in this system is a sutra that either takes conventional truths as its main explicit subject matter or is not literally acceptable. An example of a sutra that indicates conventional truths as its main explicit subject matter and is literally acceptable is a sutra that teaches that compounded phenomena are impermanent. This is literally acceptable and indicates conventional truths as its main, explicit subject matter. An example of a sutra that is interpretable in meaning that does not indicate conventional truths as its main explicit subject matter and is not literally acceptable is the Heart Sutra. Thus there are two divisions of interpretable meaning sutras: (1) sutras that indicate conventional truth as their main subject matter and (2) sutras that are not suitable to be held literally. The claim that “If the vessels are the same, it is contradictory that they are vessels of dissimilar contents” is only a wrong conception of someone who does not know how to posit composite and continuity; it is obvious that, through the force of that, {268} only wrong conceptions regarding also the presentation of the grounds and paths will be produced.   The analogy used here is that from the same lump of clay, the substantial cause, we can make three different pots, or vessels. Based on that substantial cause, one applies the fire, which is called the accomplishing condition. One bakes the shaped clay in the fire, and it becomes hardened, and we end up with containers or vessels. In these vessels we put honey, molasses, and yogurt. Because of the different contents, the first is called a “honey container,” the second a “molasses container,” and the third a “yogurt container.” So although the pots are exactly the same, due to different contents they are imputed with different names. Likewise, although dharmata, the support, is the same, since the supported—the hearer, solitary realizer, and Mahayana vehicles—are different, the support is given different names, i.e. the hearers’ lineage, solitary realizers’ lineage, and Mahayana lineage.   We can also interpret this analogy in a slightly different way: using just one vessel, we first put honey, and it becomes a “honey container.” If, instead of honey, we put molasses in the same vessel, it becomes a “molasses container;” and if we put yogurt instead, it becomes a “yogurt container.” Although the container is the same, it receives a different name due to its dissimilar contents. Likewise, the three lineages have different names depending on the three vehicles. The vessel is analogous with dharmata; there is only one dharmata, there is no difference in entity. But due to differences in the supported, there are divisions into three. Likewise, if you take one vessel and fill it with honey, it is a honey container, and so on.   “The claim that ‘If the vessels….continuity’” means that if someone who doesn’t understand this analogy says that if the vessels are the same, it is wrong to say that they are of dissimilar contents, then this person does not know how to posit composite and continuity. “It is obvious that, through the force of that, only wrong conceptions regarding also the presentation of the grounds and paths will be produced.” The conclusion is that, in a single vessel, we can have different contents. For example, in a cup, we sometimes have tea, sometimes water, sometimes fruit juice and so on. There is no contradiction.   Student: If the pot is the dharmata, what are the contents? Do understand that when we use an analogy, the analogy and the actual meaning have to be connected; however it does not mean that every single aspect of the analogy has to be analogous to some aspect of the actual meaning.
