 1  Masters Program: Ornament for Clear Realization Commentary by Geshe Tenzin Tenphel (week 42)   Monday afternoon, 20 April 2009  General teachings in English (main points):  • Study is very important. Doing retreat, even if we receive initiations and commentaries, is not enough to develop real understanding of that deity. Without study, we may get a rough idea, but it will not become deep understanding. Of course, some special beings can understand with just a little explanation. If you have heard many lam rim teachings, the next subject (Middle Way) will be easier to understand. In the monasteries, there are no formal lam rim classes, but once you have studied and then you receive lam rim teachings, you can understand them easily. In the monasteries, the more difficult texts and commentaries are studied, which allows one to understand the easier texts. • We create suffering with our minds, but we blame our suffering on outside circumstances, such as people who criticize us. In order to begin to remove deeper obstacles, we need to change our habitual ways of thinking: these are like the first level of obstacles.  • There are many types of meditation, but we need to mainly become aware of what we are doing with our minds. Real practice involves transforming one’s mind into the positive, not just reciting mantras, thinking of deities, etc. To become aware of one’s mistakes does not require a lot of analysis; it is relatively simple to do. There are nine divisions by way of objects. The phrase [first paragraph in the Explanation], “from the adherence to the brief and the extensive scriptures as truly existent objects to be engaged in, through to the adherence to the three types of paths that are excellent as truly existent objects of utilization,” includes all nine divisions by way of objects.   The entity of the conceptions is the conception of apprehendeds to be engaged in as truly existent objects of utilization. The description here is quite similar to the previous discussion on the conceptions of apprehendeds to be engaged in that are objects of abandonment of the path of seeing. Although the description is similar, in the context of the objects of abandonment of the path of seeing, we are talking about acquired conceptions, whereas here, in the context of the objects of abandonment of the path of meditation, the objects of abandonment are innate conceptions. The various examples given in the context of the peak application of path of seeing and the peak application of the path of meditation are also different.   In fact, if we were to compare the examples in the context of the peak application of the path of seeing and the peak application of the path of meditation, we would see that they are not the same. Recall that one example of a conception of apprehendeds to be engaged in, in the context of the peak application of the path of seeing, was the conception of the Mahayana paths and results as truly existent objects of utilization. In the context of the path of seeing, we are talking about the acquired form of this conception. Does this mean the innate form of this conception is not to be abandoned by the peak application of the path of meditation, since it is not explicitly stated as an example in this context? In fact, there is an innate form, and it is an object of abandonment of the peak application of the path of meditation.   It also works the other way around: if you look at the various examples here, posited in the context of the peak application of the path of meditation, the objects of abandonment are the innate conceptions, but if we look at the acquired forms of these conceptions, we can posit these [acquired forms] as objects of abandonment of the peak application of the path of seeing. In fact, there are as many acquired conceptions of true existence as there are objects of knowledge, and there are likewise as many innate conceptions of true existence as there are objects of knowledge. However, this does not mean that when we abandon the acquired conceptions of true existence, we have to contemplate each of these conceptions of true existence. The same is true in the case of the innate conceptions: it is not that we need to contemplate all these conceptions of true existence, as many as there are objects of knowledge. In fact, when we say that the acquired conceptions of true existence are abandoned, we need to apply this to individual continua. A person who abandons the acquired conceptions of true existence abandons those in his/her continuum, and likewise with the innate conceptions.   To illustrate what I mean, let us say that at the time of Shakyamuni Buddha, there was a bodhisattva on the path of seeing who abandoned the acquired conceptions of true existence. If we speak of a conception of this watch in front of me as truly existent, did that bodhisattva abandon the

The first two have to do with conceptions that adhere to the brief and extensive Dharma scriptures as truly existent objects to be engaged in. The brief Dharma scriptures take care of sentient beings who like the brief/, or what is brief; the extensive Dharma scriptures take care of sentient beings who like what is extensive. We are familiar with the expression, “brief, middling, and extensive Perfection of Wisdom Sutras.” The brief is the 8,000-stanza Perfection of Wisdom Sutra, the middling is the 20,000-stanza Perfection of Wisdom Sutra, and the extensive is the 100,000-stanza Perfection of Wisdom Sutra. In general, all bodhisattvas are of sharp faculty, but within bodhisattvas we can differentiate dull, middling, and sharp faculty bodhisattvas. The 8,000-stanza sutra is said to be for those of dull faculty, the 20,000-stanza sutra for those of middling faculty, and the 100,000-stanza sutra for those of sharp faculty. The 100,000-stanza Perfection of Wisdom Sutra contains many words, which contain many meanings. If this were explained to bodhisattvas of dull faculty, it would not be suitable—it would be too many words, and too many meanings. For this reason, it is said that the 100,000-stanza Perfection of Wisdom Sutra is suitable for bodhisattvas of sharp faculty.   On the other hand, there is a different [i.e. alternate] explanation: to someone of sharp faculty, there is no need to explain too much; you do not need to give them many reasonings in order for them to understand a point; they are suitable to receive a brief explanation. However, those of middling faculty need more reasons to understand the same point, and those of dull faculty need many reasonings in order to understand that point. This second explanation seems to contradict the first explanation, because in the first explanation, it is said that the extensive Perfection of Wisdom Sutra is given for sharp faculty bodhisattvas, whereas in the second explanation, it is said that they need few reasons to understand the points.   It is possible to reconcile these two explanations. Let us consider a sharp faculty person. Since such a person can easily understand a point without much explanation, then no matter how much is said, this person can absorb the explanation. So, even though many words and meanings are conveyed, this person, due to being able to understand the points easily, will listen to them quite                                                  1  Tibetan: bstangs

The third point (p. 529) says, “The one in terms of behavior is the adherence to the attention that is a yoga of the perfection of wisdom (an antidote to not being aided, that is, held by the buddhas through them acting as protectors due to [oneself] not being persistent regarding the meanings of the brief and the extensive as they have been explained) as a [truly existent] object to be engaged in.” In this point, what is the object? Remember, we said there are nine divisions by way of objects. The object [in this third point] is actually the attention that is a yoga of the perfection of wisdom. You should identify the attention as the object, and not the other parts that follow in the sentence. In this point, it is mentioned that “the yoga of the perfection of wisdom is an antidote to not being aided, i.e. not being held, by the buddhas through them acting as protectors, due to [oneself] not being persistent regarding the meanings of the brief and the extensive as they have been explained).” In other words, it is saying that if one is not persistent regarding the meanings of the brief and extensive scriptures, the buddhas will not act as protectors; one will not be aided or held by the buddhas. This sounds strange, because it is said that the buddhas have compassion and kindness for all sentient beings, and that they take care of all sentient beings. You should understand this statement in point number three to mean that the buddhas, despite having great compassion for all sentient beings, are not able to aid those who are unsuitable vessels. In fact, we

Although this is the case, this fact alone is not sufficient for sentient beings to receive benefit, because if sentient beings do not follow and practice the advice of the buddhas, they cannot be protected by the buddhas. The point I am trying to make here is similar to the point that was made previously in the context of the Mahayana path of preparation: we said that the bodhisattvas on the Mahayana path of preparation have the supreme emanation body as their tutor, whereas the Hinayana practitioners do not have the supreme emanation body as their tutors. It is not sufficient that, from the side of the tutor, he or she is able to explain the Mahayana path fully. For example, the supreme emanation body is able to explain the complete Mahayana path, but this does not mean that all trainees are suitable vessels for such an explanation. It is also necessary that, from the side of the trainees, they are suitable vessels to receive the explanation.   We know the stories of Milarepa and Marpa: how Marpa made Milarepa undergo many hardships, and how Naropa had to undergo difficulties under the tutorship of Tilopa. If one does not understand these stories correctly, one may think that Marpa and Tilopa lacked compassion. However, we need to understand that the difficulties Milarepa and Naropa underwent were due to their teachers using skillful means to purify their negative karma, not that they lacked compassion. In fact, their taking care of their trainees in such a way is a sign of their great compassion. It is said that under Tilopa, Naropa had to undergo twelve great austerities and six small austerities. The twelve great austerities were circumstances in which Naropa almost died. After making Naropa pass through these eighteen austerities, Tilopa told Naropa, “You have proven yourself to be one who is really suitable to receive teachings, and who is really sincere about listening to the Dharma, and you have purified many negativities. Now I am ready to teach you the Dharma. Now you must offer a mandala.” Naropa was penniless, and Tilopa said, “You don’t have to go to the city to beg for riches to offer to me. Just urinate on the sand and shape it into a mandala.” Naropa did so, and Tilopa threw it into his face, upon which Naropa experienced great bliss and entered into a very special meditative stabilization.   To describe one of Naropa’s austerities: when Tilopa went to gather firewood, Naropa went with him, and when Tilopa looked for kerosene to burn the firewood, Naropa did the same. Naropa heard Tilopa say, “If this wood has been piled up, the oil poured on it, and the fire lit, and if someone were to go into the middle of this fire and be burnt, then this person would be suitable to be my student. A person with this kind of courage is suitable to receive my teachings.” Naropa thus lit the fire and went into the fire and sat there until the fire had died down, so he was completely burnt. When the fire died down, Tilopa revived him by uttering, “May he be revived.” Nowadays, it would be difficult to find trainees like that, but these things happened in the past! Nowadays if the teacher is not careful with what he says, the disciples may become angry!  Questions and answers   Student: What is the difference between “innate” and “acquired” here? In the first example on p. 528, how is an innate adherence to the brief Dharma scriptures different from an acquired one? Geshe-la: An acquired conception comes about through tenets, and acquired conceptions are of two types: the manifest form and the seed form. For example, animals would not have the manifest acquired conceptions, but they could have the seeds of the acquired conceptions. It is possible that a human being has contact with tenets, and, due to engaging in certain tenets, develops acquired conceptions of true existence. If this person dies and is reborn as an animal, this animal would have the seeds of the acquired conceptions of true existence, although it would not have the manifest acquired conceptions of true existence. The innate conceptions of true existence are not due to tenets; they are innate. For instance, a person may not rely on reasons, e.g. “This is truly existent

Student: But one does not acquire [conceptions of] true existence—all sentient beings have innate conceptions of true existence. If, while listening to the teachings, we have a very correct motivation such as the motivation of wishing to benefit others, the motivation of compassion, and the bodhicitta [motivation], we will create superior merit. Instead, if we have the motivation of fulfilling one’s own purpose/welfare, the motivation of gain for this life alone, or even the motivation of wishing to become famous in this life, the merit that we create becomes very limited. There is no certainty that we will actually become famous even if we have this motivation! So it is very important to generate a good motivation, and this is not a practice that requires hardship. I noticed something interesting to discuss in point number five, so let us go back to point five. It says, “The subject—someone on the hearer’s path of seeing who proceeds to solitary peace—does not pay attention to perfect complete enlightenment as an object to be attained in his continuum for the time being, due to lacking diligent achievement of the perfection of wisdom.” It is very important to be diligent in our practice. Of course, if in terms of practice we can achieve both quality and quantity, that is the best; we do a lot of practice, and the practice is also done well. But if we have to choose between quality and quantity, we should pay more attention to the quality. We may do a lot of practice, but the practice may be very poor in quality, which establishes a tendency/habit to practice badly; it is important to pay attention to the quality of our practice to make sure that whatever practice we do is done well. Doing lots of practice that is poor in quality is not very beneficial. If we neglect the quality of our practice and just try to do a lot of practice, this is simply a waste of time. In time, we may feel there is no point in doing practice, and we may give up doing practice altogether.   What do we mean by “practice”? We may recite prayers and sit upright in the meditation posture, but practice is not just these things. The essence of practice is the transformation or development of the mind. If we recite tons of prayers without paying attention to the quality of the mind, the benefit that we receive is quite trivial, and because we spend a lot of time engaging in these so-called “practices,” we can even suffer from fatigue at the end. It is very important to concentrate on the quality; at the beginning, we may not be able to do a very good practice, but we try to work on the quality regardless of how little practice we do. What do we mean by “working on the quality”? At the beginning, it is important to have a good motivation, and during the actual practice, we try to have a focused mind, not letting the mind wander here and there. Through making effort in these aspects, we will be able to build up good habits of the mind, and in time the mind will become improved, eventually being able to do practice of good quality. Through great compassion, one has even greater power over the afflictions, and if one generates bodhicitta, one has even greater control over the afflictions. In such a way, one generates subtler and subtler states of mind. In the sutra system, there are these techniques for transforming and working with the mind.   Based on these techniques, one can go on to practice tantra. One needs to start by practicing what is taught in the sutra system. On that basis, one can practice tantra. It is important to understand that this is the procedure. If one does not proceed in this way, but immediately tries to practice tantra teachings, such as carrying attachment into the path, one may make mistakes. The danger for misunderstanding is always present, especially if one misunderstands the meaning of the images, such as the father and mother deities embracing. In tantra, a very subtle mind is utilized, and on a very subtle level, there is no differentiation between male and female, and one does not rely on the coarse body. Therefore, in the practice of tantra one is not influenced by attachment. Bearing these points in mind, you must make sure not to make mistakes in your practice. You may have noticed that when His Holiness the Dalai Lama confers the Kalachakra initiation, many people come, and before conferring the initiation His Holiness always gives teachings on, for example, the Guide to the Bodhisattva’s Way of Life, Lam Rim, or lo jong. There is great significance in this. Student: I have a doubt about attachment. Although we understand it is necessary to abandon attachment, in practice it is difficult. Geshe-la: Indeed we have this tendency to search for different objects with which to achieve happiness. It is not sufficient to simply understand the faults of attachment, and how the object of attachment is not able to bring satisfaction. We need to think about this again and again. Merely through understanding that intellectually will not prevent us from expecting that there is an object that will bring us satisfaction. It is very important to contemplate faults of attachment again and again, but this must be done in a moderate manner, otherwise we may fall into extremes, and overdo it. It must be done moderately and gently.  Student: In general [related to the earlier question about accumulating merit for three countless great eons], it is said that there are no exceptions, but in particular there seem to be exceptions.
