 1  Masters Program: Ornament for Clear Realization Commentary by Geshe Tenzin Tenphel (week 18)    Tuesday afternoon, 2 September 2008  Geshe-la first gave some advice in English: I hope you enjoyed your holidays. When studying this kind of text, it is important to put it into practice. Otherwise, nothing changes. Instead of getting better, things get worse. You should not make mistakes about your practice. There is a story of a monk, who was meditating near a stupa, and his friend came and asked him what he was doing, and the monk said, “I am meditating on patience.” Then his friend slapped him, and the monk got angry, which shows he was not meditating well. Many people meditate on great compassion and bodhicitta, but then they get angry when they hear one word from someone else. You must see what kind of mind you have; if you quickly become unhappy or nervous for nothing, it means you are not really ready to meditate on great compassion and bodhicitta. You should check yourself, what level you are at, and try to develop from there. Don’t be in a hurry to do practices like great compassion and bodhicitta. Go about it calmly, carefully.   Make yourself a happy, peaceful person; don’t make yourself unhappy or nervous. Check what kinds of thoughts arise in your mind that make you unhappy, and try to remove such thoughts. You must do this kind of work. Our study and practice should go together. In our studies, we are on the path of preparation, but maybe we are not even a person of small scope! If this life is not peaceful, how we can ensure our next life will be peaceful? And if we can’t do that, we are not even a person of the small scope. Check yourself and think: “I am a small being, but I am studying a Mahayana text although I am not Mahayana.” Try to understand yourself, and judge yourself. Don’t judge other people; it’s very difficult to understand others. We may think someone is bad, but many other people that person is good. Who is right? Some people want to change the world, but they don’t think about changing themselves. This is stupid! How can you change the world if you can’t change yourself?  When we study this kind of text, if we don’t understand and develop ourselves, we may think we know many things and look down on others. We shouldn’t think that whatever we do and think is right, just because we have learned many things. This is not right. If we become this kind of person, we have become a bad person. However much we know, that much we should try to become a better person, and this means having a better mind. If you don’t have a good mind, you may look good on the outside, but you are not a good person. If you look good and your mind is also good, then double good! But it’s more important to be a good person inside than to be physically attractive and strong. Some people have weak bodies but live long, some with strong bodies do not live long.   The main point is to develop our inner qualities, but this is very difficult because our negative habits are strong. So we must go very calmly and slowly. We may make mistakes, but we should think: “Tomorrow I will try to avoid that mistake.” Even if you make the same mistake 100 times, don’t worry. Don’t put yourself down and become discouraged; this is an obstacle that doesn’t allow you to develop. Instead think: “I made that mistake again! It doesn’t matter, I will try again.” This way you keep yourself peaceful and happy, and you can improve your mind. Something may be difficult, but don’t worry; go slowly, slowly.  Don’t judge other people, but sometimes you can see others as examples of how not to behave, such as when someone gets angry. When you see how someone looks when they get angry, you can

The teachings continue:  In terms of the teachings, we have arrived at the point of discussing the path of preparation. Why are we discussing this? We were discussing the ten topics that characterize an exalted knower of all aspects.   The essential point you need to keep in mind is the structure of the eight categories. On the basis of the eight categories, you need to bear in mind the 70 topics that characterize these. If you are able to bear these in mind, you can understand what is contained in the eight chapters. In this way, it is as though you have the capital to understand the material.   To take an example, the first topic is the generated mind, bodhicitta. There are many ways of looking at this topic. From the point of view of entity, there are two divisions; from the point of view of the mode of generation, there are three divisions; from the point of view of boundaries, four divisions; and from the point of view of similes, twenty-two divisions.   The 70 topics are sub-divided into many sub-topics. For instance, the second topic, instructions, has divisions into two, ten and so forth. On that basis, if you look at commentaries such as Gyeltsab Je’s Ornament of the Essence or Lama Tsongkhapa’s Golden Rosary, you can see that regardless of whatever commentary is given to explain the points or whatever explanation is given to dispel the doubts, all are given on the basis of the structure of the seventy topics. That is the way we should study the text; we should examine the points that are explained in these texts.  This is the way we study these texts in the monastery. Here, we are studying “Parchin,” the Perfection of Wisdom. The main texts that are used are Gyeltsab Je’s Explanation: Ornament of the Essence as well as Lama Tsongkhapa’s Golden Rosary. We also look at Haribhadra’s Commentary: Clear Meaning. In Sera Je monastery, we also study Jetsun Chokyi Gyeltsen’s General Meaning. Very sharp students would also use textbooks of other monasteries, such as Gomang College of Drepung Monastery, Sera Me, Loseling, and so on. Sutra says “Also those who wish to train in the grounds of hearers should listen to this very perfection of wisdom.” {202}   With respect to these expressions, object of observation and aspect, there are many ways to understand them. In this context, the “object of observation” is the object that the mind observes; the object of the mind is the object of observation. The “aspect” is an aspect of the mind, so we are talking about a mind that observes the object.   The text says, “The subject—the sixteen bearing the support of the truths, impermanence and so forth.” Here, “the truths” refers to the four noble truths. They “are the objects of observation of the small heat level of the Mahayana path of preparation because they are the bases for eliminating the superimpositions regarding those.” The sign here – “because they are the bases for eliminating the superimpositions regarding those” – should be applied to “all the remaining objects of observation.” Thus it should also be applied to the middling and great heat levels of the Mahayana path of preparation.    “The subject—the exalted wisdom of the small heat level which stops strong adherence, exaggerated abidance, and so forth with respect to the sixteen attributes of those four truths as truly existent—is the aspect of the small heat level because it is an exalted wisdom of the small heat level that stops the superimpositions in the wrong class with respect to phenomena that are its objects of observation. Also apply this similarly to the remaining objects of observation.”  In this subject, it talks about “the exalted wisdom of the small heat level which stops strong adherence” – it stops adherence to the sixteen attributes as truly existent. The words “and so forth” include “knowing all,” (which is mentioned in the sutra citation in the following paragraph)—this refers to knowing all that is based on an erroneous reason. This will come later.   “Superimpositions” here refer to various degrees of the conception of true existence. We can divide these into three levels: the first is strong adherence, the second level is exaggerated abidance, and the third level is knowing all. These superimpositions are eliminated by the exalted wisdom of the small heat level. The exalted wisdom of the small heat level that stops these three levels of superimpositions is the aspect of the small heat level. Respectively, sutra says, “When a great bodhisattva practices the perfection of wisdom, he does not abide on forms,” and “That which is the emptiness of form is not form; form is also not other than emptiness.” {206}   Small peak level  In the phrase “From between the two, the truths not having been presented and having been presented by Acarya Arya [Vimuktisena],” “truths” does not mean truth in terms of truth and falsity. Rather, it refers to the four noble truths. There are two cases: the truths not having been and having been presented by Acarya Arya Vimuktisena. In the first case, we see “those forms and so forth that do not abide ultimately, distinguished by their entity lacking nature ultimately, are the objects of observation, and the exalted wisdom realizing that they—forms and so forth and their dharmata—are the nature of one another is the aspect.” The sutra citation also mentions forms: “‘When a great bodhisattva practices the perfection of wisdom, he does not abide on forms,’” and “That which is the emptiness of form is not form; form is also not other than emptiness.” Forms here are illustrations or bases of characterization of true sufferings. We can posit the contaminated aggregates, such as forms, as an illustration of true sufferings.  So in the context of this case, forms are used in the explanation. This means that the four noble truths have not been presented. In the second case, when the four noble truths have been presented, we see that the attributes of true sufferings are used in the explanation: impermanence, suffering, and so forth. In the first case, when the four noble truths have not been presented by Acarya Arya Vimuktisena, the bases for characterization are used instead of the attributes of true sufferings.   In the explanation of the objects of observation, we see that forms are mentioned in the first case, without explicitly mentioning the attributes of true suffering. This is also the case with the aspect, where forms are mentioned without mention of the attributes of true suffering.  19  Middling forbearance   In the phrase, “Those forms and so forth distinguished by lacking production and lacking definite emergence…,” “lacking production” refers to the lack of birth into cyclic existence ultimately, and “lacking definite emergence” means non-existence of nirvana ultimately.  Conventionally, there is birth into samsara and there is definite emergence from samsara (i.e. the attainment of nirvana). Through the force of karma and afflictions, there is birth into samsara, but there is no birth in samsara ultimately. Likewise, through becoming free from karma and afflictions, there is definite emergence from samsara/attainment of nirvana, but this definite emergence/nirvana does not exist ultimately.   The “forms and so forth” mentioned here should be correlated with the sixteen aspects of the four noble truths. They can be understood as the contaminated aggregates. By grasping the contaminated aggregates which are impermanent as permanent, which are suffering as happiness, which are unclean as clean, which are selfless as having self, one creates karma and circles in samsara. By realizing the aggregates as impermanent, suffering, empty, selfless, and so on, one becomes free from samsara and attains nirvana. Due to grasping to the aggregates in erroneous ways, one creates the karma to circle in cyclic existence.   Regarding the aspect of the middling forbearance level, it says “by observing the four truths in that manner, realizing the purity of the body and so forth through meditation is the aspect of the middling [forbearance] level.” The four truths are divided into two sets: the first two have to do with entry into cyclic existence, whereas the last two have to do with reversing from cyclic existence. Conventionally, there is entrance into and reversal from cyclic existence. However, these do not exist ultimately. “Realizing the purity of the body and so forth” means to realize the purity of the body, speech, and mind through meditation. On the middling forbearance level, by observing the four noble truths, one realizes the purity of the body, speech, and mind; this is the aspect of the middling forbearance level.   If birth into cyclic existence were to exist ultimately, then birth into cyclic existence could not be changed. The fact that birth into cyclic existence is changeable means that it does not exist ultimately. When we analyze whether or not something exists ultimately, we need to examine various objects, such as persons, the form aggregate, etc. When analyzing these various objects, the reasoning that analyzes may be the same, but the modes of analysis with respect to different objects are not the same.
