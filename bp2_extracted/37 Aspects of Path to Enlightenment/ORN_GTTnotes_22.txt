EXPLANATION Know that the subject—those of the buddhas who will arise, the bodhisattvas themselves—the objects of intent as being of three types, because there are three objects of intent due to possession of three greatnesses. Meaning of positing the achievings as the entity of joyous effort It has been explained that:  At the beginning, from the joyous effort of armor,  The joyous effort of applications, strongly engaging properly, and  The joyous effort that is undiscouraged, undisturbed, and not complacent.   Hence, there are: (i) the joyous effort of armor, (ii) the joyous effort of engaging in the practice of the applications, (iii) the joyous effort that is undiscouraged regarding all of the Mahayana qualities, and is undisturbed by discordant classes, and (iv) the joyous effort that is not complacent. {283}  It is taught that in this manner, [the achievings] are distinguished by joyous effort. Even though one may have generated the mind aspiring for supreme enlightenment, if one does not initiate joyous effort through effort in the practice of the achievings diligently, it will merely be a noble thought; one will not be able to attain unsurpassable enlightenment. However, it is inappropriate to assert that if something is the entity of the achievings, it is pervaded by being joyous effort, because the dissimilar patterns of the awareness abide unmixed like the petals of the lotus.   In this passage, we see the presentation of four types of joyous effort, which are correlated with the four achievings. Having seen this, one might think that if it is the entity of the achievings, then it must be joyous effort, but this is not correct; there is no pervasion. We can say that in order to have these achievings, one must have joyous effort. The correct meaning is that if one has joyous effort, then one would have the achievings.   The passage also mentions that if one does not initiate joyous effort after having generated conventional bodhicitta, then this is just a noble thought; one will not be able to attain enlightenment. When we practice the Dharma, it is important to keep the mind undiscouraged, undisturbed, and not complacent.   With respect to Dharma practice, we should not be complacent. In general, it is said that we should practice contentment, but this should not apply to everything, for instance, Dharma practice. With respect to Dharma practice, we should have great desire, and should not be contented or satisfied. When we talk about practicing Dharma, this does not mean simply studying texts, reciting prayers, and so forth. When somebody makes us angry, this is an opportunity to practice the Dharma. If you feel that someone is harming you and you get angry, this means you do not know what Dharma practice is about. Such conditions should not be seen as obstacles to Dharma practice, but as something that benefits you in terms of Dharma. If you do not understand this, and you think that practicing Dharma simply means studying texts, reciting prayers, etc., then your understanding of Dharma practice is actually distorted. The main point when practicing generosity is the motivation; if we have a bodhicitta motivation, the practice becomes a practice of the perfection of generosity.
