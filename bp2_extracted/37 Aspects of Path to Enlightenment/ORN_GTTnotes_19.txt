Twelve modes of observation of the path of preparation   I would like to explain something about this section. As mentioned on Friday, the twelve modes of observation of the Mahayana path of preparation can be summarized into four modes of observation: observing the four truths (1) by means of cause, (2) by means of result, (3) by means of the very entity, and (4) by means of the aspects of dharmata. This is explained on p. 140.  The first three occur during subsequent attainment, and the fourth during meditative equipoise. Perhaps this is a summarized way of looking at the twelve modes of observation. We are going into these modes in a very extensive way, but if you were to summarize them, you can consider them more easily in terms of cause, result, entity, and the aspect of dharmata. The next outline is related to stanza 1.28, on p. 141: “The objects of observation are impermanence and so forth, / Bearing the support of the truths…” This phrase, “bearing the support of the truths,” only occurs for the small heat level, and does not appear in the root text for the other levels. The debate concerns this phrase. To understand the objection, you need to have some knowledge regarding the mode of expression. For example, when we say “impermanent sound” and “the impermanence of sound,” these expressions mean different things. When we say “impermanent sound,” “sound” is taken as the principal and is qualified by impermanence, and “impermanent” is a qualification of sound. But when we say “the impermanence of sound,” impermanence is taken as the principal, and sound acts as its qualification. Regardless of whether we say “impermanent sound” or “impermanence of sound,” both sound and impermanence are indicated, but the two expressions differ in terms of the mode of indication. When we say “impermanent sound,” it is as though sound is the basis for characterization, and impermanence is the attribute that characterizes

In this way, the king’s attachment would be reduced, and when he hears that there is no hope, he will not be so shocked. If the doctor were to suddenly say “There is no hope,” the king would become very unhappy. But by approaching it in a gentle, gradual way, the king’s attachment will decrease, so eventually, when the doctor tells him there is no hope left, he will not suffer such a great shock. Stating the Cittamatra position and analyzing whether it is correct or incorrect Sutra says, “That lineage of the bodhisattvas, passing down from beginningless time, has come. What is the attribute of the six sources obtained by way of dharmata?” In the case of the Cittamatrins, interpreting the meaning of that passage, they say that if a basis-of-all is not posited, [lineage] is the potency that is suitable to produce the realizations of any of the three vehicles when the uncontaminated seed that exists upon the six inner sources meets with conditions. {242} However, if a basis-of-all is posited, when those very uncontaminated seeds existing on the basis-of-all are not nourished by conditions, they are called ‘lineage abiding in the nature,’ and nourished by the conditions of hearing, they will become special, called the ‘developmental lineage.’  Here we are looking at the Cittamatra position, and analyzing whether or not it is correct. The Cittamatrins say that there are two types of lineage: lineage abiding in the nature, and developmental lineage. And there are two cases to consider: 1) If a basis-of-all is not posited, “[lineage] is the potency that is suitable to produce the realizations of any of the three vehicles when the uncontaminated seed that exists upon the six inner sources meets with conditions.” So when a basis-of-all is posited, then lineage is posited as this uncontaminated seed that exist upon the six inner sources. 2) If a basis-of-all is posited, the lineage is posited as the uncontaminated seeds that exist on the basis-of-all.
