 1  Masters Program: Ornament for Clear Realization Commentary by Geshe Tenzin Tenphel (week 30)   Monday afternoon, 12 January 2009  [Geshe-la began by giving practical advice, and spoke about the need to complement our study with inner development and practice.]  We are studying the Ornament for Clear Realization, and there is a lot of material covered in the various commentaries, such as Explanation: Ornament of the Essence by Gyeltsab Je and Golden Rosary by Lama Tsongkhapa. These texts are very extensive, and it is difficult to absorb all the details in these. You have to start with the eight categories as a framework, and then go on to understand the seventy topics that characterize the eight categories. If you are able to hold in mind these eight categories and seventy topics, then you can go on to supplement these with details. For example, in the first category, we have the exalted knower of all aspects, and that is characterized by ten topics, the first of which is the Mahayana conventional generated mind. To supplement this, you can think of how there are two divisions by way of entity, three divisions by way of mode of generation, and twenty-two by way of analogy. This way, you can use the framework of the eight categories and seventy topics to understand the details. If you try to understand the details from the beginning, you will have difficulty remembering them. Basically, my point here is that you have to start with the eight categories, use the seventy topics to understand them well, and on top of that you can fill in the details.   In the monasteries, at the very least we need to memorize the root text, Ornament for Clear Realization, and the Seventy Topics. If you cannot do that, you must at least be able to use that information as a framework for understanding the text. In the monasteries, those monks who are more intelligent would also memorize General Meaning, or Ocean of Sport, by Jetsun Chokyi Gyeltsen, while some would even memorize Explanation: Ornament of the Essence by Gyeltsab Je and Lama Tsongkhapa’s Golden Rosary. In the monasteries, there are exams every year, and as a minimum the monks have to be able to memorize twenty-five folios of the text. They must be able to recite by memory at least these twenty-five folios for the exam. This practice of memorizing twenty-five folios as a minimum every year was started in India; in Tibet, they would memorize more. Now, in India, there are monks who are able to recite 1,000 or thousands of pages by memory.   Let us now look at the text. The context of the section we are going through is that in chapter four, we started looking at the 173 aspects of the three exalted knowers. They are divided into three:  • aspects of a knower of bases • aspects of a knower of paths and • aspects of an exalted knower of all aspects [p. 347 under 2B2C-2A1B-1A1B. Extensive explanation].  The first of these three groups, the aspects of a knower of bases, is divided into twenty-seven, which are classified in four groups: four, four, four, and fifteen.  3 Let us look at the first of the six points discussed here. Haribhadra’s commentary says “the lack of being thoroughly afflicted.” This is supposed to refer to the antidote to the knowledge obscurations that are conceptualizations, corresponding to “(1) the thoroughly afflicted.” We are not talking about the direct antidote to the thoroughly afflicted. The explanation clarifies this: “For the third path that is related to the explanation of the antidotes to the afflictive obscurations above, the six are respectively the individually ascertaining antidotes to the conceptualizations of apprehendeds. The subject—the exalted wisdoms (directly realizing that apprehendeds are not external objects) in the continuum of a bodhisattva on the path of seeing—abandon the knowledge obscurations that are conceptions of apprehendeds: having observed [apprehendeds] as external objects, adhering to (1) the thoroughly afflicted due to grasping and so forth…(i) because they directly realize (1) the lack of being thoroughly afflicted due to external objects.” Here, the antidotes are antidotes to the conceptions of external objects. Although we talk about the antidotes, is it the case that we are talking about the direct antidotes to the afflictions, the thoroughly afflicted? These six points are covered in the section we have read, so think about them and we can discuss them in greater detail tomorrow.   We are talking about these consciousnesses that adhere to external objects, i.e. to apprehendeds and apprehenders as different substances. The context of the discussion is that the objects are forms and so forth. We can discuss this in terms of sense consciousnesses, such as the eye consciousness apprehending form, etc. We can also talk about these minds or consciousnesses in terms of conceptions. Before Lama Tsongkhapa’s time, these kinds of consciousnesses were only talked about in terms of sense consciousnesses, and Lama Tsongkhapa extended this explanation also to conceptions, or conceptualizations, so he was called the “Trailblazer of the Snowy Land.” Perhaps we can discuss tomorrow as to how we can apply this explanation to conceptions. This application to the conceptions is extensively discussed in Lama Tsongkhapa’s The Essence of Eloquence: A Treatise Differentiating the Interpretable and the Definite and is quite a difficult section. If you can understand this well, you can negate all kinds of conceptions of external objects.   If I go into a lot of material today, you may become a bit dazed, so I will take some questions now.  Questions and answers  Question: In some of the phrases, it is difficult to understand the meaning, for example, “respectively (i) because they directly realize (1) the lack of being thoroughly afflicted due to external objects.” Can you explain the meaning of this? Geshe-la: This phrase explains the antidote, saying “they,” meaning the exalted wisdoms mentioned in the subject above, “directly realize the lack of being thoroughly afflicted due to external objects.” We need to understand the situation here: one can observe forms, etc., as external objects, and due to that kind of observation, thoroughly afflicted phenomena can arise. As a result of realizing that there are no external objects, the thoroughly afflicted that are based on observing forms, etc., as external objects, do not arise. This is why the text says: “they directly realize the lack of being thoroughly afflicted due to external objects.”  You should apply a similar understanding to the second point: “(2) the lack of being completely pure due to external objects.” As a result of observing forms, etc., as external objects, completely pure phenomena, such as faith, can arise. If one realizes that there are no external objects, faith and so forth that arise due to observing forms, etc., as external objects will not arise. So one realizes the lack of being completely pure due to external objects. In fact, there are no external objects. Although there are no forms and so forth that are external objects, one can observe forms, and due to their appearing as external objects, one can generate grasping, faith, and so forth. Suppose you ask: do the objects that are observed exist or not? Then you have to answer very carefully. The external objects do not exist, but the forms and so forth that appear as external objects do exist.

 6 I’d like to speak about the emptiness of duality. Here we are in the Middle Way Autonomy school. This is divided into two: Sutra Autonomy school and Yogic Autonomy school. The Sutra Autonomy school does not accept the emptiness of duality, but the Yogic Autonomy school does assert emptiness of duality. The reason the Sutra Autonomy school does not accept the emptiness of duality is because they assert external objects, whereas the Yogic Autonomy school does not accept external objects. In this respect, the Yogic Autonomy school is similar to the Mind-Only school, but in general, they are very different. The Mind-Only school asserts that imputational factors do not exist by way of their own character, but the Yogic Autonomy school says that if it is an established base, it is pervaded by being established by way of its own character. Based on these differences, the way in which they refute external objects is also different.   I will explain how the Mind-Only school refutes external objects. The view of emptiness of duality, the emptiness of apprehendeds and apprehenders being different substances, in which the existence of external objects is refuted, is well known as being asserted by the Mind-Only and the Yogic Autonomy schools. In order to understand the assertions well, we need first to understand the Mind-Only school assertion of the three characters: other-powered natures, imputational natures [or imputational factors], and thoroughly established natures. I will simply posit some examples to explain these, rather than giving their definitions.   1) Other-powered natures: an example is form.  2) Imputational natures: there are two types of these: by way of entity and by way of attributes.  a. An imputational nature that is a superimposition by way of entity: an example is form being established by way of its own character as a basis for applying/engaging the term “form aggregate.” The object of negation is an imputational factor.  b. An imputational factor by way of attribute: an example is form being established by way of its own character as a basis for applying/engaging the term “the production of form.”  In this way, I have explained the existence of two types of imputational factors: by way of entity and by way of attribute. Both of these are imputational factors that are objects to be negated. These two divisions in turn have their sub-divisions. That which is the basis for applying or engaging the term “form aggregate” is not an imputational factor that is an object to be negated. That is posited or stated as a basis of emptiness. To repeat: form being established by way of its own character as a basis for applying/engaging the term “form aggregate” is an imputational factor that is an object to be negated; however, the basis for engaging the term “form aggregate” is a basis of emptiness. Similarly, form being established by way of its own character as a basis for applying/engaging the term “the production of form” is an imputational factor that is an object to be negated, but the basis for engaging the term “the production of form” is a basis of emptiness. This is the explanation in terms of terms.   Similarly, we can provide an explanation in terms of conceptions. Form being established by way of its own character as a basis of adherence of the conception apprehending form is an imputational factor that is an object to be negated. The basis of adherence of the conception apprehending form is a basis of emptiness.  9 Questions and answers  Student: In Explanation: Ornament of the Essence, p. 343, it says: “(a) Having individually indicated the aspects of the three exalted knowers in the first three chapters …” How are the aspects of the three exalted knowers “individually indicated” in the first three chapters? Geshe-la: “Having individually indicated the aspects of the three exalted knowers in the first three chapters” simply means that the aspects of the exalted knower of all aspects are indicated in the first chapter, those of the knower of paths in the second chapter, and those of the knower of bases in the third chapter. The aspects of the three exalted knowers indicated in the first three chapters do not refer to the 173 aspects of the three exalted knowers indicated in the fourth chapter. Perhaps we can interpret these aspects in the first three chapters to refer to the characterizing topics of the three exalted knowers in the first three chapters.   In fact, this word “indicated” that has been used many times in the text is quite elusive; it is difficult to decide if something has or has not been indicated. Although the text says “having individually indicated,” in the first three chapters the three exalted knowers are explained by way of their characterizing topics: in the first chapter, we find the ten topics that characterize the exalted knower of all aspects, in the second chapter, the nine topics of knower of paths, and so forth. We do not find the 173 aspects, e.g. the 110 of the exalted knower of all aspects in the first chapter, and so on; we do not see these explicitly. It is difficult to decide if something is indicated or not. In fact, if it is an established base, we say that it is pervaded by being the subject matter of the first chapter. Although we do not see an explicit indication, it has been implicitly indicated. In the first chapter, one of the topics is objects of observation. These are actually mutually inclusive with established bases, so you would have to say that all established bases have been indicated in the first chapter, but whether or not every single established base has been explicitly indicated is another question.  10 Geshe-la: Here, “the lack of other objects” means the lack of external objects, and refers to the apprehendeds in the next phrase. The Path of Seeing  To go into some detail here, let us discuss someone who is definite in the Mahayana lineage. When this person attains the Mahayana path of seeing, s/he attains the uninterrupted path of the path of seeing. Upon entering the path of seeing, this person abides in single-pointed meditative equipoise. This acts as the direct antidote to the acquired conception of true existence. This uninterrupted path also acts as the antidote to the acquired conception of a self of persons as well as to the acquired conception of apprehendeds and apprehenders being different substances. After this uninterrupted path of the path of seeing, one attains the liberated path in which these three kinds of acquired conceptions have been abandoned. Without staying long, one arises into subsequent attainment. There is no need to stay long, because one has abandoned the objects that are to be abandoned and has done the work that needs to be done, so one promptly arises in subsequent attainment.   The period in which one abides in subsequent attainment and in the meditative equipoise that is neither an uninterrupted path nor a liberated path lasts a very long time. When I say that subsequent attainment and meditative equipoise that is neither an uninterrupted path nor a liberated path last a very long time, I am not talking about this being a single session lasting a long time. There may be many different sessions. One is in subsequent attainment, and then one enters meditative equipoise that is neither an uninterrupted path nor a liberated path, and then again arises into subsequent attainment, and so on. There are many such alternations; this period of various sessions of subsequent attainment and meditative equipoise that is neither an uninterrupted path nor a liberated path lasts a very long time. The first two objects of abandonment are aspiration and attachment. What is the difference between them? Aspiration is the desire to view the object, while attachment, having seen the object, does not want to be separated from it. The third is joy: “craving that takes joy in rebirth.” Rebirth means taking rebirth as a human or god; this is slightly different from the first two objects of abandonment, aspiration and attachment. The object of abandonment is the attachment that craves not to be separated from the attributes of the desire realm. The antidote is the “exalted wisdom of a bodhisattva that does not abide in the manner of adhering to not being separated from the attributes of the desire realm.” In other words, as the antidote to abiding in the manner of adhering…, we have the

The antidote is the “exalted wisdom of a bodhisattva that has pacified craving that takes joy in the body,” i.e. in rebirth as a human or god. Advice for practice: impermanence  With regard to the improper indicative conceptions: cleanness, happiness, permanence, and self, I’d like to say something that is relevant for practice. With regard to happiness, we sometimes feel we are happy, and become contented or complacent with the fact that we are well and happy. This state 
Masters Program: Ornament Week 30: January 12–16, 2009  
 30 of wellness is subject to disintegration; it is transitory. It is not wise to be complacent when we feel well. If we are complacent and we suddenly experience a problem, the mind becomes very unhappy. We should remind ourselves that this state of wellness in samsara is impermanent and subject to conditions, and it is a matter of time before it disappears. Then when this state comes to an end and we experience difficulties, we will be prepared. When we feel well, we sometimes get the sense that we will be well forever, but this is impossible. But if we have this mindfulness that this state of happiness will not last forever, then our minds are prepared for the time when it comes to an end and we encounter problems.   At this point in time, our minds are not completely free, or under our control. We should thus not expect that any wellness we experience will last forever. As practitioners, we should try to make sure our mental states correspond to reality. The reality is that things are impermanent, or transient; if we have grasping to states of well-being, this contradicts reality. As practitioners, we should try to make sure our views and behavior correspond to reality, without contradicting it. In our human life, we should try to avoid clinging, complacency, and attachment to the “good things” in life. The tendency to cling or grasp will produce suffering. Destroying suffering does not mean destroying the good things in life; it means destroying the negative mental states that produce suffering. We have a conception of permanence, clinging to stability until the end of cyclic existence, but in fact we are changing from moment to moment, and this life will not last forever. We will die and move on to the next life. The conception of permanence in our minds is very harmful; it will ruin our practice and study of the Dharma, and it causes us to waste our time. Therefore, it is very important to repeatedly contemplate death and impermanence.   How do we contemplate death and impermanence? In addition to remembering death and impermanence, we need to use this to remind ourselves to practice well and to not waste our time. Sometimes I discuss with other geshes about how we actually cultivate antidotes to the various afflictions, such as attachment. It is said that in order to counteract attachment, we should meditate on ugliness, in particular with relation to the body. We should meditate on a body that seems attractive as being repulsive. Without habituation, this is very difficult. If a person’s body is ugly, we do not need to make effort, but if it is attractive, it is very difficult to cultivate this antidote. Even for a beautiful body, we can contemplate on how it is unclean and so forth, but this is not easy. It is difficult for such an antidote to be effective the first time, so we need to apply it again and again. We also need find the right balance, because if we apply it too much [incorrectly], we might generate anger, so we need to find the right balance.      END 
