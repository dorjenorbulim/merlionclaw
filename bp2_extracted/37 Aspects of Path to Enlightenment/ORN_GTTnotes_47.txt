 1  Masters Program: Ornament for Clear Realization Commentary by Geshe Tenzin Tenphel (week 47)  Monday afternoon, 25 May 2009  General teachings • Geshe-la discussed various points about making offerings.  • He explained the significance of the yellow hat worn by Gelug monks: the many threads at the top represent the 1,000 buddhas of the fortunate eon; the main part of the hat represents the pure land; the red trim around the bottom represents Manjusri; the white lining represents Avalokiteshvara; the blue piece at the back represents Vajrapani; and the twelve and three lines of stitching on the back part symbolize the twelve branches of scriptures and the three baskets. Some monks in Tibet who were poor and did not have buddha statues would put their hat on the altar and make offerings to it! These hats should not be placed on the floor or in a low place. What do we mean when we say that a bodhisattva is obstructed by one rebirth? This means that the bodhisattva does not attain enlightenment in the present life; the bodhisattva has to take a rebirth and then attains enlightenment in that second rebirth. As I have explained before, setting trainees on certain levels refers to the buddhas giving them teachings so that they can work toward and attain that level. We are talking about trainees attaining the actual realizations that allow them to proceed on the path. This does not mean that they just understand the meaning of the words of the teachings; they have to attain the actual realizations.   For example, in the monasteries, in the monastic education, we start with Collected Topics and take exams. If we pass the exams, then we go to the next level, etc. In that way, we cover various subjects one by one, and through taking many levels of exams, one eventually attains the geshe degree. Such a system of promotion is really based on knowledge of the words; we are not promoted on the basis of having attained realizations. One moves from one level to the next based on having passed the exams that test mainly the words. When we talk [here] about trainees being set in the various levels, it is a matter of the attainment of various realizations, one after another.

 2  Remember that when you study these great texts, you must try to put what you’ve learned into practice, and based on practice, generate the respective experience for the various classes of realizations. If you can do that, teaching Dharma will be most effective; when you teach based on your experience, you can cause listeners to develop the right understanding and the right realizations. If you are not able to practice the teachings in their entirety and to generate the realizations, you can also teach based on your understanding; without the slightest miserliness, you try to share what you’ve understood and in this way the listeners can benefit. Of course, there are people who may not be able to practice, but among the listeners, there may be some who can practice. In this way, you are able to cause others to understand and practice the Dharma.   There was a carpenter who, when teaching his son, did not teach everything. For example, if a piece of wood was uneven on the outside, there is a specific technique for cutting the wood properly so that you can use it. The father was afraid that if he taught the son everything, the son would surpass him; out of jealousy he did not teach him this technique. One day there was a flood, and the carpenter, the father, was carried away by strong waters. As he was being carried away, he shouted to the son, “This is how you cut the wood,” trying to explain the technique, to which the son replied, “It’s a bit too late!” In this way, the father harbored miserliness toward his own son. If a father can behave in this manner toward his own son, it is possible [for teachers] to be miserly with regard to teaching the Dharma [to their students]. If one were to teach the Dharma to the students and the students became better than the explainer, wouldn’t this be wonderful? The explainer should rejoice and feel delighted; trying to hide Dharma [teachings] from the students out of miserliness is actually a huge negativity with respect to teaching the Dharma.   There was a student who attained enlightenment before his teacher. After he attained enlightenment, he met the reincarnation of his teacher a few lifetimes later. This buddha prostrated to the reincarnation, who was then a father of a household, upon which the man asked, “You are a buddha. Why are you prostrating to me”? The buddha replied, “You were my teacher several lifetimes ago, and as a result of you being my teacher, I am fully enlightened now. You are thus my lama, and you are my guru. That is why I’m prostrating to you.” The man said, “I was your teacher? I don’t remember!”   Another thing to note when teaching Dharma is that one should not be sectarian. One should not criticize certain traditions and other schools, saying, “This school is good; other schools are bad,” “This tradition is good; other traditions are bad,” etc. This is very harmful. Among students, it is also important not to promote disharmony, causing the group to be divided; this is a form of schism. One should also not cause other explainers of Dharma to be divided, by talking or behaving in such a way that the group becomes disharmonious. Bear these points in mind, and be careful when you teach Dharma in the future.   Explanation: Ornament of the Essence, p. 621: 2B2C-3B2A-3A1D-6B2. When we think about the relation between actions and results, we need to try to develop conviction in the law of karma. Having contemplated the various characteristics of the law of karma, we should use this principle like a witness to our actions. As ordinary beings, we do make mistakes. When we make mistakes, it is important to engage in confession and generate the mind of restraint, not wanting to repeat that action in the future.   Sometimes people make mistakes, and then fall into depression, feeling very unhappy about those mistakes without constructively engaging in some method to purify the mistakes. This is not helpful, so it is important to engage in confession. Sometimes when people meet problems, they have a reaction of not being able to accept reality, and they say, “This is impossible.” For example, they are in a relationship with another person, and when this relationship turns sour, they just groan and moan, saying, “This is impossible.” But of course, it is possible; it just happened! This attitude of rejection, of not wanting to accept reality, is actually a cause of mental misery. It is important to accept [reality], and it is possible to purify mistakes as well. Compounded samsara and uncompounded nirvana are not different ultimately; they are equal in being empty of true existence. This explanation here is a reference to the application of the equality of cyclic existence and peace. To explain this point a little, we must say that samsara and nirvana are not equal; however, they are equal in being empty of true existence, in being dependent-arisings. Because they are dependent-arisings, emptiness of true existence is feasible. Because the presentation of dependent-arising is feasible, the presentation of samsara and the presentation of nirvana are also feasible. If, instead, the presentation of dependent-arising were not feasible, the presentations of samsara and nirvana would also not be feasible.  8  Questions and Answers  Student: Both the fifteenth and sixteenth exalted activities [p. 621] seem to talk about setting trainees in being obstructed by one rebirth—what is the difference? Geshe-la: If one has just attained the tenth ground, one is necessarily obstructed by one rebirth. [However,] if one is obstructed by one rebirth, it is not necessary that one has just [newly] attained the tenth ground. If we compare someone who is obstructed by one rebirth with someone who has just attained the tenth ground, we would say that the new attainment of the tenth ground is a particularity, or instance, of being obstructed by one rebirth. If someone has just attained the tenth ground, one is necessarily obstructed by one rebirth, but if one is obstructed by one rebirth, one is not pervaded by having just attained the tenth ground. The sequence of presentation in this text is that [first] the trainees are set in the tenth ground just attained, i.e. they are set in newly attaining the tenth ground, and after that, they are set in being obstructed by one rebirth explained under a few outlines [p. 621].   Student 2: On p. 616, under point 26, what is the meaning of “to degenerate from the sphere of the compounded” in the sutra citation?  Geshe-la: This entire statement (“If one were to degenerate from the sphere of the compounded, the sphere of the uncompounded cannot be observed”) means that the sphere of the compounded and the sphere of the uncompounded are one entity; they are not different entities.   Student 3: Related to points 15 and 16 [p. 621], the “bodhisattva who is obstructed by one rebirth” means that the bodhisattva has to take one more rebirth. Upon taking rebirth, he/she is a baby bodhisattva. Does this baby have all the realizations of the previous lives when he/she is born? Geshe-la: Of course, the tenth grounder is not an ordinary person. There are various possibilities for taking rebirth: one possibility is to take rebirth in Akanishta pure land, where there is no need for parents, so we don’t have to think about the little baby in Akanishta. Another possibility is rebirth in the desire realm. In that case, this rebirth takes place in the context of being conceived in the mother’s womb; however, the tenth grounder is not an ordinary person, so you should not think that the tenth grounder is an ordinary baby in the mother’s womb. In the context of tantra, there are very elaborate explanations of how, while abiding in the womb, the deities’ mandala with the inestimable, or celestial, mansion is complete, and so forth.   Student 4: On the first ground, the bodhisattva has already transcended [the sufferings of] old age, sickness, death, etc.—why does the tenth-ground bodhisattva still have to take rebirth? Geshe-la: This explanation of a bodhisattva taking rebirth is probably an explanation of how the bodhisattva shows appearances concordant with the behavior [of people] in the desire realm where people age, die, etc.   Student 5: On p. 600, in point seventeen, it says, “He proceeds without swaying, due to always not having a crooked mind that pays attention to two loci.” What is “a crooked mind that pays attention to two loci”? Geshe-la: Perhaps it means that if the mind is stable, one walks in a stable way; if one does not have a stable mind, one will not walk in a stable way.  Student 6: If a tenth-ground bodhisattva has sent out emanations to different realms, when he/she attains enlightenment in Akanishta, do his/her emanations become emanation bodies? Geshe-la: Some of the emanations become emanation bodies, but not all of them; it depends on the necessity of the occasion. It is not necessary for some of these emanations to remain, so they will be gathered back into the emanator in Akanishta.

 9  Student 7: Regarding point seventeen [p. 621], it is said that the tenth-ground bodhisattvas during subsequent attainment are equal to the buddhas from the point of view of enacting the welfare of sentient beings. But it seems that the tenth-ground bodhisattvas during subsequent attainment are not equal to the buddhas, because, in terms of enacting the welfare of sentient beings, the buddhas are spontaneous, whereas the bodhisattvas are not spontaneous and still need subtle exertion. Having said that, it is true that the bodhisattvas at this point still need subtle exertion. Such statements—“the buddhas and the tenth-ground bodhisattvas during subsequent attainment are equal in terms of enacting the welfare of sentient beings”—are made from the point of view of common appearances. Likewise, the various measurements of the buddha’s exalted body, e.g. the height, length, etc. are explained from the point of view of common appearances. In the text, we see descriptions of the Buddha’s height being seven cubits, etc., but in fact, the Buddha’s height is limitless. There are stories of disciples who flew into space and tried to see the top of the Buddha’s head, but could not reach it. Likewise, the buddha’s speech is also without limit; no matter how far away one is from the buddha when he is teaching, upon hearing his speech, it is as though one was sitting right in front of the buddha. This is not correct because even those that have not been explicitly indicated there but have been explicitly indicated on other occasions are definitely necessary as branches of the stages of the paths of clear realizations. If those that have been indicated on other occasions are explicitly indicated on those two occasions, then since [Maitreya] has simply written down the entire Ornament for Clear Realization there, it follows for you that there would be a huge fault of repetition. If the other clear realizations have not been indicated on any occasion, there would be the fault that they would not be realized.       Therefore, although a certain portion of the scope and the coarse excellent qualities existing from the ground of a beginner through to the buddha ground has been indicated on the occasions of the generated mind and the exalted activities, other clear realizations that have not been explained there have been explained through connection with the [topics on] instructions, the path of preparation, and so on. Since the meanings are similar for the path of preparation, they must be complete on the occasion of that, but there are infinite differences in whether or not a single term is able to explicitly indicate [them].       It seems that many scholars have made mistakes due to not having eliminated qualms regarding this engagement through an eliminative expresser. Although it is a single isolate, since it has been indicated many times by way of the differences that are dissimilar purposes, how could there even be a mere whiff of the fault of repetition? For, explaining an isolate during the two occasions of the path of accumulation and the path of preparation is to make it known that it exists and is cultivated on both those occasions. All types of clear realizations are cultivated on the occasion of a single path of accumulation, {420} but if all the clear realizations were indicated there, then the author of the treatise that indicates the complete stages of the path would be unskillful. It seems that a single isolate has been indicated with many dissimilar names. Recall that the Ornament for Clear Realization is a commentary on the three types of Perfection of Wisdom Sutras: extensive, medium, and brief. This means that in each of the three types of Perfection of Wisdom Sutras, we should be able to find the eight, six, and three summarized meanings. This explanation of the three sets of summarized meanings—the eight, six, and three—is explained to be presented for the sake of trainees with liking for the extensive, middling, and brief [elaborations of the meanings]; they are not presented from the point of view of  the faculties of the trainees. The three types of Perfection of Wisdom Sutras—the extensive, medium, and brief—are explained to be taught respectively for sharp-faculty, middling-faculty, and dull-faculty trainees; the extensive Perfection of Wisdom Sutra is taught for sharp-faculty trainees, the medium Perfection of Wisdom Sutra for middling-faculty trainees, and the brief Perfection of Wisdom Sutra for dull-faculty trainees.   Having said that, it is also explained that sharp-faculty trainees do not need much commentary to understand certain points; middling-faculty trainees need more commentary than those of sharp faculty; and dull-faculty trainees need plenty of commentary to understand the same points.   Based on this explanation, you might think that there is a contradiction in saying that the extensive Perfection of Wisdom Sutra is explained for sharp-faculty trainees, the medium for middling-faculty trainees, and the brief for dull-faculty trainees. How do we reconcile these two sets of explanation? Those of sharp faculty can understand points that are explained based on a slight amount of explanation; since they have this ability to comprehend the material, even if a lot is said (i.e. if a very extensive explanation is given), they are able to absorb it. Those of middling faculty need more commentary to understand the same points; because their ability to grasp these points is lesser than those of sharp faculty, if too much is said, they will have difficulty comprehending the material. For those of dull faculty, if a lot of explanation is given, they will be unable to absorb the material, because they have a very limited ability compared to the other two types of trainees; to understand the same points, they need a lot of explanation, but if a very extensive explanation is given, they have difficulty in absorbing, or assimilating, all the material.   This is definitely the case in the monastery, when the students go to their teachers for teachings. Sometimes the teachings go for up to four hours. At the beginning, all the students are very attentive, sitting upright. After two hours, some are nodding off, and after three hours more are drowsy and inattentive. If you observe this group of students, those who are good in study are still attentive even after four hours, which confirms this explanation that I have given. Of course, there is no pervasion! From this passage, we can understand that while it is important to study, and it is excellent to engage in the study of such texts, we must take care not to have a deceitful motivation. Through understanding a little about such texts, pride can grow in us; pride is neither beneficial to others or ourselves. If we have pride, we will not be able to develop realizations. We need to have a motivation untainted by craving for fame, honor, or gain. These are important points to bear in mind.   If one comes across points that are difficult to understand, there is no need to become discouraged. According to one’s ability, one tries to understand the text; one should persevere in understanding the text. Generating pride is very undesirable; it can lead to praising oneself and disparaging others. As I’ve mentioned, based on our studies, we should try to integrate what has been understood into our lives and to generate realizations. Even if we cannot develop realizations, we should share with others what we have understood. There will be some among the listeners who will put the teachings into practice; in this way, we benefit others and ourselves.

Student: What is the difference between an “official founder” buddha, such as Shakyamuni Buddha, and an “ordinary” buddha, such as Guru Rinpoche, etc.? Geshe-la: You are asking the difference between “founder” buddhas, such as the one thousand buddhas of this fortunate eons, and those not included in these, such as Milarepa. The difference between a founder buddha and a non-founder buddha is mainly in the fact that the non-founder buddhas have a body that appears to ordinary beings as an ordinary person, e.g. Milarepa attained enlightenment and is not included among the one thousand buddhas of the fortunate eon. He takes the aspect of a person with an ordinary body, who can appear to ordinary beings like us. There are two ways of answering this:  1) According to the sutra system: from the sutra point of view, Milarepa attained enlightenment in Akanishta; the body seen in our realm is actually an emanation body. The complete enjoyment body is attained in Akanishta and what appears to us in this realm is the emanation body of Milarepa.  2) According to the tantra system: from the tantra point of view, I cannot answer well. You can wait for your answer until you study the module on tantra!

Student: If someone receives teachings from a yidam, are they seeing the complete enjoyment body, e.g. Lama Tsongkhapa seeing Manjusri? Geshe-la: Was Lama Tsongkhapa perceiving the complete enjoyment body of Manjusri? I would say yes, but this activity of Lama Tsongkhapa is what appears in the perspective of trainees. Lama Tsongkhapa did many activities, such as making mandala offerings, prostrations, etc., but this display of such activities was advice to his disciples: that they need to train in this way, by offering mandalas, etc. In fact, Lama Tsongkhapa is Manjusri. Of course, it is very difficult to know if this is the reality, or to know what the reality is. In the sutra system, it is difficult for the descriptions to accord exactly with reality.   Student: It is stated (p. 608) that the complete enjoyment body arises in dependence on the accumulation of merit, and the supreme emanation body from the accumulations of merit and wisdom. How is this the case, when it is said that the complete enjoyment body is the empowering condition for the supreme emanation body? The cause, the complete enjoyment body, arises in dependence on the accumulation of merit; why does its result, the supreme emanation body, arise from both accumulations? Geshe-la: This statement in the text is just a way of explanation. We should understand that the accumulation of merit mainly deposits imprints for the form bodies; the accumulation of exalted wisdom mainly deposits imprints for the exalted wisdom truth body. This does not mean that the accumulation of merit does not deposit an imprint for the exalted wisdom truth body, and the accumulation of exalted wisdom does not deposit an imprint for the form bodies. From the complete enjoyment body acting as the empowering condition, the supreme emanation body arises. The complete enjoyment body arises in dependence on having gathered the accumulation of merit; without the accumulation of merit acting as a backing support, how could the supreme emanation body arise?   Student: Is the material that we will study in the Middle Way [the next module] sufficient to generate a correct meaning-generality of emptiness? Geshe-la: It depends on the individual, but it is possible to gain a correct understanding of what emptiness is. To gain a correct meaning-generality of emptiness is to realize emptiness (conceptually).   Student: What is the difference between a correct understanding and a correct meaning-generality? Geshe-la: The fact that someone has a correct understanding of emptiness does not mean they have gained a correct meaning-generality of emptiness. To gain a correct understanding of emptiness means you can express, “Emptiness means this, not this, etc.” This does not mean that you have a correct meaning-generality of emptiness. To have a correct meaning-generality of emptiness means to realize emptiness (conceptually, not directly). The process is that you first gain a correct understanding, then you realize emptiness through a meaning-generality, and finally you realize it directly.

 36 Student: If you gain a correct inferential realization of emptiness, is it possible for it to degenerate in one lifetime, if you don't become sick or have an accident?  Geshe-la: It is possible for an inferential realization of emptiness to degenerate in the life in which it is attained, even if one does not become sick or have an accident. It may be that someone contemplates emptiness and arrives at an inferential understanding of emptiness. There is no reason why this realization will continue. If one does not put in effort to maintain the continuity of the realization, to develop one’s mind, then the realization degenerates. This is why it is crucial to develop meditative stabilization. Without talking about a topic such as emptiness, let us consider a direct perceiver upon meeting a person. We may meet someone and have a conversation, and for a few years we may not see this person, and then upon meeting this person again, it can be the case that one does not remember the person.   Student: If someone has a direct realization of emptiness in this life, is it possible to lose this after one is reborn? For example, after a certain lama passes away and the reincarnation is recognized, the reincarnation has to undergo education for many years, so it seems they have lost their realizations.  Geshe-la: One thing to note is that in the system of recognizing reincarnations of lamas, the reincarnation recognized is not necessarily the reincarnation of the lama. A better answer would be that the fact that a reincarnated lama has to undergo studies again does not mean this lama has forgotten what he/she knew in a previous life; the lama gives the appearance of studying in order to encourage their followers to study: “If a lama has to study, what about a person like me?” So the followers of the lama are encouraged to study the Dharma.   Unless one’s realization is a fully qualified one, it can degenerate. When we talk about the attainment of clairvoyance, meditative stabilization, and magical emanation, these can degenerate easily. If one were to meet someone with these attainments, one would be completely impressed. For instance, if I were to display such attainments, one would think “Wow, what a powerful being,” etc., but such attainments can degenerate. For example, if a person who has clairvoyance based on meditative stabilization generates craving at the time of death, that craving nourishes the karmic imprints for rebirth in samsara; [as a result,] the meditative stabilization immediately degenerates and the person takes rebirth in cyclic existence. It is said that in our previous lives, we have attained clairvoyances, magical emanations, etc. many times—do you remember them? If we had such powers now, we would be seen as completely extraordinary in this world!  Student: What would be some good subjects to study to prepare for the Middle Way subject? In fact, after one is promoted to the Lharam class—the class for attaining the Lharampa degree—in the six years of the Lharam class, one studies the Abhidharmakosha six times, and Vinaya six times. Student: It is taught that in our past lives, it is possible that we have generated clairvoyances, etc., many times and they have degenerated.
