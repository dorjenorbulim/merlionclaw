The uncommon Mahayana going for refuge  ROOT TEXT 4.33a Faith observing the buddhas and so forth;  EXPLANATION Having initially generated a faith of conviction and a lucid faith observing all the causes of the Mahayana path as well as their results, the buddhas and so forth, being skilled in them is called “skill in faith.” Just as it has been said, “Furthermore, faith comes first, like a mother who gives birth,” know that faith is the root of all excellent qualities, and know that, having taken all the Mahayana paths and results as objects of faith in one’s continuum, repeatedly familiarizing oneself with them establishes the root of the Mahayana path. Although refuge has been indicated as the principal meaning, skill in faith has been indicated first for the sake of identifying it as the root of the Mahayana path. {125}   Sutra says, “Without separating from the perfection of wisdom and skillful means , contemplate in the mind the buddhas of the three times, their aggregates of morality, and so forth, but do not take them as signs. Know that if a great bodhisattva does not take the three doors of liberation as signs, he will attain the unsurpassable complete enlightenment without falling into those two, [the hearer’s vehicle and the solitary realizer’s vehicle], and without dishonoring or degenerating [the bodhisattva vehicle].”  The difference between a Buddhist and a non-Buddhist is whether or not there is refuge. If one goes for refuge to the Buddha, Dharma, and Sangha, one is a Buddhist; otherwise, one is not a Buddhist. What does it mean to go for refuge? This should not be equated with attending a refuge ceremony. Some think, “If one goes to a teacher and says ‘please grant me refuge,’ and the teacher performs a ceremony to grant him/her refuge, then one becomes a Buddhist; but without attending such a ceremony, or a ritual, one does not become a Buddhist.” This is a misunderstanding.   Someone who, due to fearing the sufferings of the three lower realms, goes for refuge to the Three Jewels out of conviction that the Three Jewels have the power to protect one from this fear, is a person of small scope, or small capacity. One who has fear of suffering of cyclic existence in general and has the conviction that the Three Jewels can protect him/her from such a fear is a person of middle scope. One who does not just fear the suffering of cyclic existence but goes for refuge to the Three Jewels for the sake of all sentient beings is a person of great scope.

 3  In this context, the text describes Mahayana refuge. The Mahayana refuge is not the same as the refuge of the great scope. A Mahayana person is pervaded by being a person of the great scope; however, a person of the great scope is not pervaded by being a Mahayana person. For example, prior to generating bodhicitta, one generates great compassion. A person who has generated great compassion but not bodhicitta is a person of the great scope but not a Mahayana person. From the point of having generated bodhicitta, one is a Mahayana person as well as a person of the great scope.   In this context, there is a discussion on faith. Faith is very important. From among the various types of faith, the faith that comes from understanding is the best kind of faith, and it is important that we strive to generate this kind of stable faith; if we do not make effort to generate the faith that is generated from understanding, we will just have blind faith, which is not stable.   The beings of the three scopes have different ways of going for refuge. Beings of the small and middle scopes generate faith in their respective modes and then practice the instructions of the Three Jewels. Beings of the great scope, besides observing the objects of faith and putting into practice the instructions of the Three Jewels, also seek to become the Three Jewels. In general, beings of all three scopes go for refuge to the Buddha Jewel, the Dharma Jewel, and the Sangha Jewel. Those of the great scope take refuge in the Dharma Jewel and the Sangha Jewel in a specific way: when they go for refuge to the Sangha Jewel, they go for refuge to the Sangha Jewel of Mahayana aryas; when they go for refuge to the Dharma Jewel, they go for refuge to the Dharma in the continua of Mahayana aryas. Some of you have attended initiations bestowed by His Holiness the Dalai Lama. Before the actual initiation, he grants refuge. In the process of granting Mahayana refuge, when one goes for refuge to the Sangha Jewel, it is specifically mentioned that one goes for refuge to the “irreversible bodhisattva aryas.” Did you notice that? Maybe the translator did not translate it! When Mahayana refuge is granted and one goes for refuge to the Sangha Jewel, one goes for refuge to the irreversible bodhisattva aryas.   It is possible to go for refuge to someone who is of lower realization than oneself; for example, the person can be a bestower of vows, so even though the person is of lower realization, one can go for refuge to this person. For example, Lama Atisha had a teacher called Suvarnadvipa (Serlingpa in Tibetan), who was of lower realization than Atisha. Serlingpa was said to be on the path of accumulation at that time, while Atisha was on the path of preparation. There are stories of students who have a higher realization than their teachers; there are even students who have attained buddhahood but whose teacher has not attained buddhahood. Yet when such students meet their teachers, they still show respect to their teachers.   Not only should one be skilled in faith, but also in the objects of faith, which refer to the Three Jewels. Training in the deeds as a generality  ROOT TEXT  4.33c Joyous effort in the objects of utilization, generosity and so forth;   EXPLANATION That qualified by (i) initiating joyous effort that takes the six perfections, generosity and so forth, as the objects of utilization, and (ii) initiating joyous effort in all Mahayana paths is called “skill in joyous effort.” Sutra says, “The great bodhisattvas, having initially generated the mind [of enlightenment], from then onwards, even though they perform generosity, they do not take it as a sign.”  The meaning of indicating training in the deeds through being qualified by joyous effort is as follows. It has been taught that even though one may have generated the mind, if one does not diligently initiate joyous effort in the deeds, then that is simply a mere noble thought; one will not attain unsurpassable enlightenment. Therefore one should know that, when one trains in every division that is an instance of the deeds, one must qualify it with joyous effort.  This section emphasizes the importance of training in the deeds after having generated the mind of enlightenment. If one does not initiate joyous effort in the deeds after having generated the mind of enlightenment, then this mind of enlightenment is simply a noble thought; there is no way one will attain enlightenment. This emphasizes that after having generated bodhicitta, one needs to initiate joyous effort to train in the deeds.  Explanation: Ornament of the Essence, p. 423: 2B2C-2A1B-2A2B-1B2.  5 EXPLANATION With respect to common calm abiding, having observed any non-conceptualized image, such as that of the exalted body of the Tathagata, by having familiarized with it in dependence on the nine steps of mental abidings, one practices until (i) one engages in it without exertion without need to rely upon the antidotes to laxity and excitement, and (ii) the bliss of physical and mental pliancy has been produced. {127}   Uncommon concentration refers to meditative stabilization such as the Sky Treasury that does not conceptualize “It is this” with respect to the object of observation in dependence on having previously achieved the above [common calm abiding], the perfection of concentration that is attained on the fifth ground, and so forth. Sutra says, “Bhagavan, that is like this: the great bodhisattva, having generated the first mind [of enlightenment], from then onwards, does not observe that which will attain manifest complete enlightenment, that by which one will attain manifest complete enlightenment, and where one will attain manifest complete enlightenment, any phenomena ranging from forms [through to] the exalted knower of all aspects.”  With respect to the expression “common calm abiding” [in the first sentence above], this can mean calm abiding that is common to Buddhists and non-Buddhists, or calm abiding that is common to the Hinayana and the Mahayana. There are definitely non-Buddhists who practice and achieve calm abiding; therefore, calm abiding can be said to be common to Buddhists and non-Buddhists. If we talk about the calm abiding that is unique to a Buddhist, what would this refer to? Does it mean calm abiding that is cultivated by a Buddhist? There are practitioners of calm abiding who are Buddhists and who cultivate calm abiding for the sake of attaining a rebirth in the form or formless realms; is this calm abiding cultivated by such persons the calm abiding that is unique to Buddhists? There are non-Buddhists who cultivate calm abiding for the sake of taking rebirth in the form and formless realms. If Buddhists also cultivate calm abiding for the same reason, does this mean they have a similar practice?   Some people cultivate meditative stabilization in order to attain clairvoyance in this life; in dependence on having attained a concentration, they attain clairvoyance so that they can become famous, and among such practitioners, there can be Buddhists. So how would you interpret the expressions “common calm abiding” and “uncommon calm abiding” in the context of Buddhists and non-Buddhists? Perhaps we can say that calm abiding that is unique to Buddhists is calm abiding that is cultivated in the entity of a path, and calm abiding that is unique to the Mahayanists is calm abiding that is conjoined with bodhicitta. Included within meditative stabilization that is unique to Buddhists is the alternating meditation. This practice is one in which one alternates uncontaminated and contaminated minds.  6 EXPLANATION In dependence on having attained a fully qualified calm abiding, one will be skilled in (i) the wisdom that knows the objects of knowledge, phenomena that are the modes and varieties, in all ways and (ii) the five types of objects. Both the special insights observing modes and observing varieties have been indicated here; the special insight observing varieties exists even from the path of accumulation and the special insight observing modes is attained from the heat level of the path of preparation. Know that it is attained once one is able to induce the bliss of pliancy through analysis with the wisdom that individually investigates the meaning of emptiness.  Sutra says, “Sons of the gods, I have attained manifest complete enlightenment in all ways with regard to all phenomena; {128} I have not observed any phenomena—that which will attain manifest complete enlightenment, that by which one will attain manifest complete enlightenment, and where one will attain manifest complete enlightenment.” Know the extensive presentation of these points from the stages of the paths of the perfections composed by Je Rinpoche [Lama Tsongkhapa].  Here we are talking about special insight. Special insight needs to be cultivated from within the state of calm abiding. When one has attained calm abiding, one abides on the object without laxity and excitement. When one tries to cultivate special insight by performing analysis, this causes laxity and excitement to arise, so one must train until one can induce the bliss of pliancy of the body and mind through analysis. Once one is able to attain this bliss of pliancy, one has attained special insight.   Explanation: Ornament of the Essence, p. 424: 2B2C-2A1B-2A2B-2.  7 There are two types of intended trainees of the Ornament for Clear Realization: those of sharp faculty and those of dull faculty. Those of sharp faculty will easily realize and attain enlightenment, while those of dull faculty will realize and attain enlightenment with difficulty.   It is mentioned [in the last paragraph on p. 424] that “The intended trainees of the Ornament for Clear Realization are presented here as being definite as the two types: followers of faith and followers of Dharma.” The followers of faith are those of dull faculty who generate faith upon, for example, hearing the words of the expression of worship, whereas the followers of Dharma, the sharp faculty trainees, are followers of reasoning who do not immediately generate faith upon hearing the words of the expression of worship—rather, they undertake analysis and upon seeing that there is no damage, they then generate faith. The faith of the dull-faculty trainees is said to be great faith, whereas that of the sharp faculty trainees is said to be irreversible faith. The faith of the dull faculty trainees is called great faith; indeed, such followers of faith generate very strong faith when hearing the words of the expression of worship. The followers of sharp faculty only generate faith after undertaking analysis and seeing that there is no fault, or damage; before this, they do not generate faith, but when they see that there is no fault, they generate faith. Such a faith is an irreversible faith, because it arises due to having analyzed and seen that there is no fault.  After that, the text points out some wrong assertions.  9  EXPLANATION Implicit to what has been explicitly indicated in the root text, this is implied: “Those on the path of accumulation abiding in the middling vehicle, middling faith and so forth, realize and attain the enlightenment of solitary realizers, and those on the path of accumulation abiding in the small vehicle, small faith and so forth, realize and attain the enlightenment of hearers.” The meaning that has been explained is implicitly implied by the root text, because the superiority of difficult and easy attainment of unsurpassable enlightenment by the sharp and dull bodhisattvas who abide in the great faith and so forth has been explicitly indicated, and the attributes of the other two have not been explicitly indicated. Know that it is erroneous and at variance with the Acarya’s intention to explain the explicit and implicit meanings differently.   Here we see our system’s explanation of the meanings that have been implied indirectly [by the root text 4.34cd, p. 424]. Here when we use the word “indirectly,” it refers to something that is indirect compared to what has been indicated directly, or explicitly. What has been indicated directly, or explicitly, is that those of great faith and so forth, whether of sharp or dull faculty, will attain enlightenment, but those of sharp faculty will attain enlightenment easily, whereas those of dull faculty will attain enlightenment with great difficulty. Implicit to that is that those of middling faith and so forth will attain and realize the enlightenment of solitary realizers, and those of small faith and so forth will realize and attain the enlightenment of hearers.   The last sentence says: “Know that it is erroneous and at variance with the Acarya’s intention to explain the explicit and implicit meanings differently,” meaning that if one were to explain the explicit and implicit meanings differently from those above, it would be an erroneous explanation. Actually, you can compare this explanation here, under the implicit meaning, with the statement right before this section where it says “delineating divisions of the three lineage-bearers who abide on the Mahayana path of accumulation is an erroneous explanation of the meaning of the passage” [the bottom of p. 425]. The explanation that was pointed out as wrong in the previous passage is not the same as our system’s explanation under “meanings that have been implied indirectly” [p. 426]. So you should know that those two explanations are not the same: the one in the previous passage has been pointed out as wrong, and here the explanation under “meanings that have been implied indirectly” is our own system’s presentation. Let’s take a closer look at the special conventions of speech that reveal the complete class of Mahayana paths such as emptiness. What are these special conventions of speech? Suppose there are two persons teaching emptiness, and they use exactly the same words to describe exactly the same meaning. Suppose that through listening to the first person, one doesn’t feel anything, but through listening to the second one, one finds this speech very appealing, one’s heart is stirred, and one’s mind is moved. We can say that due to the excellent qualities of realization of this second person, the special conventions of speech that reveal emptiness are manifested. This is one possible interpretation; there can be other interpretations, but it is difficult to be very definitive about what these special conventions of speech are. In general, we would say that depending on the level of realizations of the explainer, the listener would receive different levels of benefit, or even harm. It is difficult to tell if someone has attained a sign of irreversibility or not; one needs to stay near this person and observe him/her over a long time. Without getting to know this person, it is difficult to know if the person has attained a sign of irreversibility or not. There are various stories, but in general it is very difficult to determine if the person has attained a sign of irreversibility or not. There are some people who start crying upon hearing the description of suffering—does this mean they have attained a sign of irreversibility? Probably not. They start crying when they hear about suffering, maybe because they are very timid, in that they become easily saddened. For example, a person may see a sick dog, and feel very sad and start crying. This type of timid person is also someone who is easily discouraged.   Why are these signs—the body hairs standing on end and tears coming to their eyes—mentioned in the text? This occurs in the context of someone who contemplates the suffering of sentient beings sincerely from the bottom of the heart, thinking how sentient beings, due to accumulating karma, are tormented by suffering, such that tears uncontrollably come to his/her eyes and the hairs stand on end. When you think about these signs—the body hairs standing on end and tears coming to the eyes when contemplating the suffering of sentient beings—they can possibly occur even before one has generated bodhicitta. It is actually quite difficult to think about these signs of irreversibility; however, they exist. With regard to point 2: “possesses stability of the ultimate and conventional minds of unsurpassable enlightenment, through having been blessed by buddhas and so forth to not degenerate from those two minds.” It talks about bodhisattvas who have stability of ultimate and conventional bodhicitta having been blessed by the buddhas and so forth. Do not think that the buddhas do not bless those who do not have stability in ultimate and conventional bodhicitta; they bless everyone without prejudice. Whether or not we receive the blessings depends on our mind—if we practice properly the teachings of the buddha, we will receive the blessings. It may be that we occasionally receive “bits” of blessings, due to certain types of conceptualizations in our minds. For example, we may meet a holy lama and think “Oh, this holy lama is blessing me; he is praying or dedicating for me,” and as result we feel very happy, and it seems that some blessings have been received. This kind of blessing, however, is very unstable, because our mind is unstable; this kind of blessing can vanish very quickly.   The real blessings come from properly practicing the teachings of the Buddha. It is said that all the buddhas are present in the vicinity of each sentient being. You shouldn’t think that if Shakyamuni Buddha is in India, he cannot be here now. For me, an ordinary person, if I am here I cannot be somewhere else, but it is not like that for buddhas; they can be everywhere all the time. Whether or not we receive blessings is contingent upon ourselves; it depends on our own mind; it does not solely rely on the buddhas.  51  EXPLANATION The subject—a middling faculty bodhisattva abiding on the path of seeing—(5) possesses a light body and mind, the special bliss of pliancy, because he is distinguished by having separated from the seeds of non-virtue, the objects of abandonment of the path of seeing; (6) possesses utilization of desires without strong adherence that is craving for the objects of desire even though he abides within the household, because he abides in that way through the force of skillful means in subduing sentient beings; (7) possesses always chaste conduct, because he abides in chaste conduct through directly having seen the shortcomings of objects that are included within the attributes of the desire realm; and (8) possesses perfectly pure necessities of life, because he has abandoned all wrong livelihoods by the nature of an excellent being who is empowered with respect to not forgetting the mind of enlightenment—these four are the aspects of origin. {160}   The fifth point talks about the bodhisattva possessing “a light body and mind, the special bliss of pliancy, because he is distinguished by having separated from the seeds of non-virtue, the objects of abandonment of the path of seeing.” Here, there is something to think about: in general, when we feel unwell, physically or mentally, it is due to having coarse non-virtue in our minds; for example, coarse forms of the three poisons. As the result of the presence of these gross non-virtuous states of mind, we feel unpeaceful, and when the mind is agitated, the body also feels unwell. Sometimes we have the tendency, when we feel unwell physically or mentally, to point to others as being responsible for our lack of well-being. This is a mistake, because if you examine closely, you can trace the causes of the lack of well being to your own mind. If we blame others for our own lack of well-being, this can cause our non-virtues to become stronger and stronger, and can lead to our degeneration. If we have this kind of tendency to blame others, then what will happen is that we will become unhappy at one thing after another, becoming mentally unhappy, and this will lead to a lack of well being in the body.   For example, in the cultivation of calm abiding, we talk about the goal of attaining the bliss of pliancy of body and mind—what is the technique for achieving this? Is it to stop others from harming us? No, because it is attained through working with our own minds. In the presentation of the techniques for attaining calm abiding, it talks about stopping faulty states of mind as the way to attain excellent qualities of the mind, to attain peace and happiness of the mind, and on this basis to achieve the well-being of the body as well. Nowhere is it mentioned that in order to achieve this bliss, we need to stop others from harming us. The point here is that mainly you need to work on transforming your mind if you want to attain happiness. If you can do that, you will attain well- being of the mind and the body, and regardless of what others do, it is not a problem. And of course if you attain such a state, you can achieve your own welfare and you are also able to benefit others.   The sixth point says a bodhisattva “possesses utilization of desires without strong adherence that is craving for the objects of desire even though he abides within the household, because he abides in that way through the force of skillful means in subduing sentient beings.” A bodhisattva has bodhicitta, and out of this, enacts deeds to benefit other sentient beings. In order to benefit others, a bodhisattva can take different forms. If it is more beneficial to take the form of an ordained person, he/she will do so; if it is more beneficial to take the form of layperson, he/she will do so. In order to subdue and benefit sentient beings, a bodhisattva can abide within a household. In this point number 6, a bodhisattva, even if he/she abides within a houseful, utilizes the various objects of desire, but does not have craving for them.   Point seven says that a bodhisattva “possesses always chaste conduct, because he abides in chaste conduct through directly having seen the shortcomings of objects that are included within the attributes of the desire realm.” The objects included in the attributes of the desire realm refer to forms, sounds, odors, tastes, and tangible objects in the desire realm. What are their shortcomings?

 52 We can talk about the shortcomings of food and clothing included in the form of the desire realm: these objects are such that when you utilize them, they do not provide satisfaction. It is not the case that if you use a certain amount of these objects, then you will become satisfied. In fact, there is no limit to how much you can utilize such objects such that they will definitely bring satisfaction. The point here is to reduce our craving for such objects; it does not mean destroying them or getting rid of them. We should not think: “Due to this object acting as a condition, my craving increases, so I should get rid of it.” If you do this, you may later find that you need the object, and will have to spend money to buy a new one! The point is that we have to transform the mind, not get rid of our possessions. You may think “I have attachment for this pen,” and give it away to a friend. Later, we may find that we need the pen, but we cannot get it back from our friend. We have to work on our minds, not get rid of the objects. Although the pen has the shortcoming of being an object that cannot provide everlasting satisfaction, it does not mean it is not useful! We have to learn to be satisfied with what we have; to have contentment in our mind—we have a pen, and we must learn to be satisfied with it, not constantly craving better pens, more pens, etc. It is very important to practice contentment, and to have small desire. If one does not know contentment, you will never feel satisfied no matter how much you get. If you just follow your desires without practicing contentment, of course you will be happy if you obtain the object of desire, but if you don’t, you will experience suffering. It is difficult to find all the objects we desire, therefore it is important to practice contentment. If we can see the shortcomings of the objects of desire, we can abandon craving for such objects, and can engage in chaste conduct. “Chaste conduct” in this context refers to ethics, or ethical behavior, and here ethics is the intention to guard against misdeeds.   The eighth point is that the bodhisattva “possesses perfectly pure necessities of life, because he has abandoned all wrong livelihoods by the nature of an excellent being who is empowered with respect to not forgetting the mind of enlightenment.” What are “perfectly pure necessities of life”? Does it mean, for example, good quality food, which is in the entity of being delicious? It refers to the causes of such necessities of life being pure; in other words, these necessities of life are not acquired through wrong livelihood. To give an example, if you obtain money through deceit and dissimulation and then use that money to buy good food—such as lasagna or tortelloni—this food does not satisfy the criteria of being “perfectly pure necessities of life.” It is not that from the side of the food, the entity is not pure—it is good food—but if you obtain it through deceit and dissimulation, this food is not said to be a “perfectly pure” necessity of life. Someone can, for example, obtain good food for a couple of years through telling lies, cheating, acting out of deceit and dissimulation, etc. This person may be able to enjoy good food for one or two years, but the future result is that they will be deprived of food, and this can actually take place over many lifetimes. For example, one can be reborn as a hungry ghost, or as a human being deprived of necessities of life. When one cheats others, probably there is the karma of stealing involved; due to this type of karma, one will have to suffer for a long time in the future.   For us, who study the Dharma and try to put it into practice, we have to consider carefully the short-term and long-term benefits; between the two, if we have to choose one, we should definitely choose the long-term benefits. Sometimes we experience suffering in the course of practicing Dharma, for example, when we practice ethics, etc. Indeed, it is difficult to practice ethics in the world, and we may encounter hardships as a result, but we must remember that such practices will lead to happiness over a long period of time, for many lifetimes. So if we have to choose between short-term and long-term benefit, we should definitely go for long-term benefit. Likewise, here we are engaged in this Masters Program—there is hardship involved in participating in this six-year program, but we must think of the benefits: for example, you will be able to practice much better due to having studied these texts; your practice will become very far-reaching and rich; you will be able to understand more of what is taught by high lamas, such as His Holiness the Dalai Lama; there will be better understanding of the terminology used in Buddhist texts; and you can become a teacher or a translator and bring much benefit to many sentient beings in many places.
