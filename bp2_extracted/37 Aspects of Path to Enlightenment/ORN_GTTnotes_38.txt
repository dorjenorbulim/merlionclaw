 2  EXPLANATION  The eighth ground bodhisattva has attained the application of pure buddha land where he will attain buddhahood, because—through having accumulated the roots of virtue that establish the impure world of sentient beings as pure, and likewise the impure world of the environment as pure—he has attained the application of pure land.  As I mentioned before, when we talk about the application of pure land, this involves purifying one’s mind. If one’s mind is pure, one will have a pure buddha land; if one’s mind is not pure, one will not have a pure buddha land.   Transforming the mind  Let us look at this in a more mundane way. As ordinary beings, people have different kinds of experiences: people who are more patient encounter fewer hardships and difficulties, and their minds are happier, while those who are impatient encounter many difficulties, and their minds are unhappy. This is not due to the place where one is abiding, or their companions. For example, we can have two people living at the Institute: one person can be very happy, and the other person can be very unhappy. The place and people around them are the same, but due to their minds, they have different experiences. This is something for you to think about. Of course, when we talk about purity of mind, there are many different levels. As much as you can purify your mind, that much happier you will be; this is something that can be attained regardless of what kind of places you go to or what kind of companions you have.   Here we are talking about bodhisattvas who accumulate merit and eventually attain the state of full enlightenment; we are not talking about bodhisattvas who accumulate merit in isolation without having any kind of relationships with other sentient beings. In fact, even before a bodhisattva generates the mind of enlightenment (bodhicitta), he/she would have karmic connections with different kinds of sentient beings, and then after generating bodhicitta, again there are relationships with different kinds of sentient beings. A bodhisattva, with his/her body, speech, and mind, would establish relationships with various sentient beings. As a bodhisattva is purifying his/her mind, simultaneously relationships are established with sentient beings, and the mind to benefit others also becomes more and more developed. As the mind becomes purer and purer, the companions around oneself and the retinue also become purer and purer. In fact, through the establishment of relationships with different sentient beings, when one becomes a buddha, these sentient beings will become trainees who abide in one’s buddha land.   When we talk about a bodhisattva establishing karmic connections with sentient beings, these are not necessarily pleasant, or positive, connections. Of course, if we talk about Dharma connections, they can be very positive; through meeting sentient beings, one can make prayers such as, “In the future, may we attain buddhahood together,” or, “If I were to become enlightened before these other sentient beings, may I be able to help and lead them to the state of enlightenment by teaching him/her the Dharma.” These are examples of positive connections, but a connection can also be established through, for example, a sentient being harming the bodhisattva; the bodhisattva then has the opportunity to practice patience, and can think, “This person who is trying to harm me is actually benefitting me by providing me with the opportunity to develop my mind, to practice patience and so forth. In the future, may this person be reborn in my retinue and may I help this person attain enlightenment.” If you are able to think like this, especially in relation to sentient beings who harm you, you can see it as an opportunity for you to practice. In this way, this person who is trying to harm you is not actually able to damage you.   We talk about transforming adverse conditions into the path—this is an example of how it can be done. Of course, if you are able to transform adverse conditions into the path, this is really amazing and wonderful, but if you can’t, even the slightest condition can cause you to become upset.

 3 Therefore, we have to be careful; we have to take care of our mind. Otherwise, we can make ourselves unhappy even without external adverse conditions. Forget about transforming adverse conditions into the path—we can’t even transform ordinary conditions into the path! Sometimes, when the weather is not good, we get angry. Who are we getting angry at? There are some people who are like that. It all boils down to whether or not you are able to control, or take care of, your own mind. If you can transform your mind, you will definitely be happy.   When we talk about suffering, there are two types: suffering of the body and suffering of the mind. Between these two types of suffering, mental suffering is more serious, because if we have mental suffering, this will eventually harm our body. For example, through the mind being unhappy, the body in time will become unwell, or sick. However, if one experiences suffering of the body, there is no pervasion that the mind will be harmed; if one has a very positive attitude, even though one may be physically sick or unwell, the mind may not be affected at all. Between the body and the mind, what we mainly need to take care of is our mind. If one is able to take care of one’s mind, one will definitely experience less suffering and more happiness. If instead one tries to just take care of the body, in time one will experience more suffering. If you are just obsessed with your body, you will experience many types of suffering: when you look in a mirror and see your hair turning white, your hair falling out, wrinkles forming on your face, many dark spots developing on your skin, getting fat, etc., you will experience many types of mental suffering. Of course, it is important to take care of the body, but if we become obsessed with this, we will experience hardship. Between the body and the mind, the mind is more important.
