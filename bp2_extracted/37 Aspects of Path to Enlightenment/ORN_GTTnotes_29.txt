Here we have the four aspects for each of the first three truths, making twelve in total. Let us see how to formulate the syllogisms for these aspects. The last paragraph [bottom of p. 348] says: “The subject—the exalted wisdom of a bodhisattva directly realizing that sufferings do not exist permanently—is an aspect that observes the impermanent sufferings, because it is an exalted wisdom realizing that sufferings are impermanent.” In the next sentence, it says “Therefore, take the predicate of the probandum above as the sign for ‘It is an antidote to the superimposition that apprehends permanent sufferings.’” If you took this instruction given in the text literally, the syllogism would be as follows: “The subject—the exalted wisdom of a bodhisattva directly realizing that sufferings do not exist permanently—is an antidote to the superimposition that apprehends permanent sufferings, because it is an aspect that observes the impermanent sufferings.” However, this is a bit awkward. I think we should formulate the syllogism as follows: “The subject—the exalted wisdom of a bodhisattva directly realizing that sufferings do not exist permanently—is an antidote to the superimposition that apprehends permanent sufferings (predicate), because it is an exalted wisdom realizing that sufferings are impermanent (sign).” [Translator: Basically, the sign is different.]   This pattern should be applied to the rest of the aspects. Let us now formulate the syllogism for the second aspect, using the words from the sutra: “true sufferings are not produced ultimately” [(2) in the first paragraph]. “The subject—the exalted wisdom of a bodhisattva directly realizing that true sufferings are not produced ultimately—is an antidote to the superimposition that apprehends sufferings as produced ultimately, because it is an exalted wisdom realizing that true sufferings are not produced ultimately.” This is how we should apply the pattern.

There are four possible connotations of the word “included”: 1. Inclusion by way of subject matter: An example is saying that the eight clear realizations are included within the Ornament for Clear Realization.  2. Inclusion by way of being it: There are many examples of this, such as saying that the knower of bases is included in one of the three exalted knowers because the knower of bases is one of the three exalted knowers.  3. Inclusion by way of practice: The eight clear realizations are included within the three exalted knowers by way of practice because when the three exalted knowers are practiced completely, the eight clear realizations are also practiced completely.  4. Inclusion by way of retinue: In an ordinary sense, a retinue can refer to the ministers or soldiers in the retinue of a king. In a Dharma context, the path of accumulation and path of preparation are included within the retinue of the path of seeing.  When you encounter the word “inclusion,” you should think about its possible meanings in a very extensive way.
