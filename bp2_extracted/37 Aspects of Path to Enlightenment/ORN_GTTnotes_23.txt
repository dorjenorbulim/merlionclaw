 1  Masters Program: Ornament for Clear Realization Commentary by Geshe Tenzin Tenphel (week 23)   Monday, October 13, 2008   Questions and answers  Student: Why does the special first concentration have equanimity, rather than joy and happiness, as its benefit branch, while the second concentration still has these (i.e. joy and happiness)? Geshe-la: This is related to the explanation on p. 206. We posit the difference between mere first concentration and special first concentration as the mental factor investigation. This is present in the mere first concentration, but in the special first concentration, one has separated from attachment to investigation. Investigation has a rough aspect; by separating from attachment to it, one has equanimity feeling.   On the bottom of p. 206, it says, “The second concentration acts to separate from the attachment to investigation and analysis through the antidotal branch of internal clarity, and possesses the benefit branch of joy and happiness generated from meditative stabilization.” If you were to compare the antidotal branches of these concentrations, you would see that they are all different. The antidotal branch of the mere first concentration is posited as investigation and analysis; the antidotal branch of the special first concentration is posited as analysis; the antidotal branch of the second concentration is posited as internal clarity. Do you get the point?  The first concentration is divided into the mere and the special, but from the second concentration on, there is no such division. For the first concentration, it is possible to separate from attachment to some of the branches, whereby there is a mere and a special first concentration. For the second concentration and above, it is not possible to separate from attachment to some of the branches of their own level; therefore there is no division into mere and special.   The branches for the first concentration are investigation and analysis for the antidotal branch, joy and happiness for the benefit branch, and meditative stabilization for the basis branch. For the mere actual first concentration, all five branches are present: investigation and analysis, joy and happiness generated from isolation, and meditative stabilization; for the special actual first concentration, since one has separated from attachment to investigation, the antidotal branch consists of only analysis.   This is a very good question. What can we posit as the benefit branch for the special actual first concentration? Is it equanimity feeling, or joy and happiness, or equanimity feeling and happiness? It is difficult to say that joy and happiness from isolation do not exist on the special first concentration. It is not entirely clear what we should posit here. Perhaps on the special first concentration, you could posit three branches: the antidotal branch as analysis, the benefit branch as equanimity feeling, and the basis branch as meditative stabilization. The phrase: “Through completing the six perfections, the six objects of abandonment are abandoned” should be annotated as follows: “Through completing the attainment of the surpassing practice of the six perfections, the six objects of abandonment are abandoned.” We say that on learners’ paths, the six perfections are not present; however, there is the practice of the six perfections.  43  The seventh ground is called “Gone Afar.” Why? Through having cultivated the paths for a long time, the bodhisattva comes to the end of coarse exertion. Coarse exertion exists on the seventh ground, but not on the eighth ground. In that sense, the bodhisattva has “gone afar.”   In this section, the twenty objects of abandonment are listed first, and then the twenty antidotes. You should be able to follow these two lists. You should study this passage by correlating the objects of abandonment with the antidotes. For instance, we see “apprehending a self and sentient beings…” in the root text [1.60]. By reading the section on the antidotes, we should try to understand the antidotes to apprehending a self, apprehending sentient beings, and so forth.   Questions and answers  Student: A bodhisattva is supposed to have attained the first concentration by the great level of the path of accumulation, but now in the root text (1.65), and on the bottom of p. 227 (“…having attained a fully qualified common calm abiding…”) we have the subject of calm abiding. Why is calm abiding discussed here, in the context of the grounds? Geshe-la: In general, we say that calm abiding comes first, and special insight comes after it. Similarly, when we speak about the fifth and sixth perfections, the perfection of concentration comes before the perfection of wisdom. The perfection of concentration is like calm abiding, and the perfection of wisdom is like special insight. The surpassing practice of the perfection of concentration is attained on the fifth ground, and the surpassing practice of the perfection of concentration is attained on the sixth ground. However, it is not that there is no practice of the perfections of concentration and wisdom before those grounds. On the fifth and sixth grounds, they become surpassing practices, and their order on the fifth and sixth grounds is in the order of generating calm abiding and special insight. This explanation is related to the passage on the sixth ground. On the bottom of p. 227, there is an explanation of the sequence of the last two perfections, regarding which it says, “It will be explained below.”     END 
