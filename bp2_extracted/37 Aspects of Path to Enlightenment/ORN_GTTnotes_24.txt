EXPLANATION (1) To know just as it is with a single mind, the mental behavior of all sentient beings, being with attachment, being free from attachment, and so forth, (2) to play in the world systems of the ten directions through the clairvoyance of magical emanation of play, (3) to fully transform with extensive roots of virtue one’s supporting buddha land into the entity of gold, vaidurya, and so forth, (4) through having fully investigated the words and the meanings of the Dharma in all ways, to please the Buddha with the three doors for the sake of that, (5) by principally generating the divine eye concordant with the ten strengths, knowing faculties that are supreme and not supreme, (6) to completely purify the faults of the supported sentient beings of the buddha land where one will become enlightened {339}, (7) to abide like an illusion in all arising [from absorption] and entering [into absorption], and (8) having seen the welfare of all sentient beings when having apprehended where they will be reborn, to fully uphold a premeditated rebirth by way of compassion and prayers—through eight types like the aforesaid qualities of complete trainings, as before, the eighth ground: Immovable, is experienced.   The Eighth ground is called “Immovable.” On this ground, the bodhisattva has pacified coarse exertion, and is not moved by coarse exertion; therefore, it is called “Immovable.” Here, eight complete trainings are mentioned.   1. The first has to do with knowing the mental behavior of sentient beings, those with attachment, those free of attachment, and so forth.  2. With the second training, the bodhisattva has the clairvoyance of magical emanation of play, and is able to benefit others in that way. Regarding this second point, the Tibetan word originally translated as “play” (rtse ba) [in the phrase “to play in the world systems”] has the same spelling as “kindness.” It is more likely that the meaning here is kindness, or “to show kindness.” But in the phrase “the clairvoyance of magical emanation of play,” there is a different Tibetan word for “play” (rnam par rol pa) and in this case, there is no ambiguity. Sometimes this word is translated as “sport,” but not like football. A bodhisattva benefits sentient beings in various ways, so it is a bit like play. A bodhisattva takes different forms or manifestations, for example teaching the Dharma in one way to some sentient beings, and teaching in another way to other sentient beings, and in that way, s/he takes care of them in diverse manners. It is like the term “foe-destroyer” (Skt: arhat): the word “destroy” seems quite strong, but if you understand the meaning, you understand that the foes that are afflictions are destroyed.  3. The third point is “to fully transform with extensive roots of virtue one’s supporting buddha land into the entity of gold, vaidurya, and so forth…” It talks here about roots of virtue transforming one’s buddha land and so forth. On the eighth ground, a bodhisattva accumulates merit and performs various activities, in order to establish the buddha land where s/he will attain enlightenment in the future.  In the past, Marpa wanted to give a gift to Naropa, his teacher. He managed to acquire a bag of gold nuggets, with great difficulty. He tried to find Naropa, but Naropa had gone for a walk in the mountains. When Marpa found him, he offered the gold to Naropa. Naropa scattered the gold when

 3 he received it, and Marpa became upset. Naropa then said, “You should not generate this mind of miserliness; the mountain where I walked upon is all gold, although it seems to be rock.” When Marpa dug in the ground, he found that what was supposed to be rock turned out to be gold. If you were to go to that mountain now, however, you would find rock, not gold. It is said that this is due to the merit of sentient beings. The environment where we live depends on our merit; if the inhabitants’ merit is little, the environment will be poor, whereas if the merit improves, the environment will improve.  4. “…Through having fully investigated the words and the meanings of the Dharma in all ways, to please the Buddha with the three doors for the sake of that.” Here, a bodhisattva “through having fully investigated…” upholds the Dharma and also teaches the Dharma to others. One tries to benefit others with body, speech, and mind; for example, with a good motivation, speaking Dharma words and so forth. In this way, the buddhas are pleased. In particular, for Buddhists, we go for refuge to the Buddha, Dharma, and Sangha, and we need to make sure that our refuge is not simply words. If our refuge is just words, without being accompanied by right actions of body, speech, and mind, then this is not a pure refuge. In addition to knowing the words of the Dharma, we also need to know the meanings of the Dharma and to actualize those meanings; otherwise, if we just recite the words, but engage in erroneous behavior with our body, speech, and mind, that is not pure refuge.  5. “…By principally generating the divine eye concordant with the ten strengths…” A bodhisattva on the eighth ground has not attained the ten strengths or powers of a buddha, but can have similitudes [of these]. With the fifth training, a bodhisattva generates the divine eye that is concordant with the ten strengths, and in that way s/he can know faculties that are supreme and faculties that are not supreme. 6. “…To completely purify the faults of the supported sentient beings of the buddha land where one will become enlightened…” This refers to ripening sentient beings of the buddha land. At this point, a bodhisattva is not yet a buddha, but can make various prayers, such as saying: “Unless all the trainees in my pure land attain the five clairvoyances in the future, I will not attain buddhahood.” While on the learners’ path, bodhisattvas enact sentient beings’ welfare through explaining the Dharma, but they also make prayers such as these to explain their aspirations to others. In that way, sentient beings become ripened. One does not simply establish a buddha land. A buddha land is not empty; sentient beings will be reborn in that buddha land. A bodhisattva, through such activities, ripens sentient beings and completely purifies the faults of the supported sentient beings of the buddha land where he/she will become enlightened.   7. “…To abide like an illusion in all arising [from absorption] and entering…” “Arising” can be interpreted as subsequent attainment, and “entering” as meditative equipoise. During meditative equipoise on the eighth ground, a bodhisattva directly realizes the emptiness of true existence, and upon arising, during subsequent attainment, all phenomena appear as empty of true existence, like illusions.  8. “…Having seen the welfare of all sentient beings when having apprehended where they will be reborn …” In this point, a bodhisattva is able to see the dispositions of sentient beings and understand where they will be reborn. By understanding this, a bodhisattva will uphold a pre-meditated rebirth by way of compassion and prayers in order to benefit sentient beings.  As before, these eight trainings should be practiced by being conjoined with great compassion and the wisdom realizing emptiness. We will look at the tenth ground tomorrow.   Questions and answers  Question: Why is not possible to abandon the seeds of obscurations by observing a functioning thing? This is related to the passage on p. 218 (last sentence of the first paragraph): “…it follows that emptiness would be compounded. If you accept that, then meditating on it would not be able to abandon the seeds of the obscurations.” Geshe-la: We say it is impossible to abandon the seeds of obscurations by observing a functioning thing. In the context of this debate, one should abandon the seeds of the obscurations by observing the emptiness of apprehendeds and apprehenders being different substances. By observing a functioning thing, one cannot abandon the seeds.   Student: Why?

 7 Geshe-la: Because the emptiness of apprehendeds and apprehenders is not a functioning thing; it is a non-affirming negation. If it is a functioning thing, it cannot be a non-affirming negative. A functioning thing is either a positive phenomenon or an affirming negation. The emptiness of apprehendeds and apprehenders being different substances is not a functioning thing.   In the Autonomy school, we talk about different types of uninterrupted paths that are direct antidotes to the objects of abandonment. In order to abandon the conception of a self of persons, for example, one needs the uninterrupted path realizing selflessness. To abandon the conception of external objects, one needs to realize the emptiness of apprehendeds and apprehenders being different substances; in other words, one needs to generate an uninterrupted path realizing non-duality. A bodhisattva who wants to abandon the conception of true existence must generate the antidote, the uninterrupted path realizing the emptiness of true existence. None of these—selflessness of persons, non-duality, or emptiness of true existence—are functioning things; they are all non-affirming negatives.   In order to abandon the seeds of the knowledge obscurations, the seeds of the conceptions of true existence, one must realize the emptiness of true existence. By observing an object that is not the emptiness of true existence, how can one abandon the seeds of the conception of true existence? The direct antidote has to be the realization of the emptiness of true existence. Merit has to serve as backing support for this realization, but without directly realizing the emptiness of true existence, there is no way to abandon the seeds of the conception of true existence.   By doing prostrations, making mandala offerings, etc., we can purify negativities with the four opponent powers. Even if the four opponent powers are complete, however, one cannot abandon the seeds of the obscurations; they can only be abandoned by an uninterrupted path that is the direct antidote. For example, there are two types of motivation: the causal motivation of ignorance, and the motivation at the time of an action, such as killing. The motivation at the time is related to the karma of killing; that can be purified through proper confession and restraint. However, ignorance, the causal motivation, cannot be purified through the four opponent powers; to abandon it, one must realize emptiness. By observing a functioning thing, the seeds of obscurations cannot be purified, but by observing functioning things it is possible to purify negativities. This subject of purification and the ability to purify through various means is very extensive.    Tuesday, October 21, 2008  [Geshe-la spoke about the importance of study as a basis for practice. There is a reason for not explaining the tenth ground by means of the complete trainings, because on the resultant tenth ground itself, without explaining those complete trainings, one is able to completely understand them through just the explanation by means of the characteristics, whereby they are not explained.   This section explains that for the tenth ground, the complete trainings of the tenth ground are not explained. For the first through the ninth grounds, the complete trainings are explained, while for the tenth ground, instead of explaining the complete trainings, the characteristics, or entity, of the tenth ground are explained. Although they are not explained, one understands that the tenth ground has its corresponding complete trainings, because the complete trainings have been explained for the previous nine grounds.   The tenth ground is called “Cloud of Doctrine,” or “Cloud of Dharma.” When clouds gather in the sky, rain will fall and cause the crops to grow. Likewise, the excellent qualities of a bodhisattva are like clouds. From these cloud-like qualities of a bodhisattva, the rain of Dharma will fall. This rain of Dharma causes the excellent qualities of the trainees to develop; therefore this ground is called the Cloud of Doctrine.
