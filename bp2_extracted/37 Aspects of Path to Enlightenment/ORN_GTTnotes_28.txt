 2 realizing emptiness, the convention “antidotal knower of bases” has never been taught anywhere, either above or below.   The perfection of wisdom that is a knower of bases, that realizes impermanence and so forth, in the continuum of a bodhisattva does not act to directly damage the conception of true existence, yet it is called “a knower of bases of the antidotal class” because of being conjoined with fully complete method and wisdom.   A knower of bases of the discordant class, as explained above, is not shown to be an object to be abandoned categorically; the significance that is to be known is that, if those who are definite in the bodhisattva lineage were to be led through that path, it would be a hindrance to attaining buddhahood quickly and will would be a corrupted path. {481}   An ignorant person holds that the paths of the hearers and solitary realizers are hindrances to attaining buddhahood categorically. He is confused about the divisions of the path. I pay homage to anyone1 who is able to generate those very hindrances in the continuum!   We have looked at the knower of bases of the discordant class and the knower of bases of the antidotal class. The difference between them is said to be whether or not they are conjoined with great compassion and the wisdom realizing emptiness. If it is conjoined with great compassion and the wisdom realizing emptiness, it is a knower of bases of the antidotal class; if it is not, then it is a knower of bases of the discordant class.   Although we have this expression “a knower of bases of the discordant class,” this is not an object to be abandoned categorically. Here, it is said that this should be an object to be abandoned for those who are definite in the bodhisattva lineage. The reason is that if those who are definite in the bodhisattva lineage were to be led through this knower of bases of the discordant class, they would become distant from the buddha ground. On that basis, it is called a knower of bases of the discordant class. The bodhisattva aryas actualize the knower of bases by being conjoined with great compassion and the wisdom realizing emptiness. They do not actualize a knower of bases that is not conjoined with great compassion and not conjoined with the wisdom realizing emptiness. Questions and answers  Student: This topic of the sixteen aspects seems to be very important, because through it we can generate great compassion, bodhicitta, and so on. Could Geshe-la explain how to meditate on them?  Geshe-la: Indeed, this is a very important topic. One meditates on the four noble truths by looking first at true sufferings. One has to contemplate the three types of suffering: suffering of suffering, suffering of change, and pervasive compounded suffering. One has to contemplate these in detail, and apply to them the four aspects of true suffering: impermanence, suffering, emptiness, and selflessness. By contemplating these well, one generates a very strong awareness that does not want suffering, and when such an awareness is generated, then one will have a very firm mind of definite emergence, or renunciation: a mind wanting to emerge from samsara. If this renunciation is strong, it will lead to strong compassion.   Similarly, one should contemplate the second truth, true origins, thinking in detail about karma and the afflictions that are the origins of suffering, in terms of the four attributes of true origins. Using many reasons, one generates the awareness wishing to be free of the origins of suffering.   This is how we should proceed for each of the four noble truths, thinking about them in detail and applying reasoning. When we contemplate these various points, we must apply them to our own minds. For example, when we contemplate suffering, we need to think of what type of attitude we have in relation to suffering. If we check honestly, we see that we do not want sufferings such as sickness, cancer, AIDS, etc. When we are healthy, do we have a strong awareness wishing to be free of suffering? Probably not. This is a sign that our wish to be free of suffering is not genuine or strong; our wish to be free from suffering is just limited to wishing to be free from temporal sufferings, such as sickness, hunger, and so on. It is important that when we contemplate this, we need to mix this with our own mind. Otherwise, even if we can list the various types of suffering, it will not be of benefit to our mind.

There is a reason for setting down a conclusion of the three exalted knowers, because, by summarizing all the meanings of the clear realizations, the meaning of the others will also be summarized and realized.  Here, we have a conclusion of the three exalted knowers. We find this at the end of the third chapter, but we do not find a similar conclusion at the end of the seventh chapter (the fourth, fifth, sixth, and seventh chapters are on the four applications). Why do we find a conclusion of the three exalted knowers at the end of the third chapter? It is explained, “There is a reason for setting down a conclusion of the three exalted knowers, because, by summarizing all the meanings of the clear realizations, the meaning of the others will also be summarized and realized.” In the first three chapters, it is taught that one has to meditate on the aspects of the three exalted knowers through gathering them with the cultivation that is a union of calm abiding and special insight. Through this application that gathers the aspects of the three exalted knowers, one cultivates all the aspects of the eight clear realizations; thus there is a purpose for setting down this conclusion at the end of the third chapter.   We talk about cultivating the aspects of the three exalted knowers through gathering them together. A bodhisattva has trained well in meditative stabilization, so he has gained dexterity in it. On top of that, he also has great mental strength. As a result of that, when a bodhisattva cultivates a single aspect, the meanings of the other aspects are complete. Compared to someone who does not have mental strength and has not gained dexterity in meditative stabilization, a bodhisattva has the ability to cultivate the aspects through gathering them together. For someone who has not trained in the dexterity of meditative stabilization and does not have great mental strength, when this person cultivates a single aspect, the meanings of the other aspects are not involved in this cultivation of one aspect.   To understand this, let us take an ordinary example. There are people who have very positive attitudes and are capable of undertaking many different tasks: studying, meditating, cooking, etc. We also see some who are narrow-minded and are less capable; if they do one thing, then they cannot do other things. If asked to do a task, they complain, and so forth, and are less capable of doing many things such as working, studying, meditating, etc. These two types of people differ from the point of view of their attitude and their way of thinking. Both groups of people are the same in having the same amount of time in the day: everyone has twenty-four hours. Therefore it is important for us to train in mental strength, to have a very vast perspective, and to be broad-minded. It is important not to be narrow-minded. If we are narrow-minded, then everything becomes a problem and we are unhappy all the time. This really comes from one’s way of thinking: if one thinks in a negative way, one’s mind becomes narrow, so we need to try not to fall into such a tendency. This explanation can be found in the first paragraph under the root text (p. 345): “Therefore, those known as ‘a bodhisattva’s exalted wisdoms that, through the aspects of any of the three exalted knowers dawning well, cultivate them’ are the antidotal characteristics in this context.” We say “explaining [the instances] to be characteristics of the aspects is a wrong explanation” because “[if his explanation were correct], the contextual meaning of the reply (that, since all three—bases, antidotes, and aspects—are included as the subject matter, through being characterized, the three exalted knowers are included as the subject matter) in the previous section on the rejections of dispute regarding the statement of the purpose-and-relationship7 would become lost.” Recall that in the beginning of this text, there was a dispute regarding the statement of the purpose-and-relationship, and our system’s response was that since all three—bases, antidotes, and aspects—are included as the subject matter, through being characterized, the three exalted knowers are included as the subject matter [see p. 32, under 2B2A-1B1. Actual Reply]; this was a reply given by our system to reject this dispute. The meaning of the reply in that context, and the meaning in this context when we discussed the root text (i.e. the meaning of [antidotal] characteristics), are different; the two contexts are different. If this person’s explanation were correct, the contextual meaning of that [earlier] reply would be lost. The meaning of the reply does not apply here.   In the last paragraph (p. 346), our system says that if someone were to say that “what are to be cultivated through differentiation into the two—the object-aspects and the knower-aspects—are discordant and separate,” then this would not be correct because, for example, if one familiarizes with impermanence, one also familiarizes with the exalted wisdom realizing impermanence; and if one familiarizes with the exalted wisdom realizing impermanence, one also familiarizes with impermanence; the two cannot be separated.
