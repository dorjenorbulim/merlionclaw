 2  The subject—the uninterrupted peak application—bears the aspect of pacification by nature, because it directly realizes all phenomena as being empty of true existence.  These states of the objects of observation and aspects of the uninterrupted peak application are very profound, because regarding these objects of observation and aspects, the propounders of the two truths as contradictory pass from one to another, and dispute, saying “If it is an observed object condition, it is truly existent; if it lacks true existence, then that contradicts it being an observed object condition.”  Sutra says, “Subhuti, the objects of observation of the exalted knower of all aspects are lacking the thing. The empowering is mindfulness. The aspect is pacification. The characteristic is the lack of characteristic.”  In this passage [p. 542], we see that the objects of observation, the empowering condition, and the aspect of the uninterrupted peak application are identified. First are the objects of observation. The objects of observation of the uninterrupted peak application, as stated in the text, are all illusory phenomena lacking true existence, and the reason for them being the objects of observation of the uninterrupted peak application is “because they are the bases for eliminating superimpositions” of the uninterrupted peak application.  The second paragraph describes the empowering condition: “The subject—the Mahayana generated mind, the mindfulness that is included within the path that is the application at the end of the continuum on the tenth ground…” This subject seems to say that the Mahayana generated mind is a kind of mindfulness, but it is not a mindfulness. We know that the Mahayana generated mind is a main mind, whereas mindfulness is a mental factor. How do we make sense of this description of the subject, which seems to say that the Mahayana generated mind is a mindfulness? We must remember that mindfulness has the function of holding a previously [familiarized] object of observation without forgetting it. Here in this syllogism, it is saying that the Mahayana generated mind is the empowering condition of the uninterrupted peak application; the reason is because the Mahayana generated mind is the condition that is self-powered with respect to generating the uninterrupted peak application. We would say that the Mahayana generated mind and a mindfulness are similar in function. Of course, we can also think in terms of the fact that in the retinue of the Mahayana generated mind, there is the mental factor of mindfulness. Maybe this is how we can understand the subject described here, but this is probably not the only way to understand it.   The third paragraph describes the aspect of the uninterrupted peak application: “The subject—the uninterrupted peak application—bears the aspect of pacification by nature, because it directly realizes all phenomena as being empty of true existence.” The aspect is basically “pacification by nature.”   The fourth paragraph explains why these states of the objects of observation and aspect of the uninterrupted peak application are very profound. The reason is “because regarding these objects of observation and aspect, the propounders of the two truths as contradictory pass from one to another, and dispute.” Are the two truths contradictory? Yes, they are, because there is no common locus between the two truths—there is nothing that you can posit that is both a conventional truth and an ultimate truth—therefore we would say that the two truths are contradictory. But in this expression—“the propounders of the two truths as contradictory”—the two truths as contradictory actually has a specific connotation. In general, the presentation of conventional truth and the presentation of ultimate truth are not contradictory. In other words, when the presentation of conventional truths is feasible, the presentation of ultimate truths is also feasible. Likewise, when the presentation of ultimate truths is feasible, the presentation of conventional truths is also feasible. In fact, the presentations of the two truths are not contradictory. However, some people propound that the presentations of the two truths are contradictory, in that if the presentation of one of the two truths is feasible, the presentation of the other truth is not feasible. These are the people being referred to in the expression, “propounders of the two truths as contradictory.” There are these propounders of the two truths as contradictory who, when presenting conventional truth, are unable

In the explanation [p. 545], it begins with the syllogism, “The subject—those wrong conceptions of all propounders, beings who do not know the skill in means of the divisions of the two truths of the bodhisattvas explained above—are not causes of correct repudiations…” You need to understand the function of a correct repudiation. It is generally explained that a correct consequence is able to counteract a one-pointed wrong conception (i.e. a wrong conception that holds to one point, being very decided without wavering), and to generate a doubt; once this one-pointed wrong conception is counteracted [by a correct consequence], then the person generates a doubt. A correct repudiation then counteracts that doubt.   In this first sentence, it is stated that these wrong conceptions are not causes of correct repudiations; in other words, they are not causes of counteracting doubts [because these wrong conceptions lack the function of a correct repudiation, i.e. they lack the function of being able to counteract a one-pointed wrong conception and a doubt, and thus “do not transcend the nature of doubt when they are well examined”]. In brief, these wrong conceptions of the propounders are not able to counteract wrong conceptions, and of course are not able to counteract doubts; because they are not able to counteract wrong conceptions, therefore they are also not able to counteract doubts.   Basically, there are these propounders who, if they present conventional truths, are not able to posit ultimate truths, and vice versa; if they present ultimate truths, they cannot posit conventional truths. In other words, they hold that the two truths are contradictory, in the sense that the presentations of the two truths are contradictory. As a result of this wrong understanding, there are sixteen wrong conceptions; [therefore,] there are these disputes. We say that these sixteen wrong conceptions are not causes of correct repudiations, because they do not transcend the nature of doubt when they are well examined. Questions and answers  Student: [This question is in relation to an initiation taking place the coming weekend.] What is the purpose and benefit of practicing the six-session guru yoga for a person with a coarse mind, or who has not yet attained renunciation or bodhicitta?  Geshe-la: Whether or not there is benefit in doing the practice of the six-session guru yoga depends on the individual. What do I mean by this? For example, if someone were to read a Dharma text with Mahayana motivation, because the mind is mixed with bodhicitta, regardless of what kind of Dharma text it is, the reading of the text becomes Mahayana Dharma. Whether or not it becomes a Mahayana practice does not depend on whether the text is a Mahayana text. If one does not have the realization of bodhicitta, then one can try to contrive a bodhicitta motivation. Through contemplating well, one tries to generate a mind that is similar to bodhicitta, in terms of the object of observation and aspect. After having generated that state of mind, if one reads texts, recites prayers, etc., then perhaps that activity becomes Mahayana Dharma. The second point—“(ii) accepts that a seed produces a sprout from its own side”—refers to conventional truth. Those propounders, having seen conventional truth and ultimate truth as contradictory, “dispute” (i.e. argue) “that they are infeasible.” In other words, they say that conventional truth and ultimate truth are contradictory, and are infeasible. They say if a seed produces a sprout from its own side, it should be established from the side of the object’s uncommon mode of subsistence without being posited through the force of appearing to an awareness. Our system says that these two points are actually not contradictory. On the one hand, the generation of a sprout by a seed is not established from the side of the object’s uncommon mode of subsistence without being posited through the force of appearing to an awareness; and on the other hand, a seed produces a sprout from its own side, so there is no contradiction for our system [since according to our system, all phenomena exist from their own side].   Related to this, be aware that both the Autonomy and Consequence schools assert that phenomena are merely imputed by conception. Although both schools assert this, they do not attach the same significance to the word “merely” in the expression, “phenomena are merely imputed by conception.” For the Consequence school, “merely” eliminates existence from the object’s own side, whereas for the Autonomy school, “merely” eliminates true existence.
